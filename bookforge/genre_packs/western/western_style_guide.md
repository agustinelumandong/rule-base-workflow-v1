# Western Narrative Style Guide

This style guide establishes the core narrative conventions for prose written or edited under the Western genre pack.

## 1. Direct and Rugged Prose
- **Plain Nouns & Active Verbs:** Favor concrete, physical nouns (iron, dirt, leather, bone, lead, pine, granite) over abstract terms. Avoid flowery adjectives or purple prose.
- **Varying Sentence Rhythms:** Use short, punchy sentences during action or high tension. Mix in longer, compound sentences describing the landscape to mirror the slow pacing of trail life.
- **Show Objective Behavior:** Avoid over-explaining a character's internal thoughts or feelings. Show their state through their actions: a hand hovering near a holster, spit hitting the dust, or a tight cinch being adjusted.

## 2. Setting as a Character
- **Active Environment:** The setting must actively influence character decisions, physical strain, and plot pacing. Scarcity of water, sudden desert storms, biting cold, and vast distances are obstacles to overcome, not wallpaper.
- **Sensory Grounding:** Focus on sensory details that define the frontier:
  - **Sights:** mesquite trees, low mesalands, heat shimmer, alkali dust rising behind hooves.
  - **Sounds:** creak of saddle leather, dry wind through canyon walls, distant coyote yip, iron shod hooves on flint.
  - **Smells:** sagebrush, wood smoke, rancid grease, dry pine.

## 3. Grounded Action and Tension
- **Realistic Violence:** Combat should be fast, brutal, and physically costly. Do not romanticize injuries. The impact of a lead ball on bone or the weight of a rifle should feel real.
- **Introspection in Scarcity:** Restrict introspection to quiet campfire scenes, night watches, or long rides, reflecting the isolation of the landscape.
