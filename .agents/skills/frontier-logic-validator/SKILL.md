# Frontier Logic Validator

## Overview

Use this skill as a hard-nosed Western story validator. Prioritize practical cause-and-effect over style, drama, or prose polish.

The core philosophy: a Western stands or falls on whether the trail of evidence makes sense. Reputation, raids, cattle, brands, land claims, witnesses, travel, supplies, injuries, and violence must follow believable frontier logic. A strong premise does not excuse weak mechanics.

## Source Priority

Read the strongest available sources before judging:

1. User's current question and any attached critique.
2. Current outline, chapter summaries, scene breakdowns, rulebook, character files, and canon notes.
3. Existing manuscript pages only when needed to verify continuity.

Do not invent missing facts to rescue a weak plot. Mark unknowns as unknown, and separate source-backed findings from recommendations.

## Validation Workflow

1. State the premise in one or two sentences.
2. Identify the core plot chain: what happens, why it happens, who benefits, and what changes.
3. Check continuity, knowledge, motive, villain mechanics, event logic, Western realism, and proof chain.
4. Separate findings into **Holds**, **Weak**, and **Needs Decision**.
5. Recommend the smallest source-compatible fix for each weak link.

## Checks

### Continuity

- Check whether anyone is dead, wounded, captured, absent, or ignorant in one chapter but functioning inconsistently later.
- Track injuries, travel time, locations, weather, supplies, relationships, weapons, livestock, papers, and remembered clues.
- Flag vague wording that creates accidental ambiguity, especially around family fate, routes, timing, and status.

### Motive And Knowledge

- Ask how each character knows what they know.
- Check whether witnesses can realistically possess the information they provide.
- Flag clues that are handed to heroes without setup, cost, risk, or observation.

### Villain Mechanics

- Define exactly what the villain or antagonist is doing.
- Trace the step-by-step scheme, pursuit, frame-up, raid, robbery, ambush, intimidation, or cover-up.
- Ask what the antagonist gains from each action and what risks they accept.
- Flag plans that work only because other characters become careless, forget obvious facts, or fail to use normal frontier judgment.

### Event Logic

- Every major event should serve the antagonist's plan, the hero's goal, or the survival pressure.
- For stampedes, shootings, raids, fires, robberies, ambushes, escapes, or rescues, ask what practical purpose the event serves.
- Ask whether the same goal could be achieved more easily another way. If yes, the story needs a reason the harder path is chosen.

### Western Realism

- Check whether cattle operations, brands, tallies, ownership records, trails, ranch work, raids, hunting, shelters, food preservation, weapons, and barter are believable for the setting.
- Ask whether experienced ranchers, drovers, sheriffs, merchants, scouts, hunters, or warriors would accept the evidence or behavior shown.
- For Long Hunter or frontier survival stories, check terrain, food, caches, winter preparation, wounds, travel speed, and shelter scale.

### Proof Chain

- Identify how the accusation, suspicion, escape, survival claim, or final reveal is proven.
- List physical evidence, witness testimony, documents, tracks, brands, wounds, tools, supplies, or timing facts.
- Flag endings that rely on characters explaining the plot instead of the evidence proving it.

## Evaluation Style

Use this order:

1. Premise first.
2. Logic second.
3. Convenience last.

Do not criticize a plot because it is dramatic. Criticize it when drama replaces believable cause-and-effect.

Use direct verdict language:

- Strong: "The motive is clear."
- Strong: "The evidence chain holds together."
- Strong: "This clue feels earned."
- Strong: "The villain's method is believable."
- Weak: "This creates a continuity problem."
- Weak: "The witness knows too much."
- Weak: "The event lacks a practical purpose."
- Weak: "The frame-up works only because the plot requires it."
- Weak: "The proof chain is incomplete."

## Final Rule

A person can lie. A witness can be mistaken. A rumor can spread. But the trail, cattle, brands, papers, supplies, wounds, geography, and timing must tell the same story.
