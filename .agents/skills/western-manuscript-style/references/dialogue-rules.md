# Dialogue Rules

Use these rules when drafting, revising, or reviewing Western dialogue.

## Core Dialogue Rules

- Keep dialogue short, direct, and anchored in the scene.
- Avoid dialogue tags such as `said`, `asked`, and `shouted` when the user requests em dash action anchors.
- Let voice come from word choice, sentence length, confidence, hesitation, and what the character refuses to say.
- Do not overuse dialect spelling. Prefer rhythm and phrasing over hard-to-read phonetic spelling.
- Keep characters from explaining what the scene already proves through action.

## Em Dash Action Anchors

Use this format when the user asks for em dash action anchors:

```text
"Get on the horse." — Harlan tightened the cinch.
```

The spoken line comes first. The action after the em dash grounds the speaker without using a tag.

## Distinct Voice Checks

Before returning dialogue, check:

- The lawman sounds controlled and direct.
- The outlaw sounds dangerous without cartoon villain speeches.
- The civilian sounds grounded in work, family, fear, duty, or survival.
- The speaker's social position and experience show in phrasing.
- No two major characters sound interchangeable.

## Examples

Prefer:

```text
"You got one chance." — Cade thumbed the hammer back.
```

Avoid:

```text
"You have one chance," Cade said angrily.
```

Prefer:

```text
"Door stays barred till sunup." — Sarah set the shotgun across the counter.
```

Avoid:

```text
"The door must remain barred until morning," Sarah exclaimed.
```
