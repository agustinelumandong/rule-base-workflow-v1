# Dialogue Rules

Use these rules when drafting, revising, or reviewing Western dialogue.

## Core Dialogue Rules

- Keep dialogue short, direct, and anchored in the scene.
- Avoid dialogue tags such as `said`, `asked`, and `shouted` when the user requests em dash action anchors.
- Let voice come from word choice, sentence length, confidence, hesitation, and what the character refuses to say.
- Do not overuse dialect spelling. Prefer rhythm and phrasing over hard-to-read phonetic spelling.
- Keep characters from explaining what the scene already proves through action.
- Use contractions in dialogue unless formality is intentional for the character. "I'll be home" not "I will be home."
- Dialogue must reveal relationship pressure, not just exchange information.
- If a line is generic, rewrite it with sharper image, clearer conflict, more subtext, more personal history, or stronger rhythm.
- Avoid redundant dialogue. If two lines say the same emotional thing, keep the sharper one or make the second line escalate.
- Characters interrupt, evade, accuse, soften, and circle the real issue. Avoid polished phrasing.
- Do not make readers infer missing nouns when the emotional moment depends on clarity ("Two more what?").

## Em Dash Action Anchors

Use this format when the user asks for em dash action anchors:

```text
"Get on the horse." — Harlan tightened the cinch.
```

The spoken line comes first. The action after the em dash grounds the speaker without using a tag.

## Distinct Voice Checks

Before returning dialogue, check:

- The lawman sounds controlled and direct.
- The outlaw sounds dangerous without cartoon villain speeches.
- The civilian sounds grounded in work, family, fear, duty, or survival.
- Children and young hostages sound younger under pressure: shorter, scared, practical, and less controlled than veteran lawmen or trail hands.
- Guides, scouts, and old hands may carry more terrain-specific phrasing or warning rhythm without drifting into caricature dialect.
- The speaker's social position and experience show in phrasing.
- No two major characters sound interchangeable.

## Character Voice Reference

| Character Type | Voice Rule | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Loner/cowboy | Complete statements, no questions, laconic, controlled | "It is enough." not "Is it enough?" |
| Frontierswoman | Shorter, more direct, commands, practical | "Then saddle him right." |
| Rancher/old hand | Slightly informal grammar, measured, grounded | "That don't make it light." |
| Villain | Controlled threats, no long speeches, repeats back | Repeats part of what others say before answering |
| Lawman/bounty | Restrained, factual, no exposition dumps | States facts without emotional coloring |

**Principle:** Each character speaks differently. Dialogue reveals personality without author explanation.

## Examples

Prefer:

```text
"You got one chance." — Cade thumbed the hammer back.
```

Avoid:

```text
"You have one chance," Cade said angrily.
```

Prefer:

```text
"Door stays barred till sunup." — Sarah set the shotgun across the counter.
```

Avoid:

```text
"The door must remain barred until morning," Sarah exclaimed.
```
