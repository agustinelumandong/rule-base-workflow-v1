# Western Character Rules

## Protagonist

The protagonist should be more than a Western silhouette.

Required fields:

- Clear external goal.
- Personal motive.
- Backstory pressure.
- Moral code.
- Flaw, wound, fear, debt, or unresolved cost.
- Skills and weaknesses.
- Relationship pressure.
- Arc direction and forbidden jumps.

The protagonist should make choices under pressure. Plot should not carry them passively from event to event.

## Antagonist

A strong Western antagonist is not just "bad." They need:

- Concrete goal.
- Believable motive.
- Method of pressure.
- Moral rationale or personal code.
- Power source.
- Weakness or blind spot.
- Relationship to the setting, law, money, violence, land, or community.

## Supporting Cast

Supporting characters must have a function:

- Ally.
- Witness.
- Pressure source.
- Temptation.
- Moral contrast.
- Information holder.
- Local authority.
- Labor, trade, or survival support.
- Consequence bearer.

Recurring supporting characters need profile depth proportional to their role. Incidental characters should stay descriptor-only unless promoted.

## Representation Rules

Women, Native characters, settlers from varied backgrounds, immigrants, townsfolk, workers, and marginalized characters must not be used as flat props.

Required handling:

- Give important characters agency, motive, and context.
- Avoid default stereotypes.
- Avoid using culture as decoration.
- Use approved research for historical and cultural portrayal.
- Require human review for cultural sensitivity risks.

## Character-Driven Conflict

Every major conflict should connect to at least one character driver:

- Goal.
- Fear.
- Loyalty.
- Betrayal.
- Debt.
- Survival.
- Revenge.
- Justice.
- Redemption.
- Protection.
- Shame.
- Greed.
- Moral code.

If conflict only exists because the plot needs motion, it needs redesign.
