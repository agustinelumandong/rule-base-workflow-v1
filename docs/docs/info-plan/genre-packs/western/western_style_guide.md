# Western Style Guide

## Core Intent

Western prose should feel grounded in a specific time, place, and pressure system. It should not read like generic adventure with hats and revolvers added later.

The guide is built around seven pillars:

1. Historical and cultural authenticity.
2. Character-driven conflict.
3. Thematic depth.
4. Setting as an active force.
5. Direct, physical prose.
6. Concise, period-aware dialogue.
7. Subgenre-aware tone.

## Prime Directive

A Western manuscript must be consistent, physical, era-accurate, and character-driven.

This means:

- The story respects canon, continuity, and established state.
- Scene meaning is shown through action, silence, movement, tools, weather, distance, injury, hunger, work, and consequence.
- Historical details match the year, location, culture, technology, and social context.
- Plot movement comes from character goals, pressure, moral choice, and cost.

## Prose Rules

- Use clear, direct prose.
- Prefer concrete action over abstract explanation.
- Avoid purple prose, melodrama, and decorative metaphor.
- Let emotion appear through behavior before explanation.
- Use physical details that affect choices, not scenery for scenery's sake.
- Balance action with character pressure and consequence.
- Do not spoon-feed the reader after the scene has already shown the meaning.

## Setting Rules

The Old West setting is not backdrop. It must affect the story.

Use:

- Land: plains, deserts, mountains, rivers, forests, towns, ranches, camps, trails, homesteads.
- Weather: heat, cold, storms, drought, mud, dust, snow, wind.
- Work: ranching, mining, farming, cattle drives, law work, trade, transport, repair, cooking, tending animals.
- Scarcity: water, ammunition, food, medicine, money, horses, shelter, daylight.
- Social pressure: saloon tension, town gossip, campfire loyalty, isolation, lawlessness, church and community norms.

Avoid:

- Setting that does not change what characters do.
- Generic "dusty town" description repeated without new function.
- Modern convenience logic in travel, medicine, communication, or supply.

## Theme Rules

Western themes should appear through decision and consequence, not lectures.

Common themes:

- Freedom versus civilization.
- Law versus lawlessness.
- Justice versus revenge.
- Survival against land, scarcity, and violence.
- Redemption after wrongdoing.
- Loyalty and betrayal.
- Good and evil with possible moral gray.
- Violence and its cost.

The theme must pressure the plot. If a theme can be removed without changing events, it is not yet operational.

## Subgenre Awareness

Classic, historical, frontier romance, revisionist, outlaw, and weird-west projects should not share the same tone by default.

The active subgenre controls:

- Protagonist expectations.
- Level of moral ambiguity.
- Degree of historical strictness.
- Violence and consequence profile.
- Romance, speculative, or historical emphasis.
- Reader expectations for ending tone.

## Failure Patterns

- Modern slang or modern therapeutic phrasing.
- Characters explaining theme in polished speeches.
- Fake cowboy parody.
- Women, Native characters, settlers, or townsfolk treated as props.
- Beautiful landscape description that does not affect behavior.
- Weapon, horse, travel, or supply logic that ignores the year and place.
- Character choices driven by plot convenience instead of goal, fear, loyalty, pressure, or moral code.
