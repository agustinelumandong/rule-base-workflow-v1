The Bell Account

Shadows on the Brazos

Book Four
Chapter 1: The Notice on the Gate

Ellie Calder James saw the paper on the gate before anyone called welcome.

It had been nailed to the top rail, folded once against the heat, with two bright nail heads driven through the upper corners. The paper moved a little when the wind came across the yard. Not enough to tear loose. Enough to show that some hand had wanted it seen.

Ellie drew her horse down before Paul spoke. Dust from the Fort Griffin road lay in the folds of her skirt and in the creases of her gloves. Her fingers ached from reins, paper, and saddle leather. Five days of hard return had left the horses gaunt through the barrel and dull in the eye.

At Fort Griffin, they had put Korth's cattle ledgers into federal hands and watched strangers take charge of proof carried through floodwater and gunfire. Gideon Cruz had escaped with the cipher page, leaving behind a damaged reference to the Bell account that none of them could fully read. Ellie had ridden home believing the hardest part would be waiting for the law to move.

Calder's Reach stood beyond the gate, familiar and wrong.

The barn doors were open. No cattle milled near the lot. No one came out laughing from the cookhouse. Sukey Varn stood in the yard with her apron tied over a work dress and both hands wrapped in the cloth as if she had forgotten what she meant to do with them. Lem Trowbridge waited by the gate, hat in hand. One of the younger hands sat his horse farther down the fence line, watching the lower pasture.

Paul stopped beside Ellie. He did not reach for the paper. His eyes moved from the gate to the yard, then to the cottonwood line by the county road.

"Who put that there?" he asked.

Lem swallowed. He had grown since the first bad months, but fear still made him look younger. "Two riders before sunup. One wore a county star. He left the citation here and made me nail it where you would see. The other stayed back on the road."

"Names?"

"Didn't give any."

Paul checked the road again. His right hand rested near his Colt Single Action Army, not on it.

Ellie dismounted. Her knees took a moment to trust the ground. She went to the gate and lifted one hand before touching the paper.

"Has anyone pulled at it?"

"No, ma'am," Lem said. "Sukey said to leave it for you."

Sukey came closer, her face drawn tight. "Looked official."

That was why she had left it alone. Ellie understood that. A dirty hand on a gate notice could become another lie by sundown if the right man wanted it so.

Ellie worked the left nail loose with her thumb and the edge of her knife, then the right. She kept the paper flat against the board until both nails came free. Only then did she lift it.

The district clerk's seal had been pressed into the paper beside a deputy's return of service. Minshew's bank name followed. A foreclosure petition on a claimed mortgage lien. A return hearing date written in a clerk's narrow hand.

Her throat dried before she reached the signature.

Paul swung down and stood on the other side of the gate. He left room between them. That steadied her more than crowding would have done.

"What is it?" he asked.

Ellie read the first lines again, slower. The words sat plain enough.

Citation upon a petition to foreclose a mortgage lien against the lands known locally as Calder's Reach, the claimed lien arising from a private note transferred through Minshew's bank to interests represented by Silas Bell.

Minshew's bank appeared twice. Bell's name once. That was enough.

"This is not a demand," Ellie said.

Paul looked at her.

"It is a foreclosure suit."

"It says my father mortgaged Calder's Reach to secure a note," she said.

Sukey made a low sound in her throat.

Lem looked from Ellie to Paul. "Can they do that?"

"They can write it," Ellie said.

Paul's jaw moved once. "Writing ain't doing."

"Sometimes it is," Ellie said.

The words settled over the yard. A horse stamped at the hitching rail. From the lower pasture came the thin call of a cow separated from the rest.

Ellie heard it then. Not the call alone, but the wrongness under it.

"Where are the lower cattle?"

Lem turned his hat in his hands. "Scattered. We found the south boundary cut."

Paul turned to him. "When?"

"Before first light. Three strands cut clean, near the live oak draw. Not rustled. Pushed."

"How many out?"

"Twenty, maybe more. Rafe and Josiah rode the line when we saw it. Mizpah sent her boy over to help gather."

Ellie kept the notice in both hands. "Any shots?"

"No, ma'am."

"Tracks?"

"Three riders along the fence. One horse with a cracked shoe." Lem looked at Paul. "They wanted us to follow mad."

Paul's eyes narrowed, not with surprise, but with the kind of understanding that made him quiet. "And did you?"

Lem shook his head. "Josiah said no."

"Good."

The word came flat. It held the yard better than shouting would have.

Ellie looked down at the paper again. Bell had not come with gunfire. He had sent paper at the gate and riders to the fence. One to frighten the hands. One to turn anger loose. One to make the ranch answer wrong before the first hearing.

She unfolded the lower half.

There was the pledge statement. There was the date. There was the bank witness mark. There was a signature written in a hand meant to pass for her father's.

Rhys Pryce.

The Bell account from Fort Griffin had been a damaged thing, half hidden in wet ink and broken marks. Here, Bell's name stood clean on county paper, pressed into the same lie in a hand a judge might trust.

For a moment the yard thinned around her. The gate rail, the dust, Paul's boots at the edge of her sight, all of it fell back behind the ink.

Her father's name sat on the paper like a man wearing another man's coat.

The first letter tried to look right. The tall stroke had the old lift. The curve near the middle had been practiced. Whoever made it had seen Rhys Pryce's hand before, or a copy of it.

But Rhys had written like he drove fence posts. He bore down at the beginning of a name and let the pen run hard through the rest. This hand pressed too lightly, then stopped where her father would not have stopped. The tail of Pryce curled back on itself, neat and clerkish. Rhys had never made it neat. His letters leaned when he was tired and cut deep when he was angry.

This signature did both wrong.

Ellie's attention settled on the date.

Her fingers tightened on the edge of the paper.

That date put her father in town on a week she remembered as clearly as a scar. Floodwater had broken the north crossing. Rhys had been at Calder's Reach for three days with a fever in his chest and mud to his knees, cursing the river while he helped save calves from the low ground.

He had not ridden to Minshew's bank. He had not signed a mortgage note. He had not pledged the ranch he had refused to sell even when drought had eaten half their margin.

"Ellie," Paul said.

She had not realized she had stopped breathing.

She drew air and folded the paper enough to shade the signature from the sun.

"It is not his hand."

No one spoke.

She touched the name without covering it. "Close enough for strangers. Not close enough for me."

Paul looked at the paper, then at her. "You are sure."

"Yes."

Lem let out a breath. Sukey crossed herself once and seemed angry for having done it.

Paul's voice stayed low. "What else does it say?"

Ellie read the lower lines. Each word had the hard, dry feel of a board laid across a doorway.

"Return hearing in five days before District Judge Ansel Pryor. Bell's interest runs through Minshew's bank record and a filed mortgage note. If we do not answer, Bell asks judgment on the lien."

"Moves forward to what?" Lem asked.

Ellie folded the notice along its old crease. "Marr has asked for sequestration. If Pryor grants the writ, the sheriff can take the cattle, teams, wagons, and branded equipment named in Bell's schedule and hold them for the court. We keep them only by giving bond."

The young hand by the lower fence rode closer. "Mrs. James?"

Ellie turned.

"Josiah's bringing in three head. Rafe found where the wire was cut. He says the riders came from the county road and went back the same way."

Paul took one step toward his horse.

Ellie saw the movement and caught his sleeve before he took another.

He stopped.

She did not have to pull. He looked down at her hand, then toward the county road again.

"They want that," she said.

"I know."

"Not just from you. From any man in this yard."

Paul studied Lem, the young hand, and the rifle Sukey had set beside the porch post. He took his hand away from his Colt and rested it on the saddle horn instead.

"Nobody rides after those men," he said.

Lem's shoulders lifted. "They cut our fence."

"And left you alive to follow."

"We cannot let them think they can touch the place."

"They already know they can touch wire," Paul said. "Do not teach them they can lead you by it."

The words settled hard, but they did settle. Lem looked away first.

Ellie folded the notice again, tighter. The paper cracked along one old crease. She stopped before it tore.

Sukey came to stand beside her. "What do you need?"

"A clean cloth. My father's old strongbox from the desk. And coffee if there is any left."

"There is."

"Then make it black."

Sukey nodded and went toward the house.

Paul turned to the younger hand. "Tell Josiah to keep gathering. No one crosses the south line alone. If Rafe has a track, he marks it and leaves it. We ride for records, not riders."

"Yes, sir."

The hand wheeled his horse and went.

Ellie faced the house. The porch boards needed washing. A shutter hung a little crooked where a hinge had worked loose. The yard had not changed much while they were gone, but the paper in her hand made every board and rail seem counted by someone else.

Calder's Reach had been raided, watched, and threatened before. Men had come by night with guns. Men had tried to burn what they could not take. That had been plain danger. This was a different thing. This paper did not smell of powder. It smelled faintly of ink, dust, and a clerk's dry room.

It could still steal a home.

Paul came beside her. "County seat?"

"The deed room first."

"Minshew?"

"After."

"Bell?"

She weighed the folded notice in her hand. "He can wait until I know which lie he thinks will hold."

Paul nodded once. "Rafe and Josiah?"

"Rafe should read the fence before we leave. Josiah stays until the cattle are back in and the hands are settled. Then he can follow or meet us in town."

"And if Bell sends more men?"

Ellie looked toward the south boundary. Heat shimmered over the pasture. The scattered cattle moved in small dark pieces beyond the draw.

"Then they find closed gates and witnesses," she said. "Not gun smoke."

Paul's mouth moved in that small way that was not quite a smile. "You sound sure."

"I am not."

"No?"

"I am angry."

He stood quiet beside her.

She looked down at her father's name again. "That will have to serve until I can be sure."

Sukey returned with a folded flour cloth. Ellie wrapped the notice in it, keeping the seal flat and the signature from rubbing. She could feel the shape of the paper through the cloth. Thin. Dry. Too little to carry so much harm.

Lem opened the gate at last.

The hinges complained. Ellie led her horse through on foot. She paused with one hand on the top rail and looked back at the road.

Somewhere beyond the heat, beyond the cottonwoods and county dust, Silas Bell had put his name behind a lie and sent it to her gate. He had not shown his face. He had not needed to.

Five days.

That was all the paper gave them before Bell could turn her home into someone else's property.
Chapter 2: The Burned Deed Room

The county courthouse smelled of smoke before Ellie reached the steps.

It came down the street in a thin, bitter thread, mixed with wet ash and the sour stink of soaked plaster. Men stood outside the courthouse with their hats in their hands, talking low as if the building were a sickbed. A wagon sat near the alley, its bed blackened with charred boards and split drawers pulled from somewhere inside. Water ran from the back door in a gray trickle and gathered in the wheel ruts.

Ellie drew her horse up beside Paul's. The sun had not yet climbed high, but heat already worked at the street. Four days. The number had ridden with her since dawn, through every hoofbeat from Calder's Reach.

Paul looked at the smoke-stained wall, then at her. "County clerk's deed room?"

"Yes."

He said nothing more. He knew the shape of it as well as she did. If Bell's notice was a hand laid on the ranch gate, this was a hand laid on the book that might prove him a liar.

They tied their horses at the rail. A man Ellie knew only by sight, a feed seller from the next block, turned away when she looked at him. Two women near the mercantile stopped speaking. News had already run faster than any rider.

At the courthouse door stood a deputy with a narrow brown mustache and a clean hat set a little too square on his head. He had one hand on the door frame and the other hooked in his belt, keeping the entrance without seeming to bar it.

"Mrs. James," he said. "Mr. James."

Ellie had seen him once before at a cattle hearing, standing behind the sheriff and looking bored. Now he looked awake.

"Deputy Nix," Paul said.

Caleb Nix nodded as if pleased to be remembered. "Bad morning for county business."

"Bad morning for records," Ellie said.

His eyes flicked to her, then away. "Clerk says the fire took the rear room hardest. We are keeping folks out till we know what can be saved."

"I need the Calder deed book."

"Figured you might." Nix gave a small, sympathetic lift of his brows. "I told Mr. Talbot you would come soon as you heard."

"We did not hear," Paul said. "We saw the smoke."

Nix's mouth paused in the shape of a smile that had not quite happened. "Then you made good time."

Ellie held the folded notice from Bell inside a flour cloth under her arm. She did not like the way Nix looked at the cloth, as if he already knew what shape lay inside it.

"Is Mr. Talbot in the deed room?" she asked.

"Back there. Tired half to death. He breathed more smoke than was good for him." Nix shifted, opening the door only enough for one person to pass. "You can look, but touch nothing unless Talbot says. Some pages will fall apart if breathed on."

"Then I will not breathe hard," Ellie said.

Paul made the smallest sound beside her. Not laughter. Something close enough to remember that he was there.

Nix stepped aside.

Inside, the courthouse hallway held a dimness that did not belong to morning. Smoke had stained the ceiling beams and left black fingers above the rear door. Men had carried buckets through mud and ash until the plank floor showed gray footprints from wall to wall.

The county clerk's deed room sat at the back of the building. Its door hung open. One hinge had warped from heat. Ellie stopped on the threshold.

The room had not burned like a room caught by accident.

The front desk stood mostly whole. The inkstand had cracked, but the account trays beside it were untouched. The chairs near the wall wore only soot. The worst damage lay along the record shelves in the rear corner, where deed books had been stored by range, section, and old survey names. One shelf had collapsed inward. Charred bindings slumped together like dead things. A metal drawer stood open, its label plate blackened, its contents gone to pulp from water.

Mr. Horace Talbot, the county clerk, sat on a stool with a cloth over his mouth and nose. His eyes were red. He looked smaller than Ellie remembered.

"Mrs. James," he said. His voice scraped. "I am sorry."

The words told her too much before he showed her anything.

Ellie came in slowly. Paul remained near the door, leaving room, watching Nix behind them.

"Show me," she said.

Talbot stood with effort and crossed to a worktable where several books had been laid open on dry cloth. Their covers had bubbled from heat. The pages inside were browned at the edges, some curled, some stuck together where water had carried ash between them.

"I pulled what I could," Talbot said. "The index survived in part. Pryce, Calder, Reach, and the old north boundary notes all point here."

He touched a volume with two fingers.

Ellie leaned over it. The smell rose at once: wet leather, burned rag paper, old ink disturbed by heat. Talbot had opened the book near the P section. Pryce names stood on one page, cramped and slanted by different clerks across years. There was Rhys Pryce's purchase notation from before Ellie was born. There was a later grazing easement. There was a reference line that should have led to the full Calder's Reach deed.

The page after it was gone.

Not burned through. Not lost in a black crumble. Gone.

Two narrow strips remained near the spine. The edges were pale beneath the soot, too clean for fire. Someone had cut the page away and left the rest of the book to blacken around the wound.

Ellie put both hands flat on the table to keep from touching it.

Paul saw her face. "Cut?"

"Yes."

Talbot closed his eyes.

Nix spoke from near the door. "Could have come loose when they carried it out."

Ellie did not look at him. "Pages do not come loose with knife edges."

"No offense meant."

"Then none taken."

But she did take it. She took it and set it beside the other things she would remember.

Talbot coughed into the cloth. "I searched the floor. I searched the ash bucket. If it burned, I found no piece of it."

"It did not burn here," Ellie said.

The room went still.

Nix came a step closer. "That is a hard thing to prove."

"So is a pledged ranch," Ellie said. "Yet men seem willing to try."

Paul moved half a pace, enough that Nix noticed him without being able to call it a threat.

Nix looked from Paul back to Ellie. "If the page is gone, where do you mean to look next?"

Ellie folded one corner of the flour cloth over Bell's notice. "Wherever the paper went before it came here."

"That leaves a wide road."

"Then we had better start early."

Ellie took the Bell notice from the flour cloth and laid it on a clean corner of the table. She did not unfold it fully. Only enough to show Talbot the claimed signature and filing date.

"Was this filed through this office?"

Talbot bent over it. His eyes watered from smoke, but he forced them steady. "A notice copy came through yesterday evening. The mortgage packet was not mine. That would be bank and court filing."

"Does the missing page kill the deed?" Paul asked.

"No. A burned record does not burn the title with it. If an original or certified copy survives, the law lets us record it again. If not, proof can be taken to restore what was lost." Talbot looked at the wet ruin on his table. "But restoration takes testimony, notice, and time. Bell means to use the days before we can do it."

"Who brought it?"

Talbot hesitated.

Nix answered for him. "Minshew's clerk carried papers to the court desk near closing. I saw the boy pass the hall."

Ellie lifted her eyes then. "Near closing?"

"Near enough," Nix said. "I did not ask his errand."

That was the kind of answer that made itself clean by washing away every useful part.

Talbot's hand trembled against the table. "My assistant may have seen more."

"Where is she?"

Nix looked toward the rear wall. "Clara Whitcomb has not reported this morning."

Talbot looked down.

Ellie saw it.

It was a small thing. The old clerk's eyes did not go to the outer door, where an absent assistant might be expected to appear. They went to the side cabinet, the one with a warped bottom panel and a stack of rolled maps leaning against it.

Nix was looking at Ellie, not Talbot.

Ellie folded the notice again. "Mr. Talbot, do you have any surviving index reference to the north boundary?"

"In the side cabinet," he said.

Nix turned his head. "I thought those drawers were water-soaked."

"Some are."

Ellie crossed before Nix could offer to help. The cabinet smelled of wet wood. She moved the rolled maps aside and opened the upper drawer. Nothing inside but damp survey copies. She opened the second. More papers, all swollen and soft at the edges.

From below the lowest shelf came the faintest breath.

Ellie did not move at once. She took a survey copy, shut the drawer, and turned.

"Paul," she said, "would you ask Deputy Nix whether the sheriff has posted a guard at the alley?"

Paul understood before his face changed. He stepped into the doorway, polite as a church bell.

"Deputy."

Nix looked from Ellie to Paul. "What?"

"The alley. If someone comes back for what they missed, I would hate to be standing in here admiring the ashes."

Nix frowned. "There are men outside."

"Men talking by the street. Not men watching the back."

For a moment Nix seemed ready to argue. Then Talbot coughed again, hard enough to bend him over the table, and the deputy made a show of duty.

"Stay in this room," he said.

Paul gave him no answer at all. He simply held the doorway until Nix went out.

Ellie knelt by the cabinet.

"Clara," Talbot whispered.

The bottom panel shifted. A young woman crawled from the cabinet space with ash on her sleeves and her hair pinned badly at one side. She was perhaps twenty, perhaps younger; fear had made her age hard to read. Her hands shook so badly she had to grip the cabinet frame before she could stand.

"Do not let him take me," she said.

Paul looked toward the hall. "Nix?"

Clara swallowed. "Any of them."

Ellie rose slowly, keeping her hands where Clara could see them. "I am Ellie James. You know that?"

Clara nodded.

"Mr. Talbot says you may have seen the papers."

"I did not read them all." Clara's voice came thin. "I swear I did not."

"I am not asking what you stole," Ellie said. "I am asking what you saw."

That steadied her a little.

Clara looked at Talbot, then at the door. "Mr. Minshew came after hours. Not his clerk. Him. He had a packet tied in red string. Deputy Nix was in the hall. I thought it was bank tax work, but Mr. Talbot had already locked the deed cabinet."

Talbot shut his eyes again.

"What did Minshew do?" Ellie asked.

"He told me the court desk needed it marked received before morning. I said Mr. Talbot had to enter it. He said Mr. Talbot would thank me for not waking him." Clara's mouth worked. "I stamped the cover. I should not have."

"What did the cover say?"

"Mortgage transfer. Pryce. Calder's Reach. Minshew bank." She rubbed at her wrist as if ink remained there. "There was a signature page showing through the tie. Fresh paper. Too white for the older copies."

Ellie felt the room narrow around that answer.

"Did you see any marks?"

Clara nodded too quickly. "Numbers in the corner. Not filing numbers. Little marks beside names. Like a bell shape, maybe. I thought it was because of the church fund."

Ellie held still. "What church fund?"

From the hall came Nix's boots on the boards.

Clara flinched toward the cabinet.

Paul stepped closer to the doorway.

"Answer me," Ellie said softly.

Clara's eyes filled. "The bell fund. Deacon Vale's ledger. I saw those same marks once when Mr. Bell paid for the church bell."

Nix's shadow crossed the threshold.

Ellie folded the Bell notice inside the flour cloth and held it tight.

The missing page was no longer the only thing that had to be kept safe. Clara Whitcomb knew when Minshew came, what he carried, and whose mark had hidden itself beside the money. That made her more than a frightened clerk's assistant.

It made her a witness.

The Calder page was gone, but Bell's lie had rung a bell somewhere else.
Chapter 3: Bell Comes Calling

Paul saw Silas Bell before anyone named him.

The man came up the courthouse street without hurry, walking in the clean strip of shade along the boardwalk as if the county had laid it there for him. His coat was summer-weight black, brushed free of dust. His hat was pale and shaped well. He carried no pistol in sight, which made Paul study the men near him.

One was a lawyer by the cut of him: narrow shoulders, clean cuffs, soft hands wrapped around a leather case. The other stood half a pace behind Bell and a little to his right. Tall, plain-faced, and watchful. His coat did not sit right over the shoulder, where a holster strap or knife sheath could have lifted the cloth. He looked at doors, alleys, windows, and hands.

That would be the dangerous one, Paul thought, if Bell ever needed danger to have a name.

Ellie stood beside him with the folded notice wrapped in flour cloth under her arm. Smoke from the deed office still clung to her skirt hem. She had not said much since they stepped from the room. Paul knew that silence. She was measuring what the missing page meant and setting Clara Whitcomb's frightened words beside it.

The street had filled while they were inside. Men drifted from the mercantile and livery. Two women stood under the awning by the dry goods window. A boy with a broom in his hand had stopped sweeping. The burned deed room had made a crowd. Bell's arrival gave that crowd a reason to face one way.

"Mr. Bell," someone called.

Bell turned his head with an easy smile. "Mr. Arnett."

"Church bell sounds fine of a Sunday," the man said.

"A bell ought to carry," Bell answered. "Else there is no use hanging one."

A few men smiled. Not much. Enough.

Paul felt Ellie go still beside him.

Bell climbed the courthouse steps and removed his hat before he reached them. He was not an old man, but he carried himself like one who expected younger men to step aside. His hair had silver at the temples. His eyes were gray and mild in a way Paul did not trust.

"Mrs. James," Bell said. "Mr. James."

Paul had not given him his name. That did not surprise him. It did set one more mark on the board.

"Silas Bell," Ellie said.

"I had hoped our first meeting might come under calmer circumstances." Bell looked past them toward the smoke-stained hall. "A county office burning helps no one."

"It helped whoever cut out the Calder page," Ellie said.

The lawyer's mouth tightened. Bell's did not.

"You are direct. Your father was the same way, though he took longer to arrive at it."

Paul looked at Ellie. Her face did not change, but her fingers closed a little harder around the wrapped notice.

Bell noticed. Of course he did.

"You knew my father?" she asked.

"I offered Rhys Pryce a fair price for Calder's Reach once. More than fair, considering the state of his water rights at the time." Bell gave a small, regretful motion with his hat. "He declined before witnesses and made the matter more public than it needed to be."

"He said no," Ellie said.

"Some men dress refusal as honor because it costs them nothing in the moment." Bell's eyes moved toward the courthouse, then back. "Later, it often costs their families."

Paul felt the heat rise through his chest, not quick and wild, but steady. Bell had put Rhys Pryce, Ellie, debt, and threat on the same table without raising his voice.

The tall man behind Bell watched Paul now. Waiting.

Paul kept his hands loose.

"You came to mourn the deed room?" Paul asked.

Bell's eyes shifted to him. "I came to prevent further harm."

"You brought a lawyer for grief?"

One or two men in the street made a sound under their breath. Bell let it pass.

"Mr. Marr is counsel to my interest in the filed claim." Bell nodded to the narrow man with the leather case. "And Mr. Stowe helps me keep business from becoming disorder."

Mercer Stowe did not nod. He only watched.

Ellie looked at the lawyer. "Marr?"

"Everett Marr," the lawyer said.

"I will remember it."

Paul liked that answer more than he should have.

Bell smiled faintly. "Mrs. James, I do not enjoy pressing a widow's household."

"I am not a widow."

"No. Of course." Bell inclined his head toward Paul with courtesy so polished it cut. "My apology. Old habits of thought. The county knew Calder's Reach as your burden for some time."

Paul took one slow breath.

Bell continued before the silence could break in Paul's favor. "I am prepared to offer a settlement. Quietly, if you prefer. You and your husband may leave Calder's Reach with household goods, personal stock, and a sum sufficient to begin again elsewhere. No public fight. No ruinous hearing. No further injury to your father's name."

Ellie stared at him. "You offer to let me carry my own goods from my own house."

"I offer to spare you the cost of proving a claim you cannot support without the county deed page."

That put a murmur through the street. Bell did not look around to enjoy it. He did not need to.

Paul understood then why Bell had come outside instead of sending his lawyer indoors. He wanted eyes on Ellie. He wanted her anger seen and his calm remembered.

"The page was cut out," Paul said.

Bell looked at him with mild interest. "So I hear."

"Quick hearing."

"Fire draws talk."

"Some talk travels before smoke clears."

Stowe's gaze sharpened. Bell's smile thinned and returned.

"You have a soldier's distrust, Mr. James."

"I was never much of a soldier."

"No? Men say different. They say you have a gift for surviving ugly ground."

Paul said nothing.

Bell stepped one pace closer, lowering his voice just enough that the nearest watchers leaned without knowing it. "Then understand this ground. Land is not saved by sentiment. A ranch is water, timber, fence, beef, credit, and title. Men who cannot hold all five should not be surprised when men who can take interest."

"Take interest," Ellie said.

"Develop it," Bell said. "Bring order. Roads. Church bells. Schools. Markets fit for more than a handful of stubborn families." He looked toward the street where the crowd listened. "The frontier is wasted on men who only know how to hold land, not use it."

There it was. Not greed, not spoken plain. A belief made clean enough for public ears.

Paul watched the crowd take it in. Some men looked down. Some looked as if Bell had said what they already believed but would not say at the feed store.

The east-road freight agent stood among them in a coat Bell's contracts had helped pay for. A school trustee studied his boots beneath a roof Bell had donated timber to mend. Bell's money had entered the county as wages, credit, boards, and favors. Men who had eaten from it would need more than anger before they called it theft.

Ellie's voice stayed level. "My father used Calder's Reach."

"Your father clung to it."

Paul's hand wanted to close.

Bell's eyes came back to him, soft and waiting.

Paul turned the want into stillness. He had learned that on bad ground too. A man could spend anger or hold it. Bell was counting on him spending it.

Ellie touched Paul's sleeve. Not to stop him. To tell him she knew.

The courthouse door opened behind them. District Judge Ansel Pryor stepped out, his black coat buttoned wrong at the middle, as if someone had called him from papers or breakfast. He was in the county for the sitting of his district court. Deputy Nix stood behind him in the hall.

Paul noticed Nix notice everyone.

"Mr. Bell," Pryor said. "Mrs. James. This street is not my hearing room."

"No, Judge," Bell said. "I was attempting a settlement."

"Generous," Everett Marr murmured, holding his leather case against his ribs.

Ellie said, "I refused."

Pryor looked tired already. "Then the matter stands."

Marr stepped forward. "Your Honor, in light of the fire and the uncertainty surrounding county records, my client will apply for a writ of sequestration. Delay risks removal or waste of the stock and equipment pledged in the mortgage schedule."

Paul heard the trap open. Bell had burned the record, then used the absence of record as reason to move faster.

Pryor frowned. "The notice names five days."

"Five days from service," Marr said. "But a writ may issue before judgment upon petition, affidavit, and bond. Livestock can be driven. Wagons and harness removed."

"You think I would strip my own ranch to avoid your false claim?" Ellie asked.

Marr bowed slightly. "I think mortgaged property has a way of crossing a county line."

Paul looked toward Nix. The deputy's eyes were on the wrapped notice under Ellie's arm, not on the lawyer.

Pryor lifted one hand. "Enough. I will not hear this in the street. The return hearing remains on the docket. Five days from service. I will issue no writ without a filed petition, sworn cause, a particular description of the property, and the bond the law requires."

Today, Paul thought. The word sat badly.

Bell accepted the ruling with a graceful nod. "As the court wishes."

Pryor looked at Ellie. "Mrs. James, if you mean to contest the mortgage note or move against any writ, bring what proof you have. Copies, witnesses, survey references. I will not decide this by accusation."

"I have no wish to," Ellie said.

Bell put his hat back on. "Speaking of witnesses, Judge, I hope Mrs. James is careful whom she trusts. Gideon Cruz has made liars of better people than any standing here."

The name struck the air so cleanly Paul almost turned toward Ellie.

Almost.

He kept his eyes on Bell.

Ellie did not move, but Paul felt the change in her. Cruz was not supposed to be a public name yet. Not here. Not from Bell's mouth.

Pryor's brows drew together. "Cruz?"

Bell looked mildly surprised by his own mention. "A criminal bookkeeper once attached to Korth's cattle business. Men say he has been seen on the river roads. If damaged accounts are being waved about, I would expect his shadow somewhere near them."

"You know a lot of shadows," Paul said.

Bell looked at him. "I employ men who bring me facts."

Stowe shifted one hand at his side. Not toward a gun. Just enough to remind Paul that facts could arrive in many shapes.

Pryor's voice hardened. "This is done. Mr. Bell, file through my clerk. Mrs. James, keep your witnesses available. Mr. James, keep peace at Calder's Reach. I will not have county business answered by rifles."

Paul met the judge's eyes. "Nor will I."

Bell smiled as if that answer pleased him. "A wise husband."

There was the last hook. Small, barbed, made for public blood.

Paul let it pass.

Bell descended the steps. Men made room for him. One shook his hand. Another asked about the road work east of town. Bell answered both before he reached his carriage. Mercer Stowe stayed a moment longer, looking at Paul and Ellie as if fixing their measure, then followed.

Only when Bell was gone did Paul feel how hard his jaw had been set.

Ellie unfolded one edge of the flour cloth and looked down at the notice as if the paper had changed weight. "He knows about Cruz."

"He wanted us to know he knows."

"Then Clara cannot stay near the deed room."

Paul looked toward the courthouse hall, where Nix had disappeared behind Judge Pryor.

"No," he said. "She cannot."
Chapter 4: The Hidden Witness

Josiah Breckin did not like the loft.

It had one stair, one alley window, and too many boards that talked under a man's weight. Hay dust lay in the corners. Saddle blankets hung from pegs along the wall. A cracked harness trunk sat under the window, and the smell of horses rose through the floorboards from the stalls below.

It was better than the deed room. That did not make it good.

He stood at the top of the stair with his hat pushed back and listened to the livery settle for evening. Horses shifted below. A chain knocked softly against a stall board. Out in the street, men moved toward supper and lamps. The courthouse bell had not rung, but the town already felt as if it had been called to gather.

Clara Whitcomb sat on an upturned grain box with both hands folded in her lap. She had washed the ash from her face, but some still clung near one ear and at the cuff of her sleeve. Fear had made her small. Shame had made her smaller.

Ellie stood near the lantern with the Bell notice wrapped and tucked close. Paul watched the street through a crack in the loft door, his body still in the way of any man coming up.

"How long?" Paul asked.

"Until dark settles," Josiah said. "Then we move her."

Clara looked up. "Move me where?"

"Someplace that is not the first place a fool would search."

That answer did not comfort her. Josiah had not meant it to. Comfort was a thin blanket in weather like this. Better to give a person work.

He crossed to the window and set one finger against the frame. It opened inward with a tired groan. The alley below held a rain barrel, stacked feed sacks under a lean-to, and a narrow path between the livery and a back fence. A man could climb if he had nerve and time. A man could also shoot from below if he knew where to stand.

Josiah shut the window and dropped the latch.

"If someone comes through there, you go flat behind the trunk," he told Clara. "Do not stand. Do not look. Do not ask who it is."

Clara stared at him.

"Say it back."

"Flat behind the trunk," she whispered.

"Good. If someone comes up the stair, you get behind that feed bin. If I tell you to crawl, you crawl. If I tell you to run, you run without taking anything."

"I have nothing."

"Then you will be quick."

Ellie's eyes moved to him for a moment. He could not tell whether she approved. It did not matter. A witness had to live long enough for approval to mean anything.

Paul stepped away from the door. "Nix crossed the street twice since we came in."

"Looking here?"

"Looking everywhere but here."

Josiah grunted. That was worse. A man looking directly at a door wanted you to know he had looked. A man looking everywhere else wanted you to forget his eyes.

Ellie turned to Clara. "Tell Josiah what you told me. Slowly."

Clara's fingers tightened together. "I already told you."

"Now tell him."

"Why?"

Josiah answered before Ellie could. "Because if Mrs. James is pulled away, I still need to know what I am keeping alive."

Clara flinched at that word. Alive.

Josiah let it stand.

For a while Clara said nothing. A wagon passed in the street below, wheels knocking over a rut. Somewhere a door slammed. She looked toward the loft stair as if each sound had come for her.

"Mr. Minshew came after hours," she said at last. "Not his clerk. He said the packet needed court receipt before morning. Mr. Talbot had gone home. I told him I could not enter deed or court papers without Mr. Talbot."

"What did Minshew say?" Josiah asked.

"That I was not entering it. Only marking that it had arrived." Clara swallowed. "He said if a little stamp frightened me, county work was not my calling."

Josiah knew men who used shame like a spur. They liked it because it left no mark a judge could see.

"Deputy Nix was there?" Ellie asked.

"In the hall. He spoke to Mr. Minshew before Minshew came in. I could not hear what they said."

Paul's mouth flattened.

Josiah kept his eyes on Clara. "The packet."

"Red string. Fresh fold. Bank seal on the outside. Minshew's fingers were inked, like he had signed too many things too fast. The top sheet said mortgage transfer. Pryce. Calder's Reach. Minshew bank." She shut her eyes. "I saw the signature page through the string. White paper. Newer than the older backing copies. It did not look like it belonged with them."

"And the marks?"

"Little marks near the account numbers. Not filing marks. Bell shapes, maybe. I thought of the church ledger because Deacon Vale's book has little bell marks beside donations."

"You saw Bell's name?"

"Not written full. Initials, maybe. S.B. once. I cannot swear to more than that."

Ellie nodded, but Clara's voice broke before she could be praised or questioned again.

"I cannot say this in court."

The loft went quiet.

Paul turned from the door. Ellie did not move.

Josiah watched Clara press her hands so hard together the knuckles paled.

"You can," Ellie said.

"No." Clara shook her head. "You do not know what it is to have all of them looking. Mr. Bell. Mr. Minshew. Deputy Nix. Judge Pryor. Men who know my father owes at the mercantile. Men who can say I stamped wrong, remembered wrong, lied to save my post." Her breath came too fast. "I hid in a cabinet. That is what I did. I hid."

Josiah pulled a nail keg closer with his boot and sat across from her. The wood creaked under him.

"I have hidden," he said.

Clara looked at him, startled.

"Behind stone. Behind brush. Behind a dead horse once, because it was the only cover left. Hiding is not the shameful part."

She did not answer.

"The shameful part is staying hidden after the danger passes and calling it wisdom."

Ellie looked down at the lantern flame. Paul looked back to the street.

Clara's eyes filled, but no tears fell. "The danger has not passed."

"No," Josiah said. "It has not."

Below them, the livery door opened.

Paul lifted one hand for silence.

Josiah stood without haste and moved to the stair head. A man's boots crossed the stable floor. Not the liveryman's shuffle. Younger. Lighter. Trying to be official and careless at once.

"James?" Deputy Nix called from below. "Mrs. James?"

Paul moved to the top of the stair before Josiah could stop him, then stopped of his own sense. He stayed far enough back that Nix could hear him but not see into the loft.

"Deputy," Paul said.

"Judge wants everyone close to hand. There is talk of statements being taken tomorrow. Where is Mrs. James?"

"Busy."

"With the clerk's girl?"

Clara's breath caught.

Josiah turned his head just enough to look at her. She put one hand over her mouth.

Paul answered, "If you mean Miss Whitcomb, last I saw she was scared half to death in a burned room."

"I am trying to keep scared people from making trouble for themselves," Nix said. He climbed two steps. "If she is with you, best I know it. For her protection."

Josiah stepped into view.

Nix stopped.

"Deputy," Josiah said.

"Breckin." Nix smiled without much warmth. "Did not know you were in town."

"You know now."

"Where are you putting her tonight?"

"Putting who?"

Nix sighed. "No need to play cards with me. Bell's lawyer will ask for witness lists. If the girl is a witness, she needs to be available."

"Available to court," Josiah said. "Not to every man who asks."

Nix's hand rested on the stair rail. "You accusing me of something?"

"I am answering you."

The deputy looked past him, trying for the room. Josiah moved one boot six inches. Not enough to threaten. Enough to close the line.

For a moment neither man spoke.

Then Nix stepped down. "Suit yourself. But if she runs, that will look bad for Mrs. James."

"Then she will not run."

"You sound sure."

"I am standing on the stair."

Nix's face tightened, then smoothed. "Evening, then."

The livery door opened again. Closed.

Paul waited three breaths before speaking. "He came fast."

"He came informed," Josiah said.

Ellie crossed to Clara. "We will not make you testify tonight."

"But you will later."

"Yes."

The honesty hurt more than a softer lie might have. Clara lowered her head and nodded once.

Night settled by pieces. Ellie and Paul left before full dark so the watchers would follow them away from the livery. Josiah kept Clara in the loft with the lantern turned low and his chair set where he could see the stair and the window by turning only his head.

The liveryman, old Sutter, had agreed to bed down near the front with a double-barrel 12-bore hammer shotgun across his knees. Josiah trusted the man's double gun less than he trusted the creak of the boards.

Near midnight, the boards spoke.

Not below. Outside.

The alley window gave a small tremble. Clara woke at once from the thin sleep she had fallen into against the feed bin. Josiah put one finger to his lips.

The latch lifted a hair.

Josiah was already moving.

He crossed the loft low, took the harness hook from the wall, and slid its iron end through the window ring just as the latch rose again. The man outside pushed. The window held.

From below came a whisper. "Break it."

Clara began to rise.

Josiah pointed to the trunk.

She dropped flat.

The glass cracked under a knife hilt. A hand came through, sleeve wrapped against the shards. Josiah caught the wrist with both hands, turned it hard, and drove the man's knuckles into the frame. The knife fell outside with a dull tap.

The man swallowed a cry.

Josiah leaned close to the broken pane. "Wrong window."

The wrist tore free. Boots scraped down the alley wall. Someone cursed low. A horse snorted below, then another. Sutter shouted from the front room, late but loud.

Josiah did not fire. A shot would bring the street, the sheriff, Nix, and every explanation Bell wanted.

He stayed beside the window until the alley went quiet again.

Clara had not moved from behind the trunk. Her face showed white in the low lantern light.

"They came for me," she said.

"Yes."

"Because of what I saw."

"Yes."

She sat up slowly, shaking harder than before. "Deacon Vale keeps the ledger in the church storehouse, not the church. He locks it in a tin box under the bell rope chest."

Josiah turned from the window.

"You are sure?"

"I helped copy donation names last winter. The marks were there. Little bells beside numbers. I thought it was only his way of keeping church accounts." She wiped at her face with her sleeve. "If Mr. Bell used those marks, it will be in that book."

Josiah looked toward the broken window, then toward the stair.

The witness was alive. The hiding place was spent. By morning, Bell's people would know they had failed, and Nix would be asking clean questions with dirty ears.

Four days remained before Judge Pryor expected proof instead of fear.

"Then we move before breakfast," he said.

Clara stared at the broken pane. "Where?"

"Toward the bell."
Chapter 5: Luz Returns

By morning, the livery loft was behind them, but the broken window had followed Ellie into the brush.

She could still hear the faint crack of glass under a knife hilt. She could still see Clara Whitcomb crouched behind the harness trunk, white-faced and shaking, while Josiah stood between her and the alley. The hiding place had held for one night. No more.

Now they sat in a dry wash half a mile beyond the county road, where mesquite and scrub oak broke the shape of the camp from town eyes. The horses stood saddled. No fire burned. Breakfast was cold biscuit, hard cheese, and coffee gone bitter in a tin cup passed hand to hand.

Three days remained before Judge Pryor expected proof.

Ellie had counted them once while wrapping the Bell notice. She had counted them again while checking the cloth around Clara's written statement. She had counted them a third time when Rafe came back from the road and said no rider had followed them out of town that he could see.

That last phrase mattered. That he could see.

Clara sat with her back to a low bank, wrapped in Sukey's old shawl though the morning was already warm. Josiah stood near enough to reach her in three steps. He had slept little and showed none of it except for the stillness around his eyes.

Paul watered the horses from a canvas bucket. Rafe crouched near the road edge, looking at dust Ellie could not read. The church storehouse lay one road east with Deacon Amos Vale's ledger and the bell marks Clara remembered. Somewhere west or south, Gideon Cruz carried the cipher key that might make those marks speak.

Ellie hated choices that looked like leaving proof behind.

Paul came back with the bucket empty. "We can be at the church storehouse by midday if we move now."

"If Nix has not guessed Clara's clue," Ellie said.

"He will guess something."

"That is not comfort."

"Was not meant as comfort."

Rafe stood from the road edge. "Rider coming."

Paul reached for his Winchester Model 1873 carbine, not lifting it yet. Josiah moved Clara behind him without speaking. Ellie folded Clara's statement and tucked it inside her jacket.

"One?" Paul asked.

"One horse," Rafe said. "Rider is walking it through brush instead of road."

"Bell's?"

Rafe tilted his head, listening. "No. Bell's riders like being seen when fear helps them. This one does not."

A bay horse pushed through the mesquite first, head low, reins loose. The woman beside it wore a dark hat pulled down and a man's coat too broad in the shoulders. Dust marked her skirt to the knee. Her left sleeve had been torn and tied tight with a strip of yellow cloth.

Luz Espina stopped when she saw the Winchester in Paul's hands.

"If you mean to shoot," she said, "do it before I waste breath."

Paul's face did not change. "You had breath enough last time to sell a trail."

Luz looked at him, then at Ellie. "And you had sense enough to keep moving after."

"That is not an apology," Paul said.

"No."

"Good. I would not have believed one."

The words struck, but Luz did not flinch. She looped her reins around one hand and stood with the bay between herself and no one. That was something Ellie noticed. Luz had come in ready to run, but not ready to hide behind the horse.

"Why are you here?" Ellie asked.

"Because Cruz is alive."

The camp changed shape around the name. Rafe's attention sharpened. Clara looked from face to face, confused by a danger she did not yet know. Josiah did not move, but his hand rested closer to the worn Colt Army conversion at his hip.

"You have seen him?" Ellie asked.

"His trail. Two nights old. Then one night old. Then fresher where he cut back toward the river road."

Paul gave a dry sound. "Convenient."

Luz faced him. "Nothing about Cruz is convenient. He doubles back when he should run straight. He camps cold when a fire would save him. He leaves horses worse than he found them and pays with coin that makes men remember his face."

Rafe stepped closer. "Where?"

"Old cedar spring first. Then Marrow crossing. Then a goat camp south of the mission road."

Rafe's eye narrowed. "Goat camp has no water."

"It has a seep if you know which stone to lift."

Rafe said nothing for a moment. Then, "Could be."

Paul still watched Luz as if she were a snake in flour. "Why bring this to us?"

Luz reached into her coat.

Paul lifted the Winchester an inch.

She stopped. "Slow, then."

With two fingers she drew out a folded paper and a small coin tied in cloth. She tossed both into the dust between them.

Ellie bent and picked up the paper first. It had no full name, only a mark in dark pencil and three words: safe south road. The coin was a twenty-dollar gold piece, too much money for a friendly errand.

"Stowe?" Ellie asked.

"One of his men. Scar at the chin. Gray horse. He found me before dawn yesterday and said Mr. Bell pays better than frightened ranch wives."

Paul's mouth hardened.

Luz looked at Ellie, not him. "He offered more when I did not answer. Money. Passage. A clean name south of the river. All I had to do was put you on the wrong creek and tell them where Cruz would cross."

Ellie indicated the yellow cloth tied around Luz's sleeve. "And when you still did not answer?"

Luz glanced down as if she had forgotten the cloth was there. "He took hold of me."

"Stowe's man?"

"He was not quick enough."

"You cut him?"

"Enough to leave." Luz's mouth tightened. "Not enough to stop him from following later, if he wants his pride back."

Paul looked toward the road behind her. "So you may have brought him with you."

"Maybe." Luz did not soften the word. "I circled twice and walked the horse through stone. If he stayed on me after that, your Ranger will see him before I do."

Rafe gave no promise. He only turned and studied the east edge of the wash, already working the possibility through the dirt.

"And you came here instead," Ellie said.

"I came here after thinking about it."

That was more useful than a noble lie.

"Why?" Ellie asked.

Luz glanced toward the road behind her. "Because Bell's men do not buy a person. They use her once, then make her the loose end. I have been poor. I have been wanted. I have been called faithless by people who had reason. I have no wish to be buried for being useful."

"So this is fear," Paul said.

"Partly."

"Partly what else?"

Luz's eyes moved to Clara, then Josiah, then back to Ellie. "Partly I did not like seeing Cruz hunted by his own side."

Rafe gave a low, humorless breath. "Cruz has a side?"

"He had buyers. Paymasters. Men who liked his books as long as the books stayed closed." Luz looked down at the paper in Ellie's hand. "Now Bell wants him quiet."

Ellie unfolded the paper again. Safe south road. A pass phrase, maybe. Or bait. Or both.

"Cruz has the cipher key," Ellie said.

Luz nodded once. "Or had it when he ran."

"And Bell knows."

"Bell's men are not chasing Cruz to bring him to a hearing."

No one spoke for several seconds.

The church ledger waited east. Clara's courage waited with it. But if Cruz died before they reached him, the damaged account from Fort Griffin might stay damaged forever. The bell marks could remain marks. Bell could call them charity bookkeeping and smile while he did it.

Ellie turned to Clara. "You know the way to Deacon Vale's ledger."

Clara nodded, frightened by being addressed.

"Can you show Josiah?"

Paul turned. "Ellie."

"We cannot all chase one clue," Ellie said.

Josiah's voice came quiet. "I can take her to the storehouse."

"No," Paul said. "After last night?"

"After last night," Josiah said, "she stays with the man who knows what men do to windows."

Rafe looked from Luz to the road. "If I go with Luz, I can tell whether she lies before noon."

Paul shook his head. "Or she puts both of you in a draw with Stowe's men above it."

"Then do not let me ride first," Luz said.

They all looked at her.

She held Ellie's gaze. "Put me where your Ranger can read my horse. Put James where he can shoot me if he must. Keep the clerk away from me. I am not asking for trust."

"What are you asking for?" Ellie said.

"Use."

There it was, stripped clean.

Ellie studied the coin again. Bell's money shone in the dust as if it had never belonged to any hand at all.

"Terms," Ellie said.

Paul looked unhappy, but he listened.

"Rafe reads your trail," Ellie told Luz. "You do not ride out of his sight. You do not speak to Clara unless Josiah allows it. You do not leave a marker. You do not take a side road without saying why before your horse turns."

Luz nodded.

"If you lie," Ellie said, "Bell will not be the first person you answer to."

"That I believe."

Paul stepped close enough that Luz had to look up. "You sell us once, you will not get a second chance to spend the money."

"I know."

"I do not mean that as a threat."

"No," Luz said. "You mean it as a fact."

Ellie handed the folded paper back to Luz but kept the coin. Luz looked at it once, then away.

"Bell can have his gold back at the hearing," Ellie said, "if we live to bring it."

For the first time, Luz almost smiled. Almost.

The camp broke quickly. Josiah would take Clara by the lower lane toward the church storehouse and Deacon Vale's ledger. Ellie, Paul, Rafe, and Luz would cut toward the river roads where Cruz had doubled back like a man running out of ground. They would meet by dusk if the roads allowed it, or leave sign at the old mission well if they did not.

Before mounting, Ellie stepped beside Luz's bay.

"Tell me plainly," she said. "Is Cruz running from us or from Bell?"

Luz tightened the cinch and looked toward the west, where the mesquite thinned into heat.

"From both," she said. "But he is afraid of Bell."

"Why?"

"Because you want what he knows." Luz swung into the saddle. "Bell wants what he knows dead."

Ellie mounted with the words still cold in her.

They turned toward the river road behind a guide they could use and could not trust.
Chapter 6: Cruz's Trail

Rafe Culver kept his eyes off Luz Espina's face.

Faces were easy things. They learned to lie early and got better with practice. Hands were better. Horses were better still. A horse told what a rider feared, where a rider meant to turn, and whether the hand on the reins had chosen a road or was waiting to be forced into one.

Luz rode ahead and left, never so far that Paul's Winchester could not find her and never so close that she looked tame. Her bay's ears worked constantly, one toward the brush, one toward the riders behind. Twice she checked the horse before a fork appeared. Twice she let the animal drift over stone where dirt would have held a print.

Rafe saw it. He also saw her look back afterward, not at Paul or Ellie, but at the ground behind her.

She was hiding sign from somebody.

She might not be hiding it from them.

The river road ran low between mesquite and pale grass, bending where floodwater had once cut the bank. Morning heat rose early. Dust clung to the horses' legs. Behind Rafe, Paul rode with his jaw set and one hand loose near his Winchester carbine. Ellie kept the middle, quiet under her hat, the Bell notice and Clara's statement tucked in with the other papers that had begun to weigh more than ammunition.

"She turned before the fork," Paul said.

"I saw."

"And?"

"A liar can still know the road."

Paul gave him a look. "That meant to comfort me?"

"No."

That was all the talk the trail needed.

Luz lifted one hand and stopped near a cedar break. "Old spring."

Rafe dismounted before Paul could answer. The spring was not much to look at: a damp seam under flat stone, green moss guarded by thorn, and a trickle no wider than a bootlace. A stranger would ride past thirsty.

"Cruz watered here?" Ellie asked.

Rafe crouched. "Somebody did."

The mud held three prints, two blurred by a careless boot and one clean enough to show a narrow heel worn down on the outside. A horse had stood too long near the water and pawed once. Nearby, ash lay under a spread of dust that had been tossed too quickly. Rafe brushed it with two fingers. Still black beneath.

"Cold camp?" Paul asked.

"Cold by force."

Luz stayed mounted. "He lit no cooking fire."

"No," Rafe said. "But he had one ready."

He found the unburned twigs stacked under the lip of a stone, the way a man sets them before striking flint. Beside them lay a strip of dried beef with one bite torn from it and the rest left in the dirt.

Paul saw it. "Spooked before he ate."

"Before he drank enough too." Rafe pointed to the horse tracks. "He let the horse have water first. Then something made him pull away."

Ellie looked toward the brush. "Us?"

"No. This is older than us."

Luz said, "Bell's men crossed north of here yesterday."

Paul turned in the saddle. "You just remembered that?"

"I knew they were close. This spring told me where."

Rafe stood. "Which way did Cruz go?"

Luz pointed west. "Marrow crossing."

Rafe looked at the ground before he looked where she pointed. Cruz's horse had left the spring at a hard walk, not a run. A scared man who still had sense. The tracks went west for twenty yards, then cut through a stony wash where they would vanish from easy eyes.

"She is right," Rafe said.

Paul liked that even less than if she had been wrong.

They rode west.

Rafe let the others get six horse lengths ahead before he turned back along their own line.

Paul saw it and slowed. "What now?"

"Her gray horse."

Luz heard him. Her shoulders tightened; she kept facing the trail.

Rafe rode back to the stony wash where Luz had crossed earlier. The ground there did not keep much, but it kept enough for a man willing to look meanly at it. Their horses had scuffed the dust. Luz's bay had left a crescent chip where one shoe nicked stone. Under that, older and narrower, lay one print with a small cleft in the outer rim.

Gray horse, maybe. Scarred man's mount, if Luz had told that part true.

The print broke apart at the wash, lost them on stone, and cut north toward the same country where Bell's riders had crossed. Either the man had given up, or he had gone to report.

Rafe looked ahead at Luz. A liar would want him hurried past that sign. Luz had not hurried him. She had only gone still.

Honesty was another matter.

It made her afraid of the same road.

Rafe turned his horse back west. "Someone was behind her."

Paul's face darkened. "Still is?"

"Not alone."

Ellie looked toward the northern brush. "Bell's riders?"

"Likely where he went."

Luz finally turned. "I told you he might want his pride back."

"He wanted more than pride," Rafe said. "He wanted to know which way you chose."

No one answered that. The trail had just gained another set of eyes.

By midmorning the country opened into broken flats cut by shallow draws. Luz kept naming things that did not look worth names: a bent oak, a dry tank with a cracked bottom, a patch of prickly pear burned black from last season. Each one lined against Cruz's choices. Each one made Rafe trust her knowledge and distrust the reason she had waited to use it.

At Marrow crossing, they found the first sign of Bell's riders.

Not Cruz. Better horses. Shod clean. Four of them. Maybe five if one had doubled in another's track. They had not followed Cruz's exact line. They had crossed ahead and fanned out just enough to count exits from the creek bottom.

Rafe sat back on his heels and studied the cuts in the dust.

"They are not chasing," Ellie said.

Rafe looked up.

She had learned enough trail talk on the San Saba to know when the ground held a shape larger than tracks.

"No," he said. "They are closing."

Paul turned his horse in a slow half circle, checking ridge and brush. "How far ahead?"

"Half a day. Maybe less if Cruz slowed."

Luz's bay blew softly. She leaned down and touched its neck. "He would slow after Marrow."

"Why?"

"Goat camp."

Rafe remembered the place from her earlier naming. "You said south of the mission road."

"Yes."

"No water except the seep."

"Yes."

"Cruz knows it?"

"One of his men does."

That was the first time she had said it that way.

Rafe looked at her. "One of his men."

Luz's face gave him nothing. Her bay shifted under her.

"Name?" Paul asked.

"Tomas Rill."

"Cruz's guard?"

"Book hand. Sometimes rider. He knew where records were buried and where men hid when records went bad."

Ellie's attention sharpened. "Records?"

Luz looked away. "Cruz spread his secrets among his men."

Rafe mounted. "Then Cruz would keep Rill close."

"If Rill was still alive."

Nobody asked what that meant. The trail would answer.

South of the mission road, the goat camp sat under a limestone shelf, abandoned except for a brush pen, a split trough, and old droppings baked hard by sun. The seep Luz promised lay under a stone, exactly where she said. It had been opened recently and closed badly. Wet grit showed along one edge.

Rafe walked the camp once before he let himself look at the body.

The man lay in the shade of the shelf, half covered by a saddle blanket that had not been his. He was narrow in the shoulders, with ink stains still dark around two fingernails. His coat had been searched. Pockets turned. Boot tops checked. Belt slit open and left dangling.

Paul stood above him. "Tomas Rill?"

Luz crossed herself once. "Yes."

Her voice made him no friend. It made him once alive.

Ellie dismounted and stopped several paces back. She had seen enough death. Rafe was glad she stayed back until he finished.

"Bell's men?" she asked.

Rafe crouched near the body. "Likely."

"Why leave him?"

"He had already answered or refused."

Paul's hand tightened on the Winchester.

Rafe studied the camp instead of the wound. Blood told one thing. Camps told more. Rill had been brought here alive. His heels had dragged in dirt near the trough. Two men had stood over him. One had paced near the brush pen. Another had searched his coat after he fell.

But the search had been angry. Quick. Certain of itself.

That was how men missed small things.

Rafe looked at Rill's hands. Ink. Broken nail. No ring. Then the ground under the right wrist. A tiny mound of dust had been pressed against the side of a flat pebble, not by wind. Rafe lifted the pebble.

Nothing.

He smiled without humor. Too easy.

He checked the left side, where the dead man's hand had curled near his belt. There, tucked under a loose thread in the torn lining, was a folded scrap no wider than two fingers.

Paul breathed out. "How did you see that?"

"Man hiding paper does not hide it where he looks."

Rafe unfolded the scrap carefully. The marks meant nothing to him at first: figures, short strokes, a small bell mark, and three letters written under a smear.

S. S. Sta.

Ellie came closer then. Her face changed when she saw the marks.

"This is the same hand as the damaged Bell account," she said.

"Can you read it?"

"Not fully. Not without Cruz's key." She touched the lower corner without taking the scrap from Rafe. "But this mark repeats beside the account entries from Fort Griffin. And this could be a place."

Luz looked at the paper and went still.

Rafe saw that too. "Say it."

"Surveyor's station," she said.

Ellie looked west. "The abandoned one?"

"There is an old station past the dry windmill," Luz said. "Cruz used it once when Korth's money was being split. If Rill carried this, Cruz may have meant to meet him there."

"Or Bell's men found out," Paul said.

Rafe stood, scrap in hand. Beyond the goat camp, five riders had made no effort to hide their line until it reached stone.

"They found out."

Ellie folded the scrap into a clean piece of paper from her pocket. Her hands were steady. That mattered. Rafe had known men who shook only after danger passed. Ellie looked like danger kept giving her work.

"Then we ride for the station," she said.

Rafe looked once more at Tomas Rill, at the searched pockets, the broken nail, the little place where a dying or doomed man had hidden what his killers missed.

Rafe tucked the dead man's scrap inside his vest. Ahead, five sets of tracks ran toward a surveyor's ruin, and Bell's riders had the shorter road.
Chapter 7: The Surveyor's Station

The surveyor's station stood beside a dry windmill with one broken vane pointing south.

Ellie saw it from the rise as a low plank building pressed into the heat, its roof silvered by weather and its porch sagging on the left side. Beyond it, the land fell toward a shallow basin of grass, limestone, and scrub. No smoke rose. No horse stood tied outside. Nothing moved but the loose windmill vane, which clicked once whenever the wind found enough strength to turn it.

Rafe lifted one hand and stopped them below the rise.

"Riders were here," he said.

Paul looked toward the building. "How long?"

"Not long enough for comfort."

Luz shaded her eyes. "That is the station."

"You knew it well enough to be sure from here?" Paul asked.

"Cruz used it when men needed paper away from town."

Ellie touched the folded cipher scrap inside her jacket. S. S. Sta. The dead man had carried those letters hidden in his coat, and now the place stood empty ahead of them, stripped of welcome and maybe stripped of truth.

Three days to the hearing. Less than that, if Bell kept finding ways to make Pryor listen faster.

Rafe dismounted and walked the last stretch. Paul followed, Winchester down but ready. Ellie waited with Luz until Rafe looked back and nodded once.

Inside, the station smelled of dust, mouse droppings, and old ink gone sour in the heat.

The room had been made for work once. A drafting table stood under the north window, its surface cut by knife marks and stained with rings where ink bottles had sat. Survey chains hung from hooks along one wall. A broken leveling rod lay across two chairs. The far shelf held rolled papers tied in old cotton tape, but half the shelf was bare. The floor showed drag marks where a trunk or map case had been pulled out in haste.

Paul stepped to the rear door. Rafe crossed to the window. Luz stayed by the front, watching both the room and the road outside.

Ellie went to the drafting table.

Someone had searched it badly and quickly. Drawers stood open. Scraps lay on the floor, most of them useless: torn wrappers, a cracked pencil, a receipt for mule feed, a page with numbers and no names. The main map case under the table had been opened and emptied. Its brass latch hung twisted.

Bell's men had known what they wanted.

"Map?" Paul asked.

"Gone," Ellie said.

The word came out flatter than she meant it to.

She knelt beside the case. Dust marked the rectangular place where a large roll had rested a long time. The empty space was darker at the edges, with one fresh scrape where the case had been dragged open. A bit of green thread clung to the latch, torn from old map cloth or a man's sleeve.

"If the key map was here," Ellie said, "they took it."

Luz said, "Cruz would have used a map if he meant to prove a boundary."

"Or hide one," Paul said.

Ellie did not answer. She ran her fingers along the inside seam of the case, then stopped. A folded strip of paper had slipped behind the warped lining, too small to be worth stealing if a man was looking for a map.

She eased it free.

It was not much. A surveyor's working note, brittle and browned, written in two hands. Most of the top had been torn away, but the lower lines remained.

North line to hold at live oak draw, not creek bend.

Pryce water claim to remain inside original reach.

No pledge boundary to cross Calder improvements.

Ellie read the lines twice before breathing.

Paul saw her face. "Something?"

"Not the map."

"But something."

"Yes."

She laid the strip on the drafting table and weighted the curled edge with the cracked pencil. Her heart had begun to beat too hard, but not from fear alone. This was not the clean deed page Bell had cut out. It was not enough to walk into Pryor's hearing and win. But it was a voice from before the lie, a working note from a man measuring land before Bell's paper tried to move it.

Rafe came close enough to see without crowding. "What does it say?"

Ellie pointed. "The north line is the live oak draw, not the creek bend. Bell's petition uses the creek bend language. If he shifts the boundary there, he can make the mortgage note appear to include the improvements."

Paul's eyes narrowed. "House, barn, water?"

"Enough of them."

Luz looked toward the empty map case. "So Bell's paper moves the ranch without moving a fence."

"On paper," Ellie said. "That is where he likes to move things first."

She took Bell's notice from inside her jacket and unfolded only the lower half, keeping the seal and signature covered from dust. The words she needed sat in the mortgage description, dressed in clerk's language and made to look harmless by how dry they were.

Creek bend to north improvement line.

She set the notice beside the surveyor's strip.

Live oak draw, not creek bend.

The two phrases lay a handspan apart on the scarred table. One was clean, official, and false. The other was torn, browned, and closer to truth than anything the county office had left her.

"This is the trick," she said.

Paul leaned over the table.

"The creek bend sits east of the live oak draw," Ellie said. "If Bell makes that bend the boundary, the barn and lower water look like pledged improvements. If the live oak draw holds, those improvements stay inside the original reach."

Rafe looked from one paper to the other. "A judge will follow the cleaner page."

"Unless I can make the dirty one stand beside enough others."

She heard the answer as she said it. It was not hope exactly. It was a kind of work.

At Fort Griffin, she had wanted every paper wrapped dry and whole. On the San Saba trail, one wet ledger corner had felt like a wound. Now she saw the harder truth of it. Bell had built his claim assuming honest records would be isolated, burned, cut out, or made to look weak alone. A torn note could be laughed at. A frightened clerk could be doubted. A dead man's cipher scrap could be dismissed as criminal rubbish.

But if the torn note, the clerk, the scrap, the church marks, and Minshew's bank hand all pointed to the same lie, Bell would have to break them all at once.

Ellie laid the survey strip flatter.

She did not need one perfect page.

She needed enough damaged pages to stand together.

She searched the table again with new patience. If the first scrap had been missed, others might have been missed for the same reason: too small, too dirty, too little like victory.

In the second drawer she found a packet of rodman's notes, tied with string gone soft. Most were ordinary measurements. Chains, angles, stone markers, creek crossings. Then a name appeared in the margin.

R. Pryce present. Refused revised line.

Ellie sat back on her heels.

For a moment she saw her father not sick, not dead, not reduced to a copied signature, but standing somewhere in this heat with a surveyor and telling him no.

Bell had said Rhys Pryce made refusal public.

Here was the shadow of that refusal, written small in a working man's margin.

"Ellie?" Paul said softly.

She set the note beside the first. "My father objected to a revised line. This says he was present."

Paul's face changed in the way it did when anger had to walk through discipline before it could speak. "Bell knew."

"Bell knew enough to try again after Rhys was dead."

Rafe looked toward the door. "And took the map that would show it plain."

"Yes."

Luz shifted. "There may be more."

Paul turned on her. "Now you remember?"

"No. I see something." She pointed to the wall behind the survey chains. "That hook is newer than the others."

Ellie crossed to it. The hook held nothing now, but the wall behind it had a clean oval in the dust. Something had hung there until recently: a small framed board, maybe, or a field slate. Beneath the hook, a nail had scratched the wall with a shape like a half circle and three letters.

O.M.R.

"Old mission road," Rafe said.

Luz nodded. "It runs behind the church storehouse."

Ellie looked from the marks to the table, to the notes, to the empty map case.

The path that began with Cruz's cipher scrap had bent back toward Clara's church ledger. The proof was not one line. It was a knot being pulled tight from different ends.

Outside, a horse snorted.

Paul moved first. Rafe reached the window with him.

"Not ours," Rafe said.

Ellie gathered the survey scraps and slid them into a clean fold of paper. Luz crossed to the front door but did not open it. Her head tilted, listening.

"Two riders," she said. "Maybe three."

Paul looked at Rafe.

Rafe pointed toward the rear. "Tracks leave that way. Fresh."

"Bell's men?" Ellie asked.

"They did not bother hiding until they reached stone," Rafe said. "Same as the goat camp."

The riders outside did not come straight to the door. One circled wide. Another stopped near the windmill. They were not sure who was inside. That uncertainty would not last.

Ellie folded the notes inside her jacket, next to the cipher scrap.

"Can we get out?" she asked.

Rafe nodded toward the rear door. "If we go now."

Paul opened the rear door just enough to see. Heat and white light slid in. "Luz."

She came quickly.

"Old mission road," he said. "Fastest way."

"Through the wash behind the station. Then under the limestone shelf. If they take the main road, we beat them."

"And if they know the wash?"

Luz looked at Ellie. "Then we find out what else they know."

That was not comfort either. Ellie had stopped expecting comfort from truthful people.

They left through the rear, crouched under the window line, and crossed the first stretch of open ground while the windmill clicked once behind them. A shout rose from the front of the station before they reached the wash.

Paul looked back but did not fire.

Rafe took the lead. Luz rode second, pointing them toward a pale break in the brush. Ellie followed with the survey notes pressed flat under her hand, feeling the thin paper through her jacket.

The deed chain was alive, but it was limping.

Behind them, Bell's riders found the empty station.

Ahead, the old mission road waited.
Chapter 8: The Bell Ledger

Paul saw the old mission road by the dust on Luz's skirt before he saw the road itself.

She rode ahead through a pale break in the brush, keeping low under the limestone shelf until the land opened toward a cluster of old stone walls and mesquite. The mission had been dead longer than any man in the county had been alive. Its chapel roof was half gone. A new church stood beyond it, plain boards and a square bell frame, but the old storehouse remained beside the ruined stone, low and stubborn with a timber door darkened by years of hands.

Evening had started to gather in the cracks of the place.

Paul looked back once. No riders showed on the rise behind them, but that meant little. Bell's men had found the surveyor's station too close behind. They knew the road now, or soon would.

"There," Luz said. "Storehouse."

Rafe studied the yard. "Two horses behind it."

Paul saw them then: one claybank, one black, both tied in shade. "Josiah?"

"One horse stands like his," Rafe said. "Other belongs to someone nervous."

That would be Clara.

Ellie touched the papers inside her jacket, a motion Paul had seen often enough now to read without asking. Survey notes. Bell notice. Cipher scrap. Clara's statement. Each one thin. Together, maybe not.

They rode into the yard without calling until Josiah stepped from the storehouse door with Sutter's double-barrel 12-bore shotgun held down along one leg.

"Took you long enough," he said.

Paul swung down. "Had company."

"So did we."

Clara Whitcomb stood behind him, pale but upright. Beside her was an old man in a black coat gone green at the seams. His beard was white, his shoulders stooped, and his eyes had the offended look of a man whose orderly world had been told it was part of a crime.

"Deacon Vale?" Ellie asked.

"Amos Vale," he said. "And I will say again, church accounts are not cattle paper."

"No," Ellie said. "That may be why Bell used them."

The old man stiffened. "Mr. Bell paid for that bell frame when this county had no money to spare. He put coin into a school roof and a road plank fund besides. You will need more than a frightened clerk's memory before you make his charity ugly."

Clara dropped her eyes.

Josiah's face did not move, but Paul saw his hand shift slightly on the double gun.

Ellie stepped between them without seeming to. "Then let the ledger defend him."

Vale looked at her for a long moment. Then he turned and went back inside.

The storehouse smelled of wax, rope, old wood, and flour dust. Church goods filled one side: spare hymnals, a cracked communion pitcher, folded cloths, a box of candles. On the other side stood tools, feed sacks, and a long chest where the bell rope had once been stored before the new church frame was raised.

Vale knelt with difficulty and pulled a tin box from beneath the rope chest. He unlocked it with a key tied around his neck on black string.

"This ledger accounts for donations," he said. "Bell fund. Repairs. Roof money. Burial collections. Widows' aid. Nothing hidden."

"That is what hiding likes best," Rafe said from the door.

Vale gave him a hard look.

Paul stayed by the entrance and watched the yard through the gap in the door. Luz had taken the rear corner without being asked. She stood where she could see the back window and the line of brush beyond the storehouse. Still useful. Still not trusted. Maybe both could be true for a while.

Vale opened the ledger on a small table.

The book was large, bound in brown leather, its corners worn soft. Names filled the pages in neat columns. Amounts. Dates. Purpose. Bell Frame Fund. Rope. Casting Freight. School Roof. Widow Hale. Bell's name appeared early and often, written with care.

"There," Vale said. "Charity."

Ellie did not answer. She leaned over the book with Clara beside her. Paul watched her hands. They did not hurry. That steadied him more than a fast answer would have.

"Clara," Ellie said, "show me what you saw."

Clara's hands trembled above the page. She did not touch it. "There. Beside the amount."

A small mark sat near Bell's donation line. At first Paul saw only a clerk's flourish. A half loop. A narrow stem. Almost a bell, if a person wanted it to be one.

Ellie took the cipher scrap from its folded paper and laid it near the ledger.

Paul watched Vale's face change. Not guilt. Something worse for an honest man: recognition arriving too late.

"This mark repeats," Ellie said.

"A notation," Vale said weakly.

"Yes. But not only for church giving."

She turned pages. Clara pointed once, then again. Bell Frame Fund. School Roof. Road Plank Fund. Then, farther down, smaller entries mixed among ordinary names.

Rawley Bend.

Wexler grazing note.

Calder Reach notation, shortened and half hidden beside a bell fund transfer.

Paul felt the room narrow.

"Those are ranch names," he said.

"Yes," Ellie said.

Vale leaned closer. "Those entries were for pledges. Some men gave beef instead of coin. Some gave use of wagons. The church took what the county could spare."

"And beside them," Ellie said, "the same marks used in Bell's account."

"I did not make those marks."

Paul believed him. The old man's anger had broken into fear, and fear like that did not have time to dress itself.

Ellie turned another page. "These notes line with account marks from Fort Griffin. Not enough to decode without Cruz. Enough to show the marks are not innocent. They repeat beside names tied to land."

Clara whispered, "I saw that same mark on Minshew's packet."

Vale sat down hard on a feed sack. "God forgive us."

"God may," Paul said, watching the yard. "Bell will not."

Rafe shifted at the door.

Paul saw it too late and early enough. Movement near the mission wall. Two men low in the brush. A third at the far corner with something dark in his hand.

Luz's voice came tight from the rear corner. "Gray horse."

Paul did not look away from the door. "Yours?"

"Stowe's man. Scar at the chin."

The offer from the dry wash had found its way to the church.

"Down," Paul said.

Rafe fired once through the door gap. The shot cracked across the yard and knocked dust from the old stone. A man cursed outside. Then glass broke at the rear window and a bottle came through trailing fire.

It struck the floor near the candle box.

Flame ran at once.

Vale cried out and grabbed for the ledger.

Paul caught his coat and pulled him back. "No."

"The book!"

"Ellie."

She was already moving. Not for the whole ledger. For pages.

That was harder to watch than a charge. The ledger was burning at the edge, and Ellie had to choose. Her knife came out. She cut along the binding while smoke thickened. Clara coughed, one hand over her mouth, the other pointing.

"That page. There. Calder."

Ellie cut.

"Rawley Bend," Clara said. "Wexler. There."

Ellie cut again.

The rear wall caught near the window frame. Luz dragged a feed sack over the first run of flame and stamped it down, coughing through her teeth. Rafe fired from the doorway, then stepped back as a round punched through the door plank where his shoulder had been.

Paul shoved the table sideways, putting wood between Ellie and the window. "How many?"

"Three I see," Rafe said. "Maybe more at the wall."

Josiah pulled Clara down behind the rope chest. "Stay low."

Vale was trying to crawl toward the ledger box. Paul caught him again.

"Deacon, if you want that book to matter, stay alive to say where it came from."

The old man stared at him through smoke, then nodded once and let himself be hauled toward the door.

Ellie had a fistful of pages now, but she still bent over the ledger. The fire had reached the table leg. Heat bit at Paul's face.

"Ellie."

"One more."

"Now."

"This one names Calder and two more ranches."

Paul wanted to pick her up and carry her out. He did not. That was the work now: not taking the choice from the person who knew which page mattered.

He kicked the burning table leg away from her skirt and fired once through the broken rear window. The shape outside dropped or ducked; Paul did not wait to know which.

Ellie tore the last page free.

"Move," she said.

They moved.

Rafe went first through the door, then Luz with Clara, then Vale bent low between Josiah and Paul. Ellie came with the rescued pages pressed inside her jacket and one sleeve smoking near the cuff. Paul slapped the ember out with his bare hand and felt the skin bite.

The storehouse breathed smoke behind them.

Outside, the attackers were already falling back toward the mission wall. They had not come to win a fight. They had come to burn paper. That made Paul colder than a body on the ground would have.

Rafe lifted his Spencer carbine.

"Let them go," Paul said.

Rafe looked at him.

"We need the pages more than their backs."

Rafe lowered the barrel.

They got behind the old stone chapel wall while the storehouse burned in uneven red light. Smoke climbed into the darkening sky and made the new church bell frame look thin and black against it.

Vale sat on the ground with both hands pressed over his face.

"I kept his money in God's book," he said.

Ellie knelt beside him. Her face was streaked with smoke. "You kept records. That is why he wanted them burned."

Paul crouched near her. "Did you get enough?"

She opened her jacket and showed the pages, edges blackened, writing still clear across the middle. Bell marks. Names. Amounts. Ranch notations. Calder among them, but not alone.

"Enough to show the pattern," she said.

"Enough for Bell?"

She looked past the burning storehouse toward the road that would take them back into county law and all its traps.

"Not his hand on the order," she said. "For that, we still need Minshew's bank packet."

Behind them, the ledger room fell inward with a roar of sparks.
Chapter 9: Stage Road Ambush

Paul James saw the stage dust before he saw the stage.

It rose beyond the mesquite in a tan ribbon, low at first, then widening where the road climbed out of the shallow draw. Morning had already burned the cool out of the day. Two days remained before the hearing, but that number had begun to feel like a lie. Bell did not need days if his papers reached Judge Pryor first.

Paul lay behind a shelf of limestone with Rafe twenty yards to his right and Luz below them in the draw, holding the horses where brush hid their legs. Ellie waited beside Paul with the rescued ledger pages wrapped in cloth under her arm and the Bell notice folded inside her jacket.

The fire-blackened edges of the church pages had left soot on her cuff.

"One stage," Rafe said. "Two riders behind."

"Guards?" Paul asked.

"Couriers pretending not to be."

Luz looked up from the draw. "Bell likes decoys."

Paul glanced at her. "You say that now?"

"I said it before. He pays men to make you choose wrong."

Ellie's eyes stayed on the road. "Then we choose slowly."

The stage came into view: two tired horses, driver hunched under a sweat-dark hat, one passenger box strapped behind, and a mail bag lashed under the seat. The riders behind kept a polite distance. Too polite. One had a gray horse with a small cleft in the outer shoe.

Luz saw it too. Her mouth tightened.

"Scar at the chin?" Paul asked.

"Maybe."

"Maybe is plenty."

Paul stood with his Winchester held low and stepped into the road before the stage reached the draw.

The driver hauled hard on the reins. "What in God's name--"

"County business," Paul said.

"You wearing a badge?"

"No."

"Then it ain't county business."

Rafe came out of the brush on the right, Spencer carbine across his arm. The two riders behind the stage split without being told, one drifting left, one checking his horse too sharply.

Paul kept his eyes on the driver. "We are looking for papers sent under Bell's interest."

"Mail is mail."

Ellie stepped into the road then, dusty, smoke-marked, and calm enough to make the driver look away first.

"If there is nothing false in the bag," she said, "you will lose less time showing it than arguing."

The gray-horse rider reached under his coat.

Luz came from the draw with her short-barreled Colt Navy conversion already in hand. "Do not."

He froze.

Paul did not look at her, but he felt the choice in it. She had not run. She had not waited to see which side paid better. She had stepped into the road where everyone could count her.

Rafe took the rider's pistol. The second rider gave his up after one look at Paul.

The driver swore steadily while Ellie opened the mail bag on the stage step. Most of it was ordinary: notices, two personal letters, a seed order, a court wrapper with Everett Marr's neat hand across it.

Ellie found the first Bell packet tied in blue string.

"Too easy," Luz said.

Paul looked at Ellie.

She turned the packet once in her hands. "The paper is wrong."

"Wrong how?"

"Old outside. New fold." She lifted it near her face. "And no bank ink. Marr wants us to open this."

Rafe watched the riders. "Why?"

Ellie slid the packet open just enough to see the top page. "Because it says what we already know. False boundary. Mortgage claim. Enough to make us think we stopped the danger."

The gray-horse rider smiled.

Paul hit him once, not hard enough to break anything, hard enough to take the smile off the road.

"Paul," Ellie said.

"I know."

He stepped back.

She searched the bag again. Nothing.

The driver spat in the dust. "Told you."

Luz had not moved from the gray horse. She looked at the saddle, then at the bedroll tied behind it. "His coat is too clean for a man riding courier."

The rider said, "You got no call to touch my gear."

Luz cut the bedroll strap with one quick motion.

The roll fell open. A second packet slid into the dust, wrapped in oilcloth and tied with red string.

Paul looked at her.

Luz did not smile. "Bell pays men to make you choose wrong."

Ellie picked up the oilcloth packet. Her hands changed as soon as she touched it.

"This one matters."

The rider lunged.

Paul watched her read. He knew before she finished.

"Judge Pryor received Bell's petition, affidavit, schedule, and bond this morning," she said. "He issued a writ of sequestration. The sheriff may take the property named in the schedule and hold it for the court unless we replevy it. The writ is returnable at tomorrow's hearing."

Paul's hand closed on the bridle leather until the dun tossed its head.

Ellie looked at him.

He opened his hand.

The boy went on, words spilling now that he had started. "Sukey locked the smokehouse. Mizpah sent her boys to help gather the lower cattle in close. Josiah ain't there. Rafe ain't there. They are asking if you mean to come home."

Home.

The word did what Bell's rider and gray horse had not. It pulled Paul's mind off the road and put it at the Calder gate, with Lem trying to sound older than he was, Sukey holding a key in one fist, and Bell's men looking at every horse and hinge as if counting made ownership.

Bell had found the softest place to put pressure: not Paul's pride, not Ellie's temper, but the hands left waiting under paper they could not read.

"We ride back," Paul said.

Ellie did not answer quickly.

That was answer enough to hurt.

"If we ride back without Cruz's key," she said, "Bell keeps the account locked."

"If we do not ride back, the sheriff begins the levy."

"If we ride back with half-proof, we may stop one wagon and still lose the foreclosure."

Paul looked down the road until the heat made it swim. He knew she was right. He hated that being right could feel so much like abandonment.

Rafe came near. "I can send the boy back with orders."

"Orders do not hold gates," Paul said.

"No," Rafe said. "But scared men hold them better when they know what not to do."

Paul turned to the boy. "Tell Lem no rifles unless fired on. Cattle in close. Tools locked. Every neighbor who will stand witness, bring them. No one signs. No one lets Bell's men count alone."

The boy nodded hard, fixing each word like a nail.

"And tell Sukey," Ellie said, "my father's strongbox stays under her bed until I ask for it."

"Yes, ma'am."

Paul let the bridle go. The dun horse stood trembling, foam at the bit.

"Water him before you turn back," Paul said. "Then ride slower. A dead horse carries no message."

The boy looked like he might argue, then saw Paul's face and did not.

Paul looked west, though Calder's Reach lay far beyond sight.

Bell did not have to own the ranch today. He only had to count it under law, step over its threshold with deputies, lay hands on stock and tools and rooms, and make every next step look ordinary.

Rafe tied the gray-horse rider's hands. Luz gathered the oilcloth packet from the stage step and held it out to Ellie without being asked.

Ellie took it. "This proves the packet is false."

"But not before Pryor acted," Paul said.

"Then we make him act again."

Paul studied her. Smoke still marked her sleeve. Dust lay on her cheek. In her hands she held a packet they had risked lives to get, and beyond it stood a ruling already moving against their home.

They had won the road.

Bell had won the morning.

The thought settled cold and plain.

"We still need Cruz," Ellie said. "And Minshew's bank packet."

Paul measured the tied rider, the stage, the boy's spent horse, the road to town, and the road that led toward Cruz.

The notice on the gate had become something worse than a threat.

He was legally inside it.
Chapter 10: Cruz Cornered

The river crossing smelled of hot stone, horse sweat, and water too shallow to hide anything well.

Ellie saw the place from the cutbank above it: a bend in the river where gravel ran pale under the sun and willows leaned over the near bank. Beyond the crossing, the road broke into three faint tracks. One went toward a line of cottonwoods. One followed the river. One climbed toward rougher ground where a tired horse might stumble before dark.

Rafe lay flat beside a clump of grass, looking down at the gravel.

"He is here," he said.

Paul did not ask who.

Ellie tightened her hand on the packet under her jacket. Bell's writ had ridden with them all afternoon. Calder cattle. Two team horses. Wagons, harness, branded equipment. Returnable at tomorrow's hearing. She could see Calder's Reach in those words as clearly as if the paper had drawn it: Sukey at the smokehouse, Lem at the gate, sheriff's men identifying property while Bell's riders waited to carry it away.

Paul had said little since they left the stage road. The less he said, the more Ellie felt where his thoughts had gone.

"If we turn back now," she said quietly, "Bell keeps the account locked."

"I know."

"Do you?"

He looked at her then. The answer in his eyes was tired and plain. "I know both roads cost us."

That was as much peace as the day allowed.

They had been riding since before the heat rose off the road, and the country behind them felt too crowded for one day: the stage dust, the false packet, the split tracks, the hard push through mesquite and river gravel. Ellie kept counting it in her head because if she stopped counting, worry would do worse arithmetic. Three days had carried them from burned deed books to church ledgers to Cruz's trail. Now one more bend of river had to hold the answer Bell was trying to outrun.

Calder's Reach had never felt farther away.

Luz crouched below them, one hand on the dirt. "Cruz crossed once, doubled back, then came here again. His horse is done."

"Where?" Rafe asked.

She pointed toward a shelf of brush at the far side of the crossing. "There. Or close."

Rafe studied the line, then nodded. "He has a pistol line on the ford."

Paul took his Winchester Model 1873 from the saddle boot. "Then we do not ride into it."

They moved down the bank in pieces. Rafe went left, low through the willows. Paul took the center with Ellie behind him. Luz circled wide, not quite trusted enough to vanish, not foolish enough to make herself easy.

At the edge of the gravel, a voice came from the brush.

"One more step and I put a ball in James."

Paul stopped.

Ellie felt the day narrow.

Gideon Cruz stepped from behind the brush with his Smith & Wesson Model 3 in one hand and a folded coat over the other arm. He looked thinner than he had at the ferry station, his beard grown uneven, his eyes red-rimmed from too many cold camps and too little sleep. Dust marked his face in streaks. His horse stood behind him with reins looped around a willow trunk, head low, sides hollowed by hard riding.

"Mrs. James," Cruz said. "You are persistent."

"You are tired," Ellie said.

His mouth twitched. "That is not the same as beaten."

Paul's Winchester stayed low. "It can become the same."

Cruz angled the Smith & Wesson toward him. "Careful. I am worth more breathing than dead."

"Then start proving it," Ellie said.

Cruz looked at her for a long moment. "Bell has Pryor moving?"

She did not answer.

He smiled without pleasure. "He does. I can see it on you. How far?"

"Writ of sequestration," Paul said. "Return hearing tomorrow."

Cruz gave a low whistle. "Then he is already walking through your house in his mind."

Ellie stepped past Paul's shoulder before Paul could answer. "You have the cipher key."

"I have a memory for numbers."

"And a written strip."

His eyes shifted. Small, quick. Enough.

"Maybe," he said.

"Bell wants you dead over a maybe?"

Cruz wiped sweat from his upper lip with the back of his wrist. The Smith & Wesson did not lower. "Bell wants everyone dead eventually. Some of us sooner."

"Give me the key."

"Give me a horse that can run, a road south, and your word that James there will not shoot me before sunset."

"No."

His smile broke. "No?"

"No."

"Then you get nothing."

Ellie took the folded cipher scrap from the goat camp out of her jacket and held it where he could see the marks. "Tomas Rill is dead."

Cruz's Smith & Wesson dipped an inch.

"We found him at the goat camp," she said. "Bell's men searched him. They missed this."

Cruz stared at the scrap. His face did not soften. It closed, which told Ellie more.

"Tomas should have burned it," he said.

"He hid it."

"Then Tomas was a fool."

"He was loyal to something."

"Loyal men die poor."

"So do useful men when Bell is finished with them."

That landed. Cruz looked away toward the river, then back at the road behind Ellie.

Rafe's voice came from the willow shade. "He is watching the cottonwoods."

Cruz laughed once. "The Ranger still sees with half a face."

"Enough," Rafe said.

Luz appeared at the far side of the gravel, Colt Navy conversion drawn but pointed down. Cruz saw her and his expression changed again.

Ellie kept the scrap lifted. "You know these marks."

"I know many marks."

"This one." She tapped the small bell shape with her thumbnail. "The church ledger carries it. Minshew's packet carries it. The Fort Griffin account carries it. Tell me what it means."

Cruz's eyes moved from the scrap to her face. He was measuring how much truth could buy him how much road.

"It is not a bell," he said.

Ellie waited.

"It is an instruction mark. Bell used it because men like Vale would think it charity shorthand and men like Minshew would think it account routing."

"Instruction for what?"

"Move the debt. Alter the boundary. Prepare transfer." Cruz wet his lips. "The mark changes meaning by the number beside it."

"Then give me the numbers."

"Give me the horse."

Paul made a low sound. Ellie did not look at him.

"You want proof I can help," Cruz said. "There is your proof."

"No. That is proof you can still bargain."

Cruz's jaw tightened.

Ellie took one step closer, though the Smith & Wesson stayed in his hand. "Bell has twenty-four hours to put men inside Calder's Reach. I am done paying every frightened man in this county with time I do not have. Give me one account phrase."

"You will not understand it."

"Then let me disappoint you."

Something like respect, ugly and reluctant, crossed Cruz's face.

"Bell account," he said. "Third line after the mark. If the word is toll, it means land debt, not road money. If the word is bell, it means church cover. If the word is weather, burn or remove the matching county page."

Ellie felt the river sound fall away.

"Weather," she said.

Cruz nodded once. "That is how the deed room burned without burning everything."

Paul's Winchester shifted.

"You knew?"

"I knew the language. I did not strike the match."

"But you carried the book that told them where."

Cruz's mouth twisted. "You want confession or help? They are not the same thing."

"You," he said.

"Me," Luz answered.

"How much did they pay?"

"Less than Bell offered."

Cruz looked amused despite himself. "Then you have gotten worse at business."

Before Luz could answer, a horse nickered from the cottonwood line.

Everyone heard it.

Paul shifted, putting himself between Ellie and the sound.

A rider appeared in the broken shade beyond the crossing. Scar at the chin. Gray horse. He held a white cloth in one hand and a revolver in the other, barrel pointed at the ground as if manners could make the weapon harmless.

"Luz Espina," he called. "Offer still stands."

Luz did not move.

Cruz's eyes narrowed. "You brought them."

"No," Ellie said. "They followed what Bell bought."

The rider smiled across the water. "Mr. Stowe says you can ride clear. Money doubled. Safe road south. No questions from Bell, no questions from county men. All you do is step away from Mrs. James and let us collect Cruz."

Paul looked at Luz without turning his head.

Luz stared at the rider. For a moment, Ellie saw the whole shape of the offer pass over her face. Money. Distance. A road out. No Clara. No ledgers. No Calder's Reach. No men with papers turning her into another loose end.

Cruz said softly, "Take it."

Luz's hand moved.

Paul's Winchester lifted half an inch.

Luz reached into her pocket, pulled out the folded safe-road paper from Stowe's first offer, and dropped it into the river shallows. The current caught it, opened it, and turned the pencil mark into a black blur.

"Tell Stowe," she called, "I used his road already."

The rider's face hardened.

Ellie did not thank Luz. Not then. The choice had not made them safe. It had only made them honest about the danger.

But she saw what the choice cost. Luz watched the paper turn and sink as if it were a door closing under water. There had been a future in that paper: south road, clean name, money enough to disappear, no more Paul James looking at her as if trust had to be earned by the hour. She let it go and did not look away until the last white corner vanished.

"You will get no second offer," Cruz said.

Luz looked at him. "I know."

"Then why?"

Her mouth bent without humor. "Because I am tired of men thinking the only thing I know is price."

Cruz studied Luz as if she had become someone his old measure no longer fit.

"Now," Ellie said to him. "You are out of buyers."

He swallowed. "Not out of value."

"Then spend some of it before Bell takes it from your corpse."

The first shot cracked from the cottonwood line.

Paul pulled Ellie down behind a river stone as gravel jumped near her boot. Rafe fired from the willows. Luz's bay screamed and pulled against its reins. Cruz dropped behind his dying horse's shoulder, cursing.

"Cruz!" Ellie shouted.

He looked at her through dust and gun smoke.

"The key!"

His hand went inside his coat.

Another shot tore bark from the willow above him.

He froze, caught between bargaining and dying.

Bell's men came out of the cottonwoods at a run.

The cipher key was ten paces away, still in the coat of a man who did not yet know whether saving it was worth his life.
Chapter 11: The Cipher Key

Rafe Culver saw the fight split in two.

Most men watched gunfire. Rafe watched purpose. Two of Bell's men fired at Paul and Luz to hold them behind the river stones. One put shots into the brush where Rafe had last fired. The fourth man, the scar-chinned rider on the gray horse, saved his powder.

He ran for Cruz.

That told Rafe what mattered.

Cruz crouched behind the shoulder of his dying horse, one hand inside his coat and the other still gripping the Smith & Wesson. The horse thrashed once, then went still enough to be cover and not enough to be shelter. Gravel jumped around Cruz's boots. Ellie was pinned behind a river stone ten paces away, close enough to see the coat, too far to reach it.

"Cruz!" she shouted. "Throw it!"

Cruz looked at her as if she had asked him to throw away his own skin.

Rafe fired the Spencer once and drove the scarred man sideways into the cottonwood shade. The man stayed up. He rolled, came behind a trunk, and fired back fast enough to cut leaves from the willow above Rafe's head.

Good shot. Disciplined.

Stowe's training, if not Stowe himself.

Paul fired the Winchester from the river stone and dropped one of the men coming across the gravel. Luz took the other man's horse line with a shot from her Colt Navy that sent the animal screaming sideways and broke the rush. She neither cheered nor looked proud. She reloaded with hands that knew fear and work both.

Rafe shifted left. From there he could see Cruz's coat open where his hand had gone inside it.

Paper. Oilcloth. A narrow strip tied in dark thread.

Then Cruz jerked.

At first Rafe thought he had moved by choice. Then the blood showed along his ribs, dark against dust.

Ellie saw it too. "Cruz!"

"Still breathing," Cruz snapped, though his voice had lost its edge.

The scarred man shouted from the trees. "Give it over, Cruz. Mr. Bell pays for silence too."

Cruz laughed, and the sound turned into a cough. "Bell pays once."

"More than they will."

"They are not paying."

Rafe liked him a little for that and disliked him for making it easy.

Cruz pulled the oilcloth from his coat and held it. Bargain lived in him like a second spine.

"Horse," he called to Ellie.

"No," Ellie said.

"Then I burn it."

Paul's Winchester shifted toward him.

Rafe said, "Do that and Bell wins before you finish dying."

Cruz looked toward Rafe through the dust. "Always the Ranger with the cheerful word."

"I was never cheerful."

Another shot broke the stone near Cruz's knee. This one came from higher ground. Rafe had missed a fifth man.

That failure hit him cold.

He rolled back, found the muzzle smoke above the far bank, and fired into it. The fifth man ducked. Not dead. Not gone.

Barely in time was not the same thing as enough.

Ellie moved.

Paul reached for her and missed her sleeve by an inch. She crossed the open gravel low, one hand pressed to her hat, the other empty. Rafe fired again to keep the fifth man down. Luz shot at the cottonwood trunk where the scarred rider hid.

Cruz stared at Ellie as she slid behind the dead horse beside him.

"You are mad," he said.

"You are slow."

His mouth twitched. Blood had reached his belt.

"Listen," he said. "The strip reads by phrase, not letter. Mark first. Number second. Word third. The word tells the work. Toll for debt. Bell for church cover. Weather for burn or remove. Iron for bank transfer. Thorn for witness trouble."

Ellie held out her hand. "The strip."

He clutched it once.

"Cruz."

"There is an account phrase," he said. "Only one that puts Bell's hand near Calder. Bell-three-weather. Pryce line. Minshew hold."

Ellie's face changed. "Say it again."

"Bell-three-weather. Pryce line. Minshew hold." His breath caught. "That means remove the county page, hold the bank packet until the hearing pressure starts."

"Written?"

He pushed the oilcloth into her hand. "Inside. My strip. My phrase mark. Enough for a judge if the judge wants truth more than clean paper."

A bullet struck the dead horse with a wet slap.

Rafe shot toward the trees and saw the scarred rider fall back at last, one hand to his shoulder. Not dead. Hurt enough.

Ellie tucked the oilcloth inside her jacket.

Cruz caught her wrist.

Paul saw and started forward.

Rafe lifted a hand. Wait.

Cruz's grip was weak. His face had gone gray under the dust.

"Bell marked Calder's Reach before Korth ever rode," Cruz said.

Ellie went still.

"Minshew was one banker," Cruz said. "One. Bell had others. Land men. County men. Church men who did not know what their books carried." He coughed again. "Men like Bell do not buy land. They buy time until they can steal it wearing a coat."

Ellie bent closer. "Who else?"

Cruz smiled with blood on his teeth. "Now you want the horse."

"Cruz."

"Bank packet," he whispered. "Minshew kept it because he feared being the only man left holding Bell's rope."

The scarred rider shouted something from the trees. The words vanished under the river and gunfire, but Rafe saw movement: two men pulling back, one circling for a clearer shot at the dead horse.

"Move!" Rafe called.

Paul came then, firing as he crossed. He caught Ellie by the arm and pulled her back from Cruz. She fought him for half a breath, then let herself be moved because the oilcloth was inside her jacket and she knew what mattered now.

Cruz lifted the Smith & Wesson with both hands.

For a moment Rafe thought he meant to shoot Paul.

Instead Cruz fired at the man circling the horse. The shot went wide, but it made the man duck, and that was enough for Ellie to reach the next stone.

One useful thing, Rafe thought. Late, bitter, and still useful.

The fifth man fired from the far bank.

Cruz folded sideways against the dead horse.

Rafe shot back and this time saw the man drop his rifle and slide behind the bank.

Silence returned in pieces: first the firing stopped, then the horses, then the river seemed to return.

Bell's men withdrew through the cottonwoods with the scarred rider between them. Rafe let them go because Paul was calling his name and Ellie was already trying to stand.

"Rafe."

He crossed to Cruz.

Cruz was not dead yet. Close enough that the difference mattered only to the truth.

Ellie knelt beside him. "The strip is safe."

"No such thing," Cruz said.

"It is with me."

His eyes found hers. "Then make it louder than me."

He looked past her toward the west, where the sun had begun to lower and Calder's Reach lay under Bell's twenty-four hours.

"He will call me thief," Cruz whispered.

"You were."

That made him smile once, small and real. "Good. Say that too."

Then the breath left him.

Rafe stood over Gideon Cruz and felt no victory in the dust.

They had the key. They had the strip, the phrase, and the method. They had a dead criminal's truth wrapped in oilcloth and blood.

Bell would know exactly how to attack that.

Ellie rose with one hand pressed over the packet inside her jacket.

"Show me," Paul said.

Ellie looked at him.

"Not all of it," he said. "Enough to know he did not die handing us another fog."

She hesitated only a moment. Then she took one of the rescued ledger pages from her inner fold and laid it against a flat river stone. The page was blackened at one edge. Three ranch names ran down the center with small bell marks beside them.

Rafe crouched close enough to see, far enough not to shadow her hand.

Ellie opened Cruz's oilcloth. Inside lay a narrow strip marked with columns: signs, numbers, and words written in Cruz's tight, slanted hand. She put her finger on the first bell mark on the ledger, then found the matching sign on the strip.

"Bell mark," she said. "Number three. Word weather."

Paul looked toward Cruz's body.

"Pryce line. Minshew hold," Ellie said. "The phrase he gave matches the strip."

"Meaning?"

"Remove the county page. Hold the bank packet until Bell needs court pressure."

Rafe felt the old trail sense in him settle around the proof. Not belief. Not yet. Something better. Direction.

Ellie touched another mark beside Rawley Bend, then another beside Wexler. "These are not the same phrase. But they use the same method."

"Can you read them?"

"Partly. Not enough for court. Enough to know Cruz told truth about the key."

Paul stared at the strip as if it were a weapon made for another man's hand.

"Then Bell will want that burned next," he said.

Ellie folded the strip back into the oilcloth. "He will have to stand in line."

"Minshew next," she said.

Paul looked toward the road home. Then toward town.

Rafe watched him make the same choice again.

No one spoke for a while after that.

The river kept moving, bright over the shallows, unconcerned with dead men and account marks. Luz caught her frightened horse and stood with one hand on the bridle, watching Cruz as if she had expected to hate him cleaner once he was dead. Paul gathered Cruz's Smith & Wesson and kicked it away from his hand. Ellie folded the oilcloth twice, then once more, making the packet as small and tight as fear could make it.

Rafe went through Cruz's coat because someone had to. He found a tin of damp matches, two coins, a torn strip of bank paper with no writing left on it, and a small brass button that might have come from a better coat in a better year. No letter. No photograph. No proof that Gideon Cruz had ever expected anyone to mourn him.

"Anything?" Paul asked.

"Nothing useful."

Ellie looked up at that.

Rafe slipped the brass button into Cruz's pocket again. "Nothing useful to us."

They dragged Cruz away from the dead horse and laid him under the willow shade. There was no time to bury him right, and no shovel to pretend otherwise. Paul found a flat stone and set it near his shoulder, not as a marker anyone would trust, only as a refusal to leave him looking discarded.

Luz watched the stone go down.

"He would have sold us for less than Stowe offered me," she said.

"Maybe," Ellie answered.

"Maybe?"

Ellie touched the packet under her jacket. "He did not, at the end."

Luz looked toward the cottonwoods where Bell's men had vanished. "Endings are short. It is the rest that tells on a person."

Rafe liked that better than he wanted to.

Paul wiped river grit from his hand and looked west again. "Bell will hear before we reach town."

"Bell hears everything," Ellie said.

"Not everything," Rafe said.

They looked at him.

He nodded toward Cruz's body. "He did not hear that choice until too late."

Ellie finally breathed as if the air had found a way back into her chest.

Then she turned toward the horses. "Minshew."

The name put the day back on all of them.

Rafe took one last look at Cruz under the willow. A thief, a coward more than once, a clerk of stolen money, and at the last a man who had spent his last breath making Bell's account readable.

It was not redemption.

It was evidence.

The key had survived.

The witness had not.
Chapter 12: Minshew Cracks

By the time Ellie reached the county town, the church smoke had gone from her clothes and Cruz's blood had not.

It marked the edge of the oilcloth packet inside her jacket, dried dark where her hand had pressed it against her ribs during the ride. The cipher strip was safe. The ledger pages were safe. The survey notes were safe. None of that made Calder's Reach safe.

Bell's sequestration writ was still in force.

Twenty-four hours for men with papers to count what her father had built, what she and Paul had defended, what Sukey kept clean and Lem watched from the gate. Twenty-four hours for Bell to turn looking into touching and touching into possession.

Paul rode beside her in silence. The closer they came to town, the more often his eyes moved west, toward the road that would take him home.

"Say it," Ellie said.

He kept his eyes on the street ahead. "I want to be at Calder's Reach."

"I know."

"Wanting it does not mean I am going."

That hurt more than anger would have. Ellie reached across the space between horses and touched his sleeve once. Then they rode on.

Rafe came out of an alley near the feed store before they reached Minshew's bank. "Boy left a note with the liveryman."

"For us?" Ellie asked.

"For you."

He handed her a folded scrap. The paper smelled of bank ink and tobacco. No signature. Only a line written in a hurried hand.

Bell means to burn me with the books. Come before dark if you want his name.

Paul read it over her shoulder. "Minshew."

"Or bait," Rafe said.

"Both can be true," Ellie said.

Minshew's bank stood at the corner of the courthouse street, tidy as a lie. The front windows were clean. The brass plate beside the door had been polished. Josiah and Luz joined them behind the apothecary. Josiah had Clara hidden with Deacon Vale's cousin outside town, guarded by one of Sutter's sons and a shotgun. Luz looked toward the bank with the same expression she had worn near the river crossing: measuring exits more than doors.

"Then we go in with eyes open," Ellie said.

"You go in," Paul said. "I watch the street."

She looked at him.

"You need him talking," Paul said. "He will talk less if I have both hands around his collar."

That was true enough to pass for wisdom.

Ellie crossed the street alone.

The bank door opened before she touched it.

Thad Minshew stood inside with a Remington Model 1875 revolver in one shaking hand and sweat bright along his upper lip. He had lost weight since she last saw him. His collar hung loose. His hair, usually oiled smooth, had come undone at one side.

"Shut the door," he said.

"Yes."

Minshew swallowed.

"Say it," Ellie said.

"Yes."

Rhys Pryce.

Minshew looked at it, then away.

"No," Ellie said.

"Look at it."

"Mrs. James--"

"Look at it."

"I do not know his name."

"No."

"At first I thought I was inside the arrangement. Bell used my bank. Bell needed my books. Bell said men like us had to make order before fools wasted the county into dust."

"And you believed that?"

"I believed the deposits." He looked back at her. "I believed the road contracts. I believed the men who came in poor and left owing more. I believed what profited me. Do you want a prettier confession?"

"No."

"Good. I am too tired to invent one."

Ellie stayed by the customer table, the forged Rhys Pryce signature still folded under her hand. She had wanted, once, to see Minshew cornered. She had imagined anger would feel cleaner when it arrived. It did not. It felt like another room full of smoke.

Minshew touched the teller bars. "Then the packets changed. Bell's men stopped asking and began delivering. Papers came already signed. My marks were copied before I made them. Payments went out under initials I did not enter. When I objected, Stowe asked me how much of Calder's Reach I thought a prison man could see from a barred window."

"That frightened you."

"Yes."

"Not what you did. What might happen to you."

He shut his eyes. "Yes."

The honesty did nothing to cleanse him. It only made him more useful.

"To whom?"

"Marr. Stowe. Two land men. And courthouse money."

Ellie thought of Deputy Nix at the deed room door, asking where she would search next.

"Nix?" she asked.

Minshew's silence answered before his mouth did.

"Not by full name," he said. "Initial and route marks."

"Enough to prove?"

"Enough to start a rope if a judge wants one."

Ellie studied him across the little room and understood, with a coldness that surprised her, that Minshew had never been the center of anything. He had wanted to be. He had dressed his bank like a courthouse and talked like credit made him kin to power, but Bell had used him the way a man used a drawer: open, take, shut. The knowledge did not soften what Minshew had done. It made the shape of the crime clearer.

"How many?" she asked.

Minshew looked at her.

"How many ranches before Calder's Reach?"

His fingers touched the teller bars, one by one, as if counting without meaning to. "Rawley Bend. Wexler. A south pasture Bell folded into road frontage. Two smaller claims that never reached hearing because the owners signed."

"Signed what?"

"Settlement. Debt acknowledgment. Quit paper. Whatever Bell's lawyer put before them after the records went missing."

Ellie thought of all the clean phrases men used when they wanted theft to wear a collar.

"And Calder's Reach?"

Minshew's eyes went back to the folded signature page in her hand. "Bell wanted that one named. He said Pryce made a public lesson of him once, so the land would answer publicly."

Ellie's grip tightened on the paper until the crease bit her palm.

Ellie folded the forged signature page and put it away.

"Open the safe."

Minshew stood. "If I do, Bell kills me."

"If you do not, he kills you and burns what would have made your death useful."

"Useful." He gave a small, miserable smile. "That is all I get?"

"From me? Yes."

He turned toward the back office.

Then stopped.

Outside, a horse stamped. Then another.

Not passing.

Waiting.

Paul's voice came through the door, low and hard. "Ellie."

Minshew went white.

"Too late," he whispered.

Ellie crossed to the side window and looked through the narrow gap in the shutter.

Three riders stood across the street. Two more had moved into the alley by the bank wall. The gray horse was not among them, but their stillness had the same training.

Bell's men had come before the safe opened.

Behind her, Minshew's breath shook.

"Open it now," Ellie said.

The first shot broke the front window.
Chapter 13: The Bank Under Fire

The first shot broke the front window and turned the bank light white with flying glass.

Paul came through the door before the pieces finished falling. Ellie was already down behind the customer table. Minshew stood in the open with his Remington hanging useless at his side, staring at the hole in the window as if no man had ever shot at money before.

Paul caught him by the collar and threw him behind the teller counter.

"Safe," Paul said.

Minshew blinked.

"Move."

Another round punched through the door and buried itself in the wall behind the cashier's desk. Rafe's Spencer answered from the street outside. Josiah's borrowed double gun answered from the alley mouth. Luz shouted something in Spanish from the rear, then a horse screamed and hooves scattered across the boards.

They were not robbing the bank.

Paul knew it by the shots. Robbers shot men. Bell's riders shot windows, back walls, record shelves, lamp glass. They wanted paper burned, men blamed, and Minshew dead enough to carry all the sin quietly.

Ellie crawled to the counter with soot still dark at her cuff from the church storehouse. "Minshew."

"I cannot open it while they are shooting."

Paul shoved him toward the back office. "You can open it faster while they are shooting."

The banker stumbled through the half door into the rear room. Ellie followed. Paul stayed at the front long enough to drag a filing cabinet across the broken window gap. It would not stop a rifle ball. It would make a man choose his line.

Rafe slipped in through the door, hat brim torn where a shot had found it. "Five outside. Maybe six. One trying for the rear wall."

"Fire?"

"Likely."

Paul looked toward the back office. "Keep them from the rear."

Rafe nodded and vanished again.

Josiah came through the alley door backward, the double gun leveled through the opening until he kicked it shut with his heel. A round struck the outer plank and threw splinters across his sleeve.

"Two in the alley," he said. "One carrying bottles. One watching the roofline."

"Roofline?" Paul asked.

"Man wants the back office window from above."

Paul looked up. The ceiling boards were old pine. A rifleman on the roof of the next building could put a ball through the high glass and into any man at the safe.

"Luz!" Paul called.

"I see him," she answered from somewhere beyond the rear wall.

Paul did not ask how. A moment later Luz's brass-framed Winchester 1866 cracked from the alley, followed by a man's shout and the hard scrape of boots on shingles. She had not killed him. She had made the roof no longer comfortable. That would do.

Behind the counter, the bank ledgers sat in neat rows. Minshew had kept good books for bad work. Paul grabbed the top shelf and shoved the ledgers to the floor behind the counter, away from the window line. Paper thudded and slid across the boards.

The smell of smoke came a moment later.

"Luz!" Paul shouted.

"Back wall," she called. "Bottle broke outside."

Paul ran to the rear hall. Through the back office he saw Ellie standing over Minshew beside a framed picture that had been pulled from the wall. Behind it was a small iron safe set low into the plaster.

Minshew's hands shook on the dial.

"If you miss the turn, start over," Ellie said.

"I know my own safe."

"Then act like it."

His fingers slipped on the brass knob.

Ellie caught his wrist, not gently. "Look at me."

He did.

"If Bell's men come through that door, Paul will shoot until he cannot. Rafe will shoot until he cannot. Josiah will hold the alley. Luz has already chosen her side badly enough to die for it. Do not waste what they are buying you."

Minshew swallowed. "Left twenty."

"Then turn left."

"Right six."

"Turn."

"Left again."

The bank shook with another shot. Dust fell from the picture frame above the safe. Minshew flinched and nearly lost the dial.

Ellie held his wrist in place. "Again."

Paul watched from the doorway and understood the shape of the room. He was not guarding money. He was guarding seconds. Ellie was spending them one by one.

Smoke crawled under the rear door. A flame licked at the window curtain in the outer room. Paul crossed to it, tore the curtain down, and stamped it against the floor.

Outside, a man ran past the rear window with a second bottle in his hand.

Paul fired through the glass.

The man fell out of sight. The bottle broke in the dust instead of the bank.

"Open," Minshew whispered.

The safe door clicked.

Ellie did not reach in. She made Minshew do it.

He drew out a narrow tin dispatch box, black at the corners and sealed with wax that had been cut and pressed again more than once. His face changed when he held it. Not remorse. Recognition of his own trap.

"Give it to me," Ellie said.

Minshew clutched it.

Paul stepped into the doorway. "Do not make her ask twice."

Minshew handed it over.

A rifle shot broke the front clock. The glass face burst and the pendulum gave one wild swing before stopping.

Ellie opened the tin box on the desk. Paul watched the street, but he saw enough: folded mortgage drafts, bank transfer copies, account strips, payment slips tied in red thread, and one page marked with Bell's initials beside instructions written in another hand but countersigned in Bell's.

Ellie's fingers moved faster than panic and slower than fear. She took out the top sheet.

"Bell to Minshew," she said. "Hold Calder packet until county record altered."

Minshew made a sound.

"False debt transfer," she said, pulling another. "Korth cattle money routed through Bell account."

She laid the sheets in order despite the shooting: instruction, transfer, mortgage draft, payment slips. Paul had watched her do the same thing in quieter rooms with less at stake. Here, with smoke in the corners and glass under her boots, the order mattered more. A loose page could be called confusion. A chain could speak.

"This one names Bell's holding account," she said. "Same marks as the church ledger."

Minshew pointed with two fingers he could not keep steady. "That line is the cattle money. Korth's receipts went there first. Then Bell's office wrote them as debt purchase."

"Against Calder?"

"Against Calder, Rawley Bend, Wexler. Others." He looked sick. "Not all at once. Never all at once. Bell said a valley is taken one paper at a time."

Ellie pulled another page free. "Mortgage draft in Rhys Pryce's name."

"Copy draft," Minshew said. "Not the hand copy. That came later."

"Who ordered the hand copy?"

He pointed to the instruction sheet without touching it. "Initialed there."

"S.B."

Minshew nodded.

Another shot hit the filing cabinet and rang the metal like a bell.

Paul fired once through the broken window and drove a shadow back from the hitching rail.

"Nix?" he asked.

Ellie found a narrow payment slip. Her face hardened. "C.N. courthouse route. Two payments. One before the deed room burned. One after the notice."

Minshew sat down as if his legs had been cut. "He said no one would read initials."

"People always say that about the thing that hangs them," Paul said.

Smoke thickened at the rear.

Rafe came in coughing. "They are falling back."

"Why?"

Josiah answered from the doorway, face streaked with ash. "Because they see what they came for is gone or moving."

Luz pushed in behind him with a bloody line across one cheek. "And because a rider left town hard. East road first, then south."

Paul looked at Ellie.

She was already packing the bank packet inside her jacket with Cruz's strip, the ledger pages, and the survey notes. So much paper. So little time.

"Calder's Reach?" he asked.

Luz nodded. "Levy party. Sheriff's deputies and Bell's men. They waited until the bank caught fire."

The words struck harder than the shots.

Paul turned toward the street.

Ellie caught his arm. "If you ride blind, Bell gets what he wants."

"If I do not ride, he walks through our gate."

"Then ride with orders, not anger."

That held him because it sounded like the thing he had told Lem, and because she was right, and because being right had become the cruelest habit of the day.

Minshew stood unsteadily. "What about me?"

Paul looked at him. "You live."

"They will kill me."

"Not if prison gets you first."

Josiah took Minshew's Remington from the floor and emptied it. "I can hold him."

Ellie shook her head. "He comes to Pryor alive. If Bell can say Minshew died confessing to nothing, he will."

Rafe looked through the broken window. "Street is opening. We have minutes."

Paul made the decision in pieces because one whole choice would have split him. "Rafe, with Ellie to Pryor. Luz, find the fastest line to Calder's Reach and stay ahead of any rider if you can. Josiah, Minshew alive. I ride for the ranch."

"Alone?" Ellie asked.

"No." He looked at Rafe. "Send whoever you can after me when she reaches the judge."

Ellie held his gaze.

Then she nodded.

Josiah took a length of cash-drawer cord and bound Minshew's hands in front, tight enough to hold and loose enough that a judge would not see cruelty before guilt. Minshew did not resist. All the fight had gone out of him when the tin box opened.

"You try to run," Josiah said, "and I will bring you back slower."

Minshew looked at the smoke crawling along his ceiling. "I have nowhere left to run."

Rafe gathered the packet, then gave it back to Ellie when she held out her hand. "You carry it."

"You think I will not?"

"I think every man in the county will aim at the person carrying it."

Ellie tucked the packet deeper under her jacket. "Then they had better aim true."

Paul caught her soot-dark hand before she turned away.

"I thought we were done living out of saddlebags," he said.

Her fingers closed once around his. "Then keep our supper table where it is."

He released her because the words had given them both their work.

Outside, the town had begun shouting for buckets. Men who had hidden during the shooting now found courage against smoke. That suited Paul. Buckets would keep the bank from burning; witnesses would remember Minshew leaving alive.

He pointed to the nearest store boy. "You. Run to Judge Pryor. Tell him Mrs. James is bringing bank proof and a prisoner. Say those words."

The boy stared at the Winchester in Paul's hand.

"Go."

He went.

The bank behind them smoked and ticked with cooling glass.

Paul stepped into the street with his Winchester in one hand and Bell's sequestration writ burning in his mind.

The road home and the road to proof had split cleanly.

He took the road home.
Chapter 14: The Calder Stand

Paul reached Calder's Reach with his horse blowing foam and the sheriff's levy party already at the gate.

Six riders waited on the county road, three with deputy stars and three without. Behind them stood a two-horse wagon with empty sideboards, the kind a man might use for feed sacks, tools, or anything else he had decided could be counted and carried. Lem Trowbridge stood inside the gate with a rifle in both hands and too much anger in his face. Sukey Varn waited on the porch with the smokehouse key tied at her waist. Two of Mizpah's boys held the barn doors shut as if their weight alone could keep law from walking in.

Paul rode between Lem and the gate before anyone spoke.

"Lower it," he said.

Lem did not move.

"Lem."

The young man's jaw shook. "They said the writ lets them take the teams and cattle. One of Bell's men says it covers the house goods too."

"Lower the rifle."

This time Lem heard the order under the words. He lowered the muzzle.

Paul swung down. His legs felt the ride from town, but he did not let the yard see it. He looked at the men outside the gate. One deputy he knew by sight. The other two were county men who had learned to let paper do their pushing. Mercer Stowe sat a bay horse behind them, clean-coated, calm, his hands folded over the saddle horn.

No Bell.

Not yet.

The lead deputy unfolded a paper. "Paul James?"

"You know me."

"Writ of sequestration out of the district court. It commands the sheriff to take into custody the property described in Bell's schedule: Calder-branded cattle, two team horses, wagons, harness, and branded equipment, returnable at tomorrow's hearing."

"The writ," Paul said.

"That is the paper."

"Does not name the house, smokehouse, feed stores, or the land."

The deputy's eyes shifted toward Stowe and back. "No lawful officer said it did."

"Then keep Bell's private men off what the writ does not name."

Stowe smiled a little.

The deputy held up the paper. "We have authority to identify the listed property and take it into custody."

"You will identify it with witnesses," Paul said. "You will match every brand and description in that schedule. You will open no lock. You will write nothing we do not see. Your deputies may execute the writ. Bell's hired men may not."

One of the non-deputies laughed. "Hear him giving law lessons."

Paul looked at Lem, Sukey, the boys, the hands gathering by the corral. "No rifles raised unless they fire first. No signatures. No man alone with Bell's men. Sukey, you keep the house keys. Lem, you take two hands and stand by the barn. Mizpah's boys, one of you ride the fence and bring any neighbor willing to watch."

"Watch what?" the younger boy asked.

"Everything."

The deputy frowned. "You interfering with court authority?"

"I am making witnesses."

That put silence in the road for a moment.

Stowe nudged his horse forward. "Mr. James, this can be peaceful if you do not teach these hands to mistake delay for courage."

"It will be peaceful if your men remember Bell's schedule is not the whole ranch."

The gate opened by Paul's hand, not theirs.

That mattered.

The first hour was knives drawn slowly. Bell's men counted horses under Lem's stare. They noted saddles, harness, plows, feed stores, and the lower water trough. Every time one man wrote, Paul made a ranch hand read the line aloud. Every time a deputy stepped toward a door, Sukey stood in front of it with her key at her waist and her chin lifted high enough to shame him.

Luz arrived from the lower road near midafternoon, her horse lathered and her face gray with dust.

"Ellie reached town?" Paul asked.

"Rafe took her to Pryor," Luz said. "Josiah has Minshew alive. I came ahead of the next rider."

"What next rider?"

She looked toward the road.

Paul followed her gaze and saw a single horseman coming fast from town. Not Bell's men. A message rider. Or bait. In this day there was little difference until a man survived reading the paper.

Before the rider reached them, trouble came from the barn.

One of Stowe's men had stepped past Lem and laid a hand on the gray team horse's bridle.

"Levy mark," the man said.

"You do not mark our horses," Lem snapped.

"Court says I count what is under claim."

"Count with your eyes."

The man shoved him.

Lem's rifle came up halfway.

Paul crossed the yard at a run and caught the barrel before it rose.

"No."

"He put hands on me."

"Then he wasted hands."

"Paul--"

Paul turned Lem by the shoulder until the young man had to look at him and not the smirking man by the horse. "You fire, Bell owns the story before Ellie opens her mouth."

Lem's breath came hard.

"Give me the rifle."

For a terrible second, Paul thought he would refuse.

Then Lem let go.

Paul took the rifle and passed it to Sukey, who held it like a broom she meant to break over someone's head if the Lord looked away.

Stowe had watched the whole thing.

"Discipline," he said. "I admire that."

"No," Paul said. "You count on it failing."

Stowe's smile thinned.

At the smokehouse, another deputy tried the door. Sukey slapped his hand with the key ring hard enough to make metal sing.

"Not in the writ," she said. "And not supper."

A neighbor laughed once, then swallowed it when Stowe looked over.

Good, Paul thought. Let there be neighbors. Let there be memory.

The message rider reached the gate and drew up hard. "From town."

Stowe moved first. Paul stepped between him and the rider.

"Hand it here," Paul said.

The rider hesitated. "For Mr. Bell."

That was when Paul saw the carriage turning from the road beyond the cottonwoods.

Silas Bell had come after all.

The carriage rolled up behind the levy party, polished black under the dust. Bell stepped down slowly, as if arriving at a sale already agreed upon. He wore the same calm from the courthouse steps. His gaze moved over the yard, the hands, the open levy list, the barn, the house, the porch where Sukey stood with keys and rifle both.

"Mr. James," Bell said. "You have made a military affair out of arithmetic."

"Your men keep confusing counting with owning."

"Ownership is often a matter of proper counting." Bell took the message from the rider and did not open it. "Judge Pryor will soon have all the papers before him."

"He will."

Bell looked toward the house. "Mrs. James is not here to protect her sentiment."

"She is where she needs to be."

"No," Bell said softly. "She is where sentiment always sends people when arithmetic has finished with them. To a room full of pleas."

Paul said nothing.

Bell let his gaze travel across the fenced pastures, the barn, the smokehouse, the hands, the neighbors beginning to gather along the road. "Land is not saved by loving it. Land is saved by water rights, credit, roads, labor, markets, and a man willing to make use of all five. Your wife's father never understood that. He mistook refusal for principle."

Paul felt the name Rhys Pryce move through the yard like a struck match.

"With damaged pages, a dead criminal's strip, and the word of a banker who will say anything to save his neck?"

Paul kept still.

Bell had heard too much too fast. Even with Nix under suspicion and kept away from their final route, the habit of Bell knowing things had not died yet.

"You are quiet," Bell said.

"I am listening to a man afraid of paper."

Stowe's hand moved near his coat. Not to draw. To invite a draw.

Paul saw Lem see it. Saw the hands stiffen. Saw Sukey's grip shift on the rifle.

He lifted one hand, palm down.

"No one raises," he said.

Bell smiled. "How long do you think lowered guns will hold land?"

Paul looked at the counted horses, the open barn, the house with its crooked shutter, the hands who had not fired, the neighbors watching from the fence, and the road where Ellie's proof was fighting without him.

"Long enough," he said.

Bell's eyes cooled.

Paul stepped close enough that only the men at the gate could hear him clearly. "A man who owns land through fear never truly owns it."

Bell's polite face showed the smallest crack.

Then the courthouse bell began to ring in town, faint over the distance.

Every head turned.

Bell opened the message at last.

Paul did not know what it said. Not yet. But Bell read it once, and the certainty in his face hardened into something that had to pretend.

"The hearing is not finished," Bell said.

"No," Paul answered. "But you heard it start."

Bell folded the message once and turned toward his carriage. "Finish the levy," he told Stowe. "Bring the return to town."

The driver was already gathering the reins when Bell stepped inside. The carriage turned hard on the county road and ran for the courthouse, leaving its dust over the men who had expected him to remain and watch possession become fact.

Stowe had heard it too.

Paul saw the enforcer's attention slide from Bell to the deputy's levy list on the wagon seat. A small movement. A practical one. Not panic. Calculation. The kind of calculation a man made when he understood his employer might fall but meant to carry away whatever paper could still be useful.

Stowe turned his horse one step toward the wagon.

Luz moved before Paul could.

She came from the lower side of the yard with a rope in one hand and dust streaked across her face. "Leave the book."

Stowe looked down at her. "You have made a habit of poor bargains."

"This one is free."

He smiled. "Move."

Luz did not. "You offered me a road south. Then you sent men to kill Cruz before he could use one. That tells me what Bell's roads are worth."

Stowe's hand dipped.

Paul's voice cut across the yard. "No one raises."

Luz's rope snapped up, not at Stowe's throat, but around the bay's bridle. She pulled hard enough to turn the horse's head and spoil the draw. Lem and two hands were there an instant later, not firing, just bodies and hands and anger held in check.

Stowe came out of the saddle fighting. The rope burned across Luz's palm. His forearm caught her mouth before Lem and the hands forced his arms behind him. She staggered once, touched blood at her lip, and kept hold of the bridle.

Stowe looked from Luz to Paul to the neighbors by the fence. Too many eyes. Too much daylight. Not enough legal cover for the thing his hand wanted to do.

"Hold him," Paul said.

The hands took Stowe from the saddle. He fought once, then stopped when he saw the wagon still empty and every witness watching.

"Court paper," Sukey said from the porch, keys bright at her waist. "Not escape."

Luz picked up the deputy's levy list from the wagon seat and held it out to Paul.

Paul took the list from her without weighing the hand that offered it.

The yard held. The rifles stayed lowered. The empty wagon waited in Bell's dust while his enforcer stood bound beside it.
Chapter 15: The Bell Account

Ellie reached the courthouse with smoke still in her hair and Minshew walking between Rafe and Josiah like a man being carried by his own fear.

The hearing room had filled, men along the walls and women gripping gloves and handkerchiefs on the benches. Dust lay thick on the plank floor. The open windows gave no wind, and heat held the room down.

District Judge Ansel Pryor sat at the front table with Bell's papers stacked before him.

Silas Bell stood beside Everett Marr as if the room belonged to him already. Dust still marked the lower edge of Bell's black coat from the fast carriage ride back from Calder's Reach.

"Your Honor," Marr was saying, "the sheriff has begun executing the writ at Calder's Reach. There is no cause to dissolve it or delay judgment on Mr. Bell's lien merely because Mrs. James continues to gather broken scraps and frightened stories."

Ellie stepped inside before Pryor could answer.

Every face turned.

Rafe's hand stayed near his holster, not touching it. Josiah held Minshew by one arm. Clara Whitcomb and Deacon Amos Vale were already inside; Sutter's son had brought them from their hiding place when Pryor called the hearing. Clara stood pale and straight-backed, with the deacon near enough to catch her if her knees failed. Ellie carried the tin dispatch box against her ribs.

Bell's eyes went to the box first.

Then at Ellie.

His expression stayed smooth. That was how she knew he had felt the blow.

"Mrs. James," Pryor said. "You are late."

"No, Judge," Ellie said. "I am in time."

Marr gave a thin laugh. "In time with a prisoner and a burned bank box?"

"With proof," Ellie said.

Pryor looked past her to Minshew. "Mr. Minshew, are you here of your own accord?"

Minshew swallowed. "No, sir."

"That may be the first honest thing he has said in this room," Ellie said.

A murmur moved through the benches.

Pryor struck the table with the heel of his hand. "Quiet."

Bell smiled gently, as if saddened by the scene. "Your Honor, I will not object to hearing her. A desperate owner should be allowed to spend her last words."

Ellie set the tin box on the front table.

It made a small, hard sound.

"Not last," she said.

Pryor measured the box against Bell's stack of clean papers. "Begin with what you can prove."

He motioned to the district clerk. Ellie, Clara, Deacon Vale, and Minshew raised their right hands in turn and swore to tell the truth. The clerk entered their names in the minute book before Ellie turned to Clara.

Clara's hands were shaking. She tried to hide them by gripping her skirt. The room saw anyway.

Marr saw first. "Miss Whitcomb, you are the clerk's assistant?"

"Yes, sir."

"Not the clerk."

"No, sir."

"And you were frightened when the deed room burned."

"Yes, sir."

"Frightened women remember what fear tells them to remember."

Clara's chin trembled.

Ellie stepped close enough that Clara could look at one face instead of fifty. "Tell Judge Pryor what happened after hours."

Clara took one breath. Then another.

"Mr. Minshew came after closing," she said. "Deputy Nix let him through the side door. He carried a packet tied with red string. The signature page looked newer than the others, and the account corner had a small bell mark beside a number."

Nix stood motionless near the back wall in deputy blue, but his eyes shifted toward the door.

Marr spread his hands. "A bell mark. In a county where Mr. Bell has donated a church bell, that is hardly a crime."

Ellie opened her satchel and laid Clara's copied notation on the table. "Clara wrote the shape before she ever saw the church ledger. The same mark appears here, here, and here."

She set down three pieces: Clara's note, the saved church ledger page, and one strip from Minshew's packet.

Pryor leaned forward.

Bell did not.

"The official deed page is still missing," Marr said. "Mrs. James cannot prove what is not there."

"No," Ellie said. "But I can prove why it was taken."

She untied the surveyor notes from the station and placed them beside Rhys Pryce's old land receipt, the one whose crease had nearly worn through the middle. Her father's true signature sat dark at the bottom, hard-pressed, the R driven down and the y hooked open.

Then she laid Bell's forged mortgage page beside it.

"This signature is copied," she said. "The hand knows the letters but not the weight. My father drove the first stroke of his R through the paper when he was angry or certain. The false page floats. The date is wrong for the boundary it names. This mortgage paper claims land under a line that was not recorded until after the supposed pledge."

Marr stepped in. "A daughter's opinion."

Ellie lifted the surveyor note. "A surveyor's date."

She lifted the receipt. "My father's hand."

She touched Clara's note. "A witness to a new page filed after hours."

She touched the empty space where the county deed page should have been. "And a missing record Bell needed gone."

Pryor looked to Minshew. "Did you file that packet?"

Minshew's lips parted. No sound came.

Josiah's hand tightened on his arm.

"Yes," Minshew whispered.

Bell's face remained calm, but the skin near his eyes drew tight.

"Under whose instruction?" Pryor asked.

Minshew glanced at Bell and lowered his eyes.

Marr cut in. "Your Honor, Mr. Minshew is a confessed corrupt banker under armed escort. His word is worth less than the smoke on his coat."

"I agree," Ellie said.

The room shifted at that.

She opened the church ledger pages Deacon Vale had saved from the fire. The edges were browned. One corner broke when she touched it.

"That is why I am not asking you to trust him alone."

Deacon Vale stepped forward with his hat pressed against his chest. "That ledger is from our bell fund."

Bell turned to him with soft disappointment. "Amos."

The deacon flinched at the use of his name.

"Mr. Bell gave openly to that fund," Marr said. "Public charity is not secret fraud."

"No," Ellie said. "Charity is not. Hiding land marks in charity books is."

She turned the ledger page so Pryor could see. "The same bell mark appears beside Calder's Reach, Rawley Bend, and the Wexler place. Those are only the entries we followed. Each one has a number that matches a debt transfer or burned county page in Minshew's notes."

Deacon Vale's voice came rough. "I wrote those entries when money came in. I did not know what the marks meant. The marks were not mine."

"Who brought the numbers?" Pryor asked.

Vale faced Bell, and grief folded his face. "Mr. Bell's office."

Bell stepped past his lawyer.

"A church ledger full of innocent donations and a frightened old man's confusion," he said. "That is thin ground, Mrs. James."

"It was thin until Cruz explained how to read it."

Bell laughed once.

There was no humor in it.

"Gideon Cruz," he said. "A dead thief. A cattle-money clerk. A man who sold everyone he rode with."

"Yes," Ellie said. "All of that."

She took out the cipher strip.

It was bent, stained, and torn at one edge. Cruz's hand ran across it in narrow marks and hard shorthand. The room seemed to lean toward it.

"Cruz said toll means land debt. Bell means church cover. Weather means burn or remove the matching county page." Ellie set the strip beside the church ledger. "I do not ask you to take his soul on credit. I ask you to match the marks."

She read one line.

"Bell. Weather. Calder."

Then she touched the missing deed page reference.

"Church cover. Removed page. Calder's Reach."

She read another.

"Bell. Toll. Wexler."

Then she touched Minshew's transfer sheet.

"Debt purchase against the Wexler place."

Pryor's mouth hardened.

Marr had lost some color. "Coincidence of marks."

"No," Ellie said. "A system."

A sound came from the left side of the room. Not loud. Not even a word at first.

An older man in a sun-faded coat stood from the bench, hat crushed in both hands. Ellie knew his face but not his name. He had been at the mission storehouse after the fire, one of the men who watched the smoke and said nothing.

"Rawley Bend was my brother's place," he said.

Marr turned sharply. "Sit down."

The man stayed standing. "He signed because the county page went missing and the bank said fighting would cost more than the land. He died thinking he had been a fool."

Pryor looked at him, then at the ledger.

The man sat down slowly, as if standing had used all he had.

Ellie left that grief in the room where Bell had to breathe it.

She opened the tin dispatch box.

Inside lay the bank packet: Bell-to-Minshew instructions, transfer slips, forged mortgage drafts, cattle-money routes, and the small C.N. payment notes that had made the whole county feel watched.

Minshew recoiled from the papers as if they could strike him.

"This came from Minshew's safe," Ellie said. "Rafe Culver and Josiah Breckin watched him open it. Paul James held Bell's men outside the bank long enough for that to happen. The packet shows Korth's stolen cattle money routed as debt purchase. It shows forged Calder mortgage drafts before the deed room burned. It shows S.B. instructions to Minshew to file the packet after hours."

Marr found his voice. "S.B. could be any man."

Ellie turned over the top sheet.

Bell's countersign sat below the instruction, not a full name, but the same clipped hand used on public road pledges and church subscription receipts. She laid one of those receipts beside it.

"Any man with Silas Bell's hand," she said.

The room breathed in.

Bell's face had gone still in a different way now. Not calm. Held.

Pryor reached for the packet, then stopped. "And these initials?"

Ellie set the small payment slips apart.

"C.N.," she said. "Courthouse route. Paid before the deed room burned. Paid after Clara was moved. Paid before the stage packet reached you."

Deputy Caleb Nix took one backward step.

Rafe saw it.

"Stand still," Rafe said.

Nix's hand dropped toward the Colt Single Action Army at his hip.

Josiah moved Minshew aside so fast the banker stumbled. Rafe's worn Colt Navy conversion came up without flourish.

Pryor stood. "Deputy Nix, remove your hand."

Nix froze.

The room finally understood why Bell had always arrived one step ahead.

Pryor pointed to the loyal town marshal by the door. "Take his weapon."

The marshal did.

Nix said nothing as the gun left his belt. Silence was confession enough for the room, if not for the law.

Pryor withheld judgment and read. The waiting was worse than Bell's contempt. He checked Clara's note against the ledger mark, set the surveyor date beside the false boundary, read Cruz's strip twice, and held Bell's countersign against the church receipt until the men at the back wall understood the comparison.

Marr tried once to speak.

Pryor raised one finger without looking up.

The lawyer stopped.

Outside, somewhere far down the street, a horse broke into a trot. Ellie thought of Paul at the gate, of lowered rifles and an empty wagon, of all the ways a minute could become too long.

Bell looked around then. Not at the papers. At the people watching him.

His public face had been built for rooms like this. It had survived business failures, debt scares, dead riders, burned records, and the muddy work of other men's hands. But it had not been built to stand with every hidden thing named out loud.

"You think this county will remember her scraps?" Bell asked.

No one answered.

He fixed his eyes on Ellie. "History remembers owners, not widows."

Rafe made a sound under his breath. Clara went white. Deacon Vale closed his eyes.

Ellie felt the words strike where Bell meant them to strike: at her fear, her marriage, her dead father, Paul holding a ranch yard with lowered rifles because she had asked the truth to arrive in time.

She put her hand on the bank packet.

"The truth doesn't stop being true because powerful men write different papers."

For one long second, the only sound in the room was the dry tick of Pryor's pen settling on the table.

Then Judge Pryor gathered Bell's clean petition, Bell's false packet, Clara's note, the surveyor pages, the church ledger, the cipher strip, and the bank packet into one spread before him.

Not stacked.

Spread.

Ellie understood the difference before he spoke.

"Mr. Bell," Pryor said, "this court rejects your claim on Calder's Reach."

Marr started up. "Your Honor--"

"Sit down."

The lawyer sat.

Pryor's voice grew harder. "The writ of sequestration is dissolved. The sheriff will release every animal and article held under it. The mortgage instrument is forged, and the foreclosure petition founded upon it is dismissed. The clerk will preserve every exhibit laid before this court. On the sworn testimony heard here, I find probable cause for forgery, false transfer, intimidation of witnesses, and conspiracy in the burning and removal of county records. The clerk will issue the warrants."

Bell smiled again, but now it looked like pain wearing manners.

"You overstep," he said.

He turned his head slightly toward Marr. "Give notice. Put up the bond. Take the claim to the next court."

Marr laid one hand on his leather case but did not open it. "The civil judgment may be appealed," he said carefully. "The warrants are another matter."

Bell's face changed by less than a breath. One of his own men had drawn a boundary he could not purchase away.

"I should have stepped sooner," Pryor answered.

That landed harder than a gavel.

The district clerk drew three warrants. Pryor signed them one after another: Bell, Nix, Minshew. The town marshal waited until the ink left the pen, then moved toward Bell.

For a moment Ellie thought Bell would run, or reach, or command the room by force. Instead he lifted his hands slightly, palms out, as if even arrest were an arrangement he meant to supervise.

"Careful," Bell said. "Men who build counties are not easily removed from them."

"Men who burn records are," Pryor said.

The marshal took him.

Bell left without looking back at the table. More than the arrest, that told Ellie the papers had become stronger than his name.

Minshew began to shake.

Pryor looked at him next. "Thad Minshew, you will remain in custody. Your cooperation will be recorded. It will not cleanse you."

Minshew nodded once, quick and broken. "Yes, sir."

Nix was taken after him. He avoided Clara's eyes; she watched anyway, trembling harder now that she no longer had to stand against him.

Ellie reached for her hand.

Clara gripped back.

Josiah had stayed near Minshew through the hearing. Now he stepped beside Clara and gave her the grave nod he might have offered a soldier after a hard position.

"You held your ground," he said.

Clara wiped her cheek. "You got me here."

"You walked the last part."

Deacon Vale bent and picked up the browned church ledger page with both hands. "I will write the names right," he said, not to Pryor and not quite to Ellie. "The ones he used. The ones he harmed. I will write them where the bell money was kept, so nobody mistakes that book again."

Ellie nodded because her throat had tightened too much for speech.

Outside, the courthouse bell rang again. Not warning now. Calling.

Rafe leaned close. "We need to get word to Paul."

"No," Ellie said, looking at the doorway where the road ran west toward Calder's Reach. "We need to bring it."

By the time they reached the ranch, the sun had dropped low enough to lay gold along the cut fence wire.

The levy wagon still stood empty by the gate.

Paul stood inside the yard with dust on his coat and the whole weight of the afternoon in his shoulders. Bell's men had pulled back to the road. Stowe sat bound near the wagon wheel with two ranch hands and three neighbors watching him. Sukey stood on the porch with the house keys still at her waist. Lem had no rifle in his hands.

Luz stood by the wagon with the deputy's levy list tucked under one arm.

Ellie saw her before Paul spoke. Saw the rope burn across Luz's palm, the split lip, the way she stood apart as if waiting to be told whether she belonged near the victory or only near the work that bought it.

"Stowe tried to take the levy list," Paul said.

Luz lifted the ledger slightly. "He was too slow."

Rafe noticed her burned hand. "You paid for it."

"Not enough to brag."

Ellie crossed the yard and took the ledger from her. For a moment the two women held the same book between them: Bell's last count of Calder's Reach, stopped before it could become possession.

"You could have run at the river," Ellie said.

Luz's eyes moved once toward the south road, then back. "I know."

"You could have run here."

"I know that too."

Ellie held the ledger against the reversal order. "Then stay long enough to eat."

Luz looked almost startled.

Paul glanced at Ellie, then at Luz, and gave a small nod.

It was not trust entire. Not yet. But it was a place to stand.

Paul saw Ellie before he saw the paper.

He walked to the gate.

She held out Pryor's written order dissolving the writ.

Paul studied her face before taking it, then breathed like a man who had crossed deep water.

"Done?" he asked.

"Done enough for tonight."

Then he took the paper.

The sheriff's deputies read Pryor's dissolution twice. One took the levy list for his return to court after Luz and two neighbors checked the final entries. Bell's remaining men withdrew without argument.

"What about him?" Rafe asked.

Paul looked to Ellie.

The question might once have belonged to rifles. Now it belonged to the paper in Paul's hand and the witnesses by the fence.

"Pryor named conspiracy," Ellie said. "Send him in alive."

Stowe smiled without lifting his head. "You think a county cell holds men like Bell?"

Luz stepped close enough that he had to look at her split lip. "No. But it holds men like you."

That was the nearest thing to satisfaction she allowed herself.

The lead deputy removed Stowe's Colt Army conversion and took custody of him under the eyes of every neighbor Paul had called to witness.

"I will deliver him to the town marshal," the deputy said.

"Alive," Paul answered.

"Alive."

Sukey came down from the porch at last. The house keys swung at her waist, bright with use. She stood in front of Ellie, took her face in both work-rough hands, and looked at her the way women looked at a child returned from fever.

"You ate today?" Sukey asked.

Ellie almost laughed. Instead her eyes filled so fast she had to blink hard.

"No."

"Then law can wait long enough for beans."

Paul folded Pryor's order carefully and put it inside his vest, over his heart as if it were a letter.

Neighbors drifted closer without cheering; the day had been too thin-edged for that. Mr. Sutter removed his hat as he passed Ellie. Lem stood near the barn, empty-handed and embarrassed by his relief. Paul's mouth moved, almost a smile.

The lower pasture still had gaps where cattle should have been. The fence would need mending before dark if they wanted the strays back by morning. The barn door hung crooked where one of Bell's men had shoved it. Nothing looked won in the way stories made winning sound.

That comforted Ellie more than a clean yard would have.

Clean would have felt false.

This was theirs: damaged, counted, threatened, witnessed, and still standing.

Rafe nodded toward the order inside Paul's vest. "Paper did what a rifle could not."

"No," Paul said, turning to Ellie. "She did."

Ellie shook her head. "Clara did. Cruz did, late. Minshew did, afraid. Deacon Vale did. You did, by not giving Bell the shot he wanted."

"That is a long way of saying you did."

She let herself smile then because the day had earned one small thing.

Luz stood apart again, as if the smile might not include her. Ellie saw it and made the choice before doubt could dress itself as caution.

"Sukey," Ellie said, "set another plate."

Sukey looked from Ellie to Luz and back. Then she nodded once. "Already was."

Luz looked away quickly.

No one named forgiveness. That would have been too easy, and not true enough. But when the hands moved toward the house and the smokehouse key turned in Sukey's hand, Luz followed at the edge of the group instead of the road.

Behind them, the ranch waited: barn doors scarred, cattle still scattered beyond the lower pasture, smokehouse locked, porch sagging in one corner, every part of it tired and stubborn and theirs.

Ellie rested her hand on the gate wood.

For days, paper had tried to steal the land by pretending to speak louder than blood, work, witness, and truth.

Now paper had spoken back.

Calder's Reach held.
