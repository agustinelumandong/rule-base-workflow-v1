The strongest lesson for your writing system
Your system should not only ask, “Is this sentence good?”

It should ask:

“Does this sentence give the reader enough evidence, pressure, and specificity to believe the moment?”

Practical revision rules to add to your system
Use these as system-level instructions for revising Western fiction:

No unsupported expertise.
When Branton tracks, predicts, or judges a man, show the evidence first. The reader must see why he knows.

No vague atmospheric stacking.
Weather, terrain, and light must be physically coherent. Avoid lines that sound pretty but make the reader ask, “What exactly is happening?”

Dialogue must be spoken, not literary.
Use contractions. Let characters interrupt, evade, accuse, soften, and circle the real issue. Avoid formal lines unless the character would truly speak that way.

Every “could be better” line needs pressure.
If a line is generic, rewrite it with one of these: sharper image, clearer conflict, more subtext, more personal history, or stronger rhythm.

Tracking must be cinematic, not summarized.
Instead of:
“Three horse tracks. One fresh. One rider.”
Use:
“Three sets crossed the mud. Two had dried at the edges. The third still held damp beneath the crust, and only one rider had made it.”

Procedural details need concrete stakes.
Bounties, warrants, posses, deputies, dead-or-alive rules, horse condition, ammunition, money, supplies — make them specific.

Avoid redundant dialogue.
If two lines say the same emotional thing, keep the sharper one or make the second line escalate.

Character conflict should evolve, not repeat.
Clara’s concern about the gun is strong. But each return to that issue must add a new angle: fear, anger, disappointment, love, resignation, or moral challenge.
