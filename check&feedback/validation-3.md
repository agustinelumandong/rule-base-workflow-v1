*1. Duplicated action — Ch. 3, dawn camp*
"He sat up to find Finn already half risen on one elbow" is immediately followed by "Finn pushed himself up on one elbow." Both sentences describe the same gesture. One needs to go.

*2. Duplicated action — Ch. 1, tack shed*
Milt checks and hands Quade the cinch strap in one sentence, then the very next paragraph opens with "Milt checked the cinch strap once more." The second check reads as an accidental repeated beat, not a deliberate second pass.

*3. Duplicated action — Ch. 2, Draven on the ridge*
"He pulled the brim lower and nudged the bay down off the height" is immediately followed by "He gave the bay his head and sent him down off the ridge at a ground-eating walk." These describe the same motion twice in consecutive sentences.

*4. Duplicated paragraph — Ch. 15, bluff ambush*
"Quade dragged the body where the road could not miss it and took one last look at the bluff shelf above..." appears verbatim as the closing lines of the chapter, but the identical content was already stated three paragraphs earlier as "He took the rifle and dragged the body down by the road where a wagon or rider could find it plain." Two different wordings of the same action, and then the second one is repeated word-for-word. The passage at line 2659 is a duplicate of itself.

*5. Chapter 8 opening repeats Chapter 7's closing*
Chapter 7 ends with Quade and Finn arriving at the roadhouse, Quade confirming the thief is inside, and riding toward the door. Chapter 8 then opens: "Quade found the track by a torn place in the frost where the thief's horse had skidded turning out of the wash." This rewinds the action by an hour or more — they've already found the roadhouse and confirmed the thief is there. Ch. 8 appears to be a discarded earlier draft of the scene that was accidentally left in.

*6. Missing hat — continuity break between Ch. 15 and Ch. 16*
Quade's hat is shot off on the bluff road in Ch. 15, and he explicitly rides back to town hatless ("His hat lay down on the road… He left it there" / "no hat"). Cole's scene confirms the missing hat. Quade then rides out again with no hat. In Ch. 16, Draven looks at "the torn brim of Quade's replacement hat" — but there is no scene where Quade acquires a replacement. Either the acquisition needs a line, or "replacement hat" needs to be cut.

*7. Dialogue punctuation error — Ch. 14*
"'You got a dead shooter at my ranch and a boy on your table because somebody meant to kill me in my own coat.' 'Reachable ain't high on my list.'" Two quoted sentences are written as separate dialogue tags with a period and closing quote, then an immediate opening quote, but no attribution or line break between them. The second sentence belongs to Quade and is part of the same speech — they should be one line, or the period needs to become a comma/dash with the second sentence run on.

Yes, there are more. Here’s what I found on the closer pass:

8. Finn’s wound entry direction — Ch. 13
The shooter is kneeling at the far fence, firing toward the porch. Finn is standing at the porch rail facing the yard. When Quade examines the wound, he finds “the ball had gone in low and back.” A back-entry wound can’t come from a shooter positioned in front of the victim. “Low and back” needs to be “low and forward” or equivalent, or the scene needs Finn turned away at the moment of the shot.

9. Card player behavior contradiction — Ch. 5
Two observations of the same card player conflict. First: “The card player by the stove lost twice running because he kept listening toward the back table.” Then, a paragraph later: “One of the card players near the stove coughed into his fist and never once looked up.” Looking toward the back table and never looking up are opposite behaviors. The second sentence also shifts from singular to plural (“one of the card players”), which creates a minor additional inconsistency.

10. Untagged dialogue obscured by interruption — Ch. 5
The barkeep catches Quade’s sleeve at the door, then the card player observation intrudes (error 9 above), and the barkeep’s warning dialogue begins with no tag: “If you’re riding to the Calders, don’t take the creek road south.” The “Quade studied him” / “The barkeep looked past him” lines recover it, but the card player paragraph sitting between the sleeve-catch and the dialogue creates a moment of unclear attribution.

11. “Drowned on his own blood” — Ch. 19 / Ch. 20 phrasing tension
At the end of Ch. 19, after Mireya’s shot, the text reads: “Quade stayed where he was a second longer… while Hargrave drowned on his own blood in the drift.” The phrasing reads as a completed action — he’s drowning right there, in the drift. Ch. 20 then opens with Hargrave having been moved under the porch awning and still able to speak lucidly enough to deliver several taunting lines. The phrasing in Ch. 19 sets a false expectation of nearimmediate death. A small adjustment to that line would remove the contradiction.

12. Duplicate body-dragging action — Ch. 15 (clarification of error 4)
To be precise about what’s happening: “He took the rifle and dragged the body down by the road where a wagon or rider could find it plain” is immediately followed two paragraphs later by “Quade dragged the body where the road could not miss it and took one last look at the bluff shelf above.” These aren’t two different wordings of the same action — the second is a verbatim-style repeat of the first, same subject, same object, same purpose. One of the two needs to be cut entirely.

*1. Duplicated sentence — Ch. 2*
"They had the look of supplies gathered for men who expected them to be taken. Supplies gathered for men who expected them to be taken or moved." The second sentence is a near-verbatim repeat of the first, extended by two words. One of the two needs to go.

*2. Ambiguous pronoun — Ch. 1*
"The covered palm, the bottom slide, and the bent card made the cheating evidence plain. He sat alert, still, and ready for the dealer's next move." The last noun before "He" is "The dealer's eyes lifted," so the pronoun reads as the dealer. The intended subject is Hunter. The paragraph needs to establish Hunter as the subject, or the pronoun needs to be replaced.

*3. Subjectless fragments — Ch. 1*
"Hands stayed away from the weapons. Voice stayed low." These fragments have no subject. The reader understands they belong to Hunter, but the text presents them as authorial observations rather than Hunter's deliberate actions. This may be an intentional stylistic choice, but it creates a floating quality that breaks the close-third-person POV maintained throughout.

*4. Duplicated beat — Ch. 4 closing*
"Three Iron Riders remained." appears twice within four paragraphs at the end of Chapter 4, with only a single paragraph between the two instances. The first occurrence is a clean count statement after Hunter's assessment of Len's movements. The second follows three sentences later as a structural echo. It reads as a copy-paste artifact rather than intentional repetition.

*5. Duplicated paragraph — Ch. 10*
The description of Lila appears twice in close succession. The first instance: "The scar of the past months did not show on her face, but she checked the road before she looked at the yard." The second instance, six paragraphs later: "The scar of the past months did not show on her face. It showed in the way she checked the road before she looked at the yard, in the way her hand rested near the porch post before she relaxed her fingers." The second is a revised and expanded version of the first — both are present when only the second (and better) one should remain.

*6. Double lever-work — Ch. 4*
Hunter fires once through the gap at the hearth stones, then "worked the lever while rolling back from the gap." One shot, one lever-work — correct. But the very next sentence has him drop behind the overturned table and "worked the lever" again without firing. A lever-action rifle only needs to be cycled after a shot. The second instance either ejects an unfired round or is a remnant of an earlier draft where a second shot was described. One of the two lever-work instances needs to go.

*7. Duplicated "bounty hunter shifted" — Ch. 8*
Three uses of "shifted" attached to the bounty hunter within twelve lines: "The bounty hunter shifted his rifle two inches and fired through the side gap." / "The bounty hunter shifted. 'Right man is close.'" / "The bounty hunter shifted along the side wall and answered the second-in-command." The first two consecutive paragraphs both open with "The bounty hunter shifted" — these appear to be one action and one line of dialogue that were accidentally split into two paragraphs. The third use compounds the repetition.

*8. Winchester used forcefully from the weakened arm — Ch. 5*
Hunter shifts the Winchester to his LEFT hand (the injured-shoulder side) to free his right for the Colt. Then "The Winchester barrel rose hard under the man's wrist" and "drove the rifle butt into the man's middle." Both strikes are described as forceful impacts — but Hunter's left hand is established throughout as weakened by the shoulder graze, explicitly called out as slow, sticky with blood, and degrading under pressure in later chapters. A strong butt-strike to the midsection using that arm is physically at odds with what the text tells us about the injury. Either the strike should be described as clumsy/costly, or the action should be assigned to the right hand with the Winchester repositioned first.

1. “Zeke’s marks” — Ch. 4
Branton thinks “The next sure water lay wherever Zeke’s marks said it lay, and charcoal on hide did not make water rise from the ground.” This happens in Ch. 4, well before Branton meets Rawlins — who introduces himself as “Zeke Rawlins” and gives Branton the hide map only after dying in Ch. 5. At this point in the story, Branton doesn’t know the trapper’s name and doesn’t have the map. The line names Zeke and references his map before either exists in the narrative.

2. “The dead man’s black wrist cloth” — Ch. 4
After shooting the young third man through the chest, Branton pulls “the dead man’s black wrist cloth free” to use as a decoy. Two paragraphs later the same man is described as “the wounded young man behind him was still breathing” and subsequently speaks at length before reaching for his boot knife. He is called a dead man before he is dead.

3. Willard returns injured — Ch. 12, no explanation
Willard was sent south to Tascosa at first light at the end of Ch. 2, with two working arms and just scared from the bodies. When Branton returns to Whitaker’s at the end of Ch. 12, “Willard stood near the porch, one arm in a sling, face bruised but alive.” Nothing in the intervening chapters explains how or when he was injured. He rode toward safety, not toward the fight.

4. Description mismatch — “charcoal on hide”
The Ch. 4 line uses “charcoal on hide” to describe the marks. When Branton actually finds and describes the hide map in Ch. 5, it is “a folded scrap of hide with old trap marks scratched into it” — scratched, not charcoal. Minor, but the medium of the marks doesn’t match across the two descriptions, and the Ch. 4 line shouldn’t exist at all given error 1.
