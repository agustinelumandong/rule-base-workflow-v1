Chapter 1: North from Ghostpine
The brace leather creaked when Jed pulled it tight.
He sat on the edge of the bench outside Elkheart's lodge and worked the buckle one hole farther than comfort wanted. The right leg had stiffened in the night. It always did after a day on uneven ground. He pressed the shin once through the leather and hide. The ache above the knee settled into its morning place. He stood, took one step, then another, and let the brace seat under him.
Ghostpine had already gone back to work. A woman carried two pails from the creek side. Somebody drove nails into a wagon board near the lower sheds. Smoke came up from three roofs. On the north side of the yard Ketch lay under new dirt and two plain stones. Elkheart had set the stones himself.
Jed rolled his blanket, tied his kit, checked the knife, and laid the little cloth of dried meat on top. Nothing else needed deciding. The bill of lading had gone into fire before dawn two days earlier. Stone room. East face. Second ridgeline past Frostfang. Four days north of Spur. The line stayed in his head and nowhere else.
Elkheart came across the yard with no hurry in him. He stopped by the horse and checked the cinch with two fingers.
"You tied it low."
"It will hold."
"Till the horse swells."
Jed bent, took the strap back up, and fixed it a little higher. Elkheart watched the work, not Jed.
"North road is not empty," the old man said.
Jed set the saddle blanket flat with the heel of his hand. "It never was."
"I do not mean trappers."
"I know what you mean."
Elkheart looked at the grave once, then back at the horse. "That is one man. Road may hold more."
Jed took the bridle down from the peg. "More men still shoot one at a time."
Elkheart gave him the look he saved for stubborn work animals and stubborn men. "You are leaving on bad ground with a bad leg and a secret another man already bled for. Say the thing plain at least."
Jed slipped the bit into the horse's mouth. "I am leaving because staying puts more eyes on the same road."
"Better." Elkheart rested one hand on the horse's neck. "Now hear mine. Fresh cuts above the first ridge cannot be trusted. If the road shows you an easier turn, weigh it twice."
Jed nodded once.
Elkheart went on. "And if the road shows you a boy named Nemi, do not refuse him because he is young. He knows one old crossing."
Jed looked over. "You sent him ahead."
"His aunt sent him to kin near Spur. I told him only this. If he sees a man with two fingers missing and a leg bound in hide, he should speak before the ground kills that man."
Jed snorted once through his nose. "Kind of you."
"Kindness had nothing to do with it."
That was true enough to leave alone.
Jed hung the canteen, tied the bedroll behind the cantle, and put one boot in the stirrup. Mounting still took work. He caught the horn, hauled himself up, and dragged the bad leg over after the rest of him. The horse shifted under the load and stood when he asked it to.
Elkheart stepped back.
"If Draven is on the road, he will read your limp before he reads your face."
"Then I will not give him a second look."
"You may not get that choice."
Jed settled the reins in his left hand. Three fingers and a thumb were enough if he kept them there. "Choice is what a man takes when the road brings him less than he asked for."
Elkheart let that pass. "Ghostpine will still be here if the north gives you nothing but snow and graves."
Jed looked at the yard, the stones above Ketch, the men on repair work, the creek smoke low in the cold air. "That is why I should leave now."
He touched heel to the horse and took the north track out of town.
The first climb ran through black timber and old snow with pine roots under it. The horse picked its way and Jed stayed light in the saddle where he could. He did not waste a look back until Ghostpine had already dropped behind one shoulder of land. When he finally turned, the roofs were half lost between trunks and smoke. The grave could not be seen at all.
He rode another mile before the first sign checked him.
A spruce branch had been cut clean at knee height beside the track. Too clean. Too fresh. The white of the cut had not darkened yet. Jed drew up, slid down from the saddle, and put two fingers to the wood. No snow packed on top of it. The cut had not seen a full night.
He looked uphill, then down across the track. No second mark near it. No camp ash. No boot cut he could trust through the crusted snow and pine fall.
Fresh cuts above the first ridge cannot be trusted.
Elkheart had done him the favor already. Jed took the horse off the track long enough to circle the place through the trees. Fifty yards above he found the real turn, old and half drifted, barely more than a game trace where wagons had once worked through on drier years. The clean-cut branch below had aimed any rider straight toward a shelf of rotten snow and broken stone.
He came back down, mounted again, and took the old trace instead.
By noon the country opened into harder ground where the timber thinned and the wind had room to move. Jed ate in the saddle, drank once, and kept the horse walking. The road ran north. Frostfang waited ahead. Somewhere beyond it lay the stone room and whatever had made Ketch die naming it.
Late in the day he crossed a bare rise and saw a rider far off on the south shoulder of another ridge.
One horse. One man. No hurry in the line. The distance was too long for a face, but not too long to say the rider used cover when the land gave it.
Jed rode down off the rise at once and put timber between himself and the open. He did not push the horse harder than the ground allowed. A lame horse and a lame rider only made better targets.
Near dark he found a mean shelter where two stone slabs leaned against each other above a wash. Enough room for him, the horse on a short line, and a fire no taller than his hand. He gathered dead wood, cut what he needed, and struck spark into dry grass under the rock lip.
The little fire caught and stayed low. Jed sat with his back to stone, cup warming in one hand, knife near the other, and listened to the dark beyond the horse's breathing.
The road north was not empty. Elkheart had been right about that much.

Chapter 2: South Trail Sign
The saddle leather popped when Clementine hauled the cinch tight.
She checked the gray mare's girth a second time, tied down her bedroll, and rode south out of Ghostpine before the yard fully woke. The snow had softened along the lower line where shade broke late. She kept the mare at a working pace and let the country sort itself by sign instead of speed.
Draven had vanished north at the end of one trail and south at the start of another. Men like him did not vanish for long. They bent out of sight and came back where the hurt was likely to pay.
By midmorning she came on a wood hauler working a mule team through a muddy cut below the ridge road. He had one chain loose in his hands and a tongue full of irritation for it. Clementine drew up ten feet off and waited until he looked over.
"You seen a dark horse this morning?"
The hauler spat to one side. "Seen three horses and one mule I would gladly trade away. Which dark horse."
"Rider alone. Keeps his seat still. Might have a second horse on a lead."
The man squinted at her, then south. "No lead horse. One rider before dawn. Came off the lower creek and turned north at the split."
"You sure about north."
"I still know where my own road runs."
Clementine took that. "He say anything."
"Not to me."
She nodded once. "How long."
"Three hours. Maybe four."
That put enough shape on the day. Clementine thanked him, touched the mare on, and took the lower creek road instead of the split. If the rider had come from a camp, the ground behind him might still speak plain.
The camp smell reached her before the fire ring did. Wet ash. Cold grease. One scorched strip of wool. She stopped under cottonwoods and listened. Creek water moved below the bank. A crow worked somewhere upstream. Nothing else claimed the place.
She dismounted and led the gray in.
The camp had broken wrong. One blanket scrap burned half through and trampled into mud. A coffee pot sat kicked on its side. Two tie marks cut bark on a willow. One thong had been sliced clean instead of untied.
She crouched by the ash. Cold. More than one night gone, less than three.
The blood lay behind the fire where a man would have sat with his back to driftwood and heat. Not enough for a body left there. Enough for a wounded rider or a dead one moved after. Clementine followed the drag mark to the creek lip and found it stopped at the water. No body below. They had either loaded him out or washed enough of him to hide the first read.
She climbed the bank on the far side and found the boot sign that mattered.
Square heel. Left edge worn down harder than the right.
Draven put his weight that way when he stood after a long ride. She had seen it in snow, in barn dirt, in town alleys, and once in blood. The heel mark turned north.
She looked over the rest of the ground before trusting the single print. Two other boot kinds crossed the camp. One city-made pair with a narrow toe. One heavier pair with more mud in the heel. Three horses had watered at the creek. One had been led lame or half spent. The drag on the right hind told her that much.
A stripped saddle lay under brush twenty yards off. The fleece was dark with old blood. One stirrup leather was gone. Not Draven's saddle. Not the kind he would keep under him for more than a week.
Clementine took the saddle skirt in one hand, checked the maker mark, and let it drop. Cheap work. Bought east or stolen cheap. Near the willow line she found the broken spur strap that went with it.
The camp had not been a rest stop. It had been a sorting place. A wounded man, two sound men, Draven among them, and then a turn back north.
She climbed the watch rise above the camp because any man waiting there would have wanted the south road in sight. The grass on top had been flattened by one blanket or one body laid out full length. A tobacco twist end sat under a stone where a heel had crushed it. From there the whole bend below lay open except the far north bank. Whoever watched that camp waited for trouble from behind, not ahead.
That fit too well.
Clementine came down and worked the north edge of the place one more time. A thorn branch beside the water had been cut and trimmed. Not enough to make a marker for strangers. Enough to leave a private nudge for the right eye. She looked at the branch, then at the creek line turning north under cottonwood shade.
"You came back."
She said it to the empty bend and got only water noise for answer.
The line out of camp told the rest. Three horses north. One rider breaking away from the others once, twice, then joining again. Draven had either used the camp to gather men or peeled them off later. It did not matter. The track still bent north, and north meant one thing now.
Jed.
Clementine mounted, rode the first half mile easy to keep the sign whole, and found the split where the wood hauler had told it. The north branch showed one rider from early morning and older wagon scuffs beneath that. She took the north road without another look south.
At the turn she reined in once and checked the saddle strings on the gray mare. The rifle sat where it should. The trail kit rode close. Her coat still held Ghostpine smoke from the last fire she and Jed had shared inside walls.
Draven had circled. He had not run.
That changed the road for both of them.

Chapter 3: Marrow''s Wagon
The river rope snapped against the post when the current caught it.
Jed heard the slap before he saw the ferry. He came down through cottonwood shade to a landing chewed up by mud and spring water and found the flatboat lodged crooked against the far bank. One guide rope still held on his side. The other had gone. The river worked the loose end slow and mean through drift and brown water.
The wagon sat above the landing with both rear wheels sunk half to the spokes.
A broad old driver stood by the lead pair with a wrench in one hand and a look that said the day had already made itself unwelcome. Two passengers watched from the rear opening under the wagon cloth. Others sat farther in where the dark hid them.
Jed drew up ten yards off.
The old driver looked over. "If you brought a bridge, I will hear your price."
"You don't have enough for a bridge."
"That is what worries me."
Jed swung down and walked to the bank. The river ran high, too high to risk alone with a loaded horse and no second hand on the far rope. He studied the flatboat, the snag line under it, and the break in the guide rope.
"When did it part."
"An hour past first light." The driver came up beside him. "Boat hit a log. Rope went. River took the joke and kept telling it."
Jed looked across. "Post still holds."
"So far."
"You got spare rope."
"Some. Not enough to waste."
Jed nodded toward the stranded ferry. "You pull from the bank, not the deck. Need a line across first. Then cut the drift from the stern side before you haul."
The old man watched him from the corner of his eye. "You talk like a man who means to leave before the work starts."
"I talk like a man who knows what the work is."
That brought a short grunt. "Name's Gideon Marrow."
Jed gave him none back.
A clean-coated passenger stepped down from the wagon and picked his way through the mud as if it had done him a private wrong.
"That can be charming in town." The clean-coated man looked at Jed. "On the road it mostly wastes time."
Jed glanced over.
The man gave a small smile. "Martin Vale. Since names are being spent like rope."
"Then save yours."
Vale's smile thinned instead of growing. Good.
From the wagon opening a woman climbed down with a small iron-bound box tucked in both arms. She carried it close and did not hand it off even to steady herself. A blanket-wrapped young man sat just behind her, pale under his hat brim. Near the front bench a lean man in a preacher's collar rested one hand on a long canvas case. At the back, half behind a grain sack, sat a dark-haired boy watching all of them with no wasted motion at all.
Ruth set the box against the wagon side and looked at the river. "Can it be cleared today?"
Jed answered her, not Marrow. "If the post holds and if one man gets across with the far line."
The blanket-wrapped young man spoke from the wagon bed. "You volunteering."
"You able."
The young man gave a dry sound that was not quite a laugh. "Not in this shape."
Marrow jerked his chin toward him. "That one is Caleb Pike. He came to me half-shot and full of bad timing."
Caleb held the blanket tighter over his middle and did not argue it.
The lean man by the canvas case climbed down next. "I can pull line if it comes to that."
Marrow looked at him. "You can pull what I point at, preacher."
The man took the insult easy. "That too."
Jed looked at the case, then back at the man's hands. Not soft hands. Not clerk hands. Marrow noticed where he looked.
"Ansel Wren," the old driver said. "Carries the Book and something longer."
Wren said nothing to improve that.
Ruth glanced from Jed to Marrow. "If he crosses the line, what does he want for it?"
Jed looked at the wagon, the river, the trapped flatboat, the road north blocked until one of those changed. "Passage to the Spur road."
Vale gave him another of those little town smiles. "Cheap, if true."
"You sell dearer lies?"
Vale let that one go.
The boy at the rear had climbed down without anyone marking when. He came up carrying a coil of smaller rope over one shoulder. He stopped beside Marrow and looked at Jed once, then the ferry, then the far bank.
Marrow said, "That is Nemi. He speaks when it matters and keeps the rest."
Nemi shifted the coil from one shoulder to the other. "Current pulls harder below the stump line."
Jed looked at him.
The boy nodded toward the lower half of the river. "Cross above it or lose the rope."
Marrow huffed once. "See. Speaks when it matters."
Jed took off his gloves and checked the guide rope at the post. The fibers had torn under strain, not rot. Good. That made the river the problem and not the whole setup.
"You got an axe."
Marrow lifted the wrench. "You think I shape ferries with this."
"A real axe."
Ruth pointed with her chin toward the tool box under the seat. "Left side."
Jed pulled it free and checked the edge. Honest enough.
Marrow said, "You still have not given a name."
Jed set the axe down by the bank. "You still need a line across more than you need a name."
That held for a breath. Then Caleb looked up sharper than before.
"You hear that."
Everyone stopped.
At first there was only river. Then, from far down the opposite bank, a horse knocked stone loose on the slope and sent it ticking into the water.
Ruth's hands went to the iron box at once. Wren's eyes moved to the long case. Vale took one careful step back from the open bank and called that discretion. Nemi turned his head south and stayed still.
Marrow swore under his breath. "Not today."
Jed looked across the river and saw two mounted shapes come out between scrub and rock on the far rise. Too far yet for faces. Close enough to say they were not driftwood and not luck.
Ruth said, "They know me."
Nobody answered that because the sentence had already done its work.
Marrow looked at Jed, then the river, then the riders across it. "You wanted passage. Earn it. Ride guard till Spur road and I will count you one of us till then."
Jed kept his eyes on the far bank.
The river was too high. The wagon could not move back fast. The riders had found the landing at the same hour he had. He could still ride away alone and leave them to their own trouble. He could. That was true.
Then one of the riders across the water put his hand to his mouth and called something Ruth heard before the rest did. She went still from the shoulders down.
Jed picked up the axe.
"Get your rope."

Chapter 4: The Locked Box
The ferry rope sang when the current caught it.
Jed stripped off his coat, looped the dry line over one shoulder, and tested the bank mud with one boot. The water below ran brown and hard around the stump line Nemi had pointed out.
Across the river the two riders came farther down the slope and reined in where stone gave them a clear look at the landing.
One of them cupped his hands to his mouth.
"Ruth Bell." His voice came thin over water but plain enough. "Tell your dead husband we came to settle short weight."
Ruth stood by the wagon wheel with the iron box against her skirts. She did not answer.
The rider called again. "You hear me, widow? That box does not belong to you."
Caleb shifted under his blanket and winced. Wren set one hand on the long canvas case but did not open it. Vale looked from Ruth to the far bank with the interest of a man who had paid for theater and found better than expected.
Marrow spat into the mud. "You know those men."
Ruth kept her eyes on the far bank. "I know the kind."
"That was your name they used."
"It is still my name."
The rider on the far bank laughed once. "Maybe for another hour. Hand over the box and we will let the old driver keep his team."
Marrow turned his head enough to show Jed the side of his face. "If they start shooting while we're on that boat, I will take it personal."
Jed looked at Marrow. "Then keep moving."
He handed his knife belt and hat to Nemi. The boy took both without a word. Jed fed the line around his chest, stepped into the water above the stump line, and felt the cold go at him like teeth.
The first stride hit only to the knee. The next dropped him to the thigh. He angled upstream and kept the rope high while the current shoved at his bad leg. On the third step the river tried to turn him. He planted hard, took two breaths through his nose, and went on.
Behind him Marrow called, "Keep your feet."
"Keep your feet."
Halfway over the bottom cut out. Jed lunged, found stone with one boot, then nothing. The river took him to the chest and dragged him three body lengths before he struck the far gravel with both knees. He stayed down only long enough to get breath back under his ribs.
The riders had shifted uphill to keep clear sight of him. One started down the bank, then stopped when Jed pulled the knife from his boot and stood with the river running off him.
"You are wasting time," that rider said.
Jed looked once at the pistol on the man's thigh and once at the horse's lathered chest. "You are farther from the box than you were a minute ago."
That seemed to trouble him more than the knife did.
Jed climbed to the ferry post, hauled the line around it twice, and tied off high where the wet wood still held. The flatboat banged once against the bank below him. He dropped to it, crawled along the stern edge, and chopped drift free where it had jammed under the corner.
The boat swung broadside, then answered the line.
On the near bank Marrow was already driving the team down. Nemi ran beside the lead horses with one hand at the bridle cheek. Wren had the wounded Caleb braced with an arm under his shoulders. Vale and Ruth came last. Ruth still carried the box herself.
One of the far-bank riders shouted, "Shoot the horses."
The other did not fire. Too long a chance. Too much river between. He tried to edge down the bank instead.
Jed slashed the ferry clear of one more branch, grabbed the hauling line, and bellowed, "Now."
The flatboat nosed out. Marrow brought the team aboard with the care of a man walking load powder through a fire. The wagon rolled after them, axle ends groaning. Wren shoved from the stern with a pole. Vale nearly lost his footing and caught the wagon cloth with both hands. Ruth never looked at him. She kept one boot braced against the ferry rail and held the box to her middle as if it were a child too old to carry and too dear to drop.
The far-bank riders came down to the edge at last.
"Ruth," the first one called. "Hobb Lask says the coin was his before it was your husband's. He says the papers are his too."
That made her face change, not much, but enough.
Jed saw it and so did everyone else.
The rider smiled when he saw he had struck true. Ruth lifted her chin toward the far bank. "Tell him he can have his answer in Spur if he has the stomach to come there."
Marrow glanced at her. "You planning to tell the rest of us what that means?"
"Not while the boat is moving."
"Convenient."
The ferry hit the near bank hard enough to jar Caleb into a low curse. Jed jumped ashore first, caught the line, and held while Marrow drove the team up the muddy pull. The right rear wheel spun once to the hub. Nemi shoved his shoulder against the wagon bed. Wren joined him. Jed put both hands to the spoke and heaved until the wheel climbed clear.
The wagon took the bank by inches. Then it was up and on the road.
No one spoke until the ferry landing dropped behind a stand of cottonwoods and the river noise dulled.
They stopped only long enough for Jed to pull his coat back on and retie the knife at his belt. Water steamed off him in the thin sun. His leg brace felt like iron packed with snow.
Marrow pointed with the wrench toward Ruth's box. "Start there."
Ruth did not sit. She stood beside the wagon tongue, wet hem dark to the knee, hands still around the iron corners. "My husband traded with bad men. When he died, they decided some of what he held ought to pass to them."
Vale watched Ruth over clasped hands. "Coin. And papers, if the river gentleman was to be believed."
Ruth shifted the box higher against her side. "Men who want things usually say so."
Marrow gave a dry grunt. "Did your husband steal from this Hobb Lask?"
She took a breath before answering. "He stole with him. Then kept more than his share."
Caleb shifted against the wagon side. "That is worse."
Ruth met his eyes without blinking. "I know what it is."
Wren watched her for a moment. "Why keep the box if it buys this much trouble?"
Her grip tightened. "Because what is inside it does not belong to Hobb Lask either."
Vale tipped his head. "Who does it belong to?"
Ruth looked at him the way a cook looks at a fly near fresh bread. "Not you."
Marrow almost smiled at that, but it did not stay. "Are they alone?"
Ruth looked back down the road once. "They were not when I left."
Jed wrung river water from one glove and looked north. "Then we are done talking at the roadside. If Lask wants the box, he will not wait for warm weather."
Marrow nodded. "That much I like hearing from you. Move up."
They got the wagon rolling again. The road climbed out of the river bottom through brush and broken stone. Jed walked beside the lead team for the first half mile because sitting with wet clothes on the brace would lock his leg to wood. Nemi came beside him with the hat he had carried dry under his shirt.
Jed took it. "You kept it out of the water."
Nemi shrugged once.
Behind them Vale said, "If we are collecting dangers, perhaps our new guard should state his own."
Marrow answered before Jed could. "When I want card table talk, I will stop the wagon and fetch a deck."
That shut Vale for a time.
Ruth climbed in beside Caleb and kept the box against her side. Wren watched the back trail from under his hat brim. The river was gone from sight, but not from the day. It stayed in the wet of Jed's clothes and the ache in his leg. So did the voice from the far bank.
Hobb Lask now had a name, and the wagon had one more reason to keep moving north.

Chapter 5: Caleb's Wound
The wagon wheels grated against stone all morning.
The road north of the ferry left the river trees behind and climbed through broken shelves where old frost had split the rock to plates. Marrow drove standing up when the grade steepened. Jed walked the outside of the team on the worst turns and put a shoulder to the wheel when the load leaned too far toward the drop.
Caleb held himself quiet for the first mile. On the second, the blanket around his middle darkened through.
Jed saw it before the others did. He reached for the brake lever just as Caleb bent forward and caught both hands against the wagon side.
Jed caught the brake lever. "Hold."
Marrow hauled the team down. "What now."
Caleb answered by sliding off the seat and going to one knee in the road. The blanket fell open. Blood had soaked through shirt and bandage low on the right side.
Ruth stepped back at once to keep clear of him. Wren caught Caleb under the arm before he pitched face-first onto the rock.
Jed pointed toward the pine. "Get him off the grade."
They dragged him under a crooked pine where the ground leveled enough to work. Caleb breathed in short little pulls he tried to hide and could not.
Caleb pressed a hand to the bandage. "It was closed. I did not tear it."
Marrow looked down at the red cloth. "Blood does not ask permission."
Jed set down his bedroll, opened the trail kit, and looked at Wren. "Boil water."
Wren moved at once. Ruth gave up part of a clean underskirt strip without complaint. Vale stood three paces off with one hand over his mouth, more from distaste than concern.
Jed cut the shirt away from the wound with the tip of his knife. Caleb sucked air through his teeth and nearly bucked clear of Wren's grip.
"Stay down."
"You stay down."
Jed ignored that. The wound sat low and angry above the hip. The entry had crusted black at the edge from old blood and dirt. Around it, under the fresh tear, was a faint gray-black burn that had not washed clean.
Powder burn.
Jed looked at it a second longer than he meant to.
Caleb saw his face. "What."
"Shot was close." Jed dipped cloth in the hot water Wren brought over and cleaned the wound as gentle as the road allowed. "Close enough to blacken skin."
Caleb shut his eyes. "I know how near he was."
Marrow folded his arms. "He?"
"Not your business."
"You are bleeding in my road. That makes it my business."
Jed packed the wound with clean cloth, then pressed hard until Caleb hissed and tried to twist away. Wren held him firm.
Wren kept both arms around Caleb. "You want him walking again or arguing?"
Marrow checked the uphill road while he answered. "Both if I can have it."
Jed tied the fresh bandage. Caleb's face had gone the color of dirty tallow. Sweat stood on his lip though the air stayed cold.
Jed knotted the bandage tight. "No riding the rear board loose. You sit braced where the wagon throws less."
"That your doctor voice?"
"That is the only voice keeping you out of the ground today."
Caleb gave him a long look, then looked away first.
The others drifted back toward their work. Marrow checked the harness. Ruth got the coffee pot from the wagon and pretended not to listen. Vale did not pretend. He listened with his whole face.
Jed washed his hands in the little pan Wren held out and stayed crouched by Caleb.
"Who shot you?"
Caleb breathed once before answering. "A man I quit."
"Name him."
Caleb tried a smile that died before it reached full shape. "You know his name better than I do, Threefingers."
Jed's head came up.
"Who told you that name?"
"A cook did. Before Draven shot him in the throat for burning beans and talking too free." Caleb swallowed. "Same night I decided I had seen enough of that camp."
The pine above them creaked in the wind. For a moment Jed heard only that and Caleb's ragged breathing.
"You rode with Draven."
"Three weeks. Maybe less. Long enough to know he does not raise his voice when he means to kill a man. Long enough to know he likes the kind of work where the dead have no family near enough to ask after them."
Jed sat back on his heels. "And he shot you for leaving."
Caleb shook his head once. "He shot me for leaving after I heard him ask about you."
That narrowed the air around them.
"What did he ask?"
"Whether any trader, rider, or fool had passed with two fingers gone and a bad right leg." Caleb looked up at him. "I said no. He knew I lied before the word finished."
Jed glanced toward the wagon. Vale had turned away, but not so far he could not hear if the talk rose. Ruth sat still with the pot in her lap. Nemi had disappeared into the trees uphill, likely to look at trail or sky or both.
"Why tell me now?"
"Because I am tired of waking up afraid some man on the road knows his horse better than I know my own name." Caleb pulled a breath that shivered at the end. "And because if Draven is behind us, this bandage will not matter much."
Jed did not answer at once. He was looking at the burn ring again in his mind. Close shot. Not warning distance. Draven had fired that one to keep a man from getting free.
"Did he come after you alone?"
Caleb let his head rest against the pine. "Mostly alone." "Men drift around him when they think there is money ahead. Then they leave after they see enough. Or they do not leave at all."
Marrow called from the wagon, "If the patient plans to live, I would like to keep heading north before dark."
Jed stood.
Caleb caught the edge of his coat. "Do not tell them all of it."
"Why."
"Because men hear Draven's name and start measuring who they can afford to lose." His hand fell away. "I would rather not learn it is me."
Jed looked at him for a long breath. Then he picked up the bloodied cloths and tossed them into the little fire Wren had built for the water.
"Can you sit a wagon seat without opening back up?"

"Road never did. Try anyway."
Wren and Jed got him up between them. Caleb nearly folded once, then found his feet. Ruth moved aside and made room on the front board where the wagon pitched less hard. Marrow did not smile at the new arrangement, but he did not argue it either.

Jed took his place beside the wheel. "For a man who claims no trade, you ask too much."
Vale gave up a small shrug and let the road answer for him.
They climbed another hour before the light started to go flat. Caleb stayed upright, barely. Once he caught Jed looking back and said, low enough for only him to hear, "If he leaves you a sign, do not pretend it could be another man."
Jed kept walking.
That warning sat on the road with him the rest of the day.

Chapter 6: Draven's First Mark
Mud held the whole crossing plain as print.
Draven came down to the ferry near noon and reined in where the road widened above the bank. He sat a moment without moving, letting the horse cool while he looked at the landing below. The flatboat still knocked slow against the near rope. One line had been replaced. Fresh work. Good work, done in weather and haste.
He slid from the saddle and took the bank on foot.
Wheel ruts had chewed the mud deep where the wagon came aboard. Six people at least, likely more if one had stayed mounted. Team pair broad in the shoulder. Driver favoring the left foot. One lame man who favored the right leg and still knew how to keep his balance when the ground turned bad.
Draven crouched and touched the print where the heel had dragged half an inch before setting firm.
Jed.
He smiled without showing teeth.
The crossing itself told the rest. Someone had gone through the water above the stump line with a rope on his body, tied off on the far post, cut the stern snag, then hauled the ferry back into line. Not town men. Not chance work. Jed had met people with a wagon and chosen to stay among them.
That mattered.
A lone rider could vanish between ridges. A wagon could only go where wheels and horses allowed.
Draven stood and walked the far edge of the landing. There were lighter boot marks there, narrow heel, careful step. Another set broad and old, likely the driver. One small print that dug deep at the toe where a boy had shoved weight into the wagon climb. The party had more hands than Draven expected.
The trampled ground led him to a strip of brush below the bank. Blood marked one snapped willow twig. Not much. Old enough to brown.
Across the river, two horse tracks cut in and out among stone on the far slope where riders had waited and failed to get what they wanted.
Upstream, the stones stood close enough under the water for a crossing. Draven led his horse through the shallows and climbed out on the far side without breaking stride. The waiting place still showed above the bank, and the remains of a small fire had been trampled into mud.
Below the cutbank a man sat with his back to stone and one hand clamped over his side. He had not heard the approach until Draven was nearly on him.
The man reached for a pistol, then stopped when he saw who had come instead.
The man stared up at him. "You are not ours."
"No."
Draven rested one hand on his own gun and looked at the blood under the man's fingers. "Where are the others?"
The man wet his lips. "Gone north after a wagon."
"Whose wagon."
The man hesitated. Draven stepped closer. That was enough.
The wounded man pressed harder at his side. "Old driver named Marrow. Widow with a box. Boy. Preacher. One wounded rat. One lame hardcase crossed the line for them."
Draven let the pieces settle into place.
"The widow's name."
"Ruth Bell."
"And the lame hardcase."
"Never gave it."
"He did not need to." Draven glanced at the man's pistol, still half clear in the holster. "Who sent you?"
The man swallowed. "Hobb Lask."
Draven knew the name. Road scavenger. Mean enough to frighten peddlers and steal from men already down. Not patient enough to build anything worth envying.
"What is in the box?"
"Coin. Maybe papers. Lask says both."
"Does Lask work with me?"
The man blinked. "I do not know who you are."
"That answers it."
Draven drew, pressed the muzzle under the man's jaw, and asked one more question. "How long ahead are they?"
"Half a day. Maybe less if the road turns soft."
Draven nodded once and struck him across the temple with the gun barrel instead of firing. The man dropped sideways into the dirt and stayed there breathing. Killing him would have made no better road.
Draven went back to his horse and stood a while with the reins loose in one hand.
Ruth Bell. Hobb Lask. A box full of trouble. None of that was his work, but all of it pressed the same wagon Jed had chosen over solitude. That meant more mouths, more fear, more chances for a weak bargain once the road narrowed.
He liked that.
From his saddle roll he took a strip of sound pine he had cut three days earlier for kindling and never burned. He sat on the ferry post and shaved it down with his knife until it lay slim and flat in his palm. Then he cut the old mark into it. Not a letter. Not a symbol any court clerk would know. Only a narrow notch and a crossing score, the same little sign he had once left on a dead mule skin after Jed cost him two nights in the Bitter Wash.
Jed would know it.
That was enough.
Draven crossed back to the north side, rode up from the landing, and found the place where the road pinched between two split stones before climbing into timber. A lame rider who read ground instead of sky would look down there. Jed always had.
Draven set the carved sliver upright in a seam of frozen mud where the notch caught what little light the day still gave. Then he rubbed out every sign of his own stop except the horse tracks he meant the wagon to miss and Jed alone to read.
He mounted and turned north.
He did not need to rush. The wagon was already caught in its own shape.
All he had to do was keep closing until one fear inside it opened the door for him.

Chapter 8: Hobb Lask Comes In
Horse snort broke the dark before first light.
Jed opened his eyes into black, knew at once it was not a dream sound, and rolled off the blanket with the knife already in hand. The team had shifted against the line, not in sleep but in alarm. One animal blew hard through its nose. Another struck a hoof against stone.
At the mouth of the cut brush rasped.
Jed crossed the ground in three strides and caught Marrow by the shoulder. "Up."
Marrow came awake ugly and fast. He reached inside his coat, then stopped when he saw Jed already looking out. "Front?"
"And maybe the shelf."
Wren was moving at the north pinch with the canvas case in his hands. Caleb pushed himself up against the wheel and nearly fell back down. Ruth had the box in her lap before any second order was given. Vale looked human only in the shape of a man rising from blankets; the rest of him belonged to panic for half a breath.
Harness ring snapped against wood when the team jerked.
A shot came from the brush at the mouth and took bark off the left wall shoulder high. No warning in it. No waste either. The man outside knew where the fire had been and where tired bodies would rise.
Marrow shoved past Jed, got both hands on the lead pair, and hauled them back from the line. "Hold, you fools. Hold."
Another rider came in from the shelf trail Nemi had shown. The horse slid on loose stone. That gave Jed the line. He climbed three steps, caught the rider low by the boot, and dragged him sideways from the saddle. Man and horse came apart with one hard sound. Jed hit the rider once in the mouth with the hilt and once in the throat with his forearm. The pistol went off into dirt between them.
Below, Wren opened the canvas case and brought the rifle out in one smooth pull.
"North side." Marrow had no room to shout and no need. He shoved the team behind the wagon tongue and used their mass as cover.
Wren knelt by the wheel, rested the rifle barrel through a spoke gap, and fired once.
The shot filled the cut and flattened everything after it. A horse screamed at the mouth. Someone outside cursed with real pain in the words. Jed had the rider from the shelf half up by then and drove his face into stone until the fight left him. He stripped the pistol, pitched it downslope, and looked up in time to see a second shape turn off the trail line instead of charging in.
Lask had sent men to press both openings, not enough men to die in both.
That was road sense, not courage.
Jed slid back into the cut. Vale crouched by Ruth and the box with both hands empty and his eyes too wide. Caleb had a revolver out from somewhere under the blankets, but the muzzle wandered and the hand behind it shook. Nemi lay flat halfway up the right wall with a fist-sized stone ready over one shoulder.
Jed pointed at the dirt by the wagon wheel. "Stay down."
Vale dropped lower at once. "Gladly."
A third shot came from brush at the mouth. This one hit what mattered. The right trace horse lunged, slammed sideways into the line, and went down hard enough to shake the wagon. Blood pumped dark from the base of its neck.
Marrow made a sound that had no words in it and went straight to the harness. He cut leather with the knife he kept in the back of his belt, freed the lead pair from the dying animal, and took a kick on the thigh for his trouble.
Jed dropped beside him. "You keep the team. I take the mouth."
"Take it faster."
He did. Brush opened once more under a rider trying to see whether the dead horse had broken the camp. Jed fired the captured pistol from the shelf man and saw bark jump behind the rider's shoulder. Wren's second shot followed so close on it they sounded born together. The rider lurched in the saddle, turned off, and nearly spilled over his horse's neck before catching himself.

"Ruth Bell."

Jed waited. So did Wren. No one rushed after a retreat they could not see.

The road had lost one horse and gained a name that would ride with them now.
Hobb Lask was no rumor any longer. He was a wounded man somewhere ahead or behind, angry enough to keep closing.

Chapter 9: The Preacher's Case
Knife edge sawed through the last trace strap.

Chapter 10: Clementine at the Ferry
Ferry rope knocked the post when Clementine drew rein.
The gray mare came down the last slope breathing hard through her nose and stopped where the landing opened under cottonwoods. Clementine sat a moment, let the mare settle, and looked at the crossing without moving any farther in than she had to.
The flatboat still hung on the near rope. Fresh fibers showed pale where one line had been replaced. Mud at the bank was torn to soup by wagon wheels, men on foot, and a team worked past calm.
She slid down and took the landing a yard at a time.
Jed's limp was there in the ground if a person knew how to read a man through his bad leg. Not the shape of the boot. The pause in the right step when he chose where to set weight. The drag no farther than half an inch before the heel held firm. He had worked the line here. Crossed water here too. Rope scrape and bank gouge said as much.
The wagon had gone north loaded and short a clean approach. The team had balked once at the near bank and once again climbing out. One of the passengers had carried a box small enough to leave narrow square prints when the mud softened under both heels. Another had favored one side as if holding hurt under cloth.
She worked the landing a second pass before trusting the first one. Rope drag marked where somebody had hauled hard from the bank instead of the deck. One boot heel had cut deeper than the rest in the climb out, driver weight or wounded man helping wheel. The dead horse sign came later on the upper road, but the ferry already hinted at trouble bought with load, not weather. Even the flatboat planks held the scrape where a crate corner or iron box had hit wood in haste.
More than Jed now.
She crossed at the shallows upstream where the stones sat close. Cold got through the boots and bit the bones of her feet, but she had taken worse crossings in darker country than this. On the far side she found the waiting ground above the cutbank where two riders had spent time and patience to no profit. One fire mashed into mud. One willow broken low by a horse swung too tight in one place. One blood drop dried on bark where a hurt man had leaned.
Lask's kind. Not Draven's.
Clementine knew the difference. Road scavengers milled, cursed, stamped sign over sign, and left half their thinking in the dirt. Draven used only what he had to and left the rest for the next man to regret.
That sign was still uphill.
Boot heel ground to a stop between split stones.
A pale thing stood upright in the frozen seam where road narrowed before the timber climb. A sliver of pine. Carved. Small enough another rider might miss it, mean enough not to be lost by chance.
She crouched and took it between thumb and finger.
The notch and crossing score sat plain in the wood.
Draven.
Not a message for her. A message for Jed. Not where to go. Not even a threat in words. Only proof that the hunt had come near enough to touch the same road and then step away again on purpose.
She closed her fist around the sliver and looked north through the timber line. Draven liked a man less when he killed him too soon. He liked him better when he could strip sleep first.
That meant Jed had miles left before the trap shut. It also meant the trap had already started.
She went back down to the mare, checked girth and canteen, and studied the landing one last time before riding out. No reason to stay now. The road had given what it held.
At the edge of the bank she found one more piece worth taking. A dead horse had been cut loose from the traces in a hurry. Leather end, blood smear, and drag line told that story plain. So the wagon had already paid once for whatever crowd rode in it.
Good.
Paid men learned faster than lucky ones.
Stirrup leather groaned when she mounted again.
North was the only line left. She set the mare into a ground-eating trot until the trees closed and the sign thinned enough to need slower eyes. From then on she worked by read and pace together. Wagon ruts when the ground held them. Broken brush where the banked road squeezed too close. One place where a driver had cut weight by throwing cloth into a ditch. Another where a wounded rider had stepped down and leaned hard before climbing back up.
By noon she had the whole shape of it.
Jed with a wagon. Draven behind the wagon. Another set of trouble laid over both.
She opened her fist and looked once more at the pine sliver before tucking it into the pocket inside her coat.
Her voice stayed low over the saddle horn. "Not this time."
The mare flicked one ear back and kept moving.
Clementine let her.

Chapter 11: The Old Crossing
Front axle groaned when the wagon nosed at the wash.
The road ahead had been there once. That was the best that could be said for it now. Runoff had eaten the outer edge down to a soft lip over a black drop full of stone and root. Marrow stood in the road with the reins looped over one arm and kicked at the edge until half a boot of dirt slid away into nothing worth trusting.
Marrow ground his boot heel into the washed edge. "No wagon takes that and keeps its wheels."
Vale looked down the cut and then away again. "Can we unload and carry through."
Marrow turned his head slow enough to make Vale wait on the answer. "Can you carry a team through."
That settled him.
Ruth held the box under one arm and looked back the way they had come. Caleb sat on the front board with both hands between his knees and no color in the mouth. Wren walked the wash edge once, then came back with the rifle broken over one elbow.
"If we lose half a day here, we buy Draven the same." Wren looked down into the wash.
"If we lose the wagon, we buy him more than half a day." Marrow did not look away from the edge.
Jed looked at the road cut, then above it. Fresh branches had been trimmed away on the open wagon line where a person who wanted strangers moving wrong might clean the ugly off bad ground and make it read easy from a saddle. Elkheart's warning lived in that sight as plain as if the old man stood there with his hand on the horse's neck again.
Branch tip clicked against Nemi's stick.
They all looked because the boy almost never chose sound when a touch would do. He stood uphill from the wagon on a line none of them had bothered with because it was not a road if a road meant wheel width and hope.
Nemi pointed with the stick again. "Wrong."
Marrow squinted at him. "What is wrong."
Nemi pointed with the stick. Not at the wash. Above it. Into black timber where the trunks stood closer and older.
"Road."
Vale gave a little breath through his nose. "There is no road there."
Nemi looked at him once. "For you."
Jed hid the near smile that tried to come and failed. He took two steps uphill and stopped beside the boy. Between trunks an old trace worked across the slope in broken pieces. Not wide enough for comfort. Wide enough for a wagon if the driver knew his hands and the lead man knew where dead ground began.
"Old crossing." Jed stepped higher on the slope and looked between the trunks.
Nemi nodded. "Burial timber. Fresh cuts lie there." He touched the washed road with the stick. "This lies too."
That brought Marrow off the wash edge.
"You sure."
Nemi gave no speech. He climbed three yards into the timber, shoved brush aside with the stick, and showed the line again. Root-polished. Stone-packed in two old wheel ruts under the needles. Used years ago and not kindly since.
Wren looked at Jed. "Elkheart's boy."
"Aunt's boy." Jed kept his eyes on the old trace. "Elkheart only pointed me his way."
Marrow spat once and looped the reins shorter. "If the marked road is a liar, I would rather take my orders from the child than from the liar. Move."
They did it by inches. Jed took the lead horse on foot. Nemi stayed ten paces ahead and called only when ground made talk worth spending.
"Left wheel high."
"Rock under brush."
"Tree root. Cut right."
Marrow drove with both hands locked and no room left in him for temper. The wagon eased into the timber and found its weight there. Vale and Ruth walked the outside with shoulders ready to catch what no shoulders could ever truly hold. Caleb limped behind Wren with one hand against his side and the other on the wagon board whenever the grade turned mean.
Halfway through, the false line showed its teeth.
Harness leather strained while the team waited on the incline.
A cleaner trace bent off to the right through younger timber. Branches there had been clipped at shoulder height and the needles underfoot looked combed by drag brush. Easy enough to tempt a wagon. Too easy.
Jed stopped the lead pair. Nemi came back, touched one pale cut end with his fingers, and held the sap to Jed.
Fresh.
"Not this way." Jed held one palm up to stop the team.
Marrow had already started to angle the team toward it for the better turn. He checked them hard. "What do you see."
Jed climbed three steps down the false line and put one boot sole to the ground. It gave under him in a way packed wagon earth never should. He scraped the top with his heel and sent a skin of dirt sliding away from flat rock pitched over open space.
Dead drop.
Not a cliff. Worse. A shelf break hidden under enough soft earth to carry a rider and horse until the wagon tongue and front axle pushed too far in. Then everything would fold together at once.
Vale stared at it and swallowed. "How far."
Jed tossed a fist-sized stone out onto the false line and watched it punch through on the second bounce. The sound came back later than any of them liked.
Marrow blew air from his chest and looked at Nemi, not at Jed. "All right. We do it your way."
Nemi only turned and walked the real line again.
That changed something in the wagon more than a full confession might have. Marrow no longer spoke over the boy's head. Vale kept his remarks to himself for the next hour. Ruth walked closer to the wagon and farther from the open edges. Caleb put his whole bad side into obeying when Nemi lifted the stick left or right.
By the time they came out of the black timber the day had burned low and every back in the party ached from bracing against disaster not yet spent.
Marrow climbed down once the wagon stood on level ground and put his hand on the near wheel as if thanking it for not breaking. Then he looked at Nemi.
"Next time you see the wrong road before I do, say it sooner."
Nemi looked toward the timber they had just left. "I did."
Marrow held that one in his face for a breath and then laughed despite himself.
The sound did more for the camp than the level ground did.

Chapter 12: Vale's Question
Card corners tapped Vale's knuckles while the coffee heated.
The camp sat on a shelf above a narrow creek with the wagon pulled close to a stand of fir and the team picketed where they could reach thin grass between stones. No one loved the place, which made it fit the road. Marrow liked only that the ground gave him one clear way in and one clear way out. Jed liked nothing about it except the creek and the trees thick enough to break sight from a distance.
Vale sat on an upturned box by the fire and shuffled a deck with the care of a man making a show out of not having enough audience.
Wren set down the coffee pot. "Card game for one is a poor trade."
"Not if the one at least cheats honestly."
Marrow snorted and went on trimming a harness repair with his knife.
Vale let the cards settle in his palm, then looked up at Jed where he sat near the wheel tying a rawhide wrap over one worn rein end. "Stone Relay."
No one spoke at first. Not because the name was unknown. Because everyone around the fire had heard enough by now to know it belonged to Jed more than to them.
Vale tapped the deck once against his knee. "Place or man."
Jed kept working the wrap. "Neither for you."
"That still leans place."
"You lean toward trouble."
Vale smiled without heat. "Trouble is where truth loosens."
"Then go trouble your own boots."
Ruth fed one short stick into the fire. "He will not stop because you are rude to him."
"I had not noticed a stop when I was polite."
That drew one breath of near-laughter from Caleb and a glance from Marrow hard enough to take it back.
Vale spread three cards face down on his knee and turned them one after another as if the pasteboard held counsel. They showed nothing useful. He only wanted hands on them while he talked.
"A man on a plain road does not keep his name under lock unless his name costs money. A man with no paper guide does not keep moving north unless north pays better than south. A wounded guard does not refuse easy company unless the destination matters more than the company." He gathered the cards again. "That is all I mean."
Jed tied off the wrap and bit the end flat. "For a man who means little, you spend a lot of words."
Wren spared him from answering by handing Ruth the pot. "More coffee."
Spoon knocked the rim while cups passed.
Talk after that went ordinary by force instead of nature. Marrow named mileage and feed. Ruth asked Caleb whether the bandage had held. Caleb lied that it had held better than it felt. Wren spoke of nothing larger than water and wood. Vale kept the cards moving in one hand and his eyes on the ring like a dealer waiting for a weak bet.
Later, with the fire lower and the cold pushing men closer to blankets than truth, Marrow took Jed aside by the team.
"He knows enough to be a nuisance."
"He knew that before today."
"You going to put a stop to it."
"How."
"Men stop talking if they think a jaw will pay for it."
"Then I would have three quiet liars instead of four loud ones."
Marrow considered that and gave it up because it was true. "I do not mind liars on the road if I know which lie they favor. That one favors the kind with polished boots."
"That is still a lie."
"Yes." Marrow looked toward the fire where Vale now sat closer to Ruth than he had before. "Polished lies travel well."
Watch took its turns after that. Wren first at the creek side. Jed second. Marrow third by his own choice because drivers trust sleep less than other men do. Caleb lay down in pain and found no rest worth naming. Ruth kept the box under both knees and one hand through the handle ring. Vale rolled himself in his blanket with the cards inside his coat.
Bedroll strap creaked under Jed's hand when he came back from the dark.
The blanket roll still sat where he had left it. The knot did not.
He crouched at once and touched nothing for a count. The loose end had been fed back through with care, but not his care. The little flap inside the trail kit where he kept spare cartridges had been lifted and pressed flat again. The canteen thong sat half a finger wider than before. No paper was missing because no paper existed. Still, someone had gone through the whole roll searching for what they thought a man like Jed would carry.
He raised his head and looked over the sleeping ring.
Marrow snored once against the wheel and then went still. Ruth had the box under her and would have knifed any hand that came near it. Caleb slept in the shallow way of wounded men, too often awake not to be honest about it. Wren at the creek side showed only the pale line of one cheek under the hat brim. Vale lay on his side with his back to the fire, one hand tucked under his coat where the cards were.
Nemi sat beyond the team, not on watch exactly, but not asleep either.
Jed left the bedroll as he found it and walked to the horses. Nemi turned his head a little when he came close.
"You saw."
The boy nodded.
"Who."
Nemi touched two fingers to his own coat front, then drew them out and made a slow little fan in the dark.
Cards.
Vale.
Jed looked back at the blanket roll. "Did he find anything."
Nemi shook his head once.
"Good."
Nemi touched the side of his face where a man might take a fist.
"Not yet." Jed looked back at the blanket roll.
That answer did not satisfy the boy, but he let it stand.
When Jed lay back down, he kept the knife under the blanket and the trail kit under his shoulder instead of by his boots. Vale did not stir. That did not make him less guilty.
It only meant the next move would come with more care.

Chapter 13: Draven Talks to Caleb
Blanket edge hissed over dirt when Caleb slipped from it.
He froze at once and listened. Marrow's snore had not broken. Ruth still breathed through her nose in the short, hard pattern she kept around the box. Wren had last watch and had gone to the south side with the rifle. Jed lay where the wagon shadow swallowed half his bedroll. Nemi could have been anywhere and was therefore the worst part of the plan.
Caleb waited through three heartbeats, then crawled clear of the wheel and stood slow enough not to pull the wound open.
The cutbank below camp held a trickle of water and one strip of open ground where moonlight reached. He had seen it before dusk and hated himself for remembering it now. Men met in places like that because places like that let a body run in only two directions. Draven liked that kind of arithmetic.
Root caught Caleb's boot when he started down. He bit back the sound and kept going.
The ground leveled under the bank. Creek water moved over stone with no force in it at all. Caleb stopped near the cut shadow and put one hand over the bandage. Sweat had already come up under his shirt though the air could have cooled iron.
Loose gravel dropped from the bank above him.
Draven stepped into the open as if he had been there before Caleb arrived and only now chose to be seen.
No rush. No gun in hand. Coat buttoned. Hat low. The same face Caleb had watched by three different campfires, always quieter than the men around it and always more dangerous for that quiet.
Draven stopped in the moonlit strip and looked him over. "You took your time."
Caleb swallowed. "I could have stayed in camp."
"You could have. Then I would know less about you."
"You know enough."
Draven came two steps closer and looked at the bandage under Caleb's hand. "Enough to know I missed the good part of you by an inch. Enough to know some men call that luck and other men call it debt."
Caleb did not answer.
"How many with the wagon."
"You already know that."
"I know what the ground told me. I asked what you will tell me."
Caleb stared at the creek. "Driver. Widow. Preacher. Boy. Card man. Jed."
"And you."
"And me."
Draven nodded once. "You kept better count this time."
Caleb looked up despite himself. "What do you want."
"Only one thing." Draven brushed a little dirt from his cuff with the back of his thumb. "Tell me when Jed leaves camp alone."
Caleb laughed once without humor. "That sounds small because you say it soft."
"Soft words travel farther."
"And if I do not."
Draven looked at the bandage again. "Then I wait until the wagon grows tired of feeding a man who drips trouble behind him. The card man may sell first. The driver may choose road over loyalty. The widow may decide one secret is enough burden without carrying another. Men under strain make bargains they would spit on in daylight."
Caleb felt the cutbank close around him though it had not moved an inch. "You already have that worked out."
"I already have most things worked out."
"Jed will kill you if he gets hold of you close."
"Then I should avoid being held close."
There was no heat in the answer. That made it worse.
Caleb shifted his weight and nearly folded when the wound reminded him where it sat. Draven saw it. Draven saw everything that cost a man one extra breath.
Draven brushed dirt from his cuff with one thumb. "You want safe passage. I can give it. Not friendship. Not pardon. Just distance and one road left open when the rest close."
"Why would I trust that."
"Because you trust me less than the people in that camp trust you. You can feel that every time they look at you." Draven took one more step in. "I do not need your loyalty. I need your timing."
Caleb could smell the leather of the coat now. Not smoke. Not whisky. Clean leather and cold air.
"You shot the cook over beans."
"I shot him over his mouth."
"That is worse."
"For him." Draven's eyes never changed. "For you it should be useful."
Caleb shifted his weight and found the cutbank close behind him before he could give ground.
"I did not say yes."
"No." Draven let the word sit. "You came down here instead."
That landed harder than the rest.
Caleb looked away first. Creek water. Mud. The white edge of one stone where the moon touched it. Anything except the man in front of him and the fact that he had walked down to this talk with both feet.
Draven took one step back and kept his eyes on Caleb. "When he leaves alone, you tell me. Not because you love me. Not because you hate him. Because I offered you the only bargain on this road that ends with you alive and not crawling."
Then he stepped back. Just enough to show the talk was done if Caleb was wise enough to understand endings when he got one.
Draven tipped his head toward the camp. "Go on. Before your driver wakes and learns what sort of man he has tied to his wheel."
Caleb climbed the bank by feel more than sight. Twice he had to stop for the wound. Once he almost turned and fired the little revolver he had tucked in his waistband just to force the night into one clean choice. He did not. Men who drew on Draven from fear usually died from fear before the bullet even mattered.
At camp he found the blankets still and the wagon black against the weak sky. He lay down where he had come from and pulled the cover up to his chin.
The secret sat heavier than the wound did.
When sleep came, it came in cuts no deeper than a knife scratch.

Chapter 14: The Box Opens
Box latch clicked under Ruth's thumb.
She had not opened it yet. Not since the wagon. Not since Lask's men. Not since Marrow lost a horse and Wren spent powder for her road. The box sat on the wagon board between her knees while the others formed a half ring around the fire and waited in the sort of silence that had already chosen its question.
Marrow broke first because the driver in him always wanted the road named plain. "Open it."
Ruth kept one hand flat on the lid. "You know enough."
"I know a horse is dead."
Vale looked from face to face and kept his mouth shut for once. That alone made Jed distrust him more.
Wren rested the rifle across his thighs. "If we are carrying it, we should know what it asks of us."
Caleb sat wrapped in a blanket and stared at the box with the sour look men wear when they expect another person's trouble to turn out richer than their own.
Ruth took a breath and did not spend it right away. "If I open it, you do not paw through it."
Marrow nodded. "Fair."
"And what I put back in, stays in."
Vale rested both palms on his knees. "Until the next horse dies."
Jed looked at him. "You had a chance to stay shut."
Vale lifted one hand and let it fall.
Iron hinge ground when Ruth brought the lid back.
The first thing inside was coin wrapped in two pieces of old shirt linen. Not a fortune. Enough to make small men foolish and large men patient. Under that sat three folded letters tied with twine, one narrow ledger strip with names written in a clerk hand, and a watch with the chain torn off at the ring.
Ruth took out the coin first and laid the bundle on the wagon board. Then the letters. Then the strip of names. Last came the watch.
Caleb stared at it. "That is all."
Marrow leaned one shoulder against the wheel. "That is enough."
Ruth touched the watch cover once with her thumb and then looked at none of them in particular. "My husband ran freight when freight meant whatever a man could move without questions. Some of it was bought. Some of it was not. Hobb Lask worked with him when the work needed more nerve than wit."
Jed looked from the coin to the paper. "And your husband kept more than his share."
"Yes."
Marrow leaned one shoulder against the wheel. "Coin explains greed. Papers explain persistence."
Ruth lifted the ledger strip. "Names."
Wren put out one hand. "Read them."
She did. Not all at first. Enough. A ranch hand from Spur. A widow in Dry Wash. One freighter who never got paid for a winter run. One gambler in North Spur whose brother ended up dead over a false count. Small names. Common names. The kind no court ever gathers into one room because no court rides that much dust for men and women who lack friends in waistcoats.
Vale let out a low breath. "That is not a debt note. That is a wound list."
Ruth folded the letter shut. "Yes."
She opened one of the letters next. The paper had softened at the folds. Her husband had written one of them. The hand was quick and mean, the kind that pushed hard through cheap paper. He named Lask twice and bragged once about keeping both coin and proof because proof owned a man longer than silver did.
Ruth folded the letter shut before the third line.
Caleb looked from the coin to the paper. "Why not throw the names in the fire and toss the silver in the creek."
Ruth's head came up. "Because some of those people were wronged and do not know by who."
"And that matters more than getting us clear."
"It matters enough that I am still carrying it."
Marrow stared at the box for a long count. "Lask wants the coin because coin is coin. He wants the names because they tie him to your husband tighter than rope."
Wren rested one hand on the rifle stock. "And tighter than a grave."
Jed took up the watch and opened the case. No portrait inside. No initials. Only a hairline crack on the face and old dirt ground into the hinge. He set it back down.
"How many men know what is in this."
Ruth thought before she answered. "Lask. Me. One dead husband. Maybe one clerk who copied the strip and is dead himself by now."
Vale gave a small smile with no pleasure in it. "And now six road companions of mixed character."
Caleb shifted against the blanket. "Seven. Count the boy."
Nemi sat by the fire with both hands around his cup and did not dignify that with a look.
Coin tapped wood when Ruth counted it back into the box.
Marrow watched each piece go in. "It stays shut on the road unless I say otherwise."
Ruth nodded once.
"It rides under the front board where I can lay a hand on it. Not under your blanket where a midnight thief can be blamed on dreams."
Ruth nodded again. Slower this time, but still yes.
Jed pointed at Vale without turning the point into a full accusation. "And if anybody here gets curious after dark again, I will know where to start."
Vale's eyes narrowed, only a little. "You speak as if the box interests me."
"You speak as if everything does."
That earned no answer.
Wren gathered the letters and held them out. Ruth took them back and laid them flat under the coin bundle before closing the lid. The latch caught with one hard sound.
Marrow pushed off the wheel. "Then that is done."
Jed closed the watch case with his thumb. "No. Done is when Lask stops riding."
Nobody argued that because nobody had a better measure.
When the fire burned low and the box went back under the front board, the wagon carried the same weight as before and less ignorance. The second burden sat heavier.

Chapter 15: Snow Without Cover
Runner chain clinked against the wagon side in the cold.
The snow field opened after noon with no timber left to hide behind and no rock bigger than a saddle horn from one end of the white stretch to the other. Marrow drew the team up at the edge long enough to look across it, then clicked them forward because there was nothing else to do but cross.
Jed walked beside the left wheel instead of riding. A mounted man made a better mark on open ground and a slower one if he fell. Wren rode the rear board with the rifle wrapped in canvas against the damp. Ruth sat over the box. Vale kept his collar up. Caleb breathed through his teeth every time the wheel cut a frozen rut. Nemi ranged a little ahead and to one side, where the snow showed every fresh print like writing.
Only the wagon moved out there.
That was the bad part.
Men could live in trees and stone. Men could hide in brush. Men who wanted to send a message favored the ground where a target had nowhere to turn and plenty of room to imagine.
Marrow lifted one hand without looking back. "Keep it tight."
No one needed the order explained.
The field had no sound but harness, wheel grind, and the little breath the team threw into the cold. Jed looked from ridge lip to ridge lip and found no dark shape that held still too long. That did not mean the shooter was absent. Only skilled.
Nemi turned once from ahead of the team and made a low hand sign close to his thigh.
No track near. No cut sign. No brush edge broken because there was no brush to break.
The boy had nothing to read but distance and still read danger in it.
Ruth saw the sign from the seat and did not ask him to repeat it. Vale kept glancing toward the left ridge as if nerves might eventually produce a clear target where none had shown itself yet. Wren held the wrapped rifle across both knees and watched the higher shoulder where a patient marksman would rest if he wanted all the time in the world.
Rifle crack crossed the flat.
The shot struck snow ten feet off the right wheel and threw white grit across Vale's coat and Ruth's skirts. The team lunged. Marrow hauled them straight before panic could twist the wagon broadside and break an axle.
Marrow hauled the lines short. "Down."
Everyone was already lower than they had been.
Jed dropped to one knee in the churned track and looked toward the far ridge where the land lifted in one clean white shoulder. Too far for a face. Not too far for a practiced rifle. The snow burst told him enough. The shot had not hunted flesh. It had hunted certainty.
Wren stripped the canvas off the rifle and sighted along the ridge line. "I have no body."
Jed stayed on one knee by the strike mark. "Because he does not want one yet."
Caleb looked at the far ridge and swallowed once. "That was him."
Vale snapped his head around. "You know the sound of one man from this distance."
"I know the kind of miss." Caleb did not look away from the ridge. "A bad shot kills the wrong thing. A good shot leaves you measuring the next one."
No one disputed that either.
Snow crust cracked under boots when they gathered in.
Jed walked to the strike mark and put two fingers into the hole the bullet had made. Deep enough for hard lead and flat angle. Fired from above and farther out than most men would trust. The sort of shot a man took only when he wanted the target to measure him instead of bleed from him.
Draven.
He stood and looked back at the wagon. Ruth had both hands on the box. Vale had lost color and was trying to hide that fact behind annoyance. Marrow sat set against the seat as if anger could add another horse to the team. Wren kept the rifle up though he knew as well as Jed that a second shot would come from somewhere else if it came at all.
Jed stood and looked back at the wagon. "Move."
Marrow did not waste a word answering. He slapped the lines and took the team forward at the best pace the field allowed. No one argued for a search. No one asked if the shooter might have gone. The shot had done what it came to do.
It had told them range.
Halfway across the white, Caleb spoke low enough that only Jed and Ruth heard it. "He can keep this up all week."
Ruth did not look at him. "Then we do not give him a week."
That was the first plain thing she had said since the box opened, and Jed marked it.
The ridge on the far side came slow. Every yard felt borrowed. Wren found nothing with the glass but still watched until his eyes watered. Nemi never once looked back. He kept his eyes on the next fold of ground and the next, as if the only way to defeat a long shot was to lead the wagon out of its language.
When timber at last returned in a dark line below the rise, the whole party breathed differently. Not safe. Only less bare.
Marrow did not stop under the first trees. He pushed another half mile in before he let the team stand.
No one climbed down fast. Men and women who have been held in a rifle's mouth a long time carry the shape of it in their knees after the danger passes.
Wren checked the ridge one last time before he lowered the rifle. "He let us go."
Jed brushed snow crust off one knee. "He let us think that."
Ruth climbed down from the seat and looked back through the trees toward the white they had left behind. "Then the next ground matters more than this one."
Marrow spat into the snow shadows. "It always did."
Jed looked back once across the white stretch they had crossed. The field showed only one road through it and one message buried near the right wheel.
Draven had not wanted blood.
He had wanted all of them to know he could choose it later.

Chapter 16: Caleb Breaks
Pot lid knocked when supper boiled over the edge.
Marrow swore, hooked it back with the spoon, and set the pot aside before half the beans could spill into the fire. The smell of burned starch rode up with the steam and sat over the camp like one more sour thing no one had asked for. They had made timber before dark after the snow ground, but the cover did not feel like cover anymore.
Vale broke the silence first.
"We crossed open white because one man wanted to prove a point. I would like to know whether we mean to keep carrying the point with us."
Jed sat near the outer log and sharpened a broken awl point with his knife. He did not look up. "Then ask plain."
"Plain is this." Vale held both hands out toward the fire. "Draven follows you. Not me. Not the widow. Not our driver. You."
Marrow kept stirring the pot though it did not need stirring. "He is not wrong."
Ruth looked from one to the other. "Lask follows me. The road can hold more than one enemy."
Vale held both hands out toward the fire. "Lask fires to take a box. Draven fires to tell a story."
Wren sat with the rifle across his knees and watched Jed instead of Vale. "The shot proves the road around us has narrowed."
Caleb bent over his cup without drinking from it.
Marrow set the pot down. "If Jed leaves tomorrow and takes Draven with him, does this road improve or worsen."
No one answered first because everyone had already answered inside and did not like the sound of it.
Jed saved them the courtesy. "Worsen."
Vale tipped his head. "Convince me."
"You lose the man who reads bad ground. You lose the man Nemi trusts on the line ahead. You keep Lask on Ruth's back and still have no promise Draven peels off." Jed wiped the awl point clean on his trouser leg. "Men like him finish what gets under their skin."
Vale laughed once. "That sounds almost flattering."
Marrow kept stirring the pot. "That sounds like survival."
Still, the doubt had been spoken aloud now, and spoken doubt does not go back into men as if it had never touched air.
Caleb's cup handle snapped in his hand.
The little clay ear broke off and dropped into the dirt by his boot. Everyone looked at him because the sound was too small for so much attention and because men under strain break what they are really holding somewhere else.
Caleb stared at the cup stump in his hand. "He spoke to me."
No one moved for a breath.
Ruth put down her own cup first. "When."
"Two nights ago." Caleb did not look at any of them. "Below camp. Cutbank."
Vale let out one soft breath through his nose. "There it is."
Marrow's face changed without rising in volume. "You left camp alone after I named the rule."
"Yes."
"Why."
Caleb looked at the fire. "Because I knew if he wanted talk, he would get it one way or another."
Jed's knife stayed still in his hand. "What did he offer."
"Safe passage if I told him when you left camp alone." Caleb swallowed and forced the rest through before courage went bad on him. "He said the wagon would turn on you sooner or later and I should choose the side that leaves me breathing."
Vale laughed once and got no company in it.
Wren's voice came low. "Did you accept."
"No."
"Did you tell him anything."
Caleb thought too long.
Marrow's hand tightened on the spoon. "Boy."
"Only count and shape. Not route. Not plan. Nothing he could not read himself." Caleb finally lifted his head. "I know that does not help me any."
Marrow looked straight at him. "No. It does not."
The fire made the silence after that. Popped once. Sank. Shifted one ember against another like teeth grinding in sleep.
Jed stood.
"Then I leave at first light."
Ruth looked up fast. "That is not what any of us said."
"It is what the road says." Jed slid the knife back into its sheath. "If Draven wants me out from under this wagon, he should have it."
Caleb half rose and nearly tore the wound again. "No."
Jed turned on him. "You have another better use for my company."
Caleb looked from Jed to Nemi. "The burial trail." Then he stopped because there was no more in him to name.
Stick butt knocked the fire ring stone.
Nemi had come up from the dark without any of them hearing him. He stood on the edge of the light with the stick in one hand and his blanket over one shoulder.
"He stays," the boy said.
Marrow frowned. "Why."
"Trail ahead takes wagon slow. Wrong foot. Wrong wheel. Wrong man with reins." Nemi looked at Jed and then at the rest. "You need him."
Vale almost smiled. "A moving speech."
Nemi ignored him. "Burial trail takes more than it gives. Elkheart said that."
Jed let the words settle. So did Marrow. The driver knew as well as any of them that once a line became team, wheel, and cliff instead of man and saddle, losing the lead hand cost more than pride.
Wren rested both palms on the rifle stock. "Then the matter is not trust. It is passage."
Marrow took the spoon out of the pot and pointed with it. "Passage keeps men alive long enough to earn trust back."
Caleb's shoulders caved in a little. "I did not sell him."
Vale looked over. "Not all the way."
Ruth answered that one before Jed could. "You enjoy every ugly minute a road gives you."
"Only the truthful ones."
Jed looked at Caleb. Shame had done more to him than the wound. That was plain. Shame also did not make a man harmless.
Jed turned back on Caleb. "You speak to me first next time."
Caleb gave a ragged nod. "There will not be a next time."
"See that there isn't."
That was as close to mercy as the fire was getting tonight.
Marrow passed the pot around at last. No one spoke much while they ate. Agreement had come, but only because the trail ahead demanded more than their distrust could yet afford.

Chapter 17: Clementine Finds Lask
Bit ring clicked when Clementine checked the mare on fresh sign.
The wagon trail held north in the ordinary way for another rider to follow, but a second pull cut off from it through brush and shallow wash where one horse had carried bad weight and another had begun to stumble under a wounded man. Clementine sat a moment in the saddle and read both lines together.
Jed would still be on the main road.
The wounded line could tell her what sort of men had crossed his path and whether they rode under Draven or merely beside his trouble. That answer was worth half a day if the answer came fast.
She turned the mare into the side line.
The sign grew sloppier within a mile. Men in pain and men angry at pain make the ground talk louder than they mean to. Blood showed once on sage. Then again on a cottonwood root where somebody had dismounted wrong. By late afternoon she found the camp they had made because they had no better legs left under them.
One drag mark crossed the wash where a man had been half-lifted, half-pulled to shade. A second set of prints wandered off twenty steps and returned in a stumble line. Lask's people had not chosen this camp. Hurt had chosen it for them.
Two horses. Three men.
One was dead before she arrived, wrapped only to the chest in a blanket and left with both boots still on. Another lay against a stump with his shoulder bandaged in a shirt torn crosswise. The third had enough life left to get to his pistol when he heard her come in and not enough sense left to keep his hand from showing first.
Rifle hammer drew back under Clementine's thumb.
The rifle stayed steady in her hands. "Do not."
The man froze with his fingers on the grip.
"Kick it clear."
He did. Pain from the movement bent his face into something uglier than fear and less interesting.
Clementine stepped in close enough to take the pistol with her free hand. "You ride for Lask."
"I rode with him."
"Past tense is cheap."
He looked at the dead man by the blanket and gave a little shrug that cost him. "Past enough."
Clementine dismounted, kept the rifle on him, and took the pistol with her free hand. The wounded man by the stump watched all this with the dull eye of someone already halfway busy staying alive. The one she had spoken to still had enough fear left to answer clean.
"Lask where."
"Ahead, maybe. Or circling for the wagon again if he still thinks he can take the box before Spur."
"Draven with him."
That brought a real reaction. The man blinked once and then shook his head too fast.
"No. No chance. Lask hates men who stay quiet that long."
"Then who fired the long shot on the white."
"Not us." He licked dry lips. "We hit camp once and got hit back. After that we were only trying to stay on horses. Lask said another man was riding the same road but not the same business."
There it was.
Two threats. Same road. Different hunger.
Clementine believed him because the fear sat in the right place. Men lie upward when they think it buys them room. This one lied sideways, trying only to keep from being shot for another man's quarrel. That kind of fear usually points true.
Clementine moved her aim to the man by the stump. "He talk too."
The stump man coughed blood into his beard and wiped it away with the heel of his hand. "Lask wants the widow's box. That is all. He said the cold-eyed rider can have the cripple if he catches him, but he would not split a bullet with him."
"Cold-eyed rider." Clementine let that sit and watched both of them through the pause.
"Did not give a name," the first man said. "Did not need to. Lask only said he was bad company and let him keep his distance."
That fit.
Draven would never ride under Lask. Lask would never stomach serving as another man's flank noise. Men like that crossed roads. They did not share them.
She took one step closer. "How far ahead is the wagon."
"Depends when they turned off the snow line."
"Give me better than that."
The man looked at the pistol in her hand and then at the dead one by the blanket. "A day. Maybe a little more. They lost one horse and still kept moving."
Clementine took one more minute because a live mouth was wasted if it walked away with half a lie still in it.
"How bad is Lask hurt."
The stump man answered this time. "Not gut bad. Shoulder or upper arm. Enough to make him foul and slower at the draw. Not enough to send him home."
"How many still with him."
"Two if both stay. One if the smart one leaves before dawn."
That matched the camp in front of her. Lask's kind lost men by greed first, blood second.
That matched what the ground had said at the ferry and after.
Clementine backed toward the mare and held the rifle steady. "You tell Lask if he asks that the next shot comes to finish, not to teach."
"What should I call you."
"You should not."
She mounted in one clean swing and turned back toward the main road before either man could decide whether pride should outrun sense.
Rein leather stretched when she swung north again.
The wagon trail waited where she had left it. Same worn wheels. Same limp sign at the places where the ground softened. Same pressure, only clearer now.
Draven wanted Jed. Lask wanted the box. The wagon sat between both wants and had no room left for error.
Clementine gave the mare her head and let the road pull hard.

Chapter 18: Burial Trail
Wheel rim scraped stone when the wagon entered the narrow line.
The burial trail did not look like a road so much as a wound left open in the ground and only partly covered by needles. The trees stood close enough to rake the wagon cloth on both sides. Rock pushed up through dirt in long ribs. On the left, the slope rose into black timber. On the right, it dropped away through brush and broken shelf to a ravine no one wanted to measure.
Everyone walked.
Marrow held the reins low and spoke to the team in the same voice he once used for calm crossings. Jed took the right wheel where bad ground might pry it off line. Wren worked the rear brake. Ruth carried the box under one arm. Caleb came last among the walkers because he could not hold the pace and knew it. Nemi moved ahead of the lead pair and touched tree, rock, and rut with the stick as if reminding the trail that he remembered it better than the trail remembered itself.
Nobody wasted words.
Vale hated silence enough to wear it badly. That was plain in the way he watched every other back in front of him, looking for the first mouth weak enough to open. He did not get one. The burial line took speech away from all of them because footing came first and footing left no room in the mind for much else.
Cloth tore under Vale's fingers.
Jed heard it because the sound was wrong for the rest of the line. He did not turn at once. Not until the next bend let him look back without taking the wheel hand off the wagon.
Vale had dropped something pale on a low twig and was three steps past it already.
Marker.
Nemi saw it too. The boy did not speak. He only let the lead pair pass, stepped to the side as if checking one rock near the rut, and stripped the cloth free in the same motion. By the time Vale looked back, the marker was gone and Nemi had tucked it into his sleeve.
Vale said nothing.
That told Jed as much as the cloth had.
Jed let the wheel roll another yard before he trusted his hands enough not to turn and strike the card man with the brake handle. Anger would have felt good there. It would also have put the wagon in the ravine.
They went on.
The next stretch narrowed so sharply that Jed had to walk sideways between wheel and rock with one hand on the rim and one hand against the wall. Marrow's voice would have cursed there and then steadied the team in the same breath. Without him, the work passed down the line in touches and grunts. Wren checked the brake every ten steps. Ruth took the lead rope once when Nemi had to run ahead to test a shelf. Caleb slipped, caught himself on the wagon board, and kept his mouth shut through the pain because the trail had no use for complaint.
The trail took a full afternoon to cross less country than a clean wagon road would have crossed in an hour. At one shelf the right wheel had to be walked over stone by hand while Marrow eased the team one hoof at a time. At another, Wren lay flat and shoved dead branches into a wash scar to give the rear wheel one more inch of bite. Ruth handed the box to Jed for that part and took it back without either of them making a display of trust out of it.
Once the lead pair shied from a dead pine root clawing out of the bank and nearly crossed traces. Marrow settled them before the panic reached the wagon. Nemi was already at their heads by then, palm to cheekbone, speaking so low the words did not matter. The horses took the calm anyway. Everyone else did too.
Caleb nearly went over once when the wound pulled him short on a downhill step. Vale caught his sleeve before he could drag the rifle or the brake man with him. That bought Vale one look from Marrow which might have been thanks if Marrow had believed in public thanks.
By the time the trees opened enough for evening to exist again, all of them were carrying the trail in their legs.
Brake lever knocked wood when Marrow stopped for camp.
They made it in a little basin no bigger than a stock pen and only better than the trail because it was level. The team stood with heads low. Wren worked the wheel checks without needing to be told. Ruth set the box under the front board. Caleb sat down where he landed and did not rise again until coffee reached him. Vale cleaned his hands with snow and watched Nemi across the fire.
Jed waited until full dark before he walked the edge of camp and found the little strips of cloth the boy had taken.
Nemi sat on a stone with the stick across his knees. Jed held up one strip.
"How many."
"Three."
"You saw the first one."
Nemi nodded.
"Why not call him on it."
The boy looked toward the fire where Vale sat in profile and let his hands warm over the cup. "Trail first."
That was the right answer. Jed knew it and still disliked it because correct delay still leaves rot alive for one more night.
Jed looked at the coals. "Draven never sees these."
Nemi took the strips, fed them one by one into the coals, and watched them curl black.
Across camp Vale lifted his eyes once and found Jed looking back at him. Nothing passed between them in words. Enough passed anyway.
The burial trail had not broken the wagon.
It had only narrowed the room left for betrayal.

Chapter 19: Marrow's Last Drive
Wheel hub cracked against stone.
The sound went through the wagon like an axe head through green timber. Marrow hauled the team down before the right rear wheel could climb half off the shelf and shear the whole bed sideways into the drop. The trail there ran along a granite face no wider than a loaded wagon and one careful horse at a time. Above it, broken ledge. Below it, air and hard rock enough to finish anything that slipped.
Jed was off the line before the wagon stopped moving. He got his shoulder under the bed and felt the weight settle ugly above him.
Jed shoved harder under the wagon bed. "Hold them straight."
Marrow already had the reins wrapped once around his wrist. "I was born trying."
Wren came in at the axle with the tool roll. Nemi went to the lead pair and laid one hand against the near horse's cheek where a frightened driver might have chosen the bit. Ruth and Caleb dragged loose stones toward the wheel track. Vale stood clear for one breath too long, saw who noticed, and then jumped to the brake lever as if that had been his thought from the first.
Hammer tap rang on the axle pin.
Jed knelt in the road grit and drove the bent pin out while Wren held the broken wheel clear enough to save the hub from biting deeper into the shelf. Marrow stood above them, one boot braced against the front board, one hand locked on the reins, one hand free to steady the wagon bed when the team shifted.
Vale grabbed the brake lever with both hands. "Faster."
Marrow did not look at him. "Bring me a better wheel if you know where one grows."
The broken spoke had splintered near the hub. Not repairable in any way a sane driver would trust. They would have to lash the spare rim and pray over poor work because the road offered no shop and no second chance.
Jed spat grit from his mouth. "Wren, pass the strap."
Wren did. His hands stayed steady, but the line of his jaw had hardened. Men know the kind of stop that invites a rifle. They know it in the bones before proof comes.
Ruth looked from the wheel to the ridge above them. "How much time."
Jed spat grit from his mouth. "More than I like."
The team tossed their heads together. Nemi spoke low into one ear and one shoulder went down again. For the space of three breaths the wagon seemed almost willing to believe in repair.
Then the shot came.
No warning crack across open snow this time. Only one hard report from stone somewhere above and behind them and Marrow taking half a step backward as if the road itself had shoved him.
The reins jerked once through his hand.
Blood spread dark through his coat front under the tied loop at his wrist.
Ruth said his name. Vale ducked. Caleb stared. Wren did neither. He grabbed the wheel before it could slip and shouted, "Hold it now."
Jed was already on his feet.
Marrow looked down at the blood as if it belonged to another man who had chosen a bad place to stand. Then he looked at the team. Not at the wound. Not at the sky. At the team.
Marrow kept one hand locked on the reins. "Finish it."
That was all.
His knees folded under him and the tied reins dragged his arm down with the rest of him. The lead pair felt the change at once and surged. Nemi hung on to the near bridle. Jed caught the wagon bed with both hands and shoved upward while Wren jammed the spare rim into place with all his weight behind it.
Stone grated. The wheel climbed half a finger higher.
Wren braced both boots against the stone shelf. "Again."
Jed drove shoulder and hands into the spoke. Caleb limped in at last and put what strength he still owned into the rear corner. Ruth set the box down in the road and braced against the brake arm. Vale took the opposite side because there was no safe place left to stand without being seen for what he was.
The wheel seated ugly, but seated.
Wren drove the new pin home with three fast hammer blows. Jed lashed the hub with rawhide and one trace strap cut short for the purpose. None of it would have pleased Marrow. All of it would hold long enough if God or the road still had humor left in either of them.
Only then did Jed go to the driver.
Marrow sat against the wheel with the reins still wound to the wrist and the blood already cooling in the cloth. Eyes open. Mouth shut. The shot had taken him through the chest clean enough to leave no work for a healer and no time for a confession.
Wren crouched once, touched two fingers to the neck, and rose again. "Gone."
No one said anything for a long breath after that. The shelf did not allow much.
Jed cut the reins free from the dead hand, then stopped and tied them back around the wrist exactly as he had found them.
Ruth saw him do it and said nothing.
Vale looked uphill toward the unseen rifle line. "He can watch us grieve when we camp."
Jed turned his head. "Then live till camp."
That was enough to start them moving.
They lifted Marrow into the wagon bed because the road gave no grave and no ground fit for one. Nemi took the lead pair. Jed took the reins from the front board and drove the wagon forward on the patched wheel while Wren walked the right side with the rifle ready and Caleb kept one hand on the brake.
The wagon moved because it had to.
Marrow rode with the reins tied to his wrist because he had died in harness and there was no other honest way to carry him from that shelf.

Chapter 20: Vale Makes His Offer
Brake chain shuddered when the wagon stopped for night.
They made camp in scrub timber below the shelf country with no one satisfied by the ground and no one able to demand better. Marrow's body lay under a blanket in the wagon bed with the tied reins still around the wrist. The patched rear wheel stood canted enough to promise trouble by daylight. The team had foam dried white on the flanks and stood with heads low under the line.
Without Marrow, even small tasks asked louder questions.
Who set the fire. Who laid out the feed. Who chose the watch order. Who spoke first when no driver stood ready to end a foolish line with one look.
Ruth took the feed sack without asking. Wren cut kindling down smaller than needed because small work keeps a hand from closing on worse things. Caleb made himself useful by fetching water no one wanted to drink and then stood with the bucket as if awaiting judgment from it. Nemi checked the traces twice and said nothing about the places Marrow would have tightened better.
Jed took the north side to check the wheel straps and the ground beyond camp. Wren watched the body while he cleaned the rifle. Ruth sat by the wagon tongue with the box at her feet. Caleb kept to the far side of the fire and stared into it as if it owed him an answer about surviving one more night. Nemi vanished into the brush and came back with armloads of deadfall none of the others had noticed.
Vale kept the cards in his coat until dark fully settled. Then one edge clicked against his ring finger.
Vale kept the cards in his coat and looked around the fire. "We are short a driver."
No one answered.
"Short a horse. Short one patched wheel. Short trust. Yet still rich in one thing." He looked from face to face. "Bargaining material."
Ruth's eyes came up first. "Say it plain."
Vale spread his hands to the fire. "Draven wants Jed alive and useful. We want to go on breathing. Those interests do not forbid each other."
Caleb shut his eyes once.
Wren rested the rifle across his thighs. "Finish it."
Vale did. "Hand Jed over when Draven comes close enough to name terms again. We walk away alive. He gets the cripple and his precious memory. The rest of us keep the road under our own boots."
The firewood popped once between them.
Ruth answered first. "No."
Vale turned to her with the patience of a man explaining sums to a child. "You are carrying a box that already cost one horse and one driver."
"And I still said no."
Wren did not lift his voice. "Same answer here."
Vale's smile tightened. "You would die for him."
Wren rested the rifle across his thighs. "No. I would not sell a man to buy my own walking room."
That left Caleb.
Vale looked at him and did not bother to hide the point of the look. "You above all should understand terms."
Caleb's hand shook once around the cup. "I understand rot."
"Then choose the less rotten bargain."
Nemi fed another stick into the fire and said nothing. Ruth watched Caleb with the hard stare women save for weak men who are still deciding whether weakness is their nature or only their present condition.
Caleb swallowed. "I do not know."
That answer sat uglier than agreement because it still left room for one.
Vale sat back as if honesty pleased him. "That is closer to wisdom than these other replies."
From beyond the fire ring, Jed's voice came through the dark.
"You talk prettier when the man named in the bargain is outside earshot."
Everyone turned.
Jed stepped into the light with road dust up both legs and a wheel strap over one shoulder. He had heard enough. That sat plain in the stillness of his face.
Vale did not flinch. "Then hear the rest in comfort. Your presence brings Draven. Your absence may take him. That makes you a cost center on poor ground."
Jed set the strap down by the wagon wheel. "You are not half as clever as you think."
"No. I am exactly that clever. Clever enough to know fear changes arithmetic."
Ruth stood. "And cowardice dresses itself up as arithmetic when cards are the only trade a man has ever learned."
Vale's eyes flicked to the box and back. "You speak as if I am the only one here who wants to leave this road alive."
Wren kept his eyes on Vale. "Wanting it and buying it are not the same thing."
Jed looked at Caleb last. The younger man held the cup with both hands now, though no heat remained in it.
"You wavering."
Caleb's answer came slow and ugly. "I heard him out."
"I know that already."
"Then you know enough."
Nemi looked from one man to the other. "Marrow did not die to hand the wagon to Draven."
No one answered the boy because none of them could improve on it.
Jed let the silence sit. Breaking Vale's mouth would have been easy. Turning the camp into open sides before dawn would have been easier than putting it back together. He did none of that.
Instead he crouched by the patched wheel and ran both hands over the strap lashings. "Wheel first. Then the rest."
Vale gave a little breath through his nose. "A moving speech."
Jed looked up. "You are alive because Marrow died with a wagon under you and because we still need every hand till this wheel holds one more day. Spend that luck carefully."
That landed harder than a threat because it was only fact.
No one at the fire tried to lift Vale after that. Not even with eyes.
The offer had been made. The answer line had been drawn. Nothing in camp could go back to pretending otherwise.

Chapter 21: Spur Below
Wagon tongue creaked as the team topped the rise.
Spur lay far below on the south road, little more than a scatter of roofs and smoke from that height, but plain enough to name. Beyond it the country folded north again toward Frostfang and then farther still into the harder ridgeline Jed had been carrying in memory since Ghostpine.
Ruth looked down first. Caleb looked longest. Vale looked as if he were measuring the price of abandoning one road for another and finding both costly. Wren only marked where a rifleman might sit if he wanted the ridge line both watched and held.
Jed looked past Spur.
Second ridgeline. East face. Four days north from there if the weather held and the patched wheel did not fail before the room was reached.
Vale looked down toward Spur and then back at Jed. "That is your supply town. You could still choose it."
Jed kept his eyes on the farther line. "Could."
"Will not."
"No."
Marrow's wagon rolled on past the sight of safety because safety had arrived too late to matter.
Caleb looked back twice before the first bend took Spur out of sight. Ruth did not look back at all. Wren marked the town only once, the way he would mark a water line or a bad crossing, as something useful left behind for a reason. Nemi watched the higher ridges and the snow pockets under them instead of the roofs below. Even Vale kept his mouth shut for a stretch, which told Jed the nearness of safety had troubled him more than open danger had.
The road gave them one low shelf where they could have turned the team south and reached town by another half day. Marrow would have chosen that road before his death because drivers count axle and feed before pride. Ruth saw the shelf and drove past it. Caleb opened his mouth as if to name the choice and then shut it again when Jed kept walking north of the tongue without so much as a glance at him.
They camped that night under stunted pine below the ridgeline with Spur still plain back through the trees if a person stood in the right place and wanted torment more than peace. Ruth buried Marrow before dark in ground shallow enough to hate but deep enough to keep a body from dogs. Wren said the words because someone had to and because the old driver had earned at least that much from the living. Jed tied the reins to the grave marker stake for one hour before taking them back to the team.
Hoofbeats stopped just beyond fire range.
No one reached for a weapon too late. Draven had announced himself too many times in smaller ways for shock to be useful now.
He sat his horse in the dark between two thin pines where the fire showed him enough to mark his face and not enough to make a clean target for Wren without the risk of hitting horse first.
Draven sat his horse between the pines and gave Jed a level look. "You found a slower way north."
Jed stood on the near side of the fire. "Still found it."
"Yes."
Draven's eyes moved once over the ring. Marrow absent. Clementine absent. Vale too still. Caleb too pale. Ruth by the box. Wren by the rifle. Nemi where the dark and the boy worked well together.
"I said before I wanted you alive," Draven went on. "That is still true. Alive men can answer questions. Dead ones are only useful to men who love digging."
Vale gave a tiny smile at that, then lost it when Ruth saw him.
Draven kept talking to Jed and no one else. "Name the place and the rest walk away."
Ruth's hand closed over the box handle. Caleb looked at the ground. Wren did not move at all. Vale held himself very still, which told more truth than speech would have done. Nemi shifted one step nearer the wagon wheel where a boy with a knife could still matter if the dark came apart.
Jed answered after one breath and no more. "No."
Draven tilted his head. "Short answer for so expensive a choice."
"You asked short."
"I can ask longer." He rested both hands on the horn. "You know what I mean to spare and what I mean to spend. That should be enough by now."
Wren let his thumb rest near the rifle hammer. "Come one step farther and the count changes."
Draven looked toward the gun only once. "If you fire, fire to kill. I dislike half-decisions."
That was for Wren. The real pressure stayed on Jed.
Draven rested both hands on the horn. "Spur is behind you. Graves are behind you. A broken driver is under your dirt now because you would rather carry a place in your head than name it and be done."
Jed looked at the horse, then the man on it. "You keep saying done as if you know the shape of it."
Draven smiled without warmth. "Closer than you do."
The fire log settled and sent one spray of sparks up between them.
Then Draven touched heel to horse and backed out of range as calmly as he had entered it.
Draven touched heel to the horse and kept backing out of range. "Think overnight. Some men grow honest when they count graves in the dark."
He was gone before the last spark died.
No one in camp spoke for a while after that because each of them had just been priced and none liked hearing their lives weighed aloud.
At last Ruth said, "He thinks we will do his work for him."
Vale looked into the fire. "Men often do."
Jed turned away from both of them and looked north, where neither Spur nor Draven could be seen from where he stood.
The next road was Stone Relay or nothing worth naming.

Chapter 22: Clementine Comes North
Branch tip brushed Clementine's sleeve at the camp edge.
She had the mare in shadow below the pine line and the rifle tied flat along the saddle so it would not catch light from the fire if she shifted wrong. Through the trunks she could see the whole camp well enough to count what mattered.
She stayed there longer than caution alone required. A careless return tells camp too much. A patient one lets a rider learn whose hands drift near weapons, whose eyes keep leaving the same patch of dark, and who has already begun living as if tomorrow will not arrive.
Marrow was gone.
She knew that before she knew the grave because no driver stood near the team and because the wagon itself had the tired, ownerless look of a tool still in use after the hand that kept it true had gone into dirt.
The team confirmed it. Horses learn a driver's hands and carry the lack of them in the neck and jaw. These stood quiet in the wrong way, taking orders but not settled by them. One patched wheel leaned in the fire glow. One grave stake stood just far enough off from camp to say they had made room for the dead because the road gave them no way around it.
Jed stood north of the fire, not sitting, not resting, just occupying the stretch of dark where men stand when they know a camp can break both from outside and within. Ruth was by the box. Wren by the rifle. Caleb folded in on himself. Nemi impossible to count clean until he shifted by one tree and gave himself away.
Vale watched Jed.
Not Draven's retreat line. Not the woods where a second rider might still be listening. Jed.
That was enough.
She let her eyes travel once around the rest of the ring to test the first conclusion against the others. Ruth carried fatigue in the shoulders and not surrender in the jaw. Wren still guarded the camp as if it belonged to no one and therefore had to be kept by force of attention alone. Caleb had the look of a man who had heard a bargain he could not unknow hearing. Nemi moved least and watched most.
Saddle leather creaked when Clementine leaned forward and looked again through the branches. The camp was smaller than it should have been, meaner around the edges, and held together only because the road ahead was worse than the people inside it.
She counted once more.
No Marrow. No extra horse to replace the one lost. One patched wheel. One fire ring nobody trusted.
And Vale with his eyes in the wrong place.
That mattered more than reunion.
Clementine let the mare stand and listened.
No low talk from men planning to bolt before dawn. No prayer from Ruth. No false ease from Jed.
Only the kind of quiet camp keeps after an offer has been made and not forgiven.
Rein ring tapped the bit when she brought the mare forward.
The horse stepped into the edge of the firelight and every hand in camp moved some version of ready except Jed's. He only turned.
Clementine came in slow enough to show no charge and straight enough to show no fear.
Vale watched the mare come in through the fire edge. "You took your time."
Jed answered him without looking away from her. "He said that too."
The line almost brought a smile and did not. Marrow's absence still sat too heavily over the fire.
Ruth stood first. "You came on the back road."
"No other road held much sense." Clementine slid down from the saddle. "Lask's wounded men are behind me and not with Draven. One of them is dead already. The rest are poor company and worse riders."
Wren gave one short nod. "That matches the shape."
Caleb finally lifted his head. "You saw Draven."
"Not tonight. I saw what he leaves." She looked at Jed then, only for a breath. "And what he asks."
Jed took that and gave nothing back in front of the others. There was no room here for old habits dressed up as comfort.
The lack of greeting did more for her than a softer camp would have done. Jed still knew where he was.
Vale spread his hands near the fire. "Since the count grows again, perhaps we may all live longer than expected."
Clementine turned on him at last. "You sound disappointed."
Vale's smile held a second and then had to work harder for itself. "Only surprised."
"Keep being that."
Nemi came out of the dark enough for the fire to catch one cheek and stood near her mare as if he had always been there.
The horses settled faster after she entered camp than the people did. Clementine marked that too. Animals do not care who carries old grudges. They care who arrives certain.
Nemi stood near her mare and looked at Vale. "You know him."
"The type." She kept looking at the card man. "That is enough."
Ruth gestured once toward the fire. "You should eat if you have not."
"I ate the better half of a rabbit at noon and the worse half by dark." Clementine untied the rifle and leaned it by the saddle. "Save the beans for people who plan to sleep."
No one laughed. Not because the line failed. Because the camp had forgotten how.
Still, the balance had changed.
Jed was no longer the only rider in camp who knew Draven from before the road turned mean. Vale was no longer the only one reading people while pretending not to. And for the first time since the ferry, the fire ring held someone who had come in by choice rather than necessity.
She took the whole altered ring in once more before she sat.
Jed's eyes touched her once and then went north again, where Stone Relay still waited and Draven still wanted the same answer.

Chapter 23: Stone Room
Boot sole ground on granite while Jed checked the approach.
The east face rose ahead in broken shelves and black seams where old water had cut the stone and then gone elsewhere. Frostfang sat far behind them now. Spur farther still. The second ridgeline had done its work and brought them here, but the last line into the room still offered more than one way for men to die on it if they chose the obvious approach.
Draven chose the obvious approach because he wanted to watch Jed refuse it.
Jed chose Nemi's forgotten crossing instead.
Wren looked at the obvious line and then at Jed. "Not the cut?"
Jed shook his head. "Too easy to hold from above."
Nemi tapped one low stone with the stick and then climbed across a split no full wagon would ever try. The line there bent behind a tooth of granite, crossed a narrow run of frozen seep, and came back under the east face at an angle that put the cliff over their right shoulders instead of above their heads.
Draven saw the turn late. That was late enough.
The party reached the wall by hard footing and single file. Ruth carried the box. Caleb carried himself and little more. Clementine watched the back trail. Vale watched the stone in front of them with the look of a man who had already started owning what he had not yet seen.
The last twenty yards cost more than the longer climb had. Frozen seep turned one shelf slick as bone. Jed crossed first, then turned back and took the box from Ruth long enough to get it over the stone lip. Wren came next with the rifle held high and the free hand flat to the wall. Caleb slipped once and barked his shin, then caught himself before he could tumble into Nemi and take the boy with him. By the time they stood at the hidden slab, all of them were breathing harder than they liked.
Clementine crouched by the outer edge for one short look down the alternate line. "Two riders correcting left."
Jed did not need names for that count.
"How long."
"Not long enough to waste."
Stone lid scraped against stone wall.
The opening hid behind a broken slab half sunk into the face where no rider passing below would give it a second look. Jed and Wren put both shoulders into the stone and shifted it clear enough for a man to slip through sidewise. Cold air came out first. Then old paper smell. Old grease. Iron. Dust held a long time without light.
Vale exhaled before anyone else spoke. "You did not carry a place in your head. You carried a grave with shelves."
Jed ducked inside with the lantern.
The room was deeper than a simple cache and smaller than a full cellar. Stone on all sides. Two shelf runs cut into one wall. Crates with the lids warped. Tin boxes. Rolled cloth gone stiff with age. Three ledger books tied in twine. Loose letters bundled by hand, not clerk string. One shallow lockbox broken open years ago and never repaired. No gold. No silver stacked for empire dreams. Only the working remains of men who had used one hidden room for years and then died before they could come back clean.
Lantern light found ordinary things first and made them worse for being ordinary.
Needles wrapped in oilcloth. Spare mule shoes. A packet of receipts for flour and lamp oil under false names. Two old revolver cylinders gone red with neglect.
No piece in the room belonged to legend. Every piece in it belonged to use.
Ruth came in after him and looked at the ledgers with something like disgust and recognition together. Wren stayed near the opening with the rifle. Clementine took the outside edge of the threshold and watched both room and slope. Caleb leaned one shoulder to the wall and breathed shallow from the climb.
Vale did not wait for permission. He crossed to the first shelf and lifted one ledger before Jed could stop him.
Vale cracked the ledger open under the lantern. "Freight counts. Names. Amounts. Places." His eyes moved faster now. "This is more than road theft. This is a hidden trade laid out proper."
Jed took the book from him.
Pages rasped under his hand.
He read enough in one look.
Dead freight names. Dry-goods marks. False handoffs. Notes against men already buried in the first two books and men unknown to him now because the room had outlived its keepers.
No single master at the back of it. No institution to blame. Just years of small theft, hidden storage, crooked runs, and men who believed dead country kept better books than towns did.
Ruth looked from the ledgers to Jed. "What is it."
Jed set the ledger back on the shelf. "A relay cache. Letters, ledgers, hidden goods. Old work from dead men."
Vale's hand went to his coat without him noticing. "Dead men leave profitable paper."
Jed looked over at that. "No."
"No what."
"No one owns this."
Vale laughed once, not kindly. "You found a room full of proof and freight and your answer is no one owns it."
"That is the only clean answer in here."
Ruth looked from Jed to the ledgers. "If those names go back out on the road, they make new Lasks."
Wren kept his eyes on the doorway. "And new excuses for old men."
Caleb slid down the wall until he could sit. "You burned the bill of lading and still came here. That means you never meant to keep it."
Jed set the ledger back on the shelf. "Now you are thinking."
Outside, boot grit clicked on stone.
Draven had reached the wrong side of the approach and was correcting that fact. Not running. Just coming.
Clementine's voice came in low from the threshold. "We do not have long."
Jed took one last look through the room.
Proof. Names. Goods. Enough for a greedy man to call it salvation and for an honest one to call it rot preserved too long.
He picked up the nearest ledger again, weighed it once, and chose the room's end then and there.

Chapter 24: Fire in the Relay
Coat buttons snapped under Vale's hands.
It happened fast once the room's worth had been named aloud. Vale moved for the shelf and the box at the same time, one hand shoving ledger bundles under his coat, the other grabbing at Ruth's arm as if she were just another carry piece he could manage on the way out.
Ruth hit him with the box before he got a clean hold.
The iron corner caught his cheek and tore skin open to the ear. He cursed, came back harder, and drove her against the wall with one forearm across the throat. Papers slid half into his coat and half to the floor.
Wren brought the rifle up and found Ruth in the way. "Leave it."
Vale got Ruth in front of him before the rifle could settle. "I am leaving with all of it."
Jed had no more time left for talk.
Wren shifted his footing for a shot and then had to give the line up again when Ruth's shoulder filled it. Caleb dragged the revolver free but kept the muzzle low because one bad shot inside stone would hit the wrong body before it found the right one. Clementine took half a step toward the opening and stopped there, already choosing between Vale and whatever shape Draven would take once smoke made courage cheap.
Match head snapped and flared.
He struck it against the stone and put it to the loose letter bundle on the shelf where oil stain had already darkened the paper. Fire caught small, then wider. One old ledger went black at the corners before Vale even understood what he had seen.
Vale saw the fire take the paper and bared his teeth. "You fool."
Smoke rolled low under the stone roof.
Draven came through it with the knife already in hand.
No speech. No warning. Only one close shape out of gray smoke and paper ash, going straight for Jed because Jed was the only part of the room that ever mattered.
Jed caught the wrist with his left hand, took the first cut across the bad forearm instead of the throat, and drove them both into the shelf hard enough to bring one crate down across their shoulders. The room shrank to stone, heat, and the breath of another man close enough to kill with if either one slipped.
Outside the doorway Wren shifted for a shot and could not take it. Clementine moved on the threshold, looking for Vale through Ruth instead of through smoke. Caleb dragged himself upright with one hand on the wall and one on the revolver he should probably never have trusted.
Vale pulled Ruth toward the opening and stuffed the last ledger inside his coat with his free hand.
Vale dragged Ruth toward the opening. "Move."
She stamped the top of his foot with her heel. He jerked. Not enough to free her. Enough to ruin the smoothness.
Boot heels hammered the threshold stone.
Clementine fired once.
The shot took Vale high in the chest as he broke into the light with Ruth still half across him. He hit the threshold sideways and dragged her down under him before the box spilled from her hands and clanged against the outer stone.
Inside, Draven kneed Jed in the thigh brace hard enough to numb the whole leg and tear the hold loose. Jed gave ground one step and no more. Smoke thickened. Fire took the next bundle and climbed into the warped crate lid behind it. Draven came again with the knife low, aiming for the part of a man that still wants to stand after a leg goes bad.
Jed did not meet him square. He turned into the rush, took the shoulder, and drove them both through the doorway where stone gave way to open air and one clean drop to the side. Draven slashed once across his ribs and lost the knife when the hilt struck the threshold lip.
They hit the stone outside.
Draven rolled first. He was better at that kind of fall and had been better too long. He came up on one knee with one hand already searching for the dropped blade.
Jed found it first with his boot and kicked it clear.
Then he went in close. At that distance there was no patience left in either man, only bone, breath, and whoever had more strength still in him.
They hit each other without style.
Elbow. Forehead. Knee to the bad leg. Hand to the throat.
Draven got one thumb into the old forearm cut and nearly opened Jed there by pain alone. Jed answered by driving the heel of his palm into Draven's broken-cold cheek where old damage still lived under the skin. The second strike made the eyes water. The third put Draven back against the threshold stone.
That was enough space.
Jed took the knife from the ground and drove it in under the ribs while Draven was still trying to rise.
The man stiffened once, looked down as if surprised only by the exact place, and then sagged against the threshold with the smoke of Stone Relay rolling over both of them.
Inside, fire had the shelves now. Paper flared and collapsed in sheets. One tin box burst its latch with a dry pop and spilled old cartridges into the heat. Wren hauled Ruth clear of Vale's body. Caleb dropped to one knee beside the box and pushed it out into the open with both hands. Nemi stamped one little flame that had run for the wagon blanket before it could grow.
Smoke rolled out of the room in one hard breath and carried the stink of old glue, oilcloth, and paper that should have rotted years earlier. No one tried to save another ledger after that. The fire had already made the choice for them and was doing it more honestly than any living hand could have done. Ruth stood with one hand on the box and watched the doorway until the last hope of salvage burned out with the shelves. Behind her, Caleb coughed smoke into his sleeve and Nemi kept stamping sparks dead before the dry grass could learn the same lesson too late. The mountain took the rest. Smoke climbed straight in the cold air above them there that morning. The stone held heat longer than the men did. The ash settled on stone.
Vale lay on his back with the coat full of half-burned paper and no future left in him at all.
Draven died where the room opened to air.
Stone Relay burned because there was no one left alive who could carry it clean.

Epilogue: No Road Back
Wheel spoke ticked under morning frost.
The wagon stood in thin light below the burned face with smoke still lifting from the stone crack where the room had been. Nothing useful remained inside. One wall had held. The shelves had not. Paper was gone. Cloth gone. Ledger leather curled black and split where the fire had eaten through it.
Marrow lay in the wagon bed under a second blanket now. Vale lay apart under stones because no one wanted him near the driver. Draven had been left at the threshold after the knife came out and the breathing stopped. The mountain could keep him.
Ruth checked the team first. Wren checked the wheel lashings. Caleb moved slow but moved. Nemi said almost nothing and still managed to do more work than any two tired adults among them.
They would take Marrow down toward Spur and bury him where the ground did not fight every inch.
Ruth checked the box once and then wrapped it back up without opening it. Caleb watched that and looked away before she caught him staring. Wren tightened the wheel lashings himself and then checked them a second time, not because they needed it but because tired men sometimes need one certain task before they can start a day. Nemi found a strip of level road for the first descent and set the lead pair on it with the same quiet hand Marrow had once used. Nothing in camp looked repaired. It only looked ready to move because staying had no use left in it.
Ruth climbed to the seat and gathered the lines with no show made of it. The choice passed without debate because there was no one left to contest it and because the team had already started listening to her voice. Wren took the first place on foot beside the wheel. Caleb lifted himself to the rear board with a wince he tried to hide. Nemi walked ahead where the narrow track first bent south, testing each patch of frost with the stick before the wagon reached it.
Saddle leather creaked when Clementine came alongside Jed.
He stood above the burned opening with one hand on the brace strap and the other hanging loose by his side as if the knife fight had spent every unnecessary motion in him. She looked at the smoke once, then at him.
"What burned in there."
Jed took a little time before answering. "Enough."
That was not the whole truth. It was the true piece worth saying.
Clementine looked past him toward the stone crack. "And what did not."
"The part that rides in memory till it dies with the rider."
She accepted that because she knew him well enough to hear what had been said and what had been refused.
Below them Ruth climbed to the wagon seat. Wren handed up the rifle. Caleb took the rear board with less complaint than pain would have excused. Nemi went to the lead pair and put one hand near the cheek of the near horse the way Marrow had taught him without ever saying he was teaching.
Road keeps teaching after the teacher is gone.
Clementine looked at the wagon, then back to Jed. "You heading south."
"For the first mile."
"Same here."
That was as close to an agreement as either of them had ever trusted.
She studied his face one moment longer. "Leg hold."
"Long enough."
"That was not what I asked."
Jed adjusted the brace strap once. "Then yes."
Clementine gave one short nod and did not soften the look around it. Care had always worn its hard face first with her.
They rode out with the wagon at first light and kept one line together where the track allowed it. Frost broke under hoof and wheel. No one tried to name what came next because naming too much had done enough harm for one book.
Spur did not show at once. The road dropped by careful turns, then straightened through a wash where stone remembered last season's water better than the men crossing it did. Smoke from the burned room thinned behind them until even that mark was gone. What remained was the creak of weight, the ring of bit chains, and the steady proof that the living had chosen motion again because the dead ask for burial, not witness.
At the first fork worth the word, the wagon kept the Spur road and Jed and Clementine stayed with it for a little longer, then less, then only by sight.
Ruth never looked back from the seat. Wren did once and lifted two fingers from the rifle stock in something too small for farewell and too deliberate for accident. Caleb kept both hands on the rear board. Nemi turned all the way around only after the wagon had taken the bend and the riders had become two dark marks on the pale road.
Jed and Clementine kept the same pace a little longer after the wagon disappeared because neither had much taste for making the split dramatic. Her mare snorted first. His gelding answered a beat later. Then the track widened enough for choice to show itself plain.
Clementine looked west once where the weather sat thin over low country. "You will not keep south."
"No."
"Good. It would fit you poorly."
Jed almost smiled at that and let it die before it became a thing requiring answer. He looked north instead, where the higher country waited beyond sight and beyond any promise a rider could trust.
Clementine settled her reins. "If Lask lives through the week, I may cross him again."
"If he does, he will cross you poorer."
"That sounds close to hope."
"It is close to arithmetic."
That earned the small breath she sometimes spent where other people used laughter. It was enough of one to keep.
Then the parting became final in the only way roads know.
No promise passed between them.
No road back did either.
For the first mile, that was enough.
