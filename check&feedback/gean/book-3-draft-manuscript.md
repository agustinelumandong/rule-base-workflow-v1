Chapter 1: North from Ghostpine
The stirrup leather creaked when Jed hauled himself into the saddle.
The right leg took the mount hard that morning. He caught the horn, dragged the bad side over, and settled before the brace could slip under him. Below the yard the grave sat on the north side of Ghostpine, packed flat and marked by two stones. Elkheart had laid them there after the burial and left them plain.
Jed looked once at the mound.
That was all the waiting he had.
Elkheart came from the lodge side and stopped by the horse's shoulder. The old man wore the same mended coat he had worn at the burial. Nothing in his face asked Jed to stay.
"North road is not empty."
Jed kept one hand on the reins. "Never was."
"Not like before."
Ghostpine had already gone back to its own work. Smoke lifted from one roof. A man checked a wagon wheel by the fence. Somebody carried water between cabins. No place on that slope meant to stop moving for long over one death or another.
Jed said, "I know what rides behind me."
Elkheart shook his head once. "You know one man. Road may hold more."
Jed looked north through the trees. Past Frostfang lay the country Ketch had named with his last clear strength. Stone room. East face. Second ridgeline past Frostfang. Four days north of Spur.
No paper held it now. The words lived in one head only.
"All the better reason to ride alone."
"Alone is not the same as hidden."
The brace pressed a sore place along Jed's calf where the strap buckle had rubbed it raw the day before. He shifted once and let the ache settle where it belonged.
"If I take another rider, that rider learns where I turn."
"If you take no rider, road still learns you."
The horse blew steam through its nose.
Elkheart laid one hand on the cheekpiece and held the space a little longer. "Boy named Nemi went east yesterday. To kin near Spur. If road shows you that boy, hear him before you refuse him."
Jed said nothing.
Elkheart let the bridle go. "Ketch knew old crossings. Not all of them died with him."
That carried more weight than the rest. Ketch had taken the wider trail into the ground and left only enough behind to make this ride necessary.
Jed settled the reins. "I hear what needs hearing."
Elkheart nodded once. "Then go before Ghostpine starts looking like a place to stay."
Jed touched heel to the horse and took the north track out.
The first climb ran steep through black timber. Pine roots crossed under a crust of old snow and the horse had to pick among them. Jed sat light where he could and spared the right leg when the footing narrowed. Below him Ghostpine shrank between the trees. Then the track bent and the settlement was gone.
He rode an hour with nothing but leather, bit ring, wind in pine, and the horse under him.
No fresh rider showed at first.
At a shelf above a frozen run he turned in the saddle and checked his back trail. One horse. One rider. Nothing moving in the timber behind him.
The narrow part beyond forced him down to lead the horse. Ice held along the shaded bank and the current below had only a skin of freeze on it. Midway through that passage he found sign.
The edge of the run had been chipped where another horse reached for water. Four pieces of broken ice lay on the bank, fresh enough to keep their corners. Nearby droppings had not yet weathered flat. One horse had stood there by noon yesterday and gone north after.
On the far side a spruce branch had been brushed wrong.
Jed led his horse on until the shelf widened and mounted again.
By midmorning the track split. One line turned west toward hunting cabins. Jed took the upper road without slowing. Ketch had given him Frostfang, Spur, second ridgeline, east face. Enough if a man held it plain and did not start decorating it with guesswork.
He ate in the saddle. Dried meat. Bread heel. Cold water that tasted of tin. The horse got grain later.
By afternoon the timber thinned and the country opened into harsher ground. Old snow clung in seams. Dead grass showed between black patches of thaw. A broken stone line rose ahead and cut the sky.
He knew that kind of country. It gave distance and took away cover.
Halfway across the first open rise he drew up and listened.
No sound came back. No second bit. No hammer on stone. No crow noise over carrion. Only wind.
That did not quiet him.
The road Elkheart warned him about was not the sort that always announced itself with a rider in sight. Sometimes it worked through what had been left behind. A fire scar where none should be. A cut trace in mud. A body. A name carried by the wrong man.
Late in the day he found shelter where two stone slabs leaned together against the wind. Grass showed dry underneath. Enough for the horse and one mean fire.
He unsaddled with the stiffness of a man who had carried too much of himself in one posture. The right leg argued when he bent. He rubbed the horse down with burlap, checked the near fore for heat, and fed a careful portion of grain.
Fire took on the third strike. He kept it small. Enough for coffee. Enough to warm his hands.
No map or paper rode in his bedroll. No map. No paper. Ketch's words stayed where they belonged because any fool with a blade could search a dead pocket.
He sat with the cup between both hands and looked south through the gap in the stones.
At first the mark on the slope looked like another thaw seam.
Then it moved.
Horse and rider. Too far for a face. Near enough to know the rider had not come into that country blind. The figure crossed below the ridge and vanished into a fold of land before Jed could judge more than that there was one of them and that the rider knew how to use distance.
Jed set the cup down without a sound.
He fed dirt over the fire until no light showed and stepped outside into the dark. The shoulder stood empty now. The open ground held still.
That did not mean he had been mistaken.
He went back under the stone, loosened the knife at his belt, and lay down with the reins wrapped once around his wrist.
The wind worked the ridge through the dark hours. Once the horse lifted its head and listened. Once Jed did the same.
No one came into the shelter.
Still, when the first gray touched the east, he was awake before it and already listening for what the road meant to send next.

Chapter 2: South Trail Sign
Ash stung the air before Clementine saw the dead fire.
The smell sat low among the cottonwoods by the creek. Wet charcoal. Old grease. Leather left open too long. She brought the gray mare in under the first branches and listened before going farther.
The creek moved over stones. A crow worked somewhere upstream. Nothing else.
She led the mare the last stretch by hand. The ground told enough before she reached the ring. Whoever used that bend had left in haste or under force.
The fire lay in kicked mud and drowned coals. A blanket scrap had burned at one edge and been trampled out. A coffee pot sat dented in the grass. Two horses had stood among the trees not long before. Bark showed fresh rub where they had been tied and one thong had been cut clean, not untied.
Clementine crouched and put two fingers in the ash.
Cold. A day old, maybe a little more.
She stood and turned through the rest of the place.
A man had bled there.
No body remained, but the dirt behind the fire had been soaked and then covered in a poor hurry. Ten feet off, a drag line ran toward the creek bank where heels had caved the earth under weight.
They had carried the wounded man out.
At the water's edge she found one full print in the slide. Narrow forefoot. Left heel worn harder on the outside than the right.
She knew the habit inside that boot.
Draven wore a left heel down that way when he had spent too long in the saddle and not enough on level ground. Old injury. Never enough to slow him, only enough to tilt the foot when he planted in a turn.
She studied the rest of the bank.
Three horses had watered there. One big stock horse. One lighter animal that had danced under the rein. The third had come in tired, with forefeet cutting short and close.
The hoof tracks left the bank on a slant that first pointed south. Clementine followed them thirty yards and stopped where the lead horse had turned.
The line bent back north.
She stood in that turn and let the shape of it settle.
Draven had not fled the story when Ghostpine broke behind him. He had looped south long enough to shake expectation, crossed some other camp or business, and then come back north on Jed's line.
She tied the mare where she could watch the open and read the camp a second time.
There were four boot kinds if a person stayed patient. One broad and blunt, likely the owner of the big horse. One city-cut pair with cleaner sole edges than the rest. One dragging step from the wounded man. And Draven's left-leaning heel.
The broad boots had built the fire and worked the pot. The city-cut boots had circled the outer edge of camp more than once. The wounded man had come in walking, though not well. Near the fire she found a square of shirt cloth stiff with dried blood.
Gut wound or lower side. Enough to weaken, not enough to drop him clean.
Under frost-killed sedge she found a broken spur strap. Cheap leather with the buckle torn free. Not Draven's. He never trusted bad leather past the first time it showed weakness.
That meant one of the others had come apart and stayed with him after.
Because Draven did not need loyalty. He needed use. A hurt man could still carry a message. A frightened one could still watch another man's fire.
Clementine crossed to the far side of camp and found the saddle thrown into brush. The tree was cracked on one side. One stirrup leather gone. Blood had dried dark into the fleece underneath.
Wounded rider. Changed mount.
At the edge of camp a willow twig had been split and twisted into a limb. Not accident. Not ornament.
Draven did that when he was made to wait.
She broke the twig free and dropped it.
Then she rode the rise above the creek bend, because the ground there was good for a man who wanted to watch a camp without joining it.
The climb proved her right. Grass had been pressed flat under the rise and a tobacco twist end lay by a stone where a boot heel had crushed it. The position gave a full view of the camp and the southern approach.
Not the northern one.
That mattered. Whoever watched from there expected trouble or company from the south.
Near the rise she found where the horses had left. One had gone first. Two followed. The fourth line came after a pause and cut across the others at a weaker angle.
Wounded man again.
By noon the track climbed out of creek country and onto rougher ground where the thaw had been kind to anyone reading sign. Draven's line never went straight for long. It skirted open shoulders, used fallen timber where it could, and crossed bare shelves whenever the ground offered them. He was not chasing blind. He was moving in a way that left him room to step aside and look.
Clementine kept below his pace, because haste spoiled more trails than weather.
Twice she dismounted where the riders had checked up. At the first she found more blood in the grass. At the second she found where a man had knelt beside another print and studied it.
Jed's.
One horse. One rider. Northbound. The mount and dismount marks dug deeper on the right side.
Draven had found that too.
The anger in her came up clean and cold. He had come back because he believed the road belonged to whoever could read fear best. He had lived too long on that without anyone taking it from him.
Midafternoon brought a wind change and with it the smell of river water. She slowed at once. If Draven meant to leave something behind for a following eye, he would do it near water where a person was already made to look down.
She found the sliver hanging from a thorn branch at shoulder height.
Fresh-cut wood. No longer than her little finger. One end shaved blunt. Three short notches near the middle. No trade mark she knew. Nothing meant for the world at large. Only a sign that he had stood there long enough to leave a thought behind.
She took it down and turned it once in her glove.
No blood. No second message.
Just the fact of it.
Below the thorn branch the bank showed where three horses had watered. Only three now. The tired animal was gone. In the reeds she found where it had been dragged aside after being turned loose or put down.
She mounted and looked north along the water.
Jed was ahead with Stone Relay in his head and nowhere else. Draven was on the same road with time enough to read him. Somewhere beyond them both the country narrowed toward Frostfang and the old route Ketch had died naming.
Clementine touched the gray mare forward.
"All right."
She spoke to the empty bank and the trail beyond it.
"Circle if you like."
The mare took the slope and climbed. Clementine did not look south again.

Chapter 3: Marrow's Wagon
The river came up through the trees before Jed saw the landing.
The sound had stone under it and too much force for comfort. When he rode clear of the cottonwood he found the track broken open at the bank.
The ferry rope had parted in the night.
One end still held on his side and dragged the current where the far length had snapped free. The ferry itself lay jammed downstream against the bank, deck canted and one corner buried in driftwood.
Above the landing sat the wagon.
It had once been a mail wagon or something near enough that the road no longer cared about the difference. Mud climbed to the hubs. The team stood patient under the pole and blew steam in the cold air.
One man waited by the near wheel with a spoke wrench in his hand.
He was broad through the shoulders though age had dried the rest of him down. His nose had been broken more than once. His hat looked as if winter had tried to finish it and failed.
He turned when Jed came clear.
"If you've come to sell me a ferry, you are a day too late."
Jed looked from the rope to the grounded deck. "Looks like more than a day."
"Looks worse than it is." The old man spat in the mud. "River's only ugly because it knows I need across."
Jed dismounted and walked to the bank. The current ran hard through a narrowed chute. Logs swung below the ferry and banged the buried corner whenever the water shoved them right.
Not crossable alone. Not with a horse. Not without wasting half a day hunting some other ford that might not exist.
He looked back at the wagon.
Five passengers watched him from under the canvas.
A woman sat nearest the tailgate with a small iron-bound box at her feet and one hand resting on it. Across from her sat a young man with a blanket wrapped over his middle and one arm held tight beneath it. Near the front bow leaned a black canvas case longer than a shotgun beside a lean man in a dark collar. On the forward bench sat another fellow in a coat too clean for the road. At the rear, half screened by a flour sack, sat a silent boy with a blanket over his shoulders.
The old driver jerked his thumb toward them. "Those are my troubles. River's the larger one."
"You got names for both?"
"Amos Marrow." He leaned on the wrench. "As for the rest, they can spend their own names if they think you've earned them."
Jed crouched by the rope post and touched the frayed line. Not rot. Hard strain after too much wet.
"When did it go?"
"Dark."
Jed stood and looked across. The far post still held. A cottonwood farther up would take an anchor if the post failed.
"You can pull the ferry free if you get a line high on the far side."
Marrow looked at him. "Can I."
"Question is whether you have enough hands to work it."
The clean-coated man stepped down into the mud with care that looked practiced.
"We have hands." He gave Jed a small town smile. "What we may lack is reason to trust any rider who comes out of timber and starts counting them."
Jed looked at him. "Then keep your doubt. It will not float the wagon."
The smile moved one corner of the man's mouth. "Martin Vale."
Jed gave no name back.
Marrow spat again. "If either of you means to help, help."
The woman rose with the box in both hands. It was small enough to carry and weighty enough to show in the effort.
"If the wagon stays here, we all spend another night on this bank." She looked at Jed. "Can the ferry be cleared?"
"Maybe. Need driftwood cut loose and a haul from the bank, not from the deck."
Marrow nodded toward the far side. "And a man over there."
That was the hard part.
Jed studied the width again. Twenty yards if judged straight, more in the current's language. He could make it stripped down with a line tied under his arms. The river would take strength out of the bad leg and perhaps more than that, but it could be done.
Behind him the wagon creaked as somebody shifted.
He turned and took the rest of them in.
The wounded young man watched too sharply. The preacher had a rifleman's hands despite the collar. The boy at the rear looked not at Jed now but past him, up the south road, as if he expected something there before the others did.
Jed said, "How far you bound?"
Marrow answered first. "Spur road."
That gave him pause. The wagon would be slower and louder than he preferred. It would also be one more shape on the road for Draven to divide his attention across if Draven had indeed come north again.
Marrow watched his face and read enough of it. "I need one more useful back than I presently own."
The wounded young man spoke for the first time. "Useful enough to swim that water?"
His voice carried fever.
Jed looked at the bandage dark under the blanket. "Can you keep your feet if trouble comes?"
"Long enough."
That answered enough for now.
Jed pulled off his gloves. "Need every rope you've got. Need the team in doubles on the bank. Need the driftwood cut free from the downstream corner before the haul."
Marrow narrowed his eyes. "You offering instruction or work?"
"Both. If I cross first."
The lean man in the collar rose. "I can help with the lines."
His voice came even.
Marrow grunted. "Good. That makes two hands with sense."
At the back of the wagon the silent boy had already climbed down with a coil of smaller rope over one shoulder. He came around the near wheel without a sound and stopped beside Marrow.
Jed looked at him a beat longer than the rest.
Elkheart's last words came back clean. If the road shows you that boy, hear him before you refuse him.
So there he was.
Jed stripped off coat and boots by the bank and tied the rope around his chest under the arms. The water looked like melted iron where it ran deepest. He flexed the right foot once in the mud, felt the old break answer in advance, then shut that part of him down as much as a man could.
Marrow came near enough to test the knot. "If it pulls you under, cut loose."
Jed held up the knife once. "I know."
He stepped into the river.
Cold took him at the waist and then everywhere. The current grabbed sideways at once. He kept one hand high on the rope and struck for the far bank at an upstream angle. The first full pull on the right leg came like a hammer through old bone. He kept the stroke even.
The river turned him halfway across. He let it do part of the work and fought only enough to save the line. A branch struck his shoulder and spun away. Then the far bank rose under his hands and he clawed into willow roots high enough to plant a knee.
He got up, looped the line twice around the far post, then ran it to the cottonwood for a second anchor and raised one arm.
Across the river Marrow shouted and the team leaned into the haul.
The rope went tight. Driftwood shifted below the ferry with a groan deep enough to feel through the post. Jed braced and held while ax blows rang from the near bank.
The buried corner jerked free. The ferry straightened in the current and came true again by degrees.
Through the spray he saw the party as separate burdens now.
Marrow at the line with both hands.
The preacher in mud up to the thigh hacking timber loose.
Vale keeping clear of the worst of the water while never losing track of where every other man stood.
The wounded youth trying to help and nearly folding from it.
The woman watching with the iron box behind her feet.
And Nemi moving toward the next trouble before any of the grown ones had named it.
By the time the ferry came back to the landing line, Jed knew he was not riding away alone that day.

Chapter 4: The Locked Box
The wheels groaned when Marrow hauled the wagon up the far bank.
They got the ferry true by noon and the wagon across by afternoon, but the river took enough out of all of them that no one wasted speech on the climb. Jed's clothes had dried stiff on him and the right leg had not forgiven the swim. He kept a hand on the near wheel when the bank steepened and lent the wagon what strength he had left.
Marrow drove as if the river had insulted him.
"Move."
The team leaned.
"Move again."
They did.
At the top Marrow reined in under cottonwoods and climbed down. The passengers spilled out to stretch. The woman set her iron-bound box on a stump and rolled one shoulder. Vale examined mud on his cuffs as if it had no right being there. The preacher, Wren, went first to the horses with a bucket. The wounded fellow sat where he landed and held his middle until the shaking in his hands slowed.
Only the boy stayed still. Nemi stood by the wagon tongue and watched the south bank through the trees until the river hid it.
Marrow faced Jed with the reins in one hand. "You ride with us from here."
It was not quite a question.
"Until the road splits where I need it to."
"Good." Marrow nodded once. "Saves me the trouble of pretending I hired you."
The woman looked from one man to the other. "Then we should know the name of the man who dragged our crossing out of the river."
Jed did not care for that, but there was no use hiding it now. "Jed Thorne."
The wounded young man looked up fast.
"Heard that name."
Jed met his eyes. "Lots of names get heard."
The fellow thought better of whatever came next.
The woman said, "Ruth Bell."
Her hand went back to the box as she spoke the last name.
Wren tipped his head once. "Ansel Wren."
Vale gave the same easy smile as before. "Martin Vale."
The wounded man got up too quickly, cursed once when the motion caught him, and straightened. "Caleb Pike."
Marrow jerked a thumb toward the rear wheel. "And the boy is Nemi, though he spends names the way rich men spend lamp oil."
Nemi said nothing.
Jed looked once at the box. Small enough for one arm. Weight enough to matter.
Ruth saw it and turned the latch side away. "Whatever is in it is not yours."
"Did not say it was."
"You did not have to."
Marrow cut in before that line could sour further. "Enough. We make another three miles before dark or we wake in low ground with swollen axles."
The road north from the ferry ran soft and ugly. Jed rode the near flank where he could see ahead and back at once. Nemi walked beside the off lead horse through the worst patches. Wren relieved Marrow at the lines when the old man climbed down to check a heating hub.
Ruth rode inside with the box between her boots.
Caleb tried walking once and nearly folded from it. Jed said nothing. He only watched the young man climb back in with his face gone white.
Toward evening the land opened into willow flats cut by low rises. They had just topped one when Nemi stopped and looked hard south.
Jed turned in the saddle.
Three riders stood on the far lip of the road behind them.
They had not been there on the last rise.
Marrow saw them a beat later and drew the team up. Wren came off the bench with one hand already on the canvas case. Vale went still where he sat. Ruth pulled the box close against her skirts.
The riders came down at a walk that made its own threat.
Caleb muttered, "Hell."
Jed looked over. "Know them?"
"No."
Poor lie.
The lead rider raised an arm when they came within shouting distance.
"Mrs. Bell."
Ruth shut her eyes once, then opened them again with only anger left in the face.
The lead rider wore a red scarf at his throat and had a jaw that healed crooked. The two men with him were cheaper sorts in cheaper coats, but each held a gun laid easy across the thigh and knew how to use it.
Marrow spat over the wheel. "Road's wide. Use another piece of it."
The long-jawed rider smiled a little. "I aim to. Soon as the widow gives back what came off my money."
Ruth stepped down from the wagon before anyone could stop her. The box stayed in her hands.
"Your money? You put your hand to it same as he did. You only hate that he died first."
The smile left the rider. "Open it."
"No."
Wren had the rifle case unbuckled now.
Jed let his horse edge one step forward and kept his hand near the butt at his belt.
The rider looked across them and judged what stood in his way. "Name's Hobb Lask. I came for a small box. Hand it over and I will count myself civilized."
Marrow barked a laugh. "Three men and you start talking like a mayor."
Lask's eyes moved to Jed and took in the brace and the road mud. "And who might you be."
"Nobody that opens boxes for strangers."
Lask's mouth thinned. "Then you are one of the hired kind."
Ruth said, "He is no such thing."
Jed wished she had kept still.
Lask heard the same thing he did. She had a reason to care how he was counted.
He nodded toward the box. "Your husband ran goods through country he did not own. Coin and names besides. Some of that belonged to me."
"No honest thing belongs to you."
"Did not claim it was honest."
The riders spread just enough to say one could start a fight easy.
Jed read the ground. Shallow ditch to the right. Willow cover beyond. Lask had seen it too and placed his right-hand rider with that in mind.
Jed said, "If you mean to shoot, do it now."
Lask looked at him. "You in a hurry to die, brace-leg?"
"Just don't care for speeches."
The left rider lifted his carbine a shade.
That was the first true move.
Wren drew the rifle from the case as smooth as a clerk opening a drawer.
Marrow dropped behind the near wheel and came up with a scattergun from under the seat.
Vale flattened himself against the wagon boards with no ceremony.
Caleb tried to get clear and nearly pitched over from the pain in his middle.
Ruth did the only thing no one expected. She pitched the box under the wagon.
Lask opened his mouth on a hard word and the left rider fired low for the spokes.
The shot snapped through wood.
Jed's horse jumped sideways at the sound. He came across the saddle with the motion and cleared leather before the animal finished shying. His first shot took the left rider high in the shoulder. Wren's rifle broke near the same breath and the right-hand rider dropped backward over his horse's rump.
Lask fired from the center and hit nothing that breathed. The ball smashed through the wagon bow above Vale's head and left the clean-coated man yelling more from surprise than damage.
Marrow's scattergun answered and turned Lask's horse broadside with blood across its neck.
Lask kicked clear and hit the ditch on his feet, then rolled into willow cover before Jed could line a second shot.
"Down."
Jed came off the saddle on the far side with the word.
Too late for Caleb. The wounded man had made it one step from the wagon when the motion tore him open again. He folded into the road on both knees with one hand pressed to his side.
Ruth crawled under the wagon for the box.
Vale cursed her and kept himself small.
Jed went to the ditch line and listened. Willow stems shook once and stopped. One loose horse was already running south. No second shot came.
Wren moved up on his left. "He gone?"
"Not far."
Marrow reloaded with hands steady from old use. "If he was wise he'd keep going."
Jed looked at the dark track through the ditch grass. "No."
Men did not come that open and talk that plain over a box unless the want ran deep.
They found Lask's blood in the ditch a yard farther on. Bright and not much of it. Enough to sting. Not enough to stop him.
Ruth came out from under the wagon with the box in both arms and climbed back in after it as if the fight had proved only that she had been right to trust nobody.
Jed knelt by Caleb in the road and pulled the coat back from the bandage. Dark wet spread fresh through the cloth.
"You were shot close."
Caleb managed something that wanted to be a grin. "You got good eyes."
Powder burn showed at the wound edge just as Jed had guessed.
"By who."
Caleb's face locked shut. "Road business."
Jed set pressure on the bandage until the young man hissed. "Whose road."
Caleb shut his eyes.
"Draven's?"
That got them open again.
Not enough for law. Enough for the trail.
Wren crouched opposite and passed him boiled cloth from the case. "He will die quicker if he keeps standing."
"Then he rides." Marrow stood over them. "And if Lask circles with more men behind him, we all keep riding."
Jed bound the wound tighter while Caleb bit the inside of his lip and bled there instead of crying out.
By the time the wagon moved again, the sun had gone low through the willows. They left two dead riders behind them and Hobb Lask somewhere south with hurt enough to sharpen him.
No one in the wagon asked whether he would return.
They all knew it.

Chapter 5: Caleb's Wound
Marrow drove into dark because he trusted the road less than he trusted what waited off it.
By full night they found a low cut between two stone-backed rises where the wagon could be set nose-out toward the north track. It was poor camp in every way except one. A man coming in from the south had to show himself before reaching the fire.
That made it wealth.
They unhitched by lantern light. Wren built the fire low and screened it with the wagon body. Vale complained once about the ground and got nothing for it. Ruth kept the box inside the wagon until Marrow told her plain that if the wagon burned she could burn with it or come sit where the rest of the breathing people sat.
She chose the fire.
Caleb did not choose anything. Jed and Wren got him down from the tailboard together and laid him on a blanket near the lee wheel. The young man's face had gone gray. Sweat stood on his upper lip though the night was cold.
Jed cut the bandage loose.
The wound smelled wrong at once.
Not rot yet, but close enough to promise it. Powder black ringed the entry low on the belly where the shot had gone in at near touch range. The flesh around it had swollen hot. The bleeding had slowed only because Caleb had less left to lose.
Wren handed over hot water, clean cloth, and a bottle of raw spirits. "He can take a swallow."
"I need him still."
Jed poured the spirits over the wound.
Caleb arched off the blanket and nearly struck him. Wren caught the wrist and pinned it.
"Hold him."
The preacher did.
Marrow crouched near enough to see. Ruth looked away, then back. Vale stood at the edge of the firelight where road filth had finally ruined the clean line of him. Nemi sat on a feed bucket in the dark edge and watched the south opening between the rises as steadily as if the rest were a separate camp.
Jed worked the wound plain. No bullet remained in it. Caleb had luck in that one hard way and none in the rest.
"Who bound this first."
"Me."
"Who shot you."
Caleb's eyes fixed on him. "Asked that already."
"I am asking again."
He looked from one face to the next and found none soft enough to take shelter in. Marrow solved the matter in his own fashion.
"If the boy won't answer, leave the wound open and let him rot through by degrees. He can tell it to the flies."
Ruth said, "Amos."
"What. You got a gentler use for the truth."
Wren said nothing. Jed respected that.
Caleb swallowed. "South of Bitter Hollow. Camp cook took a beating off a man who believed he could. I told him stop."
Jed tied fresh cloth over the wound and waited.
"He didn't like that."
"Who."
Caleb shut his mouth.
Jed leaned more weight into the bandage.
"Draven."
The name sat by the fire and made the camp smaller.
Vale looked up sharply. Ruth's hands drew tighter around the iron box. Marrow spat into the dirt. Wren only kept holding Caleb steady.
Jed knotted the bandage clean. "You rode with him."
"Three weeks. No more than that."
"Three weeks long enough to know better."
"I know that now."
Jed sat back on his heels. A deserter from Draven's company told more than rumor if he lived long enough to stay ashamed.
"How many with him now."
Caleb shook his head. "Didn't stay to count after he put me down."
Jed believed that part.
Wren released the arm and passed Caleb a folded strip of cloth. "Bite that if the fever climbs."
Marrow stood and cracked his knees straight. "If Draven is on our trail, I want watches doubled."
"No."
Marrow looked down. "No."
"Staggered. One man in the south gap. One sleeping while the first watches. Trade every two hours. Doubled means more boots and more noise."
Marrow considered it and nodded once. "Fine."
Wren said, "I will take first watch."
"Second. You just spent your hands here."
The preacher gave him a plain look. "And you swam a river today."
"Still saying second."
Marrow cut the tie with a grunt. "I take first. He takes second. You take third. Vale takes fourth if dawn has not come. And if he walks off from that duty I leave him tied to the road for Lask or Draven, whichever arrives first."
Vale spread his hands. "You speak to me as though I dislike danger."
"I speak to you as though I know card men."
That settled the watches.
Ruth waited until the others drifted off before she came near Jed. The fire had gone low enough that both of them stood in half dark. She kept the box under one arm.
"He will come again."
Jed looked to the south opening where Nemi still watched. "Lask."
"Yes."
"Then tell me what he thinks sits in that box worth dying for."
Ruth took too long with the answer for the truth to be simple. "My husband ran goods he should not have touched. Coin from it. Names tied to it. Promises written down where no promise should be trusted. He died leaving other men to sort the account."
"Lask one of them."
"Lask thinks himself the only one that matters."
Jed let his eyes drop to the box and back. "If it gets the rest of us killed, I throw it in the first deep river we reach."
Ruth met that without flinching. "If it comes to that, I will save you the throw."
She went back to her blanket with the box under her head like a pillow no one else was meant to borrow.
Jed took third watch.
The hours before it passed in scraps of sleep and the low noises of road camp: horses shifting, Caleb muttering once in fever, Wren's boots in the dirt as he traded places with Marrow, Vale turning more than a comfortable man needed to.
When Jed woke for his watch, frost had silvered the harness. He took his place at the south gap with his coat closed to the chin.
The night was clear enough to hold shape and dark enough to lie.
He listened to the ground first. Wind ran high above the cut and did not trouble the floor where the wagon stood. Somewhere east an owl gave one low note and then nothing. The horses breathed behind him with the trust of animals too tired to think on danger unless it touched them direct.
He looked back once at the sleeping shapes.
Marrow with one hand near the scattergun.
Wren on his back like a man who woke fast.
Vale hedge-betting even in sleep.
Ruth wrapped hard around the box.
Caleb breathing thin but regular.
Nemi was not where he should have been.
Jed turned at once.
"Up here," the boy said from his left.
Nemi sat halfway up the stone sidewall above camp, looking not south but west toward the ridge line.
Jed climbed to him. "You move quiet for a warning."
Nemi pointed.
At first Jed saw nothing. Then the ridge shaped itself.
A small light moved there. Then another.
Not campfire. Not stars. Men carrying hooded lanterns or banked coals through the dark. More than one, fewer than a dozen, and traveling across the far ground where the cut would not hide them long.
Jed watched until the lights vanished behind stone.
"You know whose."
Nemi looked at him, pale in the frost dark. "Men who travel with hurt and anger make bad fire tomorrow."
It was the most the boy had said yet.
Jed took it for enough.

Chapter 6: Draven's First Mark
Draven came to the ferry at midmorning and found the landing empty except for broken rope, blood in the mud, and the river still talking to itself over the ruined deck.
He stayed in the saddle the first minute and took what distance gave him for free.
One wagon across.
One team of four.
At least six travelers from the layering of prints.
Trouble on the south bank before crossing. Two places where men had gone down. One horse bled and been led off afterward.
He dismounted only when the larger picture had settled.
The water had spoiled some edges, but not the meaning. He crouched by the rope post and touched the frayed line. Not cut by blade. Overpulled. Somebody on the far side had anchored a replacement and walked the ferry loose while the team hauled from above.
Smart enough work if a person had hands.
He had crossed by an upstream ford an hour earlier and come down from above so the landing could speak to him in order.
Now it did.
He found Jed's sign first because he expected it.
The mount mark was deeper on the right side where the man had taken the saddle after working strain into the old break. The dismount by the rope post showed the same favoring. Most men would have seen only another boot in thaw mud.
Draven smiled once with no warmth in it.
"Still northbound."
He followed the line down the bank where Jed had entered the water. Clothes stripped. Rope tied high. Good angle into current. A man who knew exactly what sort of pain he could afford and spent it like coin.
That was Jed plain enough.
Draven stood and read the rest of the landing.
The wagon crowd interested him less, but only less. A road secret sat safest in one head when that head rode alone. Jed had not crossed alone. That meant weather, injury, or bad country had pushed him into company.
Company always made purchase for the right sort of pressure.
He walked the print field slowly.
Old driver. One woman used to carrying weight. One sick or wounded man. One preacher or clerk until the firing stance in the mud changed that label to rifleman. One polished fellow who kept out of the deepest mud where he could. And one boy with light tread and no wasted motion.
The blood on the south bank came from two men, perhaps three. Hobb Lask's kind of work if blunder could still be smelled from mud. Draven had seen that breed in every territory worth riding. Small kings of poor road.
He took no issue with them unless they blocked what he needed.
At the willow edge he found where someone had stood watch after the crossing. Not Jed. The boot there turned in on one side and out on the other, a habit of fever or gut guarding.
The wounded one.
That narrowed it.
He led his horse through the grass to the cut where the wagon had camped and found cold ash, blood on a blanket scrap, the smell of sickness and spirits, and enough boot traffic to say the group had slept uneasy.
He crouched where Caleb Pike had bled into the dirt and touched the edge of the stain.
Still wet deep down.
So the boy had lived.
That narrowed it further.
Three weeks under him and then gone meant not many faces. Caleb Pike had joined up south of Bitter Hollow on a horse too good for his habits and a lie too poor for his mouth. He had lasted long enough to object to necessary work and catch a bullet for it. Draven had expected wolves or fever to finish what he started.
Instead the road had brought the boy back into his weather.
He laughed once, quiet.
"There you are."
Jed had picked up a stray without knowing he was taking one of Draven's old camp hands into company.
He walked the camp edge until he found where Caleb had slipped off in the dark to kneel by himself. Fear kept private. Shame too.
Draven knew both looks well enough.
From there he followed the wagon north at a steady pace that spared the horse without wasting the day. No need to crowd them. If Jed had taken wagon company, Jed had taken wagon speed.
By noon he found where the wagon had stopped to let Caleb climb back in. Fresh blood there. Cloth fibers in the mud where the preacher had tied a new binding.
Draven studied the road shoulder from that point.
Jed had ranged while the wagon waited. Twenty paces west. Thirty east. Checking rise and back trail both. Protection out of habit, not fear. Men afraid moved differently. They oversaw everything and read nothing. Jed still read.
Draven respected that in the narrow way one hunter respected another who made the work worth doing.
Respect had nothing to do with mercy.
At a fork where the higher road bent north, he reined in under scrub pine and took stock. He could keep shadowing the wagon and strike when weather or dark thinned their order.
Or he could begin sooner.
Pressure did not always come from lead. Often it came better from the idea of lead and the uncertainty of where it waited. A wagon party held by strangers took fright in layers. The old driver distrusted delay. The town man distrusted loss. The wounded man distrusted memory. The woman with the box distrusted being known. The preacher would distrust the moment he had to prove his hands honest by doing ugly work again.
And Jed, riding among them with that old broken leg and a place hidden in his head, would know every mile north made him harder to abandon and more dangerous to keep.
That was the kind of road Draven preferred.
He shaved a sliver from a dead cedar branch with his knife. Finger length. Blunt taper at one end. Three short notches near the middle. Nothing that named tribe or trade. Just enough shape to say a mind had paused over it.
He carried it another mile before choosing the place.
The road crossed a narrow wash there where spring water had chewed the bank raw and left clean mud. The wagon would have to slow. Jed would check the footing.
Draven pushed the sliver into the split of a thorn branch at shoulder height and stepped back.
Not hidden. Not obvious from wagon seat.
Perfect.
"Read that, then."
The light was going when he found his own camp under stone and juniper. He did not build fire at once. He picketed the horse, climbed to a rock shoulder above the road, and watched the north line until the last useable light drained out.
No wagon passed that stretch before dark.
Good.
Either Marrow had made poor miles or Jed had stopped them sooner than planned. Both helped.
He built a coal-sheltered fire then and ate cold meat with one hand while the other rested over his knee. Night settled clean. One coyote barked west. Farther off an owl worked the timber edge.
His mind went back to Caleb Pike.
The boy had not loved him, which Draven had never required. But he had watched well enough in those weeks to be dangerous if some kinder hand coaxed the fear out of him. Jed was not a kinder hand. The preacher might be. The widow might. Even the polished one, if he smelled advantage in it.
That meant the first mark had to be followed by the first talk.
Not tomorrow, perhaps. Maybe the day after if the ground offered it. Men like Caleb answered private summons after camp slept.
Draven banked the coals and stretched under his blanket with boots still on.
When morning came he saddled in frost and took the north road again at the same patient gait. By midday he would know whether Jed had seen the mark. By the next dark he meant to know whether Caleb Pike still feared him properly.

Chapter 7: One Road, Six Lies
Marrow kicked a wheel block into place with the heel of his boot.
The wagon had come into a narrow cut near dark, stone on both sides and just enough level ground at the center to hold team, fire, and sleeping bodies without forcing anyone into the brush. The south end opened only one mounted man wide. Bad ground for comfort. Good ground for knowing where trouble had to enter.
That was reason enough to stop.
Jed loosened the breast straps on the nearest horses and worked the harness in the order his body remembered even after the day had worn it thin. The right leg had gone to its evening stiffness, but the work still got done.
Marrow looked over the camp once it had taken shape.
"Nobody leaves the firelight alone."
He said it while driving the butt of the scattergun into the dirt by his blanket.
Vale glanced up from his sleeve. "That sounds less like a rule than a confession."
Marrow turned his head. "It can be both."
"If a man needs to piss."
"Then he pisses where a second man can hear him breathe."
Vale's smile came and went. "Your road manners remain singular."
"You have not seen the rest."
The fire sat low and mean in a ring of stones Wren set himself. Ruth kept the box within reach of her blanket because no one in camp was fool enough to pretend it did not matter. Caleb had the fever shine on him again, though the tighter binding held. Nemi sat near the cut wall and watched the south mouth as steadily as if the rest were a separate camp.
Jed took the first plate of beans where he stood. Wren passed it over with no question in his face. The preacher understood what silence bought and spent it carefully.
Vale waited until half the plate was gone.
"So. Are you a freight guard, a scout, or simply a man who enjoys cold rivers."
Jed scraped the spoon once against the plate bottom. "Depends who asks."
"I am asking."
"Then none of the three."
Vale nodded as if that pleased him. "Means I must keep guessing."
Marrow blew across his coffee. "You could also keep your mouth shut."
Ruth looked at the fire. "Leave him alone if you cannot improve him."
Vale turned that way at once. "Madam, I improve every camp I enter."
"No. You stir it."
Caleb laughed once before the motion punished him. Jed looked at him.
"Hold still."
"I was sitting still."
"Then keep from being clever."
That got a grunt from Marrow.
Vale spread both hands toward the fire. "I see I am among spotless company."
Jed's eyes went over at once.
Vale paused. "Bad word."
"Pointless one."
Vale studied him, then let it go.
Ruth broke the next quiet. "How far to Spur."
Marrow rubbed a thumb across his broken nose. "If the road stays road, four days. If the season remembers itself, six."
"And if your man from the river stays with us."
Marrow looked at Jed before answering. "Then maybe five."
Vale warmed again to the talk. "And after Spur. Do we part ways there, or do some of us ride toward the same hidden errand."
No one answered him.
The silence sharpened his interest instead of blunting it.
"A man who turns shy at the name of a destination usually travels toward one."
Jed said, "Most men travel toward one."
"True. Most men also name it when asked."
"Then travel with most men."
Vale laughed softly to himself. He had the kind of face that made strangers think no harm could be hiding in it. Jed had known that sort before. They asked questions because questions cost nothing. One answer in ten turned into a better stake than cards ever gave them.
Wren set another stick into the coals. "Best let the road itself name tomorrow."
Vale tipped an invisible hat. "Spoken like a man who keeps his own counsel."
"Spoken like a man tired from hauling your life through a river crossing."
That shut him for a minute.
Nemi spoke before anyone else filled the gap.
"There is a second trail above camp."
Every head but Caleb's turned toward him. The boy sat as before, looking not at the fire but at the slope above the east wall of the cut.
Marrow said, "Above this cut."
Nemi nodded.
"For what."
"A walking trail."
Marrow grunted. "Everything is for walking if you are poor enough."
Jed took the lantern. "Show me."
Nemi came up without another word. They climbed the east wall by a line only the boy seemed to see at first. The path was there once a man trusted the stone more than the dirt. Narrow, half broken, not wagon fit. A man on foot could use it to cross above camp and come down behind the south mouth without touching the road floor.
Jed crouched at the top and studied the ground.
Old sign only. No fresh men's tracks.
"How'd you catch it from below."
Nemi pointed to a scar in the stone where an old wagon hub had rubbed years before. "Moon hit there."
Jed looked down. From the right place it would have shown.
"Who taught you."
"My aunt. Elkheart. Whoever stayed alive."
That answered enough.
They climbed back down together. Marrow looked up from the fire.
"Well."
"If someone comes in on foot and means to take a high line, that is it."
Marrow looked sourer than before. "Good. Another mouth to watch."
"Better known than not."
Wren gave Nemi the last of the coffee without making a point of it. The boy took the cup in both hands and sat again where the fire barely reached him.
The watches set themselves after that. Marrow first, Jed second, Wren third, Vale fourth if nothing worse had broken before dawn. Caleb objected to being left out as if fever and a belly wound had made him proud.
"I can watch."
Jed shook his head. "You can leak."
Caleb lay back with his jaw set and said no more.
Vale took his blanket near the rear wheel but did not sleep at once. Jed could feel the man working the camp with his eyes, measuring bonds and openings.
After a time he spoke into the dim.
"Ruth."
She did not answer.
"If Lask catches us at daylight, which one of us do you suppose he shoots first."
"You, if God is tidy."
Even Marrow laughed at that.
Vale let it pass over him. "And if God is not on shift."
Ruth rolled over with the box under her arm. "Then you keep talking until a man here does the work for him."
Vale finally slept.
Jed woke for second watch to a sky gone low with cloud. Marrow traded him the scattergun without words and went down under his blanket at once.
Jed checked the south mouth first, then the upper trail, then the team. The dark held.
Near the middle of the watch a pebble clicked once on stone above him.
His hand went to the holster and stopped there.
"Me." Nemi's voice came down soft from the top path.
Jed eased his grip but did not lose it. "What."
"Fox."
"You saw it."
"I heard it miss."
Jed almost smiled in spite of himself. "Come down."
Nemi slid into the cut and stood close by with the blanket around his shoulders. "You do not trust the road."
"No."
"Good."
The boy said it with no praise in it. Simple fact.
"You trust it."
Nemi looked up toward the dark rim. "Enough to keep count."
They stood the rest of the watch mostly silent. By the time Jed woke Wren for third watch, he knew two things clean.
The cut would not hold them another night if the weather worsened.
And Martin Vale had not asked his last question about roads with names.

Chapter 8: Hobb Lask Comes In
The horse screamed before dawn.
Jed came up from sleep with the blanket half around his legs and the pistol already clear. The cry had come from the picket line on the south side of camp and ended in a wet choking snort that left no doubt what followed it.
Gunfire broke an instant later.
The first ball went through the wagon canvas. The second hit stone above the fire ring and sent chips across Marrow's blankets. The team surged against the traces where they stood half hitched for a fast start.
Jed hit the dirt and rolled behind the near wheel.
Marrow was already there with the scattergun. Wren came up on one knee beside the tailboard with the long rifle braced over it. Ruth had the box under her arm and nowhere yet to put it. Caleb fought his own blanket and the fever at the same time, trying to rise with one hand at his middle. Vale flattened along the wagon bed and swore. Nemi was gone from where he had slept.
Another shot came from the south mouth and tore through the feed sack hanging by the rear wheel.
"Stay low."
No one argued.
Marrow leaned out just enough to look and came back with anger in his beard. "Two on the road. Maybe one higher."
Jed heard the third before he saw him. Boot scrape above the east wall, then loose grit from the upper trail Nemi had shown them.
"High side."
Wren turned the rifle and fired into the dark above.
The flash lit the cut for one flat beat. In that white Jed saw Ruth dragging the box under the wagon, Caleb doubled over by the tailboard, Vale with his hands over his head, and Marrow already rising for his own shot.
The scattergun boomed.
A body went over the east lip and came down hard enough to fold one leg wrong.
Lask cursed from the road mouth. "Widow. I told you I'd take it back."
Ruth said nothing. She shoved the box farther under the axle and came up with a pistol from inside her coat.
Jed glanced at it once. "You know how."
"Enough."
That was all the time they had for honesty.
The dead horse lay twisted across half the south opening, shot through the throat. One of Marrow's team animals. Blood steamed black in the dirt. The kill had been chosen well. Block the mouth. Start the team. Make the cut feel smaller than it already was.
Lask knew road work after all.
Jed moved along the wheel rim and found his lanes.
One rider on the road shoulder using the dead horse for cover. Lask himself behind a low wash lip. The second road man shifting left with a carbine. The third had been the one sent over the high path.
"Wren. Left shoulder."
The preacher did not answer. He only leaned into the stock.
Jed came out low under the wagon tongue and fired at the carbine flash the instant it opened again. The man dropped behind the wash lip with a sound like wet hide struck with a stick. Wren's rifle cracked on top of it and the road shoulder went quiet.
Marrow seized the chance and ran for the team.
"Amos."
He never looked back. He cut the surviving horse free from the dead one's drag and hauled the lead pair sideways by the headstalls to keep them from tearing the wagon apart in panic.
Lask fired on him.
The ball cut Marrow's coat sleeve and took cloth only. Marrow kept hauling and cursed Lask by name, mother, and grave in one dry stream.
Jed used the shot. He came up over the wash edge and sent one round toward the voice. Dirt kicked near the red scarf. Lask dropped flat and rolled farther right than Jed expected. Quick man. Better than the last meeting had shown.
A smaller shape broke from the east wall and ran the upper trail above them.
Nemi.
The boy carried a stone in both hands. Before Jed could curse him back, Nemi threw it down into the wash beyond Lask's right. The rock clattered hard enough to mimic a scrambling man. Lask turned his head toward the false move.
Ruth shot him then.
The pistol cracked from under the wagon and the ball took Lask somewhere high in the arm or shoulder. He yelled and rolled downhill into brush beyond the wash.
The last road man chose that moment to leave the work to devils. His horse lunged south and was gone before Wren could chamber another round.
Silence hit all at once after that. Not peace. Just the hard pause after noise when every living thing decides whether to start again.
Jed listened.
No hoofbeats circling close. No brush rush toward camp. Only the scared blowing of the team and the last rough breath of the horse at the road mouth before that too stopped.
"Stay put."
He went first to the wash and looked over.
Blood on the lip. More in the brush beyond. Enough to say Ruth's shot had paid. Not enough to give them a body. Lask had gone out under his own will, which meant he would remember the lesson with a pulse still in him.
He followed the sign fifteen yards to where the brush broke into open stone and lost it there. Dawn had just begun to color the east. In that weak light he could see where a horse had been kept waiting beyond sight of camp.
Planned route out.
He came back to the cut.
Marrow stood with one hand on the near lead's cheek and the other braced on his own knee. Wren had the rifle reloaded and pointed at the south mouth. Caleb sat against the wheel with sweat running down his temples. Vale looked at the dead rider under the east wall as though bodies had arrived too fast for his taste. Ruth crawled out from beneath the wagon with the box tucked against her side and the pistol still in her hand.
Jed looked at her. "Enough."
She gave one short nod.
Marrow spat toward the road mouth. "Horse is done."
Jed went to the animal. It had taken the shot through the throat and bled out where it fell. No saving it. No moving the wagon until they cut the harness free and reset what remained.
The loss changed everything. Four team animals had become three. Every steep grade north would exact new payment.
Marrow knew it too. He stood over the body a moment, one hand on the mane, then bent to work the buckles loose.
No ceremony. Just labor.
Nemi came down off the east path and stopped by the fire ring.
Jed looked at him. "Do not do that again."
"It worked."
"That is not the point."
Nemi thought on that. "Then the point is old."
Jed let it go. There was no time to sort boy wisdom from boy stubbornness while blood was still wet.
Wren rolled the dead man by the east wall with a boot. No papers. Cheap cartridges. One knife. One saddle horse tied a little beyond the cut with fear in its eyes and no rider left to claim it.
"We can use that horse."
"For riding, not pulling."
"Then one of us rides and walks less."
Vale brightened at once. "A hopeful turn."
Marrow shot him a look. "Not for you."
They worked through first light. The dead team horse came out of harness piece by piece. The surviving animals had to be calmed, turned, and set in a tighter pull than they liked. Wren and Jed hauled the road clear while Marrow adjusted traces for the new imbalance. Caleb tried twice to help and nearly blacked out the second time. Ruth took the lighter work without being asked. Vale mostly avoided getting kicked.
When the sun cleared the east shoulder, the cut looked like a place that had survived instead of a place that had been struck.
Before they moved out, Marrow laid the flat of his hand once against the dead horse's neck.
"Paid for passage too."
Then he climbed to the bench.
The wagon rolled north with one less horse and a tighter turn to its wheels. Lask had not taken the box.
He had taken speed instead.
Draven, wherever he rode behind them, would see the missing team animal in a single glance and count what it meant.
Jed rode flank and kept his eyes on the back trail.
The road had grown narrower again.

Chapter 9: The Preacher's Case
Wren laid the canvas case across the wagon seat and unbuckled it.
They had stopped at noon in a stand of black pine where the road bent west around a granite shoulder. Marrow wanted the horses blown and watered after the bad dawn. Caleb needed the bandage checked again. Ruth needed nothing from anyone and said so. Vale wanted to know whether the dead rider's horse might suit him better than the wagon bench. Nobody granted the request.
Wren waited until the team had settled and the fire had not yet been lit.
Then he opened the case.
The rifle inside was long, dark, and cared for.
No preacher's prop. No old wall piece carried for history's sake. The stock bore handling marks worn smooth by years. The metal at the muzzle had been kept free of orange with a diligence near devotion. A strip of oiled cloth sat folded under the barrel. Spare cartridges rested in a leather sleeve sewn to the inside flap.
Marrow glanced over from the water bucket. "About time the sermon showed its spine."
Wren lifted the rifle free and checked the action. "You already saw it work."
"Saw enough to ask why you kept it under canvas."
Wren ran the cloth once down the barrel and looked at the oil it brought back. "Because men ask worse questions when they see a weapon first."
Vale laughed under his breath. "And how have they improved now that they see it second."
"Not much."
Jed watched from where he knelt by Caleb's blanket. The wound had held through the morning ride, though the fever still worked behind the young man's eyes. He retied the outer wrap and set the spare cloth aside before standing.
"You carry scripture too?"
Wren looked at the rifle a moment before answering. "In another pocket."
That almost passed for humor.
Ruth sat on the wagon tail and watched the same exchange with her hands resting on the box. Her face gave little, but the pistol she had used at dawn now rode under her coat where she could reach it again without crawling.
Marrow came nearer and put the bucket down. "You army."
"Was."
"Union."
Wren nodded once.
"Sharpshooter?"
"Sometimes." Wren set the rifle across his knees and began feeding rounds into the sleeve one by one. "Sometimes just the man who stayed alive after the first two choices failed."
No one spoke for a while after that. The pines held the wind above them and let only a resin smell down through camp. Somewhere off the road a jay raised trouble over nothing. The dead rider's horse, now tied with Marrow's team, kept testing the rope as if hoping the morning might still reverse itself.
Vale was the one who broke the pause.
"And after the war you found God."
Wren looked up. "No."
Vale waited.
"I found that killing did not end when uniforms came off."
The answer left no room for wit. Vale tried for some anyway and failed to locate it.
Jed took a cup of water from the bucket and drank. Wren's rifle told its own part of the man clean enough. Careful tool. Old use. No show in it. The same could not be said of many who wrapped violence in piety. This one seemed to know the difference between the two and hate that he had needed both.
Marrow squinted at the sky through the trees. "Can you put down a rider at two hundred."
"If he gives me the time."
"And at one hundred if he doesn't."
"Usually."
Marrow grunted satisfaction into his beard. "Good."
Caleb shifted on the blanket. "You were a real preacher too."
Wren looked over. "Still am."
"With that."
"With that."
Caleb seemed to weigh it, not because he doubted the statement, but because he did not yet know what to do with it. Men like Caleb often sorted the world into simpler drawers than it deserved. Road work usually fixed that if it did not kill them first.
Ruth said, "A man can carry more than one thing."
Vale leaned against the wheel. "And which thing tells the truth."
Ruth turned her head toward him. "Whichever one he reaches for first when he is pressed."
No one argued with that either.
Jed crouched by the fire ring and rebuilt it from cold ash and dry needles. The first spark caught, then another. He kept the flame low and fed it pencil sticks until it had enough of itself to live. Work near the ground always eased the leg some after long riding. Better than sitting did.
He looked up at Wren. "Who taught you that rifle."
"Men who died before the teaching was done."
"You hunt now."
"Sometimes."
"Men or deer."
Wren met his eyes. "Whichever one needs stopping."
Jed accepted that. It matched the hands.
Vale had not finished worrying the matter. "And the collar." He reached out and touched one finger to the dark cloth at his own throat, not Wren's. "You still wear it because you believe, or because people grant a preacher one extra beat before they decide to shoot him."
Wren slid the rifle back into the case halfway and stopped there. "I wear it because it is true."
"Truth does useful work."
"So does a shovel. You should find one."
Marrow barked a laugh. Ruth almost smiled and thought better of sharing it.
The road fed them again not long after. Beans, hard bread, coffee watered thin. They ate in a close ring while the horses cooled. Jed noticed Nemi watching Wren's hands more than the rifle itself, following how the man folded the cloth, checked the chamber, and closed the case with no wasted reach.
The boy said, "You do not hide it from yourself."
Wren looked at him. "No."
"Only from men who look first."
"That is right."
Nemi nodded once as if some old private account had balanced in his head.
When they moved again, the case no longer rode buried among bedding. Wren kept it upright by his knee on the bench where a quick hand could reach it. The change pleased Marrow and unsettled Vale. Jed counted both reactions useful.
The afternoon road cut through granite shelves and low timber where tracks held poorly. Good country for losing distance, poor country for trusting quiet. Jed rode ahead at one bend to read where the spring wash had eaten half the trail. The new horse they had taken from the dead rider tossed its head but followed lead without too much argument.
Behind him the wagon wheel slipped once on the rock edge and thudded back straight.
Marrow cursed the shelf.
Ruth cursed the wheel.
Vale cursed both in finer language.
By the time they cleared the stone and found a flatter pull, even Caleb managed a weak grin from the bench.
Wren did not grin. He scanned the timber at each stop as if some old camp lay hidden just beyond it and his memory knew the path.
Jed drew up beside the bench once the road widened. "You hear something."
"Not now."
"Then what."
Wren took a long look into the trees before answering. "Years teach a man that silence has kinds."
Jed waited.
"This kind has room in it."
That was well said and near enough true to matter. Jed had spent his life reading the same thing by other names. Open silence. Watched silence. Hungry silence. The country always told on itself if a man kept his own thoughts from filling the gaps too early.
"Lask won't hit again this day."
"No."
"Draven might."
Wren's fingers rested once on the case latch. "That is my count too."
They rode on.
Near dusk the road dropped into a wash with black water standing in holes and last year's reeds rattling at the edges. Jed crossed first and checked the far bank. No sign plain enough to name, but the mud had been touched upstream by something that knew where to place its feet.
He turned in the saddle.
Wren had the case unbuckled before the wagon reached the water.
Marrow noticed and said nothing.
That was as good as trust got on a road like this.

Chapter 10: Clementine at the Ferry
The mare's shoes clicked once on stone where the bank opened.
Clementine drew up above the landing and looked down at the broken ferry from the shelter of scrub pine. The river had dropped a little since the day before, but not enough to change its temper. The deck still lay canted against the snag line. The snapped guide rope dragged slower now where the current worried it along the drowned wheel.
What mattered stood in the mud above it.
She dismounted and led the mare in by the safest line. The earth had been trampled by wagon, horses, and too many boots for one clean reading, but the place still gave itself up if a person stayed patient.
One wagon across.
One crossing rigged by force instead of luck.
Three blood sign places on the south side, two plain and one lighter where a wounded man had stood too long.
She crouched by the rope post and touched the bank where the waterline had not yet blurred the print edges. Jed had gone in there. She could see the stripped-down shape of the effort in the heel grooves and the hand drag at the lip where he took the river. No one else in that party would have crossed first if another choice worth naming had existed.
She followed the print field north of the post and found the shape of the wagon party waiting while the ferry came free. One woman with weight carried close and guarded. One old driver moving from team to bank and back. One wounded young man who had no business on such a road. One boy who wasted less motion than any grown hand. One town-bred rider stepping around mud when he could. And one second rifleman besides Jed.
Jed had not taken random company. The road had built him a burden train and fastened him to it.
She found Draven's later sign a little above the landing where he had come down from the upstream ford and read the same ground after the wagon crossed. The left heel turn sat there in the mud no clearer than before. He had taken his time at the post and then moved north with no sign of panic.
Good.
Haste meant trouble. Ease meant he had already got what he wanted. This sat between.
She climbed to the watch rise above the cottonwoods and found the blanket press still plain in the dead grass. The tobacco twist end remained by the stone. Beneath her own older boot cut lay the deeper hollows where a man had watched the south approach and not the north.
Draven had waited there for someone coming up from below.
She came down and worked the rest of the landing one more time. By the broken bank edge she found fresh ash rubbed between two fingers and dropped, not from the wagon's first crossing but from a smaller fire built later by one man or two with no wish to be seen.
Draven had waited there again after the others moved on.
She found the place where he had sat. No blanket this time. Just a heel groove in the dirt, a flattened patch against one pine root, and a small scrape where a knife had pared cedar dry enough to take spark.
He had watched the north road from there.
Maybe he hoped Jed would return to the landing. Maybe he wanted only to mark the hour and let that mark ride with him.
Either way it meant the first wood sliver in her pocket had not been left in passing thought.
She took the mare north by the wagon line and found the next camp before noon.
Cold ash in a cut. Bloody cloth. One dead fire made by anxious hands and bad sleep. She studied it from the rim first and then stepped down to read the center where the wagon had stood. The wounded youth had bled there after the ferry. Someone with steady hands had rebound him under poor light. Marrow's old boots had worn a deep path between team and fire. Ruth's guarded steps had gone to the wagon and back with the box. Vale's finer tread crossed everything and committed to nothing.
Jed's sign lay around the cut in short protection loops. Not fear. Work.
There she also found where the boy's prints had joined him at the upper trail.
That mattered.
The child was not cargo then. He had eyes or country sense both.
She followed the wagon trail through noon into rougher ground. Granite shelves broke the line and wind drift blurred the open places. She slowed instead of forcing the read.
At the wash crossing with raw-cut banks she found the split thorn where the wood sliver had hung before she took it. Another sign sat near it that she had missed the first day because she had been reading forward instead of back.
Draven's horse had stood uphill from the wash while he set the marker. The animal had favored one foreleg in the dirt. Nothing grave. Enough to say the ford and the long push after had cost something.
She put two fingers to the split thorn and looked north.
Jed might have seen the sliver unless the wagon crowd had pulled his eye elsewhere. In that case the marker was already riding with him.
She mounted and pushed on.
By afternoon she found the place where the wagon had stopped to water and tend the sick man after dawn. The ground held better there.
Rifle case print by the bank.
One old driver kneeling near a harness buckle.
The wounded youth climbing back into the wagon.
And one set of prints not belonging to the party at all, set off the road in the brush line where a watcher had stood long enough to leave both heels clear.
Draven again.
He had come close there. Close enough to hear voices if the wind favored him.
She found his exit line on higher ground west of the road. He had shifted from simple pursuit into pressure work.
She rode the same high line for part of the afternoon and once caught far movement on a ridge shoulder north of the wagon.
Not Jed. Too far back from the wagon and too careless about skyline.
Draven letting himself be seen.
She did not chase him.
Better to count the roads he preferred than spend daylight lunging at shadows.
Near evening the track bent toward a granite shoulder and blacker timber beyond. There she found a tobacco wrapper folded tight and wedged under a rock at knee height.
Not camp trash. Not accident.
She opened it and found nothing written inside, only the crease pattern of a man who had folded and unfolded it while waiting.
Draven again or someone under him. No one else on that road had reason to leave such a thing at a choke point.
She burned the paper in her palm, ground the ash under her boot, and took the dark timber north at a longer pace.
By full dark she made her own camp on a ridge pocket above the road. No fire until she had walked the perimeter and found no cut sign close enough to trouble her sleep. When she did build one, she banked it low in stone and tied the mare where one hard pull on the line would wake them both.
The wood sliver sat in her coat pocket while she ate.
She took it out once and turned it in the firelight.
Three notches. Too simple for code. Just crooked enough to provoke reading.
Draven had always liked work like that when fear had already entered a camp ahead of him.
She fed the sliver to the coals.
When she lay down, she did not sleep easy.
Not because the road had turned against her.
Because it had begun doing exactly what Draven meant it to do.

Chapter 11: The Old Crossing
The wheel dropped to the axle with a crack of wet earth.
Marrow hauled the team back on instinct and the wagon stopped half crooked in the road, one rear corner sunk deep where the washout had taken the ground from under it. Mud surged black around the hub and settled there with no intention of letting go.
Jed swung down before the horses finished blowing.
The spring road had narrowed through black timber all morning. Meltwater had eaten through the low places and left a skin over emptiness in one stretch too long for a wagon to trust. The rear wheel had found the weakness first.
Marrow stood in the traces with both hands on the lead pair. "Back them a touch."
Jed waded to the buried wheel and put his shoulder to the box side. Wren came in on the other side without being told. Between them and the team's backward drag they got the wheel up one inch and lost it again when more crust broke.
Marrow swore.
Vale stayed dry on a stone and said, "Promising."
Ruth gave him one look that would have cut softer men.
Caleb leaned against the tailboard with both arms around his middle and enough fever still in him that the shade did not cool his face. Nemi had gone ahead on foot before the wheel broke and now stood farther up the road with his head turned to one side, listening to something beyond the timber.
Jed walked the next twenty yards.
The road went to ruin in plain stages once a man saw how the ground lay. Another loaded pass would have buried not just one wheel but the whole rear of the wagon. Turning back would cost half a day and might still land them in worse ground by dark.
Marrow slogged up behind him. "Well."
Jed looked east through the trees. "There may be a line off the road."
"There may be a prayer too."
Nemi spoke from ahead.
"The marked road is wrong."
They all turned.
The boy stood beside a dead pine where old blaze cuts still showed under weathered bark. He pointed into thicker timber. "There is a crossing there."
Marrow snorted. "For rabbits."
Nemi shook his head. "For wagons before this road."
Jed went to him and touched the old blaze with one finger. Another showed farther in on a black spruce, then another on a leaning cedar. Not random. A route once, abandoned when easier ground opened elsewhere.
"Who told you."
"Elkheart. And my aunt before. She said fresh cuts on old burial timber are lies. Old cuts on old timber are only old."
That sat right.
Jed stepped into the east timber and found wheel scars under the pine needles, worn nearly flat by years and still there if a man knew to search for them.
Real.
He looked back at Marrow. "We can try it."
Vale laughed once from the stone. "Can."
Marrow ignored him. "How far."
Nemi answered before Jed could. "To water. Then stone. Then the north road again."
Marrow wiped mud from his beard. "You saying it like you walked it last week."
"I walked it when the snow left."
Marrow gave him the long look old men kept for improbable help they had no choice but to accept. Then he spat into the road. "Unload the rear. Lighten the box. We shift the wagon east."
The next hour was all labor.
Crates came down first. Then grain sacks. Then smaller baggage. Ruth kept her iron box in both hands and never offered it to anyone. Caleb tried carrying one sack and nearly pitched with it before Wren took the load from him. Vale carried what could be carried without soiling him past recovery and no more.
Jed cut brush and laid branches into the worst mud while Wren and Nemi built a cord bed under the wheel. The right leg hated the twisting work, but he did not let it set the pace.
When they finally hauled the wagon free, the wheel came up with a sucking boom and a spray of black filth across Jed's coat.
Vale stepped back in time to save his own boots.
"Useful instinct."
"Coward's."
That shut him again.
They took the old route east with Nemi on the lead.
He walked through the timber as if the path lay bright before him. Jed rode just behind with the dead rider's horse on a lead and watched the boy work. The old crossing line was nearly gone in places, but not to him. A split trunk here. A double blaze there. A bank worn shallow by wheels long dead.
By midafternoon the timber pitched down into a narrow creek cut where cold water ran over flat stone.
Nemi stopped and raised one hand.
Jed came alongside.
The road crossed the creek at a point that looked easy enough to any man in haste. But a little farther down one edge of the flat showed fresh tool marks hidden low under brush.
Someone had cut there recently.
Jed crouched and studied the bank.
The stake had been driven on the downstream side where a wagon wheel would slide if coaxed onto the wrong stone and then break through a rotten lip under water. Enough to trap the axle, maybe turn the whole box if the team panicked.
Nemi touched the old scar on a birch and then the false line by the creek.
"Marked road is wrong."
Jed nodded.
Marrow slogged up and saw the stake in a blink once it had been named. "That there is murder for slow people."
"Or for men in dark."
Jed yanked the stake free and threw it into the current. "We cross higher."
Nemi pointed upstream to a shelf of pale granite half hidden under roots. The old wheel line went that way, faint but honest.
They took it in two passes. First the wagon stripped light. Then the cargo by hand over the shelf and back down to firmer ground north of the creek. No one liked it. The horses liked it least. One slip would have broken a leg. But by the time the sun turned lower through the trunks, they had all crossed and the false trap sat behind them exposed.
Marrow looked back at the water. "Who sets a trick like that in country this empty."
Jed tightened the near trace. "A man who expects company."
No one said Draven's name, but it moved through the group anyway.
Vale watched the black timber beyond the creek. "How many roads north does one enemy need."
Ruth said, "Fewer than you need questions."
They pushed on another mile before camping.
The old route climbed out of the creek bed onto stone shelves that shed water and gave the wagon better footing than the main road had. Dry camp there, tight among cedar and black spruce, with enough room for a small fire and the team within arm's reach.
When the fire caught, Marrow gave Nemi the first cup.
"You earned this."
Nemi took it and sat where the smoke drifted off the stone.
Jed watched him over the flames. The boy had saved them a wagon and maybe all their necks with it.
"Elkheart told you never to trust fresh cuts."
Nemi nodded.
"What else did he tell you."
The boy drank once and looked into the dark timber. "He said old ground keeps count better than men do."
Jed let that sit.
If the marked road could lie, then every easier mile ahead needed weighing before it was trusted.

Chapter 12: Vale's Question
Vale turned a card edge over his knuckle while the coffee boiled.
He had no full deck left. Only three bent cards he had found or saved from one place or another in his coat. He worked them anyway, one over the next, slow enough to be irritating and neat enough to prove the irritation was chosen.
The morning came pale over the stone shelf camp and left the wagon party with sore backs, damp blankets, and a little more faith in Nemi than the day before. Marrow checked harness with his usual insults. Wren cleaned the rifle barrel by habit and put the case back over it when he was done. Caleb sat with the blanket around his shoulders and the fever lower at last, though not gone. Ruth never took the box out of reach. Jed walked the edge of camp and looked for trouble sign before returning to the fire.
Vale waited until he did.
"Stone Relay."
The card clicked once over his knuckle.
No one answered.
Vale smiled at the silence in the same way another man might smile at a fish testing bait. "A place or a man."
Marrow looked up from the off lead buckle. "What."
"The name." Vale rolled the second card. "Stone Relay. I heard it years back in a room where nobody admitted hearing it before or after. That sort of name sticks."
Jed set his tin cup down by the fire and looked at him.
"Never heard it."
Vale gave her half a nod. "I believe that you believe so."
She took no offense at the line because offense would have admitted he mattered.
Marrow came upright slow. "You carrying that around till breakfast by design."
"Breakfast improves design."
Jed said, "Why ask here."
Vale's eyes came over. "Because we are at the point in any difficult journey when coincidence begins to feel poorly dressed."
"It looked poorly dressed from the start."
"True enough." Vale flipped the last card and caught it flat. "A man from Ghostpine. A woman carrying a box men bleed over. A wounded stray who knows the name Draven. A preacher with a marksman's rifle. A silent boy who reads dead roads. And the north country ahead getting harder by the mile." He tucked the cards away. "That invites one to ask whether some of us are traveling toward the same answer by different lies."
Caleb watched the fire and said nothing.
Jed did not move his face.
"If Stone Relay is a place," Vale went on, "it sounds like a place once used for passing something hand to hand. If it is a man, the man had parents too cruel for scripture. Either way I dislike gaps in company knowledge."
Marrow said, "Dislike them somewhere else."
Vale looked at Jed alone. "Well."
Jed picked up the cup and drank before answering.
"If I knew the name, I would still have no cause to improve your morning with it."
Vale's smile widened a little, not because the answer pleased him, but because refusal in itself had value. "There. Better than denial."
Ruth said, "You hear a stranger decline you and count it profit. That says more about you than him."
"Madam, profit is simply the name for a useful answer."
"No. Profit is the name men give greed when they want it seated at table."
Marrow grunted approval into his beard.
Vale did not bother fencing with her after that. He had his morsel and knew it.
Jed finished the coffee, doused the cup, and went to help hitch the team. The right leg had eased some after the shorter day on the old route, though the stone shelf sleeping did nothing kind for the hip above it. He bent, checked the cinch on the spare riding horse, and kept his eyes from Vale because looking would only encourage more of the same.
Still, the damage had been done.
A name loosed around a fire did not go back into silence clean.
They broke camp and took the north line over higher stone for most of the morning. The old crossing route joined a rough wagon trace by noon, enough to give Marrow decent footing again and enough open country that every rider looked farther than he had to. The day stayed cold and bright. No sign of Lask. No sign of Draven. That meant little. Absence had become one of Draven's cleaner tools.
Ruth rode outside the wagon some of that day on the dead rider's horse, box tied behind the saddle now instead of hugged under both arms. She handled the animal better than Vale expected and worse than Marrow approved. Caleb slept through part of the noon rise and woke ashamed of it. Wren kept the rifle case near at all times and no one commented on that anymore.
In the long afternoon stretch Vale ranged forward until he rode even with Jed.
"You dislike me."
Jed kept his eyes on the road. "Not unusual."
"No." Vale guided his horse around a patch of broken shale. "But I wonder whether I earned it myself or inherited it from the type."
"Same difference if the boot fits."
Vale laughed. "There is the trouble. A man asks one practical question and is counted a vulture."
"A vulture waits for meat. You sniff for ownership."
"Ownership is only memory with a fence around it."
Jed gave him a side look at last. "That the sort of sentence wins cards in town."
"Sometimes women."
"Road's got no use for either."
Vale accepted the hit with that same unfailing calm. "Then let me ask it plain without town polish. If trouble comes north, do you mean to steer us through it, or into it."
That question had more worth than the earlier one.
Jed answered it because the road had earned at least that much honesty among people forced to share it. "Through, if I can."
"And if you cannot."
"Then I do the next thing that leaves most breathing."
Vale looked ahead a while after that. "I can work with a man who admits the word most."
Jed did not ask what that meant. He preferred men to declare themselves in full when given time. Vale would.
They found the next camp place in a half bowl of granite and cedar, dry underfoot and sheltered from west wind. Better than the cut, worse than a town yard, which meant it was good enough. Marrow set the wagon nose to the only easy entrance and had the team fed before dark came all the way down.
The evening passed without cards, songs, or questions until after supper.
Vale lay near the rear wheel with his coat rolled under his head. Ruth slept with the box under her blanket now instead of beside it. Wren took first watch and paced the bowl rim in slow measured cuts. Nemi vanished twice into the dark edge and returned both times with no comment. Caleb slept hard at last.
Jed waited until the camp settled before he checked his bedroll.
At first nothing showed wrong.
The blanket sat where he had left it. The oilcloth wrap lay under the fold. His spare shirt and cartridge pouch had not been taken. But when he lifted the roll, the balance came back different in his hands. Not weight. Order.
He knelt and opened it fully.
The things inside had been touched and put back by a man who did not know the original arrangement well enough to imitate it.
One sock folded inward instead of out.
The small whetstone moved from the blanket edge to the shirt seam.
The spare cartridges turned brass end opposite his habit.
No theft.
Search.
Jed looked up at the sleeping shapes by the low fire.
Wren paced beyond them and did not glance his way. Ruth lay still. Caleb snored softly through his nose. Marrow, if he slept, did it like a man always half ready not to. Vale's outline by the wheel had not moved in some minutes. Nemi sat on the dark side of the bowl with both knees up, watching nothing any grown hand could yet see.
Jed put the roll back together in his own order and stood.
He did not wake anyone.
Instead he crossed to the bowl rim and traded places with Wren.
"What is wrong." The preacher stood in the dark at the bowl rim.
"Maybe."
Wren looked once at the fire and once at the wagon before giving him the watch route. "You want me to wake Marrow."
"No."
"Vale."
"Not yet."
Wren accepted that and went to his blanket.
Jed walked the rim in silence after.
A man searching his bedroll told him two things clean. One, Vale or another hand in camp thought he carried something worth stealing on his body or in his gear. Two, Stone Relay had stopped being a stray sound around the fire and become a live question among the people sharing his road.
The night stayed quiet to the ear.
That made it worse.

Chapter 13: Draven Talks to Caleb
Caleb lifted the blanket edge one inch at a time.
The camp lay under black timber where the old crossing road had joined back with rougher north track. Marrow had chosen a shallow shelf above a wash and backed the wagon against stone so only the front and south flank needed watching. The fire had burned down to a low red bed by midnight. Wren had second watch. Jed had taken first and now slept with the coat over one shoulder and one hand near the holster, the way men slept when rest was only another form of waiting.
Caleb lay two wagon lengths from him and counted breaths until the count broke.
The fever had gone lower that day and left more room for thought, which was not the mercy it first looked to be. Hurt was easier than memory. Hurt gave a man one plain enemy. Memory brought back faces, voices, and choices he had already made wrong once.
He eased upright.
The wound pulled at once. Not enough to stop him. Enough to remind him he was a fool for moving.
No one in camp stirred.
Ruth slept with the box under her shoulder. Marrow snored in short mean bursts and then quit as if even sleep had to mind his temper. Vale lay neat under his blanket with both boots off this time, gambling on morning like a man who had forgotten dawn could come armed. Nemi had vanished into the dark edge the way he often did. Caleb no longer tried to guess whether the boy slept at all or simply went still in some place the rest of them had not learned to see.
Wren stood at the south side with the rifle case open against the wagon wheel.
Caleb waited until the preacher turned for one slow pacing pass toward the team and then slid the rest of the way up. He kept one hand pressed to the bandage and moved bent over, using the wagon shadow to hide his shape until he reached the cedar brush behind camp.
The first ten steps hurt.
The next ten hurt different.
By the time he reached the wash line the body had decided on a steady misery and settled into it.
He almost turned back then.
Not because he had lost his will. He knew what waited where the wash bent south and every step toward it made him smaller in his own eyes. But shame was already on him and had been since Bitter Hollow. A man could only carry so much of that before he started calling it fate.
He went on.
The wash held black damp under willow and alder. No moon reached the bottom of it. Caleb followed by smell and touch where he had no light enough to trust his feet. Twice he stopped to catch breath against the bank. Once another step seemed to move beside his own and nearly spilled the whole of himself into a panic before an owl crossed overhead and settled farther downslope.
He knew the place before he reached it.
Cutbank on the left, a stone root jutting out of the clay, flat enough ground beneath it for two horses and a man who liked to wait where sound had to come to him first.
One horse stood there already, reins looped to a scrub branch.
Draven sat on the stone root with both hands loose on his knees.
He wore no coat despite the cold. Only the dark shirt, vest, hat low, and that same quiet shape he carried in every camp from Texas up through the territories as if weather and other men had both agreed not to put hands on him without permission.
Caleb stopped six feet off.
Draven looked him over from boots to face and then to the bandage under his hand.
"Still alive."
Caleb kept breathing through his nose because the mouth wanted to betray too much. "Looks that way."
"I shot you better than that."
"You always liked your own work."
Draven's mouth moved one side and settled. Not a smile. Never that when business was straight. "You came because you knew I would not ask twice."
Caleb hated that the statement was true enough to hurt.
"You got a way of asking that reaches a man."
"You got a way of making that easy."
The wash held them both a moment with the water working quietly below.
Draven tipped his head once toward the camp they could not see from there. "How many now."
Caleb said nothing.
Draven looked at his own fingernails as though the question were a small one and time had no cost. "Old driver. Widow. Preacher. Card man. Boy. Jed." He lifted his eyes. "That matches what the road gave me. I am asking what the road did not."
Caleb pressed harder at the bandage without knowing he did it. "Then you ask the wrong man."
Draven stood.
He did not move fast. That was one of the things that had made camp men fear him sooner than others. The quick ones could be read. The slow ones forced everybody else to imagine speed on their own time.
"No." He stepped closer by half a pace. "I ask the only man in that camp who already knows what my hand feels like."
Caleb looked past him at the tied horse because looking full at Draven made the body remember too clearly. Bitter Hollow. The cook on his knees. The shot. The dirt filling his mouth after.
"What do you want."
"Jed."
The answer came plain as shovel iron.
"You always wanted Jed."
"No." Draven's eyes stayed on him. "I want where Jed is going."
Caleb swallowed.
There it was. The thing under all the other things. Not revenge. Not pride. Not the common meanness that had driven half the road blood he had seen. Direction.
"He has not told me."
"I know." Draven took another half step and stopped there. "If he had, you would not be talking to me from both feet."
Caleb almost laughed and did not. The motion would have split him.
"Then why am I here."
"Because you hear things. You watch who leaves the fire. You watch who speaks low and who guards what when the rest sleep. You tell me when Jed walks off alone and you keep breathing through winter."
Caleb stared at him.
Simple offer. Simple threat. Draven did his ugliest work that way. He took the choice a man most wanted to deny and cut every side road away from it until the lie of freedom looked like mercy.
"And if I don't."
Draven's face did not change at all. "Then maybe I let the road decide between you, the widow, and the card man. Maybe I choose the preacher because he bothers me. Maybe I kill the boy first and let the rest understand the lesson from there."
Caleb's hand twitched off the bandage and then back onto it.
The wash had gone colder.
"You would do it."
"You came out here because you know I would."
That shut the only honest answer.
Draven reached into his vest and brought out a strip of dried meat wrapped in cloth. He tossed it underhand. Caleb caught it before thinking and hated himself again for the quickness.
"Eat that on the walk back." Draven stepped away and sat on the root once more as if the meeting had already ended cleanly in his head. "Tomorrow night if he leaves the fire. Night after if he does not. You know where this wash turns."
Caleb stood without moving.
"Why not kill me now."
Draven looked at him with something almost patient in it. "Because dead men notice less."
No more than that.
Caleb backed away first and then turned because pride had finally found its limit. The climb out of the wash hit him harder than the walk down. Twice he had to brace against roots and wait for the black swimming at the edges of his sight to settle. The meat in his hand felt like a brand.
When he reached the cedar line above camp, he threw it into the dark.
He almost threw up after.
By the time he slipped back around the wagon, Wren had turned once more toward the team and still seemed not to have noticed him gone. Maybe the preacher had missed it. Maybe not. Caleb no longer trusted his reading of other men enough to know.
He got under the blanket and lay on his back because any other way hurt more.
Sleep did not come.
When Jed rose at gray light and looked once across camp, Caleb shut his eyes and pretended the fever had taken him deeper than it had.

Chapter 14: The Box Opens
Ruth set the box on the wagon seat and turned the key.
The key had hung all morning on a cord under her dress where no searching hand had yet found it. She took it out now in full daylight because Hobb Lask had already bled for the box twice and because a road secret held too tight became its own trap if the wrong men were dying around it.
Marrow drew the team up under a stand of pine above a dry creek bend and said nothing while she worked.
That silence brought everyone close.
Jed stood by the near wheel with the dead rider's horse on a short lead. Wren leaned on the rifle case. Caleb sat wrapped in his blanket with a face gone hollow from the night and the wound beneath it. Vale took position where he could see inside the box without seeming to lunge for the privilege. Nemi stayed back under one pine root shadow but watched with the same hard stillness he gave trails and weather.
The key turned with one scrape of metal against old wards.
Ruth lifted the lid.
No gold sat under it.
Vale's disappointment showed before he could smooth it away.
Inside lay three bundles of letters tied in ribbon that had once been blue, a leather purse of coin, and a folded packet wrapped in oilcloth. Under those sat a ledger book no larger than a preacher's testament, its cover worn pale at the corners from too much handling.
Marrow blew once through his nose. "For that."
Ruth touched the oilcloth first but did not open it. "My husband kept records when he meant to prove another man had cheated him. He also kept records when he planned to cheat first."
"Useful marriage."
Ruth ignored him.
She opened the ledger.
The pages held names, dates, small sums, larger sums, and marks beside some entries that meant nothing to Jed at first glance. Not business done clean through banks or offices. Road business. Freight swapped quiet. Goods moved without customs or questions. Payment shares. Debts still owed after the last trade soured.
Lask's name appeared on the third page.
So did two others none of them knew.
Wren looked over Ruth's shoulder. "That little thing got men shot."
"Men shoot over less."
Ruth turned another page and stopped there.
Her mouth drew tight.
Jed said, "What."
She looked up once before speaking. "The coin in here is only coin. Lask wants the names with it because the names tell where the rest of his share went when my husband cheated him and then died before settling it."
Vale reached with his eyes, not his hands. "Meaning the list has use beyond memory."
"It proves he worked the same trade he now pretends was theft done to him."
Marrow spat off the wagon tongue. "That would sour a man's appetite."
Ruth untied the first packet of letters and spread them across the seat.
Some were business notes with no greeting and no signature. Some were written by men certain paper made them respectable if they kept the pen strokes neat enough. One was from her husband to a contact in Spur asking after a handoff delayed by weather. Another named a freight load shifted north and split three ways with no lawful mark attached to any part of it.
None of it yet said Stone Relay.
Jed kept his face empty.
Ruth opened the oilcloth next.
Inside lay a shorter list on folded paper, names only, some marked with crosses, some underlined twice. Lask sat there too. Beside him another hand had written:
Paid wrong. Angry enough to follow.
Vale exhaled through his teeth. "Honest husband."
"Dead husband. Do not improve him by naming him honest."
That earned her more respect from Jed than any claim of innocence would have.
Caleb held a hand out once. "Let me see."
Ruth hesitated, then passed the folded list instead of the ledger. Caleb studied it for a count of ten and looked up with a color that had nothing to do with fever.
"I know one of these."
Jed took the paper from him and looked.
Which one mattered less than the fact itself.
"Who."
Caleb pointed with two fingers that shook. "Weldon Pike."
Marrow looked from the name to Caleb and back. "Kin."
"Half brother." Caleb sat farther upright, wincing at the pull. "He ran freight and bragged too much after. Last I saw him he talked like he had stepped into bigger money than trail drives gave."
Ruth held the ledger closed in both hands. "Your brother knew my husband then."
"Looks that way."
Caleb's expression had turned old and tired in the space of one breath. Another debt road opening under his feet.
Wren said, "This is why Lask keeps coming."
"Partly." Ruth ran her thumb along the list edge. "The coin matters too. There are two hundred and some dollars in the purse. Enough for one man to keep after through winter if he thinks the rest of his share lies somewhere the names can still uncover."
Vale's eyes stayed on the closed ledger now, not the purse.
Jed noticed and filed it away.
"Any mention of where."
Ruth looked back through the letters one at a time, slower now. One Spur contact. One mention of a north run. One line about a room safe from weather where paper could keep better than in town if a man trusted stone more than wood. No place name. No directions. No clear handoff point.
Still, the shape of it pressed too near what Jed carried in memory for comfort.
Ruth stopped at the line about the room and read it again.
"What."
"No full answer." She passed the letter to Jed.
The hand was cramped and mean. The sentence sat in the middle of a list of grievances and prices: Better put the books in the room again till Spur quiets. Stone keeps mice and rain honest where men won't.
Jed read it once and handed it back.
No name. No route. No more than that. But enough to tell him the room Ketch had named had not stood empty by chance all those years. Men had used it for exactly the sort of work that filled the ledger now.
Vale held out his hand. "May I."
Ruth did not give him the letter.
"No."
Vale smiled anyway. "Then perhaps you will read the useful bits aloud for the sake of collective survival."
"Perhaps not."
Marrow stepped in before that line could stretch thin. "Pack it all back up. We already know enough. Lask wants the coin and the list. The rest is rot unless it gets us killed by supper."
Wren nodded toward the trees downhill. "He is not the only one behind us."
That tightened everyone again.
Ruth repacked the box in different order than before. Ledger on the bottom. Letters under oilcloth. Purse buried between. Key back on the cord beneath her dress. When she shut the lid, the sound of it carried farther than seemed natural in the pines.
Caleb kept looking at the place where his half brother's name had stood on the page, though the page was gone.
Jed said, "You knew where he rode."
"Not after Bitter Hollow."
"You ever hear him name a room in stone."
Caleb shook his head. "Only barns, sheds, and women he lied to."
Marrow barked one short laugh. Even hurt men had brothers like that.
They ate little after. The box had changed from burden to map in every mind there, whether any of them admitted it or not. Vale grew quieter, which worried Jed more than the earlier talk. Loud greed told on itself. Silent greed counted.
When the wagon rolled again, Ruth did not ride outside. She climbed into the bed with the box under her knees and the pistol hidden somewhere in the folds of her coat. Wren took the bench beside Marrow. Jed ranged forward half a mile at one point just to put his own thoughts in cleaner order.
Ketch had named the place. The letters proved the place had been used by freight thieves and quiet traders before or after the dead man from St. Louis came north on the same question. No paper now held enough to guide anyone there. Only the shape of it remained and that shape still lived only in his head.
Good.
And not good enough.
By late day the road narrowed between granite and pine again. Jed checked the back trail from one shoulder and found no rider plain in the open, yet the sense of being counted had only deepened since morning. He rode back to the wagon and looked once at Ruth through the flap.
She met the look.
"You wish I had thrown it in the river."
"Maybe."
"So do I." She settled the box more firmly under both hands. "But we are past wishes."
That was the truest thing said all day.

Chapter 15: Snow Without Cover
The wind struck the wagon broadside when it came out of timber.
Marrow swore and hauled the team straighter into it, but there was no shelter to claim and no line of trees near enough to matter. The road had risen all morning through broken black pine and then spilled them without warning onto open white country where snow from older storms still lay packed hard from ridge to ridge. No cedar. No willow. No wash cut deep enough to hide a dog. Only long pale ground under a gray sky and the wagon moving across it like a mark on paper.
Jed hated it at once.
The right leg hated it too, though for simpler reasons. Hard crust under the horse's hooves made every step ring up through saddle and brace alike. No give in the ground. No root or rut to vary the strain. Just the same repeated jolt laid into old bone until the body began counting each one whether the mind wished it or not.
He rode ahead and looked south, then west, then north again.
No rider showed.
That meant less here than anywhere else.
Marrow drove with his shoulders high and his jaw set as though force of spite might raise timber from the plain. Ruth sat inside the wagon with the flap tied back enough to watch both sides. Wren had the rifle case open on the bench. Vale tried once to make a joke about graveyards and white sheets and no one gave him a place to set it down. Caleb kept his blanket over the lower half of his face against the wind and the fever that still had not finished with him.
Nemi rode this stretch on the spare horse because the cold flat ground took too much out of anyone walking it. He sat small and balanced in the saddle and watched the north line as if even the horizon might decide to lie.
Halfway across the first broad rise, the horses twitched their ears east together.
Jed turned that way.
No rider showed. No movement. Only white slope, stone teeth breaking through here and there, and a stand of dead spruce a long shot off.
He started back toward the wagon.
The shot came from somewhere beyond the dead spruce.
It cracked across the open and clipped the edge of Marrow's hat clean enough to take the brim off one side before burying in the wagon bow behind him.
The team lurched. Marrow brought them back by violence and memory both. Wren was off the bench and on one knee before the echo finished running the ground.
"Down."
No one argued.
Ruth disappeared into the wagon box. Vale flattened himself under the seat. Caleb tried to get lower and winced so hard at the pull in his side that the sound carried even through the wind. Nemi slid from the spare horse and led it behind the wagon where the box gave the only cover there was.
Jed put his glass to the dead spruce line and searched the openings.
No second shot came.
No second shot came.
Wren lay along the snow crust with the rifle set on his rolled coat and worked the same line from lower angle. "Long way."
"I know."
Marrow touched the torn hat brim once as if confirming he still had a head under it. "That one was for my education."
Jed kept the glass up. "It was for all of us."
The ball had not sought heart or horse. It had sought attention. That meant Draven and not Lask. Lask would have pressed two more shots and ruined the lesson by hurrying it.
Wren said, "You have a mark."
Jed found it a breath later.
A brief wink off steel or glass between two dead trunks, then gone. Not enough to shoot at with confidence. Enough to name the lane.
"There."
Wren shifted, settled, and held.
Still no second shot.
Marrow said, "We move or crouch."
Jed looked over the ground behind and ahead. A quarter mile back lay the last stone shoulder worth any cover. A quarter mile ahead the road dipped a little, not enough to hide the whole wagon, enough perhaps to make a man work harder for his angle. Staying still meant granting Draven time and the clearest target on the plain.
"Move."
Marrow did not wait for another word.
The wagon rolled again with everyone inside it crouched low except Wren, who walked beside the near wheel with the rifle half raised and his eyes on the spruce line. Jed rode off the east side now to widen what Draven had to read. If the next shot came for him, better there than through the wagon cloth.
It did not come.
That proved the point more clearly than lead would have. Draven wanted them moving under his eye, not dying at his first convenience.
The shallow dip ahead finally took the lower half of the wagon out of the long line from the spruce. Marrow drove another two hundred yards before Jed lifted a hand and got them halted behind a shoulder of wind-packed drift and stone no bigger than a small grave mound.
Poor cover. Better than none.
Wren chambered a round and looked over at him. "He had the driver and took cloth."
"He had the team and took wood."
Marrow spat into the snow. "I prefer a man who misses honest."
Vale raised his head from under the seat just enough to speak. "Then I fear your circle of friends grows narrow."
Ruth said, "Stay there."
Vale stayed there.
Jed dismounted and walked the shoulder, gauging whether a flanking move toward the spruce line offered anything but needless distance in open country. It did not. Draven had chosen too well. Snow crust would sing under any approach and give away a crawling man long before the rifle lane shortened enough to matter.
Nemi said, "He wants us to know his hands reach."
Jed looked over.
The boy stood by the spare horse with the reins looped twice around one glove. No fear in the face. Only understanding.
"Yes."
"Then we should not give him stillness."
That too sat right.
Marrow had already come to the same answer by another road. "We drive till timber or dark. If he wants a moving target, he can study one."
They set out again.
The rest of the crossing took most of the afternoon and more of the nerves than any of them wanted to spare. Every rise looked like a rifle rest. Every dead stump became a waiting man until it proved stump again. No one spoke above what work required. Even Vale kept his wit under the seat with him.
Once Jed saw a rider silhouette far south on the white shoulder and lose it again when cloud shadow crossed.
Once Wren lifted the rifle and then lowered it because the line was too long and the certainty too poor.
Once Caleb said, "He could have killed me there."
No one answered because the truth had already been given.
By the time timber finally rose ahead in a black ragged wall, the whole party had begun to move as if sound itself were expensive. Marrow drove the team into the first trees and did not stop until trunks stood close on both sides and the snow lost its endless horizon.
Then he hauled the reins and sat bent over them for a breath.
"There." He looked at no one. "He has spoken."
Jed dismounted stiffly.
The leg had taken the plain worse than it liked. Not failure. Not near it. Just the deep straight ache of too many hard miles over frozen crust. He walked it down while Wren looked back through the tree edge with the rifle set low and ready.
Ruth climbed out of the wagon and stared south into the white they had left. "What kind of man spends a shot that way."
Jed answered because none of the others knew Draven close enough. "One who wants the fear to ride longer than the noise."
Vale came down last and brushed snow dust off his sleeves with pointless care. "Then he got his money's worth."
Marrow turned on him. "Not from me."
"No." Vale looked back toward the plain. "From the road itself."
That silenced them more than if he had held his tongue.
They camped inside timber that night, though no one called it camp with much faith. Wren took first watch by common consent. Marrow set the wagon between trees instead of in the open, team close and tied short. Ruth slept with the box and the pistol both. Caleb shook once in renewed fever and then went quiet under double blankets.
Jed sat by the fire after the others had laid down and looked into the coals without seeing them.
Draven had not asked for blood. He had asked for imagination, and the open white country had given it to him in full.
That meant the next move would come nearer.

Chapter 16: Caleb Breaks
Caleb dropped the tin cup and let it ring against stone.
The sound snapped through camp sharper than it had any right to at dawn. Marrow looked up from the coffee pot. Wren stopped with the rifle cloth in one hand. Ruth tightened both arms around the box where she sat on the wagon tail. Vale came half awake under his blanket and then fully so when he saw Caleb on his feet.
The young man stood two steps from the fire with one hand clamped to his side and the other shaking empty at his thigh.
"He spoke to me."
No one answered at once.
Jed rose from his blanket slow because the right leg liked no fast morning. He kept his eyes on Caleb and nothing else. The fever had gone out of the young man's face overnight and left another strain there in its place. Shame had cleaner lines than sickness.
"When."
"Night before last." Caleb swallowed. "At the wash."
Marrow set the coffee pot down hard enough to slop its edge. "You walked out of camp."
Caleb nodded once.
"To him."
"He was waiting."
Marrow came up from the fire in two strides. Age had left his speed in mean corners of the body and nowhere else, but those corners still worked. "And you held that under your hat while we crossed open snow under his rifle."
Jed put an arm across the old driver's chest before he got all the way there.
"Let him finish."
Marrow tried moving through the arm, failed, and settled for murder in the eyes.
Caleb looked at Jed and then away because that was easier than looking at the whole camp. "He wanted you. Not dead. Not yet. He said tell him if you left the fire alone."
Ruth said, "And did you."
"No."
Vale sat up fully now with his blanket over one shoulder. "A man may lie in several directions at once. Useful talent."
Caleb wheeled on him so fast the wound nearly folded him. "You think I sold him the snow road."
"I think you went to him once and hid it." Vale got to his feet neat as ever. "That makes the next lie cheap."
Wren closed the rifle case and laid it aside. "What else did Draven say."
Caleb dragged a breath in through his nose. "That he'd kill one of us if I held out. Maybe the widow. Maybe you. Maybe the boy first so the rest would learn quicker."
At that, Nemi, who had been crouched by the team since daybreak, looked over with no change in his face at all.
Marrow spat into the dirt. "He should have put a bullet in you and saved the trouble."
Jed did not move the arm from Marrow's chest.
"No."
Ruth said, "He came back and told it."
Vale laughed once with no humor in it. "After how many miles."
"Enough." Jed looked over at him. "You want to count guilt, count all of it."
Vale spread one hand. "Gladly. We have a wounded fool, a box with names in it, Lask hunting one side of the road, Draven hunting the other, and a leader who still does not tell us where north becomes north enough."
The word leader hung there ugly and not wholly false.
Marrow heard the same thing. "You naming him that because you believe it or because you want the rest of us to."
"What difference if the trail obeys either way."
Jed let Marrow go and took one step toward Vale. The leg answered with a deep morning ache and then held. "You got a useful thought under all that coat, speak it plain."
Vale held his ground. "Plain enough. Caleb met Draven. Caleb hid it. Draven then takes a warning shot and still knows where to find us. Either Caleb gave him what he wanted, or your road reading is not as private as you hope."
Ruth said, "That is not a thought. That is smoke."
"Smoke tells you where the fire may be."
Jed turned back to Caleb. "Did you tell him anything he did not already know."
Caleb shook his head too fast, then winced and steadied himself. "Numbers. Nothing more. He already had most of those."
"Did you tell him where we were bound."
"I do not know where we're bound."
Vale looked at the fire instead of speaking, which told on him more than another question would have.
Jed believed Caleb on that piece. Not because the boy deserved easy trust. Because his anger now carried the helpless edge of a man excluded from the one answer he most wanted to trade. If he knew where Jed meant to go, he would have built the confession around it from the start.
Marrow said, "Belief is costly this far north."
"So is panic."
That cooled the first edge of the thing, not enough to settle it.
Caleb looked across the camp one face at a time as if expecting to find one soft enough to stand in. None were.
"I should've said it the same night." His voice had gone rough and low. "I know that."
Ruth said, "Why didn't you."
He laughed once, ugly as a cough. "Because I thought maybe if I kept quiet and watched harder I'd see him before he saw us. Because I thought maybe not naming him made it smaller. Because I got shot in the gut for telling a man to stop kicking another man and part of me still remembers the ground more than the lesson."
That shut even Vale for a breath or two.
Jed said, "What place did he choose."
"Wash south of camp. Stone root on the left bank. Good ground to hear a man before seeing him."
"And he told you to come again."
"If you walked off alone."
Jed nodded once.
There was the shape of it now. Draven had tried to open a line into camp through the weakest hand and had not yet got what he needed. That did not make Caleb safe. It made him useful to more than one side at once, which was worse.
Marrow picked up the coffee pot again and looked into it as if deciding whether to throw it or pour. "So what now. We tie him to the bench."
Caleb flinched harder at that than at anything else said so far.
Ruth looked at Jed. "If he rides loose, every silence after this will rot."
Wren said, "If you bind him like a prisoner, he becomes one whether he has sold us or not."
Nemi spoke then from the team line.
"The burial trail is ahead."
All of them turned.
The boy came over with one lead rope in his hand. "Old ground. No room to shout on it. No room to drag a man if you want the wagon whole."
Marrow frowned. "How far."
"By tomorrow if you keep north."
Jed looked at him. "And."
Nemi shrugged one shoulder under the blanket. "Elkheart said it takes more than it gives."
That sat in the cold air with the rest.
Marrow poured coffee at last and handed the first cup to no one. He drank it himself. "Fine country."
Jed made the decision because someone had to and because Vale had almost forced that truth into the open an hour earlier.
"No ropes." He looked at Caleb. "No walking out of camp again. Not for piss, not for pain, not for ghosts from Bitter Hollow. You sleep where I can see you and you ride where Marrow can hear you breathe."
Caleb nodded before the last word finished.
"If Draven gets another talk with you, it is because I put you there."
That one landed.
Caleb lowered his eyes and stood still under it.
Vale said, "A practical middle."
Jed looked at him. "You got more to offer than comments."
"I do." Vale came to his feet and brushed the blanket off his coat. "If Draven expects the weak place to stay weak, we can use that."
Wren watched him without approval. "How."
"Let the boy go to the wash once more. Let Draven think the hook still takes."
Marrow said, "And when Draven brings three rifles instead of one."
"Then we learn faster."
Ruth's laugh had no mirth in it. "You gamble with other men the way drunks gamble with cards."
Vale dipped his head to her. "Because cards at least can be shuffled."
Jed said, "No."
Vale's face showed no injury. "No."
"We keep moving."
"And let him choose every next ground."
"He chooses anyway. I just keep him paying for it."
That ended the camp council, if it could be called one.
No one liked the terms. No one had better.
By the time the wagon was hitched and the fire stamped out, the party had changed shape around Caleb without naming it. Ruth no longer let him behind her. Marrow kept him on the bench where one glance could count him. Wren watched the south side and the boy both. Vale watched everyone because that cost him least.
Jed rode ahead into the timber with the burial trail and Draven both waiting somewhere in the next miles.
The road felt narrower already.

Chapter 17: Clementine Finds Lask
The hoof print broke at the toe where the horse had stumbled on stone.
Clementine drew rein above the narrow draw and looked down at the sign from the saddle. Three riders had come through there in poor shape. One horse favoring the right fore. One rider bleeding enough to leave dark spits in the dust where the trail widened. One man behind or beside the hurt one, helping keep him straight in the seat. They had not ridden like hunters since the day before. They had ridden like men trying to get their own damaged pieces somewhere safer before dark.
She knew then she had crossed from Draven's line into Hobb Lask's.
The difference mattered.
Draven's trail kept to high angles, broke across stone, and chose lines that granted him sight before cover. Lask's men used the road when it suited them, left broken brush when it did not, and spent more horse than the country paid back.
Clementine followed anyway.
Part of her wanted north still, toward Jed and the hidden pressure Draven was building there. But Lask ran close enough to that same road to matter and, more useful still, he bled now. Hurt men talked cheaper when the pain got ahead of pride.
She found their noon halt in a patch of alder by a cold spring seep.
The fire there had burned too hot and too quick, green smoke still hanging in the branches though the flames were long dead. One blanket had been torn for bandage strips. A saddlebag lay empty in the mud where they had ransacked their own supplies for cloth or whiskey. Blood darkened the roots beside a stone where somebody had sat while another man worked a wound.
Clementine crouched and read the ground in order.
Lask had been the hurt one. The long-jawed rider had paced and cursed while another hand wrapped him one-armed. He had not taken the bullet clean through the chest or gut. Too much movement after. Arm or upper shoulder then, exactly where Ruth's shot had likely found him under the wash bank.
Good.
Pain enough to slow. Not enough to silence.
She led the mare deeper into brush and tied her where the bit ring would not knock wood if the animal tossed its head. Then she took the draw on foot.
The country tightened over the next mile into rock gullies and half-dead pine. Good hiding ground for men too hurt to keep to open road and too suspicious to stop where wagon dust might find them. She moved at the pace the brush allowed and no faster, rifle in hand, eyes working the cuts above as much as the ground below.
By late afternoon she smelled horse before she saw camp.
Then came low voices and a cough choked off halfway through.
She climbed the left wall of the gully and found them under a broken overhang where old flood had gouged out a pocket in the clay. Two horses tied below. One man sitting with his back to the wall and a carbine across his lap. One on his knees over Lask, tightening fresh bandage around the shoulder and under the arm while Lask cursed him for every pull.
"Tighter, then." Lask's voice carried its usual bite despite the weakness in it. "You tie like a widow."
"You want to keep the arm."
"I want the box."
Clementine settled into prone behind a flat stone and watched a while longer.
Neither man wore Draven's caution. They had the look of road wolves who mistook any hidden place for a den. The sentry kept his eyes on the gully mouth and never once looked up to the rim. The kneeling man had laid the extra cartridges too far from his reaching hand. Lask himself talked too much, which hurt him and yet did not stop him.
She chose the kneeling man first.
The shot took him high through the back and drove him into Lask's lap. Before the sentry had done more than half turn, Clementine slid down the gully wall and sent the second round through the side of his neck at fifteen yards.
Then she had the rifle on Lask while he still fought the dead weight across him.
"Throw the pistol."
He looked up with both surprise and hate in his face together. "You."
"Throw it."
The pistol came out slow from under the blanket at his hip and landed in the dust between them.
Clementine stepped closer, booted it aside, and kept the muzzle on his chest. "You are in poor shape to make a bargain."
Lask bared teeth through the pain. "You should've killed me on the wash."
"I am deciding whether to correct that now."
He laughed once and winced hard enough to shut it off. Blood had worked through the fresh wrap again around the shoulder and down his side. He looked smaller sitting there with another man's body half over him, though nothing in the eyes had softened.
"Then decide."
Clementine kicked the dead man off him and crouched just beyond his reach. "You are not running with Draven."
Lask spat to one side. "I don't run with anybody."
"Good. Then speak for yourself and maybe keep breathing."
The gully held quiet around them except for the horses shifting below and the little run of water somewhere under stone.
"You were after the widow before Draven ever showed on that road."
"I was after what belonged to me before she ever married the liar carrying it."
"Box and coin."
"And names."
There it was again.
"Why names."
Lask's mouth tightened. "Because her dead husband stole with men who thought themselves clever. Clever men write things down. Then one dies and the others pretend there never was a count. Those names say the count was real."
"So you can kill them."
"So I can collect."
That answer fit him better.
Clementine shifted the rifle a little lower toward the breastbone. "Draven wants Jed alive."
Lask's expression flickered once, not fear, not ignorance exactly. Annoyance. "Then you know more than you look."
"Enough."
"Draven's got no share in my box."
"But he is on the same road."
"Then the road's crowded."
He was losing color by the breath now. The wound had reopened under the move and the fresh bandage work had done little more than slow it.
"How many men left to you."
"On this trail." He looked at the two dead and then back. "None worth naming after today."
"Where did you think the widow was heading."
"Spur first." He swallowed. "Then farther if the list gave her courage. Her husband had a north stash in mind. Never told her clean, I reckon, or she'd have gone there already."
North stash.
Not a location. A shape.
Clementine watched his face as he said it and judged he believed at least that much true, whether he had heard it from the dead husband directly or pieced it out from ledger scraps and grievance.
"Stone room."
Lask's eyes narrowed a shade.
Small move. Enough.
"You know the name."
"I know there was a place where paper kept dry and men lied to themselves in comfort." Blood showed on his teeth when he smiled. "If your brace-legged friend rides there, he rides into old filth."
Jed was already doing exactly that, then.
Clementine stood.
Lask looked up at her and must have read the decision in the shift of her shoulders or the set of the rifle because his jaw worked once before he spoke.
"You leave me, I die slow."
"You earned that road."
"Then save a bullet."
She looked at the gully, the dead men, the horses, the blood worked into dirt and clay, and the long days still north between her and Jed.
Then she did save the bullet.
She took Lask's horses, his cartridges, and the water skin that had not been fouled. She left him the bandage roll, the blanket, and the pistol beyond easy reach.
"If you live till dark, use your left hand better than your right."
Lask laughed once more, weaker than before. "Widow's friend."
"No."
She stepped back down the gully toward the mare. "Just ahead of you on the count."
By the time she rode north again, the country had already begun to close around evening. Lask's words rode with her whether she wanted them or not.
North stash.
Stone room.
Old filth.
Draven and Lask were not partners. That was the useful part. Two dangers on one road worked against each other as much as beside. Jed might still be alive because each man wanted a different thing from the same company.
That did not make the odds good.
It only made them countable.

Chapter 18: Burial Trail
Nemi stopped in the road and touched the mule's skull with two fingers.
The thing hung from a peeled pole at shoulder height where the old trail split away from the fresher wagon line. Weather had whitened the bone and left one crack running through the forehead, but someone had wired feathers through the eye sockets long ago and the wire remained dark against the pale.
Marrow hauled the team down at once.
"That the place."
Nemi nodded and did not move his hand from the bone until the silence around it had done its work.
The burial trail went north through low cedar, then vanished among stone folds and black timber older than the road they had ridden thus far. No wheel rut showed from where they stood. Only a shallow line where grass had once given way to repeated passage and then grown back thin.
Jed looked at the fresher road ahead and then at the skull marker.
The new road looked easier. Wider too. That alone made it suspect now.
"Elkheart told you this trail takes more than it gives."
Nemi dropped his hand. "Yes."
"Why use it."
"Because the other road lies when men cut it."
Marrow cursed under his breath and climbed down from the bench. He walked to the pole, looked at the skull, looked at the shadow under the cedars beyond it, and spat nowhere near either.
"Fine." He slapped dust off his hands. "We go with the dead instead of the fresh liars."
No one laughed.
They unloaded part of the wagon before taking the first yard. The trail proved too narrow and root-bound for full weight in its early turn, so crates, grain sacks, and the lighter gear went by hand in relays while Marrow and Jed coaxed the wagon between stumps blackened by age and old fire scars. Wren took the off wheel. Ruth carried her own box. Vale carried what could be carried without labor improving his character and no more. Caleb walked because the trail would jolt him worse on the bench, though each step cost him and everyone could see it.
Nemi led.
He did not lead by hurry. He led by knowing where not to place a wheel.
The burial trail crossed low stone shelves and old root webs with no room for two men side by side. On both sides of it rose timber too thick and old for wind to pass freely through. Sound died there instead of traveling. A snapped twig three yards off seemed farther than a horse team on the next ridge.
By noon nobody spoke except to name the next burden or the next obstacle.
Jed preferred it that way.
The right leg took the slow stop-start work poorly. Leading horses over root and stone meant lifting, setting, bracing, and lifting again. No rhythm to settle into. The old break answered each new angle with a different stripe of ache. He kept his eyes on the traces and on Nemi's back where the boy moved ahead through the cedars like he had been born under them.
At one narrow pass between leaning trunks, Vale dropped the crate he carried and looked behind.
"Anyone else feel buried before the dirt."
Ruth came by him with the box against her ribs. "No. Only delayed."
Marrow said, "Pick it up."
Vale did.
The trail rose through a stand of spruce so old the lower limbs had died and hung gray as bones. Beyond that it dipped into a fold of ground where stones half buried in moss stood in rows that were too deliberate to be accident and too weathered to read by any mark left on them.
Burial ground.
Nemi slowed, then went slower still.
They all followed.
No one had asked for that instruction, yet the place drew it from them the way cold drew breath.
The wagon wheels sounded ugly on that ground. Every crunch of gravel, every axle groan, every chain clink came back as if the trail itself objected to being used and had forgotten how to hide the complaint. Marrow took off his hat once without seeming to know he had done it. Wren bowed his head when passing the first row of stones. Ruth kept the box close. Caleb looked everywhere but at the markers.
Vale watched the rest of them more than the ground.
That was how Jed caught him.
The card man had dropped back with two torn strips of white cloth in one hand and was reaching toward a low branch at the edge of the trail while the others strained at the wagon over a root hump.
Jed crossed the three strides between them before Vale had the first knot tied.
He caught Vale by the wrist and pinned it against the branch hard enough to flatten bark.
"What are you doing."
Vale did not pretend confusion. That irritated Jed more than a lie would have. "Marking where a wagon can return without prayer."
Jed twisted the wrist until the cloth fell.
"For yourself."
"For whoever lives long enough to need it."
The answer had enough truth in it to be dangerous.
Ruth had turned by then. Marrow too. Wren left the wheel and came back three paces with the rifle not raised but close. Caleb stared like a sick man watching a brawl from under church pews. Nemi stood still near the wagon tongue and looked at the dropped cloth strips on the ground.
Jed took Vale's other hand and found the second strip tucked in the palm.
"You leave sign on this trail again and I break fingers one at a time."
Vale's face lost color but not its shape. "Dramatic."
"Practical."
Marrow came close enough to see the cloth and then looked out into the timber behind them where Draven's patience might already be measuring the same miles.
"He was laying a line for the man on our back trail."
Vale flexed once against Jed's grip and then went still when it bought him nothing. "I was laying a line for any return at all. We have a wounded boy, one less wagon horse, and no town within easy reach. A marked road behind us may yet save more than it costs."
Ruth said, "Or sell it."
"Only if sold to buyers."
Wren spoke before the argument could widen. "The dead do not need his cloth. We do not need it either."
Jed released the wrist but kept the cloth. "Walk in front of me till dark."
Vale looked at him. "As prisoner."
"As man with poor judgment."
Marrow said, "Which is a wider office."
No one argued that.
Nemi stooped and picked the fallen strips from the dirt. He carried them to the middle of the trail and fed them into the little cook fire they built that evening under stone. He did it without looking at Vale once.
That cut deeper than any speech could have.
They came out of the burial ground near dusk onto a ridge saddle where the trees thinned just enough to show a far piece of south country through the black trunks. No one stopped to admire it. They stopped because the horses had nothing left for another hard mile and because even Marrow admitted the wagon needed rest before the next shelf.
Camp there felt smaller than the camps before it.
Not in ground. In trust.
Vale took his blanket where Jed could see him. Caleb lay down without supper and shook once with chill before the blankets settled him. Ruth slept with the box under both arms now and the pistol in reach. Wren kept first watch with the rifle already clear of the case. Marrow sat by the fire longer than usual, looking not at the flames but at the dark below the ridge where Draven might one day find those white cloth strips and wonder why they had ended.
Jed stood a while beyond the firelight and looked back the way they had come.
No lantern.
No horse.
No sign at all.
That meant nothing good.

Chapter 19: Marrow's Last Drive
The wheel struck stone and came off the rim with a sound like a gun butt on bone.
Marrow hauled the team down at once, but the wagon had already slewed half sideways on the shelf. The left rear wheel hung canted under the box with one split spoke kicked clear and the iron tire riding too loose to hold true another yard. Below the road the mountainside fell away in ledges, brush, and open air enough to make every horse eye white.
Jed swung down into the brake line before the team could decide the matter for themselves.
"Hold them."
Marrow was already there with the lead pair by the bits and both heels dug into the shelf. Wren came off the bench with the rifle case on one shoulder and dropped it aside. Ruth climbed down with the box under one arm and the pistol easy in the other hand. Vale joined the ground with more care than dignity. Caleb sat frozen on the rear bench until Nemi took his sleeve and pulled him down.
The shelf gave them no space for panic.
Jed crouched by the broken wheel and put one hand to the hub. The spindle had held. Good. One spoke gone, another cracked, and the tire spread from the blow. A road repair, maybe, if they could get the weight off, clear the wheel, and rebind what would hold until flatter country.
"Can it run."
"If we make it."
"Then make it."
No tree nearby would take a proper block line. They had only the team for leverage and their own backs for the rest.
"Jack the axle with rock and pole. Then lash the tire and sister the spoke."
Wren nodded and went for the pry timber before the last word settled.
Ruth set the box down where the uphill wheel shadow hid it and came back. "Tell me where."
"Keep the rear from walking downhill."
She planted beside the box side without another word.
Vale looked at the drop. "If it slips while we're under it, no repair matters."
Marrow barked a laugh with no breath to spare. "Then stand somewhere your death offends you less."
Vale took the downhill brace line after that.
Nemi moved among the horses' heads with a calm hand and a quiet voice too low for Jed to catch. Caleb stood pale by the wall with a rope coil and enough shame in him to keep from offering more than he could bear.
They worked the wagon up by inches.
Wren wedged the pry pole under the axle and heaved while Jed stacked flat stone under the lift. Ruth and Vale held the box from walking downhill. Marrow kept the team locked to the road with wrists, shoulders, and plain venom. Twice the axle slipped off stone and slammed back with a jolt that shot clean through Jed's right leg. The third stack held.
"Wheel."
Jed and Wren got it off together.
The broken spoke had split near the hub, not the rim. Better. Jed cut a straight length from the spare singletree under the bed, planed it rough with the knife, and fit it against the cracked spoke while Wren bound both with rawhide and trace wire from Marrow's repair roll. The tire had spread and needed heat to seat it.
Marrow saw the problem and spat. "Fast."
Jed fed splinters and grease scrap into a tight little fire behind stone while Wren held the iron edge over it with pliers. Ruth brought water. Vale fetched stones without being told because by then he was being measured only as useful or waste.
When the iron had warmed enough, Jed and Wren drove it back down over the rim with the back of the axe and a maul head until the hoop rang true.
"Water."
Ruth poured. Steam came up white and fast.
The wheel tightened.
Good enough.
They set it back under the axle with the whole party's weight. Jed worked the nut home. Wren reset the pin. Marrow eased the team one step forward and the repaired wheel turned half a circle with no wobble big enough to kill them.
Then the shot came.
It hit Marrow in the chest.
No warning crack first. Only the body hit and the old driver thrown backward off the team line as if a mule had kicked him. The sound reached them a breath later from somewhere across the open south face below the shelf.
Ruth dropped behind the box side. Wren dove for the rifle. Vale flattened against the stone wall. The horses lurched and might have taken the wagon with them if Nemi had not somehow stayed at their heads until Jed reached the line.
"Hold."
The command came to horses and men both.
Jed shoved the team back against the uphill wall and grabbed Marrow under the shoulders. Blood had already spread dark across the old man's coat. Entry high on the left side. Bad.
Marrow's eyes were open.
"Damn him." The words frothed red at the lip.
Draven.
No one had to name it.
Wren slid into a kneeling rest behind the repaired wheel and searched the south country. "Long angle. Broken rocks below the shelf. I need another flash or movement."
None came.
That was Draven too. One shot, full value, then silence thick enough to do the rest of the work for him.
Jed eased Marrow down with his back to the stone wall. Ruth came over on her knees and put both hands to the coat, pressing above the wound and below it in the way a person did when there was nothing clean to be done and still the hands refused idleness.
Marrow caught Jed's sleeve.
"Reins."
"I got them."
"No. You keep them till Spur or ground takes them. You hear."
Jed nodded once.
Wren fired at last movement below the shelf. A puff of stone dust jumped far off and then the country swallowed the line again. Whether Draven had moved or only changed position, none of them could tell.
Caleb crouched by the wheel with both hands over his mouth. Vale looked from Marrow to the south face and back, for once with no cleverness left in him.
Marrow breathed shallow and fast. "Tie them short. No waiting on me."
Jed looked at the team. Then at the repaired wheel. Then at the road ahead no broader than before and meaner now for what it had taken.
Wren kept his eyes on the south face. "We move now. He may be shifting for another line."
Ruth looked up. "He is dying."
Marrow answered that himself. "Then I can die on a wagon same as anywhere."
There was no kindness in the road north. Only choices about where to lay the cost.
Jed and Wren lifted him to the wagon bed. Ruth climbed in beside him. Caleb tried to help and nearly spilled before Nemi caught his elbow and set him back straight. Vale took the lead rope on the spare horse without being asked. Marrow's blood marked the boards from tailgate to blanket.
The wagon rolled again.
Wren kept the rifle out and watched the south face through every opening. Jed rode the team line now where Marrow had stood, reins wrapped once around his wrist the way the old man preferred on bad ground. The repaired wheel held.
By dusk they had made a half mile that felt like ten and found a pocket of timber off the shelf deep enough to hide low firelight.
Marrow lasted until they got him under canvas shadow.
He looked once at the team, once at the reins in Jed's hand, and once at the dark beyond the wagon where the road still ran north.
"Paid before the pass closed."
Then the breath left him.
No one said anything for a long while after.
The wagon stood there with one mended wheel, one dead driver, and one more debt on every soul riding in it.

Chapter 20: Vale Makes His Offer
Vale waited until the fire had burned low and the dead had settled into the camp like weather.
Marrow lay under a tarp at the far edge where the ground stayed driest. They had wrapped him in one blanket and tied the corners because no one wanted the night wind lifting cloth off his face while the rest tried to sleep. Ruth had sat with him first, then Wren, then no one. The team stood close and quiet as if they too knew the hand that had driven them longest was no longer walking among them.
Jed took first watch and then refused to sleep when Wren came to relieve him.
That left him outside the circle when Vale began.
The card man had chosen his place well. Near enough to the fire that Ruth, Caleb, and Wren could all hear. Far enough from Marrow's tarp to keep the dead from answering with their own accusation. Nemi sat two wagon lengths off in the dark and looked at nothing any of them could yet see.
Vale held his hands out to the coals and spoke as though continuing a conversation that had not yet started.
"There is one bargain left on this road that has not been named plain."
Ruth looked up first.
"Then keep it unnamed."
"I think not." Vale shifted his gaze from her to Wren and then to Caleb. "Marrow is dead. Lask still breathes somewhere behind. Draven sits across every bad opening ahead with a rifle and the patience of winter." He turned one palm up to the firelight. "And all of it grips to Jed."
Wren did not move.
"You choose a hard hour for cowardice."
"I choose the hour for arithmetic."
Caleb stared into the coals. Ruth's hand drifted to the pistol hidden under the blanket near her knee.
Vale saw all of it and went on.
"Draven has shown his terms without speaking them whole. The wagon means nothing to him. The box means nothing to him. He wants Jed alive long enough to lead him where this trail ends." Vale nodded toward the tarp where Marrow lay. "So hand Jed over while breath still means trade and the rest of us may yet ride to Spur."
Caleb looked up at that with naked hatred.
"You'd sell him."
"I would exchange one certainty for a chance, yes."
"You'd sell any of us."
Vale let that pass. "He is the pivot. You know it as well as I do."
Ruth said, "No."
The single word cut the camp cleaner than shouting would have.
Vale turned toward her with something almost gentle in his face. "You are protecting a man you scarcely know."
"I know enough."
"Do you." He nodded at the box tucked under her blanket edge. "Without him, Draven's eye might come off that box and your husband's filth inside it."
"Without him, Draven gets what he wants and kills us in better order after."
Wren spoke next.
"No."
Vale looked at him. "From scripture or judgment."
"From both." Wren's hands rested on the rifle laid across his knees. "A man who bargains with a wolf while standing in blood should not expect the bargain to outlive the smell."
For the first time that night, Vale's patience thinned.
"You say that because you still imagine clean lines where the rest of us have mud."
"No." Wren's voice stayed level. "I say it because I have met men like Draven and men like you. The first kills from need, pleasure, or habit. The second always says there was no better number to work with."
Caleb gave a hard short laugh at that and then shut his mouth because laughing still hurt.
Vale looked to him at once. "And you. If Draven had not shot you through, you would still be under his hand. Spare me righteousness from a door left halfway open."
Caleb half rose before the pain knocked the strength out of it. "I walked away from him."
"After he put lead in you."
"Still farther than you ever walked from your own kind."
That landed.
Vale's eyes flattened.
Ruth slid her hand fully onto the pistol then. "Enough."
Vale ignored her and looked toward the dark beyond the fire where Jed stood outside the ring, hearing all of it without coming in.
"You hear, I hope."
Jed stepped into the light at last.
"I hear."
Vale held his gaze. "Then answer it."
Jed looked at each of them once before answering. Ruth with the pistol ready under the blanket. Wren steady behind the rifle. Caleb sick and furious. Nemi dark and still. Marrow dead under canvas. Vale measuring all of it as if campfire ethics were no more than a changeable stake.
"If any hand here wants me traded, say it now."
No one but Vale moved.
That was the answer, yet Vale was fool enough or brave enough to force it one step farther.
"I do."
Jed nodded once, almost grateful for the plainness.
"Good."
Jed crossed the last two steps to Vale before the man had time to decide whether plainness carried consequence. One hand caught the card man by the coat front and pinned him against the wagon wheel with enough force to knock the breath out first and the pride after.
"You speak trade so easy because the goods are never yours."
Vale's hands came up, not to fight, only to save his throat from the coat collar biting into it.
"Practical," he managed.
"No." Jed held him there. "Cheap."
Ruth said nothing. Wren said nothing. Caleb watched with the hard starving look of a man hoping one hurt in camp might finally find the right target.
Jed released Vale and stepped back before the moment turned into more than it needed to be.
"You ride in front of me at dawn. You leave no sign, ask no names, and keep both hands where men can see them. If not, you walk. If you walk off, no one follows."
Vale straightened the coat slowly. "You mistake me for frightened."
"No. I count you selfish."
That shut him better than force had.
Nemi spoke from the dark then, the first word from him since Marrow died.
"Road below Spur by tomorrow."
Everyone looked that way though nothing yet could be seen.
Jed took the watch after that because sleep had no purchase left in him. Wren did not argue. Ruth kept the pistol under the blanket and the box against her ribs. Caleb lay awake longer than the rest, eyes on the tarp where Marrow's shape no longer moved. Vale rolled over twice before stillness finally took him.
Only Nemi and Jed stayed fully in the night.
At one point the boy came to stand beside him near the team.
"He meant it."
Jed knew which man he meant. "Yes."
"Would the others."
"Not tonight."
Nemi looked south where no light showed. "Tomorrow changes men."
Jed rested one hand on Marrow's lead horse, feeling the animal's warmth through hide and winter coat. "It changes road. Men show what was there."
Nemi took that in and said no more.
When dawn came thin through the trees, Marrow was still dead, Vale was still with them, and the bargain he had named hung over camp anyway because once spoken on a road like that it never went all the way back into silence.

Chapter 21: Spur Below
The ridge fell away under Jed's horse and put Spur below them like a handful of boards dropped in a basin.
He drew up at the shelf edge and looked down through morning haze. Two streets at most. Livery, storehouse, roof lines, one church steeple too white for the country around it. Smoke rose from three chimneys and ran flat in the wind. The south road entered by the creek and the north trail left again toward harsher country where no one built unless forced.
Spur had never mattered to him except as a landmark in Ketch's dying directions.
Now it mattered because it proved the count.
Stone room. East face. Second ridgeline past Frostfang. Four days north of Spur.
He looked beyond the basin.
There.
Past the first ridge shoulder and the next dark timber line rose the second ridgeline, harder in shape than the rest and cut on the east face by a pale scar of exposed rock. A cold eye would not call it a room or a hiding place. A man carrying Ketch's words had no need to call it anything else.
He heard the wagon before it reached the shelf and turned back.
The team came slower now under his hand than under Marrow's, not because the animals had forgotten the road, but because the road had learned new ways to tax them. The repaired wheel still held. Ruth rode on the bench with the box at her feet and one hand near the pistol under her coat. Wren kept the rifle case open across his knees. Caleb sat in the wagon bed wrapped against wind and weakness. Vale rode where Jed ordered, ahead and plain to see. Nemi came last on the spare horse, small and steady.
They drew up in a loose half circle and looked down at Spur.
Marrow's absence sat there among them like another rider.
Ruth said, "That little."
Wren nodded. "Enough for burial ground and beans."
Vale studied the basin and then the north country beyond it. "Also enough to prove none of us are bound there."
No one answered him because the truth had already shifted.
Jed looked north again.
Draven would know it too if he had read any true piece of Ketch's trail. Spur was not the end. Spur was the measure.
Nemi alone looked unsurprised.
"Open camp below."
Jed followed the tilt of the boy's chin.
Half a mile north of Spur and below the second ridgeline lay a spread of canvas and rough sheds near a stream crossing. Not town. Not homestead. Temporary camp. Too large for one family, too small for a proper freight station.
Men moved there now.
Riders between tents. One wagon with no team hitched. Two picket lines. Smoke from a cook fire. No effort at concealment because the camp did not yet know it had been seen from above.
Vale said, "Well."
Wren had the rifle half out of the case before the word finished. "Too open."
Ruth's voice dropped. "Draven."
Jed nodded.
No one else would set camp exactly where the north country narrowed and travelers had to choose between descending to Spur or holding the harsher line beyond it. Draven had done what he always did. Got there first where patience could work for him.
"Count them."
Wren took the glass. "Four plain. Maybe six if the tents hold more."
Jed had already caught enough shape for one more count of his own. "Vale."
The card man looked at him and then at the camp below. "If you mean to accuse me of correspondence, I would ask first for paper and better weather."
"I mean your eyes are good. Count."
Vale took the glass. "Four moving. One likely down with the team wagon. Another, maybe, under canvas or on watch where he is not obliged to be seen." He passed the glass back. "You may have seven. You do not have ten."
Still enough to kill them in bad ground.
They moved off the skyline at once and made a hidden halt in scrub pine twenty yards below the shelf. Jed kept the team under cover and posted Wren with the rifle line back toward the basin. Ruth and Caleb stayed by the wagon. Vale stood where he could be watched by all three. Nemi vanished uphill and returned before anyone finished asking where he had gone.
"One horse came from that camp not long ago." He pointed south along a side cut below the shelf. "Went down, not up."
Draven scouting or sending a scout.
Jed looked at the side cut, the camp below, and the second ridgeline north.
He could still take the party down to Spur, bury Marrow proper, buy feed, gather gossip, and lose time.
He could also ride straight north and admit the camp below had already narrowed the game to one last push.
Ruth solved part of it by naming what the others circled.
"If we go into Spur with this box, men will count it by supper."
Vale said, "If we avoid Spur, men below camp will count us by noon."
Wren looked at Jed. "So."
Before he could answer, a voice came up the slope from the cut below.
"No need to work so hard at the choice."
Draven rode into sight alone on a gray horse with both hands plain on the reins.
He had chosen his line well. Near enough to speak without shouting. Far enough that no pistol in camp could do more than bark at him. His rifle sat in the scabbard, not in his hands. That meant he wanted the moment more than the kill.
Ruth's pistol appeared at once anyway. Wren lifted the rifle. Jed held one hand out without taking his eyes off Draven.
"Not yet."
Draven stopped under a stunted pine and looked over the whole party, weighing each of them in open daylight.
"Less one driver."
No one answered.
"And still you keep moving north."
Jed stepped out from the wagon line. "You came up to save us the walk."
Draven almost smiled. "I came up because the ridge gives no gift to men who misunderstand where they stand." He looked once toward Spur and then north. "You know now the town is not your answer."
Ruth said, "What do you want."
Draven's eyes went to her, then to the box, then back to Jed as if neither were more than roadside brush to the matter. "What I have always wanted. The place he carries and the chance to see it before any frightened hand burns it or buries it under lies."
That told Jed one more useful thing. Draven did not yet know whether the place had already been compromised through Ruth's husband or the dead trader. He only knew the lines converged.
"Trade."
Vale made the smallest motion at that word.
Jed noticed.
Draven went on. "You give me the location. These others go down to Spur alive. I watch them do it."
Ruth gave one hard laugh. "You shoot from open snow and ask credit after."
"I did not shoot the widow on the snow."
Wren kept the rifle half raised. "No. You shot at the space around us and called it mercy."
Draven looked at him with the recognition one killer gave another after enough sign had been traded. "Preacher."
"Only one of the two names matters to you."
Draven let that pass. His eyes returned to Jed. "One place for six lives. Good price."
Jed looked at the camp below and the ridge beyond it. He looked at Ruth's box, Caleb's drawn face, Wren's rifle, Vale's stillness, Nemi's unreadable calm.
"No."
Draven breathed out through his nose as if he had expected the word and only wanted to hear it.
"Then I take the slower road."
"You already are."
For the first time a little heat entered Draven's face.
"By tomorrow, one of them will be worth less than the secret in your head. I wonder which one you choose first when the count gets honest."
He turned the gray horse before anyone answered and rode back down the cut toward his camp, not hurried, not careless.
Wren kept the rifle on him until the trees took him.
Only then did anyone move.
Ruth sat down hard on the wagon step. Caleb looked sick in a new way. Vale said nothing, which in him counted for more than talk.
Jed looked north to the second ridgeline and knew the last part of the road had begun.

Chapter 22: Clementine Comes North
The branch bent and stayed bent where the horse had passed under it too hard.
Clementine drew the gray mare down behind a split boulder and studied the camp below through the last cedar on the slope. She had come in from the burial trail by a harder line than the wagon could have taken, following Lask's words north and then leaving the road entirely when Draven's newer sign began to cross the older ground. The sign had grown plainer near Spur. More men. More horses. Less need for Draven to hide every cut once he had the country below him and the second ridgeline behind.
Now she could see why.
Jed's party sat under scrub pine below the ridge shelf, wagon tucked out of the skyline, team close, rifle out, no fire showing in the open. Men who had already been counted and meant to survive the count if they could. Draven's lower camp lay half a mile off where the road to Spur and the north trail both had to declare themselves. Good ground for a waiting wolf. Bad ground for everyone else.
Clementine lay flat behind the stone and read the upper camp in order.
Ruth first. Box still close, pistol closer.
Wren second. Rifle case open, no pretense left.
Caleb pale and weaker than he had been farther south, but upright enough to matter.
Vale where he could watch all faces without turning his own far.
Nemi near the team with that same quiet that never seemed childlike once the road tightened.
And Jed at the north edge looking beyond the rest, not because he trusted the camp, but because the camp was no longer the whole of the problem.
Marrow was not there.
She found the tarp shape near the wagon after another moment and understood.
No surprise in it. Only a hard little hollowing of the chest where one more capable hand had gone out of a narrow road. Jed would have counted that absence in plain arithmetic before naming any other hurt.
Clementine watched Vale longer than the others.
The clean one stood a half step away from the group and too often toward Jed rather than Draven's lower line. Not fear exactly. Calculation. A man deciding which danger he might someday persuade into business.
Good to know.
She shifted the rifle butt in the crook of her shoulder and kept still.
The lower camp moved without panic. That meant Draven had not yet committed to a rush or a strike. One rider came up from the Spur road with a grain sack and two tins tied behind the saddle. Another took a horse to water and back. No laughter down there. No waste either. Draven had six or seven hands as the ridge would suggest and each of them knew enough to keep the shape of camp lean.
She counted three possible shooting lanes from her boulder to the lower line and one cleaner lane to the upper camp if Vale or another fool forced the question too fast.
Still she did not reveal herself.
Draven counted impatience as one of the common sins and harvested it where he could. Better to know the field first.
Late in the light, a movement by the upper team line caught her eye. Nemi had turned and was looking upslope not at her exact stone but near enough that only a worse hunter would have mistaken the meaning.
The boy knew she was there.
He did not point. Did not call. He only looked once, then back to the horses, giving her the choice of whether she wished to remain a rumor.
That too was good to know.
She let a count of fifty pass before moving again.
By then Jed had come back toward the center of camp and Vale had gone to the wagon side where the others could see both hands. Something had happened there before she arrived. The shape of the people told it even if no words had.
When the sun finally dropped low enough to flatten the ridge shadows, Draven rode partway up from his own camp, stopped in a cut below the upper shelf, and spoke to them from there. Clementine could not catch every word from the boulder, but she saw enough.
No rifle in hand.
Offer posture.
Jed refusing.
Ruth hard and still.
Wren ready to cut the whole talk off with lead if it turned one shade worse.
And Vale, at the edge, listening too closely for terms.
When Draven rode back down, Clementine chose her hour.
She led the mare by the reins through the rock shadow to a narrow deer line that came out above the upper camp on its blind side. Nemi heard the small stone she dislodged and looked up again. This time he lifted one hand palm down, not warning, only acknowledgment.
Jed turned at the same instant.
His hand found the pistol before his face found hers.
She stepped into the last of the light with the rifle low and the mare quiet beside her.
"You took your time."
The words came from Jed without heat, which was its own kind of welcome.
"You stayed alive long enough to complain."
Ruth let out a breath she had been keeping since the first shape moved at the camp edge. Wren lowered the rifle one inch and no more. Caleb stared as if a ghost had ridden out of the ridge. Vale looked at Clementine and then at Jed and learned something from the distance between them that no one bothered to explain.
Nemi said, "I told no one."
Clementine glanced his way. "Good."
Jed came the last two steps and took the mare's reins from her hand. His eyes flicked once over her coat, her rifle, her saddle roll, reading for holes and blood before manners. Finding none, he nodded toward the fireless center.
"Come in."
She did.
No one asked where she had come from until she had a cup of water in hand and the mare tied with the others.
Then Ruth said, "Draven."
Clementine looked around the little camp and gave them the count plain.
"He is not riding with Lask."
Caleb sat forward at once.
"You found Lask."
"I found his men and what was left of him." She drank once more, then set the cup down. "He was wounded in the shoulder. Two men left with him. No more now."
Wren's eyes narrowed a little. "Dead."
"Yes."
Ruth did not smile. "Good."
Vale folded his arms. "And what did the good news cost."
"Two rounds."
That shut him a moment.
Jed said, "What did Lask know."
"Enough." She looked at Ruth and then at the box. "There was a north stash tied to your husband's papers. He had the shape of a stone room even if he did not know the road. And he knew Draven wanted something else on the same trail."
Ruth's face hardened but did not break. She had stopped being surprised by her husband's dirty reach and now only counted fresh length in it.
Jed said nothing for a while. Then:
"Vale listened to Draven's offer with too much care."
Straight to it. Good.
Vale took the hit with less calm than before. "I listened because sane men count bargains before refusing them."
Clementine looked at him. "And because men like you prefer live wolves you can talk to over dead ones you can't use."
The clean one smiled without pleasure. "You arrive late and judge quick."
"I arrived in time."
That ended the line.
She gave them the rest then. Lask's camp. The names. The way the lower road pinched. The fact that Draven had six or seven hands in open camp and likely one moving scout besides. The fact that his lower ground was strong only as long as Jed's party stayed above deciding and below action.
When she finished, no one spoke for a beat.
Then Nemi said, "The old crossing north is not his."
All eyes went to the boy.
He pointed not toward Spur or Draven's camp, but beyond, toward the second ridgeline in the darkening north.
"There is another water line under the east face. Forgotten if you do not grow up hearing where it freezes first."
Jed looked at him and then at Clementine.
At last the shape of the next move had room to exist.

Chapter 23: Stone Room
Nemi stepped into the creek without testing it first.
The water ran black under shelf ice and looked deeper than it was. Clementine held the gray mare at the edge while Jed led the wagon team one careful head at a time into the narrow crossing the boy had named. No road mark showed there. No old cut. Only a seam between stone ledges where the stream had worn the bed flat and where an animal might pass if guided by someone who knew how the cold settled under the overhang.
That was enough.
Draven's camp still sat below Spur behind them, counting the obvious roads and waiting for Jed to choose badly. Nemi's forgotten line gave them something else, though not comfort. The creek cut rose hard north after the crossing and pinched the wagon so close between stone and root that twice they had to unload half the bed and relay it by hand. Marrow's repaired wheel held through all of it as if old spite had stayed in the spokes after the man died.
By full afternoon they climbed out of the creek into harsher country.
The second ridgeline stood before them now, no longer a shape beyond other shapes but the thing itself. Pale rock on the east face. Black timber shouldering both sides. Wind with no town smell in it. The world above Spur had gone back to what it was before anyone drew a street.
Jed dismounted on the last rise and looked a long while at the stone face.
"There."
He said it low, but the whole party heard.
No one mistook the word.
Vale came nearer than he had been allowed all day and peered up the slope as though the right angle might turn bare stone into confession. Ruth kept the box under one arm and the pistol tucked close. Wren brought the rifle clear of the case and looked not at the face but the timber lines to either side where men might wait if they had guessed enough. Caleb stood by the wagon wheel breathing through his teeth and trying not to bend. Clementine watched Jed because the place mattered first through what it did to his silence.
Nemi pointed to a notch in the brush well below the obvious shelf.
"Not there. Lower. Behind the dead spruce."
The dead spruce stood gray and half fallen, ugly enough that most eyes would have passed over it on their way to the clearer scar above. Jed went there first. He moved aside the hanging branches and found the cut in the stone behind them, no bigger than a shed door and half hidden by angle and shadow until a man stood inside the right ten feet of country.
The stone room sat beyond.
One chamber only. Dry floor. Masoned walls gone green in the joints but still sound. A narrow shelf built into one side. Two old lantern hooks. Three freight crates collapsed in one corner under mildew and rat-chewed canvas. No gold gleam. No hidden empire. Only the hard plain evidence of a place men once used because it kept weather out and questions farther still.
Vale let out the breath he had been saving and almost laughed.
"Well. Not treasure."
Ruth stepped in after him and looked around once. "No. Worse."
The crates and shelf held paper.
Not fresh. Not all legible. But enough.
Ledgers. Wrapped packets of letters gone brittle at the folds. Receipts with names bled by damp but not erased. Tallies in three different hands. Freight marks. Route notations. Town names from farther south. Spur once or twice. Cimarron Bend in one margin half rubbed away. The room had been exactly what Ketch's dying words and Ruth's dead husband's letter both suggested: a relay cache for stolen goods, split proceeds, and records too dirty to keep in town rooms where clerks might ask the wrong question.
Jed crossed to the shelf and touched one ledger edge with two fingers as if confirming it had the same weight as memory.
"This is it."
Clementine looked at the papers and then at him. "Enough to answer the dead trader."
"Enough to answer part of him."
Vale had already taken one packet in hand.
Wren's voice stopped him.
"Put it down."
Vale did not at first.
"There may be names in this room that matter more than the rest of our lives." He looked from the papers to Jed and back. "Burning them blind would be a sin against good accounting if nothing else."
Ruth kept the pistol steady. "Good accounting is how men like your friends lie to themselves in columns."
Vale ignored her. "This proves a larger trail. Not a myth. Not one dead husband with a pocket habit. A real line of goods and profits. There is value in that."
Jed looked over. "To who."
Vale smiled a little with the papers still in hand. "To any man who can read a network before another man burns it."
That was plain enough at last.
Caleb said, "He wants the room same as Draven."
"No." Vale turned the packet once between his fingers. "Draven wants pursuit finished. I want what pursuit was chasing."
Ruth drew the pistol.
Wren did not raise the rifle because he did not need to. The room was too tight and Vale knew it.
Clementine watched the door.
Jed said, "Put it back."
Vale held still another second. Then he set the packet down on the shelf with care that made the gesture insult worse, not better.
He let the words out with a thin, impatient smile. "You all mistake scale for rot. This room could name routes, depots, partners, safe buyers. It could turn every loss on this road into leverage if held by a hand with sense."
Ruth's pistol never moved from his chest. "My husband died counting that way."
"And still counted better than the men who buried him."
The sound outside came then.
One horse snorting.
One man shifting rock under a boot.
Too near.
Nemi was already at the door before anyone else fully turned. He looked out once and flattened to the wall.
"Down slope. Three. Maybe more behind."
Draven had not stayed blind after all.
Wren took the left side of the doorway. Clementine the right. Jed shoved Vale back from the shelf and went past them both into the opening just in time to see the first rider break from cedar below. Not Draven. One of the lower camp hands, rifle up and too slow to use it.
Wren shot him off the horse before the barrel settled.
The animal screamed and bolted downslope into the brush.
Another gun cracked from lower still and blew stone chips off the dead spruce trunk.
Draven's men had found the upper line.
Jed came back inside the room and looked at the papers once more.
All that blood and road and winter for shelves of proof no one living could safely own.
He made the choice then because the choice had been waiting longer than any of them.
"No one takes this out."
Ruth nodded first.
Clementine next.
Wren said, "Good."
Vale looked as though someone had put a knife through a favored card.
"You cannot mean to burn the whole answer."
Jed met his eyes. "Watch me."

Chapter 24: Fire in the Relay
Jed kicked the lantern shelf free from the wall and sent glass into the stone.
Oil spread over paper and old wood faster than any prayer would have liked. One match would finish it once he had the room's people set where they needed setting. Smoke already began to rise from the cracked lantern reservoir and the spilled lamp wick at his boots.
Outside, another shot struck the dead spruce and came through the branches in splinters.
Wren fired back from the left of the doorway without hurry. Clementine took the right and worked the slope below, forcing Draven's nearer hands to keep cover instead of rushing the entrance. Ruth kept the pistol on Vale and the box under her left arm. Caleb sat against the far wall with a rifle he barely had strength to hold, useful now mostly as another barrel shape in the dark behind the smoke. Nemi stayed at floor level by the door and listened harder than anyone looked.
Vale still had his eyes on the shelf papers.
"If you burn that, you bury more than thieves."
Jed struck the match.
"Good."
He touched flame to the oil path and stepped back.
Fire took the lowest packet first. Dry paper folded inward black and then flared. The old crate canvas in the corner caught next. Smoke rolled up against the stone roof and banked down again with nowhere easy to escape.
Vale moved then.
Not toward Jed.
Toward Ruth.
He hit her on the shoulder with enough force to knock the pistol arm wide and the box loose against the wall. The shot went into the ceiling stone. Caleb flinched hard enough to lose the rifle. Vale seized Ruth by the coat front and dragged her in front of him before Wren or Clementine could turn their lines without risking her.
Vale coughed smoke and hauled Ruth harder against him. "Out. Out with the ledger or all of you can explain honor to ash."
Ruth slammed her free elbow back into his ribs.
He grunted and held on.
Jed crossed the room in two strides, but the first shot from outside drove him low before he could reach them. Stone burst by the doorway and smoke folded the room tighter still. Draven's men had understood the fire and were pushing for the threshold before the records vanished.
Nemi shouted once from floor level.
"Right side."
Clementine shifted and shot the first man through the doorway gap before he cleared the trunk outside. The body fell backward and clogged half the entrance. Wren fired over it at another flash lower down. The return shot hit the door stone and sent dust into everyone's eyes.
Vale dragged Ruth toward the back of the room and bent for the box without releasing her.
That was his mistake.
The moment he reached, Ruth went dead weight and then twisted under his arm the way only a person used to defending close space could. She tore free enough for one breath.
Clementine used it.
The rifle cracked from the doorway and Vale jerked backward with Ruth's coat still in one hand. The shot took him high through the chest and spun him into the burning shelf, where the old papers collapsed with him in a dirty burst of sparks.
Ruth crawled clear of the fire on both knees, dragging the box by its handle.
Jed was already moving for the entrance because the lower slope had gone suddenly too quiet.
Draven came through the smoke on foot with knife first and rifle abandoned outside.
He had judged the room right. Too close now for long guns to decide it clean. He slashed for Jed's face in the first pass and the blade missed by the width of one head turn, shaving coat collar and skin below the ear both. Jed struck his wrist, took half the force, lost the rest to the old break driving under him as they hit the stone threshold together.
The fight narrowed to bone, knife, breath, and smoke.
Draven was stronger than the lean frame promised and faster at close work than any man carrying that much patience deserved to be. He drove Jed backward over the threshold stone and into the dirt outside where the dead spruce shadow and the room's firelight crossed badly. Jed trapped the knife wrist once, lost it, caught it again against his own chest, and felt the blade work down inch by inch toward the leather over his ribs.
Draven's breath stayed even.
"Should've let the others go."
Jed got one knee under him and drove it up. Not for the groin. For the hurt shoulder he had guessed from older sign and newer movement. The blow took enough out of Draven's left side to break the pressure one instant.
That instant was all there ever was in close killing.
Jed tore the wrist wide, trapped the forearm against the threshold edge, and rammed the skinning knife up under the ribs and then higher when the first line met bone.
Draven's mouth opened with no sound in it.
Jed drove once more.
Then the weight came off him all at once and fell across the threshold stone with the knife still in its hand and the life already running out of it.
For a moment the only sound was the room burning behind him and Ruth coughing inside the smoke.
Then Wren fired one last measured round downslope and silence took the rest of Draven's camp apart.
Jed rolled onto one elbow and looked at the body.
Elias Draven lay half in firelight, half in dirt, with one hand still trying to hold a knife that no longer belonged to him. The long road behind his face had ended there with no speech fit to carry past the blood.
Inside the room, Vale burned where the shelf had gone over him.
Ruth had the box and nothing else.
Clementine stood at the doorway with the rifle low now, smoke behind her and no softness in the eyes. "Done."
Jed looked once more at Draven and pushed himself upright.
"Done."

Epilogue: No Road Back
The wagon creaked when Ruth set Marrow's reins across the brake handle.
First light had just touched the ridge east of the burned room. Smoke still lifted out of the stone cut in thin gray threads where the last of the papers and crate wood settled into themselves. The fire had spent most of its anger in the night and left only blackened edges, a ruined shelf, and a stink of old ink and older lies gone to soot.
Marrow lay wrapped in canvas at the back of the wagon. Draven lay under a cairn of stone below the threshold where no marker named him. Vale had less grave than that. They had dragged what remained of him clear of the embers and covered it with loose rock on the slope side where animals would find poor purchase. No one argued for better.
Ruth took the bench because the horses knew her hands well enough by now and because no one else wanted Marrow's seat while the leather still held his shape.
Caleb climbed up beside her slower than a healthy man but faster than one who meant to die. The wound had not healed, only chosen not to finish the job yet. He carried Wren's spare blanket around his shoulders and a face full of thoughts he would not be able to outrun by reaching Spur.
Wren walked once around the wagon before climbing to the rear board. He looked at the wrapped body, at Ruth, at the north face where the room had burned, and then settled with the rifle across his knees as if all roads from there onward would require the same plain answer.
Nemi stood by the spare horse and looked east where the old crossing line would eventually bend toward the kin waiting beyond Spur. He had said little since the fire, not because silence had taken him, but because the work that followed killing rarely needed his part of speech.
Clementine came out of the timber last with the gray mare on a loose rein and stopped beside Jed where he stood a little apart from the wagon tongue.
He had slept less than an hour and looked no worse for it because weariness had long ago found its permanent place in him and stopped asking leave.
Clementine nodded once toward the burned room. "What lived in there."
Jed looked at the smoke and then at the second ridgeline beyond.
"Enough."
She accepted that with no sign of injury. A younger version of either of them might have mistaken silence for withholding. The road had taught both better.
Ruth gathered the reins.
"We go now or not at all."
Jed stepped to the wheel and looked up at her. The box sat under the bench wrapped in sackcloth, smaller somehow than before. Ledger, names, coins, and all the dead men's ugly arithmetic still rode in it, but no room north of Spur remained to match it now. Whatever choices Ruth made with those papers would be choices made in open world, not under stone.
"You know what you carry."
She settled a hand on the wagon seat. "Enough of that too."
Caleb looked over at Jed then, face drawn and earnest in a way that would have embarrassed him a month earlier. "I owe."
Jed shook his head. "You live first."
"Still owe."
"Then take the count up with whoever you become after Spur."
That seemed to hurt the young man more than any speech of forgiveness would have. Good. Debt named too early often turned back into vanity.
Wren touched two fingers to the hat brim. "You coming south."
Jed looked down the line of the road where Spur lay beyond the first shoulder and then north where nothing civilized waited for miles and no reason remained to keep pushing that way except habit.
"Not today."
Ruth clicked the horses forward before the answer had fully left him. The wagon rolled, one repaired wheel and all. Marrow went south in his own seat, covered and silent. Caleb and Wren rode with him. Nemi mounted the spare horse only after the wagon had taken ten yards, as if he preferred to follow motion rather than begin it.
At the bend below the burn, Nemi turned once in the saddle and looked back at Jed. He lifted one hand, not farewell exactly. More like a count closed honest.
Jed answered with the same.
Then they were gone down the road toward Spur.
Clementine stood with the gray mare beside him until the wagon sound had thinned into distance.
Her mouth twitched as if the line amused her less than she wanted it to. "No promise again."
"No."
"But not the old kind."
He looked at her then.
"No."
She mounted first. He followed after, slower over the right leg than he liked and with no one there now to pretend the wound would ever become a stranger to him. They took the same road south for the first mile because the mountain gave them no other and because neither of them minded that fact enough to fight it.
At the fork above the first creek, Clementine reined left.
Jed held the main line and looked once across.
No more needed saying.
She touched heel to the mare and rode on.
Jed watched until the timber took her and then turned his own horse down toward Spur with the smoke from Stone Relay thinning behind him and no paper left alive to tell the whole of what had burned there.
