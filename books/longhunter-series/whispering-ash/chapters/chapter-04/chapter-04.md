# Chapter 4: First Winter

## Scene 1

Winter came first through the water bucket.

The creek skinned over at the edges before dawn, thin ice breaking when Jake drove the wooden bucket through it. Cold water climbed over his knuckles and bit deep enough to make his fingers lock around the handle. By the time he carried the bucket back to the village, the wet cuffs of his trousers had stiffened. His heel, still raw from the long march, split open again on the frozen path.

The village fires burned low in the mornings. Smoke hung under the trees and crawled along the ground before it rose. Bark shelters held some of the wind, but not enough. Dogs curled nose to tail near ash beds. Children stayed close to their mothers. The old men moved slowly, wrapped in hides, eyes narrowed against the cold.

Jake carried wood until his arms shook.

Food grew thin. The corn mush had more water in it. Meat came in smaller strips. Some days the smell of roasting meat drifted through the village and no piece came to him. He learned to scrape the inside of an empty bowl with two fingers and lick the paste from his skin. He learned which women threw away burnt crusts and which guarded even those.

Near-starvation did not come in one great blow.

It came by spoon and strip. A bowl filled lower. A piece of meat cut narrower. A task added before the meal instead of after. Jake began to know how far he could walk after eating only thin mush, how long his hands would grip a bucket before shaking, how quickly smoke made an empty belly turn sour.

He watched dogs for what they found near the ash pits. He watched children for what they dropped. Once he took a burnt crust from under a bench and ate it with ash still on one side. A boy saw and laughed. Jake chewed anyway.

The village itself tightened under winter. Women scraped bowls cleaner. Men checked stores with hard faces. Children were pulled back from fires sooner, wrapped in skins, and slapped if they wasted food. Hunger made no one kinder. It only made each hand faster to close.

His shelter was a poor lean-to at the edge of the work ground, bark sheets held by bent sticks and a hide too old to keep rain out. It had been enough in autumn. In winter it became a trap for cold. Wind found every crack. Frost gathered white on the inside of the bark. When rain changed to sleet, water ran down the back wall and froze in beads before morning.

Jake slept curled around his knees.

He woke stiff and slow. His hands opened badly. The hide scraper slipped. A bundle of wood slid from his arms. At the creek path, he had to stop twice where the climb bent around stone. No one needed to strike him much. The cold did more.

The worst cold came before dawn.

That was when fire beds had sunk low and men had not yet risen to stir them. Jake would wake with his knees drawn high and his feet numb inside worn moccasins. He learned to move toes before fingers, fingers before wrists, wrists before standing. If he rose too fast, black specks crowded his sight and the shelter wall tilted.

Cold stole skill before it stole life.

The first thing it took was grip. The second was judgment. A man with warm hands knew a full bucket from a half one by weight. A freezing man hauled whatever his fingers could hold and spilled half before reaching the fires. Jake began to see why old women cursed wasted water and why men looked at the sky before deciding how much wood to burn. Winter made every mistake larger.

He tried to save strength by carrying less.

The gray-haired woman saw the lighter bucket and sent him back to the creek before he had set it down.

After that he carried full loads and paid for them with shaking legs.

One morning he could not untie the cord around a wood bundle. The knot sat before him, plain and simple, but his fingers would not pinch. A woman took the bundle from him, opened the knot with two pulls, and struck him across the mouth with the loose end.

After that he slept with his hands under his arms when he could.

One gray morning, snow began before sunup.

It fell fine at first, no larger than ash flakes, then thickened until the village blurred. Jake was sent for wood from a stack near the timberline. He made one trip. Then another. On the third, his knees lost strength near the pine where Thorn Blackroot often stood watching.

Jake set the wood down because dropping it would bring a blow.

He reached for the bundle again.

His hands would not close.

The snow filled his hair and lay white on his bare wrists. His breath came in thin pulls. The woodpile seemed far away though it was at his feet. He bent, missed the sticks, and went down on one knee.

The ground stayed under him.

No sound came from his mouth.

Boots stopped nearby. Moccasins, patched at the toes with neat sinew. Thorn Blackroot stood over him with a hide cloak drawn around his shoulders. Snow clung to the scar on his cheek and melted there.

Jake tried to rise.

Thorn put one hand on his shoulder and pushed him back down.

"Still."

The word was English, rough and low.

Jake stayed still.

Thorn lifted one of Jake's hands and looked at the fingers. He rubbed the thumb hard between his own palms until pain returned in bright stabs. Then he did the other hand. No kindness showed in his face. His grip was hard enough to hurt.

Pain meant the fingers were not dead.

Thorn pulled Jake upright, took half the wood bundle under one arm, and pointed with his chin toward a small fire near a bark shelter.

Jake followed.

## Scene 2

The fire was no larger than a hat crown.

Thorn crouched beside it and fed it splinters no thicker than quills. No heap of wood went on the coal. No hard breath scattered ash. One hand cupped against the wind while the other set each sliver where the coal could take it.

Jake stood too close.

Thorn struck his shin with a stick.

"Smoke first. Heat after."

Jake stepped back.

The old man pointed to the wet hem of Jake's trousers, then to the fire, then to a line of sticks angled under a bark sheet. The lesson came plain. Wet cloth close to flame would scorch before it dried. Slow heat did better work.

Thorn took a strip of dried meat from a pouch and broke it in half. He kept one piece and tossed the other into Jake's lap. It was tough, smoked hard, and smaller than two fingers. Jake chewed until his jaw ached.

The food did not fill him.

It kept him upright.

Thorn ate his half in three bites and did not look at Jake while doing it. The old man gave no speech over mercy. He had not saved Jake from labor, from hunger, or from the village. He had stopped a frozen hand from becoming a dead hand because dead hands did no work and learned nothing.

That was all the kindness the act allowed.

Jake kept his face still and took the lesson as it came.

Thorn held up a piece of bark from the shelter wall. The bark had been set with the grain wrong, curled edge facing the wind. Snow melt ran behind it and into the sleeping place.

"Bad."

He turned the bark and set it against the frame the other way, overlapping the lower piece like shingles on a roof. Then he tapped the edge with one finger.

"Water down."

Jake watched.

Thorn took a green stick and bent it. It cracked. He threw it aside. He took a dead stick from under the bark eave and snapped it clean. Dry heartwood showed pale inside.

"This."

Thorn pushed bark against the wind side and made Jake hold it while he drove a stake with the back of the hatchet. He set dry wood under the bark lip, then pointed to the green stick and shook his head. He dragged pine boughs from the mud and laid them crosswise.

"Off ground."

Jake lifted one end with numb hands while Thorn shoved poles beneath it. Snow went against the outside wall. Ash stayed near the fire stone. Thorn spoke only when Jake put a thing wrong, and then his hand or boot corrected the work before his mouth did.

When the gray-haired woman called Jake back to work, Thorn did not stop her.

Jake lifted the wood bundle.

It was lighter because the old man had taken half of it.

That was all the saving he got.

The rest of the day tested whether the lesson had entered him.

At the next fire, Jake moved wet sticks back from flame instead of feeding them straight in. He shaved one with the knife until pale dry wood showed and set only the shavings to the coal. A woman snapped at him for taking too long. He kept his hand steady and waited until the shavings caught.

The flame lived.

The woman took the bowl of coals without striking him.

That was not approval.

It was one less blow.

By dusk, Jake's shelter had iced again. The old bark curled. One stake had pulled loose from frozen ground. Wind drove snow across the sleeping place. Jake stood in front of it with his arms full of sticks and saw plain what Thorn had seen all along.

It would kill him.

He set the sticks down under the eave of a nearby shelter, out of snow. Then he went to the timberline and cut saplings with a dull hatchet borrowed under a woman's eye. Each stroke jarred his wrists. He cut four uprights, then two longer pieces for the roof line. He dragged them back one at a time.

No one helped him.

Thorn watched from the fire.

Jake drove the uprights into the ground with a stone. The frozen soil fought him. He used the same sense his father had taught for fence posts: lean the post against the wind, pack the base tight, wedge stone where earth would not hold. He crossed the roof pieces and tied them with strips of bark fiber, using the square farm knot his fingers remembered better than his mind did.

Thorn came close once.

He looked at the knot and grunted.

Not praise. Not correction.

Jake kept working.

## Scene 3

The shelter took three days of stolen minutes.

He worked between wood hauling, water carrying, hide scraping, and fire tending. A bark sheet came from a fallen chestnut near the creek. Pine boughs came from low branches where snow had bent them down. Mud came from a spring seep that had not frozen under the leaves. Stones came from the creek path, carried two at a time in the fold of a torn hide.

Jake built low, lower than he wanted. A low roof held heat. The open side turned away from the wind. Bark overlapped from bottom to top so meltwater ran out instead of in. Pine boughs lifted his sleeping place off the ground by the width of his hand. A small trench cut with a stick carried water around the back.

The wind side took the most time.

Jake first stacked snow against it because snow was there and his hands were tired. By midday the inner bark sweated. By dusk that wet place had begun to freeze. He scraped it away with a stick and started over, this time setting brush between shelter wall and snow so air could stay trapped without water pressing straight through. Thorn watched from the path and did not help.

When Jake packed the brush too loose, wind moved it.

When he packed it too tight, the bark bowed inward.

The third try held.

He learned to test it by putting his cheek near the seam. Fingers lied when they were numb. A cheek still felt the thin knife of air coming through a bad place.

The first frame leaned.

Jake saw it only after he stepped back. The wind side sagged inward, and one roof pole sat wrong against the upright. He could have left it. Darkness was near, and his hands had started to shake again. Instead he took the frame apart and set the upright deeper, packing stones at the base and stamping snow into the gaps. Farm fence work came back to him: a post that leaned when new would fall when weather tested it.

The second frame held.

The bark sheets were worse. Frozen bark cracked if bent too fast. Wet bark froze to his fingers. One sheet split down the middle and left him with two poor pieces instead of one good one. He used those near the lower side where overlap mattered less, then carried a better piece from the fallen chestnut by dragging it on a cord.

No one mistook it for a cabin.

It was not one.

It was a hole in the weather with rules.

The first night, smoke from a nearby fire blew into it and made his eyes water.

Thorn stood outside in the dark.

"Too low there."

The old man pointed to the smoke gap.

Jake raised one corner before dawn.

The second night, sleet struck the roof and ran down the outside.

No water touched his back.

He woke before sunrise with cold feet, an empty belly, and a throat raw from smoke, but the frost had not formed inside the bark. His blanket was damp only at the edge. The pine boughs under him still held shape.

He sat up slowly.

That was the first thing in the village he had made that stayed made.

The gray-haired woman found him mending the trench with a stick after the morning water trip. She looked at the roof, then at the trench. No smile came. She pointed to the hide rack and sent him back to scraping.

Still, she did not pull the shelter down.

That became another kind of permission.

Jake used it carefully. He did not make the shelter larger. He did not add a door flap fine enough to draw notice. He did not move it closer to the better fires. He repaired only what weather had already shown wrong. More boughs under the sleeping place. A stone at the trench mouth. Bark fiber wrapped around a weak tie. Mud pressed into one seam and covered with dry leaves so it would not freeze into a useless lump before setting.

Each repair taught a later one.

Thorn came at noon with two strips of meat and a blackened clay bowl. Inside the bowl were acorns cracked and leached of bitterness, mixed with a little grease. He set it on a stump and pointed.

"Dry. Smoke. Keep."

Jake picked up one acorn piece, smelled it, and put it in his mouth. Bitter still, but food.

Thorn took a strip of meat and held it too close to flame until fat dripped and the edge burned. Then he moved it high in the smoke, above heat but below the bark cover.

"Fire eats," Thorn told him. "Smoke keeps."

Jake nodded once.

The old man's eyes narrowed.

Thorn tapped two fingers against his own mouth, then pointed at the meat hanging in smoke.

Jake swallowed the acorn.

"Smoke keeps."

Thorn grunted and turned away.

That became the shape of the days. Thorn showed. Jake did. Thorn corrected. Jake did again. No ceremony marked it. No one in the village treated him as anything but a captive. Boys still used the ash name. Women still gave him the worst work. Young men still watched for a reason to strike him.

But Thorn's lessons stayed.

Jake learned to bank coals under ash so they lived through the night. Wet wood could be shaved down to the dry heart. Bark peeled better after a thaw and cracked if forced in deep cold. Meat belonged in smoke, not flame. Snow packed outside a shelter wall could stop wind, but it had to stay away from bedding.

Food preservation became another quiet school.

Thorn showed him acorns first. Crack. Leach. Dry. Keep away from damp. Then strips of meat. Cut thin. Hang high. Smoke slow. Turn before grease gathered and dropped. Then roots laid near heat but not in it, dried enough to last two days instead of rotting in one.

Jake had known food from fields.

Corn in shocks. Apples laid apart so one rot did not take a basket. Beans dried on vines. Meat salted when salt could be spared. The mountain taught different terms. Damp came from below as often as above. Smoke could save food or ruin it. Fat fed flame if hung too low. Roots froze hard outside and spoiled soft inside if a man trusted the skin.

Thorn made him sort one small pile of roots three times.

Good. Bruised. Rot starting.

Jake missed one bad piece in the good pile. Thorn cut it open with his knife and set the brown center on Jake's palm.

"This kills later."

Jake looked at the root, then at the drying rack.

Not every danger came with a weapon.

Jake spoiled the first root bundle by setting it too close to wet ground.

Thorn picked it up, broke it open, and held the blackened center under Jake's nose.

"Waste."

Jake buried the spoiled pieces away from the dogs and did the next bundle again.

The next one kept.

Small successes came like poor rations, never enough and still worth taking.

One morning his coals lived.

Another morning his shelter roof shed rain.

On the third morning, the gray-haired woman sent him for water and found the fire still breathing when he returned. She looked once at the coals under their ash bed and gave him no word. Silence was better than a strike.

The fourth morning brought wind hard enough to flatten smoke along the ground.

Jake woke before the first call to work because the shelter did not shake apart. Bark creaked. One tie groaned. Snow hissed against the wind side and slid down instead of coming through. He lay still a moment, listening to the shelter hold.

Then he got up and repaired the groaning tie before anyone could order him elsewhere.

Cold made the bark fiber stiff. He warmed it against his wrist, twisted it tighter, and braced the stake with a stone. The work took time he did not have. When the gray-haired woman called, he went at once, but the shelter stayed behind him stronger than before.

At noon he found the weak tie still holding.

That mattered more than a full bowl would have, because a full bowl lasted only until hunger returned. A good tie changed the next night. He touched it once, quickly, then left it alone. Too much pride in a thing could make a man keep handling it until it broke.

Thorn saw the touch.

The old man used his stick to point at the trench mouth, where ice had formed a lip. Jake had not seen it. Meltwater would back up there by dark and run under the wall.

Jake took a flat stone and broke the ice away. The trench opened. Water moved.

Thorn walked off without a grunt.

No praise, then.

Still a lesson.

That was how he began to choose lessons before they were given.

At fires, he watched which wood women saved for coals. At meat racks, he watched how strips were turned. At shelters, he looked at roof pitch, wind side, trench, and bedding. No one invited him to learn. He stole learning with his eyes and paid for it with more work.

That evening Thorn sat across the low fire from him while snow ticked against bark.

Thorn looked across the low fire. "You watch."

Jake fed one sliver of wood into the coal. "I watch."

"Not enough."

Jake waited.

Thorn pointed beyond the shelter, toward the timber and the ridges hidden by dark.

"Learn."

The word sat between them with the smoke.

Jake looked at his hands. The rawhide cuts had closed ugly. New cracks crossed the knuckles. Dirt lived under every nail. Those hands had held a farm hoe, a felling axe, a hide scraper, a water bucket, and now bark, coal, and meat smoke.

He set another sliver of wood where the coal could take it.

"I learn."

Thorn's face did not change.

The old man lifted one coal with a forked stick and set it in a blackened bowl. He covered it with ash, then uncovered it, showing the red heart still alive beneath gray. He pointed to Jake's chest, then to the coal, then to the ash.

Jake followed part of it.

Not all.

Thorn did not explain the rest. He set the coal back and made Jake do it. The first time Jake smothered it. The second time he left too much air and the coal ate itself fast. The third time the red stayed under ash long enough for Thorn to grunt.

That lesson stayed longer than warmth.

Outside, the village settled into winter dark. Jake's shelter held against the wind at his back. The fire kept breathing under ash. The meat strip above it took smoke and did not burn.

Jake stayed awake longer than he needed, watching the coal, the smoke, the old man's hands, and the way the cold moved around what had been built right.

By morning he would ask for more without using the word ask.

More work. More watching. More correction.

If their ways kept a man alive, he would take them hard.

The next morning he proved it before Thorn came.

Jake rose while the fires were still low and took one coal from his own ash bed into the clay bowl. He carried it to the gray-haired woman's fire pit, shielded by both hands. Wind tried to take it at the shelter corner. He turned his body and saved the coal. At the pit, he fed it bark curls first, then shaved dry heartwood. Flame came small and clean.

The gray-haired woman saw the fire catch.

She pointed to the water bucket anyway.

Jake went for water.

But when he returned, the fire was still alive, and no one had rebuilt it for him.

That evening, he banked his own coals the same way.

Not perfect. One edge burned too open. He covered it with ash and waited, watching red sink under gray. Snow pressed against the shelter wall. The roof held. The coals held. His belly was still empty enough to ache, and his hands still cracked when he closed them, but the night had one less way to kill him.

Jake sat beside the ash bed until his eyes stung.

He would learn the rest the same way.
