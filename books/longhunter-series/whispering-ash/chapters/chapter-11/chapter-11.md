# Chapter 11: The Lone Scout

## Scene 1

The white man stopped below the cave mouth and did not step closer.

That kept him alive.

Jake watched from the dark with the knife held low against his thigh. The overhang behind him smelled of wet stone, old smoke, and the bitter roots he had boiled for the wound. Rain dripped from the lip of rock in steady drops. Each drop struck a flat stone near the cold fire and marked time better than any clock he had known.

The man below stood in laurel with his rifle angled toward the ground. Not loose. Not careless. His right thumb rested near the hammer. His left hand held the forestock as if he could raise the gun before a squirrel crossed a limb. A rain-dark hat shadowed his face. Beard covered most of his jaw. One eye narrowed more than the other, either from an old hurt or from habit.

He had come alone.

That counted for something.

Not enough.

Jake shifted no more than the width of a finger. Pain moved from his thigh into his hip. The wound had closed some under moss packing and boiled root, but travel had opened it again. Blood had dried into the binding. If the man rushed him, Jake would have one quick cut and then trouble.

The man looked at the cave mouth, then at the strip of smoke staining the wet rock above it.

"I know you're in there."

Jake did not answer.

The man waited. Rain ticked on his hat brim. Water ran from the rifle barrel and dropped from the front sight.

"Name's Caleb Thorne."

Jake kept the knife still.

"If you wanted me dead, you'd have shot by now. If you had a gun worth shooting."

Jake's rifle lay behind him under a fold of the ash cloak, dry but low on powder. He had three balls left. One was already seated. The pan was empty. Rain had taught him caution.

Caleb Thorne looked toward the trees on the uphill side and then back at the cave.

"I'm not after your fire. I'm not after your pack. I smelled smoke and came careful. Man can do worse in this rain than share dry rock."

Jake watched the brush behind him.

Caleb noticed.

That counted too.

The white man did not turn fast. He only shifted his feet so one stood back of the other, then let his gaze travel along the lower brush. His rifle came up a little, no more than a handspan. A man used to woods did not waste movement when the woods began speaking.

Below them, in the wet laurel, something broke a dead branch.

Caleb's face changed. Not fear. Reckoning.

Jake heard it then. Weight in brush. Wet leaves dragged aside. A low huff came from the thicket, followed by a tearing sound where claws ripped through rotten wood. The cave had held smoke under the rock since morning. He had roasted one piece of squirrel there before rain drove water down the slope. He had buried scraps under stones, but blood and grease had their own trail.

Caleb took one step sideways from the cave mouth.

Good.

The bear came through the laurel with its head low.

It was black and wet, with mud on both forelegs and burrs in the fur under its throat. Not the largest Jake had seen sign of in the mountains, but large enough to kill a wounded man and a careless one. Its nose worked at the air. Its small eyes caught the cave shadow, then Caleb's shape below it.

Caleb lifted the rifle.

The bear came faster.

The shot cracked under the rock and filled the gorge with smoke. Caleb had aimed well, but rain, brush, and motion stole enough from the ball. It struck the bear high near the shoulder. The animal lurched, bawled, and kept coming.

Caleb stepped back to draw his knife.

His boot slid on wet leaves.

Jake came out of the cave.

There was no room for planning. If he stayed inside, the bear would drive Caleb backward into him or come in after blood and smoke. If Caleb died below the cave, Jake would be trapped with a wounded bear and an empty rifle close by. He took the two steps the leg allowed, then fell more than ran down the short slope.

The bear struck Caleb with its chest and shoulder.

Caleb went sideways into stone. His knife hand stayed up, but the bear's head drove under it. Jake hit from the side and wrapped both arms around the bear's neck and shoulder. Wet fur filled his hands. The animal smelled of musk, mud, old blood, and rotten wood. It twisted with a strength that tore Jake's grip almost at once.

Farm wrestling had taught him how to hold a hog.

Thorn's trial ground had taught him how long a man could hold pain.

Neither lesson fit a bear.

Jake hooked one forearm under the animal's jaw and jammed his knee against a stone. His wounded leg buckled. Pain ran white up his side, but his grip bought two breaths.

Caleb used them.

The knife went in behind the bear's foreleg.

The bear bawled in Jake's ear. Its body slammed him against rock. Claws tore bark from a root beside his face. Caleb struck again, lower. Jake lost his hold and rolled downhill through wet leaves. The bear turned with him, mouth open, breath hot and rank.

Jake's hand found a stone.

He drove it into the bear's nose with everything left in his arm.

The animal jerked back.

Caleb came from the side with the knife in both hands and buried it deep. The bear rose enough to show its chest, then collapsed against the slope. Its hind legs kicked twice. Mud sprayed. One claw scraped Jake's boot leather and cut a strip from it without touching flesh.

Then the animal lay still except for breath.

Caleb backed away with the bloody knife held out.

Jake did not move.

The bear's last breath left it in a long wet rush.

Rain filled the silence after.

Caleb bent, braced one hand on his knee, and spat into the leaves. His hat had gone somewhere in the brush. Blood ran from a cut above his ear into his beard. His rifle lay under the bear's hindquarters.

"That was poor company."

Jake pushed himself up on one elbow. His thigh binding had opened. Blood showed dark through the cloth.

Caleb saw it.

The rifle under the bear saw him too, in its own way. A man who wanted Jake dead could take it once the carcass was moved. A man who wanted him robbed could take the cave. A man who wanted a debt could name one and keep naming it until Jake paid twice.

Jake reached for his knife.

Caleb did not move toward him.

"Easy. I like my ribs where they are."

Jake got the knife in hand anyway.

Caleb wiped his own blade on wet grass, then looked toward the cave. "You got fire under that rock?"

Jake breathed through his nose until the pain settled enough for speech.

"Coals."

"Good. Powder's wet if we leave it out here. Meat'll spoil if we stand and admire it."

Caleb's talk came plain, not town-polished, not trader-sweet. He moved like a man used to being alone and not wanting to die for pride. He recovered his hat first. Then he crouched near the bear and worked his rifle free without turning his back on Jake.

Jake watched the hands.

Caleb opened the pan, cursed under his breath, and shook water from the lock. "You got dry tow?"

Jake gave no answer.

Caleb nodded at the silence. "Fair."

He took a small horn from inside his shirt, unscrewed a wooden plug, and poured a pinch of fine powder onto his palm. The plug had been packed with greased linen. Dry. Jake marked that. A horn could be sealed at the mouth and still betray a man at the plug if he was careless.

Caleb saw him watching.

"Old trapper showed me. Keep the plug wrapped or the horn lies to you."

Jake held out no gratitude.

He pointed with the knife toward the bear. "Cut high. Meat's fouled low."

Caleb looked at the carcass, then at the wound paths. "You know bears?"

"Enough."

"Enough is better than most."

They worked without calling it agreement.

Caleb took the clean cuts from high along the back and hindquarter. Jake stayed seated near the stone and used his knife when the carcass had to be held open. The wound in his thigh made kneeling bad and standing worse. Caleb noticed and did not speak of it. That counted more than talk would have.

Rain washed blood downhill in thin red threads. The smell brought flies even in wet weather. Caleb dragged the offal away from the cave mouth with a forked branch and covered the worst of it with leaves and stones. Jake pointed once to a lower hollow where runoff would carry scent away from the overhang.

Caleb followed the point.

No argument.

The bear was not only meat.

It was trouble lying dead at their door. Blood would draw fox, wolf, and maybe another bear. The hide would take too long to cure right in rain. The fat could help boots, fire, and wounds if rendered before it spoiled. The bones could be cracked, but cracking bones made sound. Every useful piece had a cost.

Caleb looked at the carcass and rubbed rain from his beard.

"Too much for two men to clean before dark."

"Then take what keeps us alive."

Caleb nodded. "Meat. Fat. Hide strip for binding."

Jake cut a narrow strip from the edge of the hide and tossed it to him. Caleb caught it, tested the pull, and used it to wrap his bruised wrist where the bear had struck him against stone.

That was the first sign the bear had cost Caleb more than blood on his face.

By the time they carried the first meat under the rock, Jake's hands shook. He set the knife down where he could reach it and knelt by the cold fire. Ash covered the coals. He used a twig to open the bed, then fed in dry curls of bark from a pouch he had kept under his shirt. The bark smoked, darkened, and caught. He built the flame low, tucked behind stones, where the overhang hid most of the light.

Caleb watched the fire lay.

"You keep smoke close."

Jake set two damp sticks where the heat would dry them before they burned. "Smoke climbs when fed wrong."

"So do men."

Jake looked at him.

Caleb held up one hand, palm out, then sat across the fire with the rifle over his knees. "No harm meant."

The bear meat took time. They cut it thin and set strips on green sticks. Fat dripped into ash and spat. Rain ran past the cave mouth in silver strings. Beyond the rock, evening gathered in the laurel. The dead bear lay half covered below, black against wet leaves.

Jake tightened the binding on his thigh. The cloth stuck where blood had dried. He pulled anyway. Breath hissed through his teeth before he could stop it.

Caleb's eyes went to the wound and away.

"Lead?"

"Knife."

"Man?"

Jake turned a strip of meat.

Caleb accepted the silence. "Man, then."

The fire made small light in the cave. It showed Caleb's face better now. The narrowed eye had a pale mark near the lid. An old cut had crossed it. His hands were broad and scarred, with two nails split down the middle. A strip of rawhide mended one boot. His coat had been patched with cloth from two other coats, neither one matching.

Not a soldier.

Not a town man.

Not a trader fat from scales and lies.

Still a stranger.

Caleb took one strip of meat when it had browned enough and held it out on the stick, not across the fire but halfway, where Jake could take it without coming close.

Jake took it.

They ate with rain between them and the world.

After a while Caleb drew a line in the ash with a charred stick. He made a ridge, then another, then set three small stones beyond it.

"River runs there. I came down from this side." He tapped one ridge. "Saw smoke yesterday, not yours. Too much of it. Men with horses."

Jake's hand stopped near the meat.

Caleb saw that too.

"Not saying they follow you. Not saying they don't. I keep away from horses when I can. Horses mean men who carry more than they need and take more than they carry."

Jake leaned forward and marked a second line in the ash, lower and crooked. "River bend."

"You've been there?"

"Crossed below it."

"Bad crossing."

"Bad men behind it."

Caleb looked at the ash map. "Then we both dislike the same direction."

Jake did not answer.

Caleb used the stick to mark west. "There's land beyond the next run of ridges. Not town land. Not yet. Springs tucked under rock. Game trails. Hard to get a wagon through. That keeps fools away for a season or two."

"You going there?"

"I was."

"Was."

Caleb cut another strip of meat. "A bear changed my afternoon."

Jake ate slowly and watched him over the fire.

"Why tell me?"

Caleb shrugged, but the movement pulled at his bruised ribs and made him stop short. "Because you came out of a cave with a knife for a man you didn't know."

"Bear would have come in."

"Likely."

"You had a rifle."

"Empty after one shot."

"Then I came out for me."

Caleb's mouth bent, not quite a smile. "That suits me better than charity."

The answer sat plain between them. Neither man owed the other a story of kin, sorrow, or old losses. They had a dead bear, a fire, wet powder, meat enough for a day, and the same need to keep moving.

Caleb showed him the horn plug again, slower this time. He cut a bit of greased cloth and wrapped it around the threads before seating the plug. Jake showed him how he banked coals under ash with punk wood and a split of dry heartwood, enough to wake fire without a tall flame. Caleb showed a way to fold a strip of bark over powder while priming in rain. Jake showed where bear fat could seal a cracked boot seam until proper work could be done.

None of it made trust.

It made use.

Use could carry two men farther than suspicion alone.

Before dark they tested that use.

Caleb tried the creek line first, meaning to see whether any man had followed his trail to the cave. He made it twenty paces before his bruised ribs stopped him straightening. Jake saw the hitch and pointed to a higher line under laurel.

"Less bend."

Caleb looked at the slope. "More climb."

"Less ribs."

Caleb went that way and found his own boot track filling with rain. He brushed it with pine and came back slower, face gray under the beard.

"You see more than you say."

Jake fed the fire one sliver. "Saying less keeps meat from burning."

Caleb sat down with care and did not laugh. That was good. Laughing would have pulled the ribs.

Night came early under rain. Caleb slept first because Jake pointed at him and then at the back wall. Caleb studied him for a long moment, then lay down on his side with one hand near his knife. Jake sat by the fire with the rifle across his lap and listened to the rain, the river below, the small movements of the white man sleeping badly against stone.

The fire tried to betray them twice.

Once, fat dripped from a strip of bear meat and flared blue at the edge. Jake pinched wet ash over it before the light could jump along the rock. Caleb opened one eye, saw the flame gone, and closed it again. Later, smoke thickened when a damp stick shifted. Jake pulled it free with the knife tip and set it aside to dry longer.

Caleb's breathing changed after that.

Awake, then.

"You bank fire like Cherokee," Caleb murmured.

Jake did not look at him. "Fire burns the same."

"Men don't."

The rain filled the space after it. Jake let the comment die there. Caleb did not reach for more. That mattered. Some men could not stand quiet unless they owned it.

Near midnight, Caleb woke without a start, which told Jake he had not truly slept deep.

"Your turn."

Jake did not lie down at once.

Caleb sat up and reached for his rifle. He checked the pan by touch, then set the gun across his knees. "I'll not cut your throat in your sleep. Too much work after the bear."

Jake looked at him.

Caleb's face stayed tired and plain.

Jake lay down with the ash cloak over him and the knife under his palm.

He slept in pieces.

Before dawn, the rain thinned to mist. Gray light showed the cave mouth, the covered carcass below, and the ash map blurred by damp. Caleb had already risen. He stood outside the overhang with his rifle loaded, looking down the slope where the bear had come through.

Jake joined him with pain pulling at every step.

Caleb nodded toward the west. "If you're moving, that way gives more cover."

"If you're moving."

"I am."

Jake looked at the brush, the river line below, the wet stone that had kept him alive one more night. Alone, he could travel slower and hide tighter. With Caleb, he would have another rifle, another pair of hands, and another mouth to feed. He would also have a man at his back who had not yet earned the place.

Caleb waited.

That helped decide it.

Men who pushed too soon wanted something.

Jake tested him once more before leaving.

He picked up one wrapped bundle of bear meat and tossed it toward Caleb, not gently. Caleb caught it against his ribs, failed to hide the pain, and set it into his own pack without complaint.

"You slow me with those ribs," Jake told him.

"You slow me with that leg."

"Then we both know the price."

Caleb tied the pack. "Knowing price keeps men from buying foolish."

That answer held.

Jake went back under the overhang, wrapped bear meat in bark, and tied it with strips of hide cut from the carcass. Caleb packed his powder horn and cleaned the knife. They buried the fire under wet ash and scattered stone over the blood nearest the cave. Jake took one last look at the overhang to see what sign remained.

Too much.

But rain would work on it, and no camp stayed clean after a bear died at its mouth.

Caleb saw him studying the ground and came back up the slope.

He did not step into the cave mouth. He stopped below it, where he had stopped the day before. "We can drag brush."

"Brush draws eyes if it lies wrong."

"So does blood."

Jake looked at the slope. Caleb was right. That annoyed him less than it should have.

They dragged one fallen limb across the worst of the churned mud, then left the rest open for rain. Caleb wanted to cover more. Jake shook his head and pointed to the bear trail through laurel.

"Animal sign should look like animal sign."

Caleb wiped rain from his beard. "And man sign?"

Jake shouldered his pack. "Should leave."

Caleb moved first, down the slope and away from the cave, giving Jake the higher ground until the trail narrowed. Jake followed ten paces back. When Caleb chose a muddy patch, Jake clicked his tongue once. Caleb stopped, looked where Jake pointed, and stepped instead on stone.

After another hundred paces, Caleb lifted a hand and pointed to a broken fern where something had crossed before dawn.

Jake saw deer hair on the stem.

He nodded once.

They moved that way through the morning, not together like friends and not apart like enemies.

Caleb took the front when the ground opened, rifle ready for what might rise. Jake took the front in laurel where a smaller sound mattered more than a faster shot. When the slope steepened, Caleb slowed without saying it was for Jake's leg. When the trail pinched under rock, Jake waited without saying it was for Caleb's ribs.

At a creek, Caleb knelt to drink from the easiest place.

Jake caught his sleeve and pointed upstream to a stone lip where no mud would take a knee print.

Caleb looked at the mud, then at the stone, and moved.

"You always this fond of hard water?"

"Only when soft mud talks."

Caleb drank from the stone lip.

On the far bank, Caleb repaid it.

Jake chose a deer run under laurel because it hid the body. Caleb caught his eye and pointed to bark scraped high on one stem. Not deer. Pack strap, old but plain once seen. Men had used that run before. Jake studied it, then shifted uphill through fern where water would take the trace apart.

Caleb did not smile over being right.

That made the correction easier to keep.

Near midday they found old sign of men: a cut branch healed over at one edge, a fire pit scattered and weathered, a rusted hoop from a small keg half buried in leaves. Caleb crouched by it and touched the iron.

"Trappers passed a season back."

"How many?"

"Two. Maybe three."

Jake looked at the fire pit. "Careless."

"Or dead."

That ended the talk for a while.

That was how the alliance began.

Not with loyalty.

Not with friendship.

With a bear dead in the rain, a fire buried under stone, and two men moving west through wet laurel toward land neither one had yet seen.
