# Chapter 10: River and Stone

## Scene 1

The river was black under morning cloud.

Jake reached it after a night of broken sleep in laurel, with one strip of dried meat gone and Red Hollow's pursuit somewhere behind him. The water cut through a stone throat between steep banks. Foam spun around boulders. The far shore rose in a wall of rhododendron, slick roots, and dark rock. No easy ford showed itself.

That was why he had come to it.

A clean ford would hold prints. Mud would keep the shape of his heel. Gravel would show where he left the water. Men who knew trail could read a crossing as well as a page if a man gave them straight lines, broken twigs, and hurry.

Jake had hurry.

He could not give them the rest.

He crouched under a hemlock and listened before he moved into the open. Rain had not yet started, but the air carried it. The river covered small sounds. That helped him and hurt him. If Red Hollow's young men had closed the distance in the night, water noise would hide them until they were near enough to shoot or throw.

No bird cried behind him.

No stone clicked.

No Cherokee voice moved through the timber.

Jake unwrapped the ash cloak from his shoulders and bound it tight under one arm, high against his ribs. The hidden flint, dried meat, needle, and sinew had to stay dry. Powder mattered too, but the flint mattered more now. Powder without fire was black dust. A man wet through with no fire was meat waiting for cold.

He cut a dead branch for a staff and tested it against the bank.

It held.

For now.

The first step into the river took his breath. Cold seized his calves and climbed fast. The bottom dropped sooner than he expected. His boot slid from a flat stone into a slot between rocks, and water struck his thigh with enough force to twist him sideways. He jammed the branch downstream and leaned into it until the current stopped turning him.

One step.

Then another.

He angled upstream so the current would carry him toward the far bank instead of below it. Water climbed to his belt, then his ribs. The ash cloak pressed under his arm, wrapped tight but not tight enough to keep every edge dry if he went under. The branch shook in his hands. He moved it, set it, leaned, and moved one foot. Each motion had to finish before the next began.

Halfway across, a voice carried from the bank behind him.

Not clear.

Not close.

Enough.

Jake did not turn.

A stone shifted under his right foot. The river took the opening. His knee buckled. He slammed the branch down to save himself, and the dead wood snapped under his weight.

The current spun him.

For one breath the world was water, stone, and white noise. His shoulder struck a boulder. His mouth filled. The ash cloak tore free from his grip, then caught under his elbow as he clamped down. His thigh hit something under the water, a stone edge or broken limb, and heat opened under the cold so sudden that his leg stopped obeying.

He caught a rock with both hands.

The current tried to peel him off it.

Jake dug his fingers into a crack and held. His boots dragged behind him. Water beat against his face. He got one knee under him, lost it, got it again, and shoved sideways into a pocket behind the rock where the current broke around stone.

Air came back in a hard gasp.

The far bank was ten paces away.

It might as well have been a mile if he let the leg fail.

Jake shoved off from the boulder and half crawled, half swam toward the bank. His boots found bottom twice. Both times the river tried to pull them from under him. He used both hands on stone and root. The last pull tore skin from his palm, but it put him on mud under rhododendron.

He lay there with water running off him and blood running warm down his thigh.

Behind him, on the other bank, a man shouted.

Jake rolled into brush.

He did not look back until leaves covered him. Through gaps he saw only gray water and the dark line of the bank he had left. No body showed. No gun lifted. The river took any print he had made in the crossing and swallowed the blood that had started there. If they followed, they would have to choose their own crossing under his eyes.

That would cost them.

It had cost him too.

He dragged himself uphill through rhododendron, keeping low. Each push sent pain up the thigh. Blood mixed with river water and ran into his boot. At a flat rock under a pine, he stopped and cut the cloth away.

The wound lay high on the outside of his thigh, a torn line the length of two fingers, deep enough to worry him and ragged at one end. It had not struck the great blood line inside the leg. That was the mercy. It still bled too much.

He looked back at the river before treating it.

The water had already taken the red from the stones. His crossing showed only broken foam, rain-dark rock, and one bent grass stem near the bank. Good. The river had done its part. Now the leg had to do its part, and that bargain looked poorer.

No time for fear.

No time for clean work.

He took the needle and sinew from the wrapped pouch, then put them back.

Stitching there with wet hands, no fire, and men behind him would make a poor job worse. First stop the bleeding. First keep dirt out. First stay able to move.

Pine grew above the rock.

Jake broke a twig and looked for old scars in the bark where resin had hardened amber and white. Thorn Blackroot had shown him resin on cracked hands, split soles, and cut bark. Fire starter. Sealant. Bitter medicine for men who had no better. Caleb Thorne was still only a name he had not met, but Thorn's lessons had come before hunger, before snow, before running. They came now through Jake's hands.

He scraped resin with the knife point and warmed it between thumb and forefinger until it softened. Dirt clung to one piece, so he threw that away and scraped again. He packed the cleaner resin over the cut. It burned like the river had left teeth in him.

Jake pressed his lips shut and held it there.

The bleeding slowed.

He tore a strip from the tail of his shirt, wrapped the thigh tight, and tied the knot where he could reach it. The first knot slipped on wet cloth. He retied it with a square pull and tucked the end under. Then he stood.

The leg held.

Poorly.

Enough.

He tested it before trusting it.

One step downhill pulled too hard at the wound. One step across slope was better. One step over a root nearly opened the cut again. He cut a staff from a young maple, trimmed the side twigs, and used it to take weight where the thigh failed. The staff left marks. He used it only on stone when he could and dragged it through leaves where he could not.

Every help made sign.

Every sign invited pursuit.

He changed the bandage once more before leaving the pines.

The first wrapping had done its work in haste. Haste did not last. He cut another strip from the shirt tail, folded it narrow, and set it over the resin so pressure fell along the cut instead of across it. The knot had to hold against walking but not stop the blood below it. He pressed a thumb under the lower edge until the pulse moved against it.

Still alive.

Still costly.

The shirt was shorter now. The cold would find that missing cloth by night. A man paid for each repair with something else.

He moved from the pine before blood or broken bark could make a sign too plain. Ten paces on stone. Three through moss. One pause to bend back a fern with his foot. Then into laurel where rain would strike first when it came.

By noon hunger bent him.

He had saved the last strip of dried meat for night, when cold would make sleep thin and dangerous. Day hunger had to be paid with what the ground allowed. He found a seep under a bank where green shoots grew thick. Some roots a man could eat. Some roots would empty him until he could not stand. Jake took only what he knew. He dug with the knife, scraped mud away with his thumbnail, and chewed slowly.

Bitter.

Stringy.

Enough to keep walking.

He found sour berries under wet leaves and took a handful, no more. Birds had worked the bush. That was good. White mold on one cluster made him leave the rest. A rotten log gave him grubs. He ate two and gagged on the third, then ate it anyway.

A man alone did not get to be proud about food.

The rain began after midday.

First as a fine hiss in the high leaves. Then harder, running down trunks and making every stone black. It helped cover trail. It also took heat from him. His shirt clung. The bandage on his thigh swelled and loosened. He stopped under a rock lip and tightened it. Blood showed through but did not run. Pine resin held.

The next problem was shelter.

Not comfort. Shelter.

He needed stone above him, dry enough tinder, and a place where smoke could leave without drawing every eye below. He needed a wall at his back. He needed a way out that did not put him in the arms of the first man who found the entrance. All of those needs fought each other in rain.

He found the cave near dusk.

It was more overhang than cave, a shallow bite taken from the slope above a wash. Laurel grew below it. A pine leaned across one side, its roots holding a pocket of dry needles under the stone. The back wall was damp but not running. The floor slanted toward the entrance. No large animal stink. No old bones except squirrel and bird.

Jake stood below it and studied the ground before entering.

Bear sign would turn him away.

Fresh boot sign would turn him away faster.

He found neither.

Still, he checked the cave like a place that wanted him dead.

He tossed a stone inside and waited. Nothing moved. He crouched near the mouth and smelled. Wet rock. Old leaf mold. No cat musk. No bear grease. No man smoke under the new rain smell. He used the staff to rake through leaves at the back and found only beetles and a small white bone.

Only then did he crawl in.

He climbed inside, leaving no straight scrape in the mud if he could help it, and sat with his back to stone. The relief of not moving almost made him close his eyes. He did not. He took the ash cloak bundle apart and checked each thing. Flint dry. Sinew damp at the edge but usable. Needle safe. Powder horn wet outside, dry inside. Dried meat damp at one corner.

He ate the damp corner and saved the rest.

Fire came next.

His hands shook from cold, hunger, and the wound. The first spark died in wet dust. The second caught on punk shavings and went out when his breath came too hard. Jake stopped, set both hands under his arms until the shaking lessened, and tried again.

Thorn's voice came back without sound: fire eats. Smoke keeps.

Jake shaved dry heartwood from the underside of a dead branch found under the pine roots. He made a small nest of inner bark, punk, and scraped resin. The third spark caught. He fed it with slivers no longer than his finger. Flame lifted, small and mean, then steadier.

He built stones around it and kept it low.

Smoke crawled along the roof, found the lip of the overhang, and slipped out through laurel. Too much smoke would mark him. Too little fire would leave him shaking until the wound stiffened. He fed it as if feeding a wary animal: little, then less, then one dry stick when the coals were ready.

The cave made him bargain with every need.

A larger flame would dry the bandage and warm the thigh. A larger flame would throw more smoke along the rock. More smoke might hide in rain, or it might hang under the ledge and carry down the wash when the wind turned. A deeper seat in the cave kept him from sight. It also put damp stone against his back and left the fire too far from his hands.

Jake moved three times before settling.

Back to stone. Fire to the left. Rifle within reach but not across his lap where sleep might knock it. Knife under his right hand. Pack behind the hip, dry side up. Ash cloak where it could cover him and still be grabbed fast.

The order mattered.

If he woke confused, his hands would find what his mind had not yet named.

Outside, rain erased what the river had not.

Jake stripped the bandage from his thigh and checked the wound by firelight. The resin had blackened at the edges. Blood had clotted under it. He warmed more resin on the flat of his knife, not enough to burn, enough to soften, and pressed it along the torn end. Then he wrapped the thigh again with the cleanest strip left from his shirt.

The smell of pine filled the cave.

He held the leg near the heat, not close enough to loosen the resin. Steam rose from his trousers. He took the last good strip of meat, cut it in half, and put one half back into the pouch. Then he ate crumbs from his palm one at a time.

Night came with water and stone.

Twice he woke from a doze with the knife in hand and no memory of taking it. Once a branch rolled down the wash and struck rock below the cave. Once an owl called from the far slope. Near midnight, the rain slackened and he heard the river again, lower now under distance but still strong.

He slept in pieces.

Morning gave him mist and a worse leg.

The cut had stiffened. When he stood, the first step nearly dropped him. He caught the wall and waited until the black specks left his sight. The wound needed rest. Red Hollow's men would not grant it. Hunger would not grant it. Weather would not grant it. The cave could hold him one day if no one found it, but one day without food would make the next crossing harder.

He chose work that could be done close.

He set two small snares in runs below the laurel, using sinew and bent saplings. Poor sets, hurried sets, but better than an empty hand. He gathered more dry heartwood from under deadfall and found a shelf of fungus that would take a coal. He cut strips of bark and laid them near the fire to dry. He dug more roots near the seep and carried them back in the ash cloak.

The first snare failed before noon.

A rabbit run crossed under laurel where the dirt had been kicked clean. Jake set the loop too high because bending low pulled at the wound. When he checked it, one twig was bent and the loop hung empty. The animal had passed under it or backed away. Jake reset it lower, then sat on wet stone until the pain in his thigh stopped pulsing.

The second snare held nothing but a tuft of fur.

That still told him the run was true.

He did not curse either failure.

Cursing spent water in the mouth and air in the chest. He took the empty loop apart and looked at what had gone wrong. The guide stick had leaned too far out. The bent sapling pulled at an angle that opened the loop as much as it closed it. Thorn would have tapped both mistakes without needing speech.

Jake reset the spring lower and turned the loop with two fingers.

The wound throbbed while he crouched. Sweat cooled under his shirt though rain had chilled the day. He put one hand on a stone and waited until the leg stopped jumping under him. Then he crawled back instead of standing, because standing would show his head above the laurel and because pride had no meat in it.

Rain returned before the voices did.

It came down colder than the day before and found every weak place in the overhang. One drip struck near the fire. Another ran down the back wall and gathered under his pack. Jake moved the fire two handspans, built the stones higher on one side, and dug a shallow groove with the knife point to lead water away from the coals. The groove filled too fast. He lined it with bark and tried again.

The coals lived.

Barely.

He held his hands over them and watched steam rise from the torn bandage on his thigh. The wound had begun to swell around the resin. Red at the edges. Heat under the skin. Not good. He cleaned it with boiled water, biting down on a strip of bark while the heat ran through him. Then more resin. More cloth. A tighter knot.

No one would do the work gently.

No one would do it at all if he failed.

The cave became a small counting place. Three dry sticks. Two damp roots. One half strip of meat. A powder horn that had to stay corked. A rifle that could bring food or bring men. A knife that had done too much and still had to do more.

At midday he heard men on the far bank.

The voices came thin through rain and water. Cherokee. At least two. Maybe more. Jake crawled to the edge of the overhang and looked down through leaves. He saw no bodies, only a dark movement between trunks across the river. One man gave a short call. Another answered from downstream.

They had reached the crossing.

They had not crossed.

That meant the river had bought time, not freedom.

Red Hollow would know the same thing. A hurt man could not climb forever. A starving man could not vanish by wanting to. The far bank held his last certain sign, and the water had broken the line but not the hunt. Somewhere beyond the river, men were deciding whether to cross, circle, wait, or send one scout down to find easier water.

Jake counted what that gave him.

One afternoon if they chose poorly.

Less if they chose well.

Nothing if one man had already crossed below him.

Jake backed into the cave and killed the flame, leaving coals under ash. Smoke thinned. He sat with the rifle across his knees and waited until his leg cramped. The voices faded. One gunshot sounded far downstream in late afternoon, not aimed at him unless they had fired at sign or anger.

The cave held.

For now.

That evening one snare gave him a rabbit too small for a meal and too large to ignore. He killed it clean, thanked nothing aloud, and used every part he could. Meat over coals. Bones cracked for what little they held. Hide scraped and hung because waste was a sin hunger punished.

Strength returned by spoonfuls.

So did caution.

The rabbit hide became another small tool.

Jake scraped it with the back of the knife and stretched it on two twigs near the fire. It would not cure right in such weather, but it could wrap tinder for a day or patch a moccasin if need pressed. The gut he cleaned for cord, poor cord, but cord all the same. The work kept his hands busy while his ears stayed on the river.

Once, near dark, he heard a stone turn below the cave.

He killed the fire with ash and held the knife until his fingers cramped. A raccoon came into the wash, nosed the covered offal, and vanished when it caught his scent. Jake waited long after it left before waking the coals again.

Fear wasted less when it had work to do.

On the third morning, the rain ended. Sun touched the far ridge and made the wet leaves shine. Jake knew he had to move before the drying ground began keeping sign again. He packed the flint, powder, needle, sinew, and half strip of meat. He scattered ash, buried the rabbit remains, and brushed the cave floor with pine boughs. No hiding would erase everything. The work only needed to slow a man long enough for distance to matter.

He had just tied the ash cloak when a branch cracked below the cave.

Jake froze.

Not deer.

Too measured.

Not Red Hollow's young scouts.

Too little noise for boys certain of blood.

The sound came once, then stopped.

That was what made it dangerous. Animals blundered, then fled or fed. Men stopped to listen after making sound. Jake eased his weight off the bad leg and set his shoulder against stone so he could move without falling. The cave that had sheltered him now became a trap with one mouth.

He put the powder horn behind his knee and the pack behind his hip.

If the man came in, Jake would not have room for a rifle swing.

He slid one hand around the knife and eased back into shadow. The rifle lay beside him, loaded now, but a shot would call every ear in the hollow. The knife would keep silence if the man came close enough. The leg might fail before that.

A shape moved through brush below the overhang.

Tall. Broad through the shoulders. A full beard dark with rain left over from leaves. A rifle in both hands, barrel low but ready. Hat brim bent and stained. One eye narrowed against the light or an old scar.

A lone white man.

The sight struck Jake differently than Red Hollow's scouts would have.

A Cherokee scout meant one danger. A white man meant several. Militia. Trapper. Trader. Runaway. Friend to nobody. Friend to the men behind him. A man who might see the ash cloak and raise his rifle before asking anything. A man who might speak English and still lie with every breath.

English could cut a man softer than a knife.

It could make him answer before he had measured the room. It could pull up a farm voice, a family voice, a remembered porch voice, and use all of it against the body that had survived by silence. The danger sat in his mouth before the man spoke. His tongue wanted to shape a question. His hand knew better.

The white man's boots told another story.

Not militia issue. Not town clean. Patched with rawhide near one heel and mud worked deep in the seams. His coat had old powder burns near the cuff. A trapper, maybe. A man alone, maybe. A man sent ahead because he looked harmless to other whites, maybe.

No answer was safe yet.

Jake let the knife settle flat against his palm.

He stopped below the cave mouth and looked at the smoke stain on the rock.

Jake did not speak.

The man kept the rifle low.

"I know you're in there."

Jake tightened his grip on the knife.
