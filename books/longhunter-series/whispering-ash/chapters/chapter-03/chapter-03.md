# Chapter 3: Pale Captive

## Scene 1

The village showed itself first by smoke.

It rose from the hollow in thin gray columns, too many for a single cabin and too steady for a passing camp. The smoke carried the smell of bark fires, cooked meat, damp hides, old ash, and dogs. Jake smelled it before he saw the first shelter through the trees.

The party slowed at the edge of the hollow.

Dogs came first. Two lean curs burst from between bark shelters and ran toward the trail, teeth showing, tails high. A boy called them back in Cherokee. One dog stopped. The other came close enough to smell Jake's trousers, then barked at the dried blood on his shirt.

Jake stood with his hands tied behind him and did not move.

The scarred warrior jerked the rawhide once, and Jake stepped forward.

The hollow widened under the ridges. Bark shelters stood in rough lines with smoke holes dark at the top. Hides hung from poles. Strips of meat smoked over low fires. Wooden racks leaned under drying skins. Children stood near doorways and watched the captives pass. Women looked up from work and looked back down again. Men came from the far side of the clearing, some carrying guns, some hatchets, some nothing at all.

No one rushed to Jake.

That made the watching worse.

The party brought the captives into an open place near the center of the village. Jake's wet boot dragged in dust. His heel had rubbed raw on the march and each step bit deeper. The smaller captive from the trail sagged beside him. The older bearded man stood with his chin down and his breath rattling.

The scarred warrior spoke to the gathered men.

Jake caught only pieces. Sounds he had heard on the trail came again. The creek word. The halt word. The short word used for him. When the warrior pointed at Jake's pale face and then at the blood on his shirt, several people laughed.

One woman stepped close and touched Jake's sleeve with two fingers. She rubbed the homespun cloth between them as if judging poor hide. A boy reached for Jake's hair. Jake turned his head enough that the boy's fingers closed on air.

The boy grinned and spoke a word.

Others repeated it.

The sound passed through the watchers, changed by each mouth but close enough that Jake knew it had become a name. A girl laughed and pointed at his face. An older man lifted ash from the edge of a fire and rubbed it between finger and thumb, then pointed at Jake again.

The word came back as a name, spoken like soot thrown in his face.

The name was Whispering Ash.

Jake did not know the full meaning then. The ash gesture, the pale skin, and the laughter told him enough. His quiet had been noticed. They had made a thing of it and pinned it to him in front of the village.

He kept his mouth shut.

A man cut the rawhide at his wrists. For one breath Jake's arms hung free and blood rushed into his hands with burning pins. Then his shirt was pulled over his head. A knife cut the mended leather strap at his shoulder. His trousers stayed on, but the shirt, strap, and small belt pouch were thrown onto a pile with goods taken from the farm.

Jake reached once for the strap.

A hand struck his chest and drove him back.

The watchers laughed again.

His father's square knot showed in the leather where the strap had been mended. Jake looked at it until a boot scuffed dirt across it.

They took the things that made him look like a farm boy and set them where all could see. The strap. The belt pouch. A broken clasp from his coat. A small bit of flint wrapped in cloth. The scarred warrior shook each piece once, spoke over it, and tossed it aside. Men watched the pile. Women watched Jake. Children watched both and learned where to laugh.

One boy picked up the flint and held it between two fingers. Jake took one step before he could stop himself.

The rawhide at his wrists had been cut, but there were hands all around him. A man caught his arm and twisted it back. Another hand shoved between his shoulders. Jake's bare feet slid in dust. The boy with the flint hopped away, grinning, and dropped it into the pile as if he had done brave work.

The ash name came again.

This time more mouths took it.

A woman near the meat poles lifted a handful of cold ash from a dead fire and blew it from her palm. Pale dust crossed the light and settled on Jake's chest. It clung to sweat and blood. Children laughed harder. A dog sneezed and shook its head. The older bearded captive looked away.

Jake did not wipe the ash off.

That made one of the boys come closer. He rubbed two fingers through the ash on Jake's shoulder and held them up for the others. The boy spoke the name again, slower, making the others repeat it after him. Jake stood with his jaw shut and his hands loose, though every place in him wanted to strike.

Striking would bring rope.

Rope would bring a post.

A post would bring no way out.

So he stood and let them spend their laughter.

The scarred warrior took Jake by the shoulder and turned him toward a woman with thick gray hair tied at the back of her neck. She looked him over as one might look at a dull tool. Her hands were cracked from work. A bone scraper hung at her belt.

She pointed toward a woodpile.

Jake did not step soon enough.

The scarred warrior shoved him so hard he nearly fell. The village watched him stumble. The ash word came again from a child near the fire.

Jake set his feet under him and walked to the woodpile.

## Scene 2

The work began before the dust from the trail had settled on his boots.

The gray-haired woman pointed to split sticks stacked beside a shelter and then to a fire pit near the hide racks. Jake bent, took an armload, and carried it across the clearing. His arms shook from the march. Splinters dug into the bare skin of his chest. He set the wood where she pointed and reached for another load.

No one thanked him.

That was clear enough.

Wood came first, load after load, until his shoulders ached and his hands reopened at the rawhide cuts. Water came next. A wooden bucket was thrust into his grip, and a girl no older than his sisters pointed down a path to the creek. Two boys followed him for the first trip, laughing when he chose the wrong fork. One threw a pebble that struck his calf.

Jake did not turn.

The creek lay below the village in a cut of stone and rhododendron. Cold water ran fast over black rock. Jake filled the bucket and carried it back with both hands. The handle bit his palms. Water slopped against his legs on the climb and soaked the raw place at his heel.

At the top, the gray-haired woman took the bucket and poured half of it into a kettle.

She pointed back down the path.

Jake went.

The creek path taught him faster than any mouth could. A root crossed the first turn and caught his toe if he climbed too close to the bank. Loose shale lay under leaves at the second bend. A low branch struck the bucket rim if he carried it on the right side. On the third trip he changed hands below the shale, ducked before the branch, and kept both feet on the stones where moss had not grown.

Two boys waited above the bend and made sport of guessing when he would spill.

He spilled anyway.

The bucket struck his knee and water ran down both legs. Laughter came from above. Jake went back to the creek, filled the bucket full, and climbed again. This time he reached the top with water still near the rim. The girl who had first pointed him toward the creek looked at the bucket, then at his hands, then away.

No praise came.

He had not expected any.

The second trip was worse. The third made his arms shake. By the fourth, Jake learned to hold the bucket away from his knees on the climb and rest it once against a rock where the path bent. A boy saw the rest and called out. The gray-haired woman looked down from the village edge.

Jake lifted the bucket before she could make a sound.

After water came hides.

They set him near a rack where a fresh deer hide had been stretched hair side out. The smell struck him in the throat. Blood, fat, smoke, wet hair, and old flesh. A woman put a dull scraper in his hand and showed one short motion with her own wrist. Down and away. Down and away. She watched him try it once, struck his knuckles when the angle was wrong, then left him there.

Jake scraped.

The tool skipped. Fat smeared under the edge. Hair clung to his fingers. Flies came thick around his face. He had skinned hogs and helped with deer before, but this work was different. The hide had to be cleaned without tearing it. Too shallow and nothing came loose. Too deep and the scraper bit through.

Failure taught him the angle.

Down and away.

Scrape. Wipe. Scrape. Wipe.

By noon the hide had taken skin from three of his knuckles. Fat worked under his nails. Smoke settled in his hair. A strip of flesh stuck to the scraper and would not shake loose, so he pinched it off with two fingers and dropped it in the waste pit. The gray-haired woman saw him toss it short. She pointed at the pit, then at the strip on the ground.

Jake picked it up.

A dog lunged for it. Jake drew his hand back before the teeth closed. The dog barked in his face. He held the strip until the dog stopped jumping, then put it in the pit himself.

The woman watched that, too.

She said nothing.

He went back to the hide.

The rack leaned when he pulled too hard. He learned to set one knee against the lower pole. The scraper chattered if he let his wrist stiffen. He learned to keep the wrist loose and the pull steady. The hair came free in damp clumps. Under it the skin showed pale and slick, not clean yet, but closer to clean than it had been.

Work done badly brought a blow.

Work done well brought more work.

There was no third thing.

Smoke drifted from a nearby fire and stung his eyes. Children came and went. One boy muttered the ash name. Jake kept the scraper moving. When his hand cramped, he shifted grip. When the hide pulled loose from one peg, he caught it with his elbow and set the peg again before anyone could step in.

That was his first small defiance.

No one would correct that work for him.

At dusk, they gave him a bowl of thin corn mush with no meat in it. He sat on the ground near the woodpile and ate with two fingers. The smaller captive was nowhere in sight. The older bearded man lay near another shelter under guard. Jake did not call to him.

A call had no use here.

A boy came close and spoke the ash name again.

Jake looked at him.

The boy held his grin for a moment, then looked away first.

Jake went back to the bowl.

## Scene 3

Thorn Blackroot watched from the shade of a pine.

Jake did not know his name that first evening. He knew only the shape of the man. Old, but not bent. Scarred across one cheek. Long hair streaked gray and tied back with a strip of dark hide. His shoulders were narrow under a deerskin shirt, and a knife handle showed at his belt. He stood with both hands empty and wasted no motion.

Others came close to stare at Jake.

The old man did not.

From twenty paces off, the old man stood still as a stump, eyes set deep under gray brows. When Jake lifted the bowl, those eyes went to his hands. When Jake stood and carried the empty bowl to the woman, they went to his feet. When Jake reached for another armload of wood without being ordered, the old man watched longer.

Jake kept his own eyes low.

Not down in surrender. Down because feet told more than faces. The old man's moccasins were patched at the toes. One seam had been repaired with sinew so neat it looked like a vein in a leaf. The man placed his weight evenly and turned only when he meant to turn.

The next day began before sunup.

Wood first. Then water. Then fire tending. Jake learned which shelters wanted dry sticks and which wanted coals carried in a clay bowl. The dogs had their own places, and he kept clear of their tails. Some children threw stones. Some only watched. The gray-haired woman struck fast with the back of her hand if water spilled near the hides.

Fire tending carried its own punishments. Too much bark made smoke that rolled low and brought a slap. Too little left a kettle slow and brought a hard look from the women near it. Wet sticks hissed and blackened. Dry sticks vanished too fast. Coals broke if he lifted them wrong in the clay bowl. Once he dropped a coal near a child's foot. The child yelped. A woman knocked Jake sideways with the heel of her hand before the coal had even gone dark.

After that he carried coals with both hands under the bowl and his eyes on the ground ahead.

Wood had to be sorted. Punk wood for quick flame. Dry heartwood for lasting heat. Bark for kindling. Green sticks set aside unless there was nothing else. No one taught him by naming the piles. They taught him by taking wrong sticks from his arms and throwing them at his feet.

Jake sorted by smell, weight, and sound.

Rotten wood broke dull. Dry wood snapped clean. Green wood bent before it gave. He made those lessons his own because the work would not stop long enough for him to hate it.

Strength had to be saved.

At the farm, he had worked hard because work needed doing. In the village, work was a measure placed on him by others. If he spent himself early, they found more. If he faltered, they struck him. If he looked angry, they laughed.

So he made his face plain.

Jake carried what he was given. The hides they stretched, he scraped. Fires stayed low or high as gestures taught him. When a boy kicked over a neat stack of kindling to make him restack it, Jake set each stick back in place, slower than before, straighter than before, until the boy lost interest.

That was another small defiance.

No speech. No raised hand. Just work done so clean that the mockery had no hook.

Thorn Blackroot saw that too.

The old man appeared at different hours and never from the same place. One morning he stood near the drying poles while Jake scraped an old hide gone stiff along the edges. Another time he sat on a stump beyond the woodpile, working a strip of sinew between his fingers. Near dusk, Jake saw him at the trail mouth with two dead squirrels tied to his belt and a flintlock across one shoulder.

Thorn did not speak to him.

He did not speak for him either.

When a child threw a clod that struck Jake behind the ear, Thorn looked once at the child and then looked away. The child stopped throwing. When Jake staggered under a load of wet wood, Thorn watched until Jake got his feet back under him. No hand came to lift the load. No order came to make it lighter.

That made Thorn worse than the others in one way.

The others struck because they wanted him low.

Thorn watched as if low or high made no difference until Jake proved which one he was.

On the second evening, Jake was given the scraped hide back to smoke. He hung it too near flame. Heat curled one edge before he saw it. The gray-haired woman hissed and seized the hide from the pole. Jake expected the back of her hand. Instead she thrust the pole at his chest and pointed to a place farther from the fire.

Jake moved it.

Thorn stood across the smoke and watched the new distance.

Jake saw the old man's eyes go from fire to hide to Jake's feet. He shifted one foot out of the smoke path before he knew why. Smoke lifted cleaner then, crossing the hide without choking him blind.

Thorn turned away.

That was the first lesson the old man gave him, though no one called it one.

On the third day, rain came cold through the hollow. The hides were moved under a bark lean-to, and Jake was made to haul water anyway. The path turned slick. Mud grabbed his raw heel. He slipped near the creek and spilled half a bucket. A boy behind him shouted. Jake filled it again.

On the climb back, the same boy hooked a foot around Jake's ankle.

Jake went down hard. The bucket struck stone and rolled. Water ran into the mud.

The boy laughed.

Jake rose with mud on his chest and one hand bleeding from the rocks. He picked up the bucket and went back down the path.

The boy followed.

At the creek, Jake filled the bucket. He lifted it with both hands and turned. The boy stood in the path, still grinning, blocking the way.

Jake stepped forward until the bucket pressed the boy's knees.

The grin failed.

Jake gave him no word.

The boy raised a hand as if to strike him.

A man's voice cut through the rain from above.

One word.

The boy dropped his hand.

Thorn Blackroot stood on the path above them with rain running from his hair and down the scar on his cheek. His face held no kindness. He looked first at the boy, then at Jake, then at the full bucket.

Thorn pointed uphill.

Jake carried the water past the boy.

That evening the beating came anyway.

It started over a hide peg. Jake had reset it twice, and the wet ground would not hold it. The hide sagged. A young warrior seized him by the shoulder and drove him against the rack. Jake caught the post with one hand and stayed upright. The warrior struck him in the ribs. Jake bent. A second blow hit his back.

The ash name came from somewhere behind him.

Jake straightened before the next blow.

That was enough to anger the man.

The warrior grabbed a hide thong from the rack and swung it across Jake's shoulders. Pain ran hot over wet skin. Jake's knees dipped. He forced them straight. The thong struck again. A third time.

Jake fixed his eyes on the mud between his boots.

Another blow lifted.

Thorn Blackroot stepped into the space beside the rack.

No hurry showed in Thorn. No shout came from him. Thorn's hand closed on the thong before it fell and held it there. The young warrior turned, angry, but the old man's grip did not move.

Thorn spoke in Cherokee, low and rough.

The young warrior answered too fast.

Thorn looked at him until the answer died.

Then the old man took the thong from his hand and dropped it in the mud. Thorn pointed at the sagging hide peg, then at Jake, then at the scraper lying near the rack. The meaning came plain enough. The work was not finished.

The beating was.

The young warrior spat near Jake's feet and walked away.

Thorn Blackroot looked at Jake once. No smile. No pity. No promise.

Thorn pointed to the peg.

Jake bent, drove it deeper with a stone, and pulled the hide tight.

The old man watched until the work held.

Then he walked back into the rain.

Jake kept scraping until dark.
