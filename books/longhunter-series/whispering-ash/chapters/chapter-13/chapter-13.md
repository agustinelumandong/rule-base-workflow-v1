# Chapter 13: The Gorge

## Scene 1

Jake found the gorge by following game trails that had not been cut by any plow, wheel, or shod horse.

The first track showed in a muddy bar below a bend in the river. A doe had come down before daylight and set one forehoof deep near the water, then turned away from the open bank as if the place had been used too often. Two smaller prints crossed behind her. Farther up, under alder brush, old droppings lay black and dry. The trail climbed from there into a cleft where spruce and stone narrowed the light.

Jake stood below it a while before he went in.

The river made noise enough to cover a boot on gravel. That was good and bad. It hid a man moving in, but it also hid a man already there. He crouched by the mud, touched one print with two fingers, and looked at the brush where the deer had passed. Hair clung to a broken twig. The break was fresh. The bark had not dried.

He took the ash cloak from his shoulders and folded it over one arm so it would not catch. Then he climbed from the bar into the cleft, walking where stone took his weight and moss covered the rest. Twice he stopped and listened. Once he backed out three steps and came in again by a different line, because the first way left too much sign in the mud.

The cleft opened after twenty paces.

A flat shelf lay above the river, not broad enough for a settlement and not poor enough to pass by. Spruce grew close on the upper side. Rock shouldered the lower side in a gray wall, with the river running hard below it. A spring came out from under a moss shelf at the back of the flat and ran clear over gravel before dropping in a narrow run toward the river.

Jake went to the spring first.

He lay belly-down, set his mouth to the cold water, and drank until his teeth ached. No bog taste. No rot. No dead thing lying upstream. The water came clean out of stone, sweet enough to make him drink again after he had already had his fill.

He washed his hands and watched the mud run from his knuckles.

The place had been waiting for work, not for comfort.

That suited him.

He rose and moved along the back wall. His heel found old deer crossings where the ground dipped. Turkeys had scratched under beech leaves near the spring run. A fox track crossed the deer trail and vanished under laurel. Higher up, bear claw marks scored a fallen log, old but plain. Squirrels scolded from spruce limbs and went silent when he looked too long.

Game used the gorge because it gave water, cover, and a way through stone.

A man could use it for the same reasons.

Jake walked the lower edge first. The river ran below him, too far down for easy water hauling from that side but close enough to hear day and night. The rock wall dropped almost straight for most of its length. At the west end, stones tumbled in a slope a man might climb if he had time and both hands. At the east end, the river mouth narrowed to a gap where two men with rifles could hold off more than two men trying to come in.

He did not have two men.

He had himself, an axe, a knife, a rifle with more use than beauty, a coil of rope, one worn blanket, a little meal, salt, a pouch of lead, and the ash cloak that had followed him from village to trading post to Caleb's fire and away again.

That was enough to begin.

He climbed the back side next.

The slope rose hard through spruce and laurel. It would punish a man running uphill. It would punish a man carrying stolen goods worse. Near the top, the gorge narrowed into a deer pass between two stone teeth. Jake knelt there and looked back down. From above, the flat shelf showed in broken pieces through limbs, not as a cabin site, not as a claim, only as brush, stone, and shadow. Smoke would betray it if he built foolish. Cut timber would betray it if he left pale stumps open to the sky.

He marked that in his head and then made himself mark it with work.

A man alone could forget the thing that did not hurt yet. Thorn had cured him of that. Cold killed when a man waited until night to patch bark. Hunger killed when a man waited until his belly was empty to set snares. Men killed when a man waited until he saw them in the doorway.

Jake took a flat stone and set it beside the deer pass. Not to claim it. To remind himself. First watch point.

He stayed there longer than a rested man would have.

The back pass gave him one way out if the river mouth filled with men. It also gave any man above him one way in. A hunter following deer could find it. A boy chasing a lost dog could find it. McCrea's sort of man could find it if coin set him walking long enough. Stone did not keep secrets. It only made a man pay for reaching them.

Jake crawled ten paces beyond the pass on hands and knees and studied the ground from the outside. Ferns covered most of the sign. Deer had worn the center low. A man's boot would show at once in damp soil near the first bend. He cut a dead spruce twig and laid it across the path at ankle height, not fixed, only resting. If it lay broken later, something larger than a rabbit had come through.

He put a second marker under laurel where no deer would brush it, a sliver of pale wood turned bark-up. Small. Nearly nothing. But if he came back and saw pale wood, he would know a hand had moved it.

No wall stood yet. No door. No neighbor's lantern in sight. No hired watchman. Only signs a careful man could read before a careless man killed him.

That was the price of not asking permission.

He found a second place below it where laurel made a screen. A man could lie there with a rifle and cover the climb. He cleared no brush there. He only broke one dead twig already loose and laid it aside, then set two stones under the leaves where his knee could find them again in the dark.

The lower mouth needed the same kind of answer.

He went back down and studied the river gap from inside the gorge. A man coming from the water would step first on slick stone, then gravel, then a short rise where roots crossed the trail. Good for slowing. Bad because the rise hid feet for three steps. Jake cut no brush there yet. Fresh cuts shouted. Instead he moved two fallen limbs to make the easier side look worse and the worse side look easier.

If a man chose the easier-looking side, he would step where Jake could see him from the cabin shelf.

That was not safety.

It was one more breath.

Lower down, near the spring, he found the place where a cabin could stand.

The shelf was not level. No ground in the gorge was level. But one patch held high enough above the spring run that water would not lie under it, and close enough to timber that logs could be dragged without wasting a day on each one. The back wall would stop north wind. The cabin front could face the river mouth, with the door turned a little away so any man coming up from the gap would have to cross open ground before he saw inside.

Jake paced it heel to toe.

Six paces one way.

Five the other.

Too small for pride. Big enough for a bunk, a hearth, a peg wall, and room to stand with a rifle in hand.

He paced it again and made it seven by five. A man needed space to turn when winter clothes stiffened and smoke pressed low. He had slept in bark shelters tight enough to teach that lesson. He had crawled from one near dawn with ice in his hair and breath rattling in his chest. He had no wish to build a poor hole and call it home because he was in a hurry.

Home.

Jake's jaw set. He looked at the ground harder.

The word had weight only if he paid for it now.

Seven by five meant more logs than six by five. More logs meant more axe noise. More axe noise meant a longer chance for someone on the river to stop and listen. A bigger hearth meant more stone to drag. A better roof meant more bark, more poles, more days with no crop in the ground and no meat hanging.

Small would be faster.

Small would freeze him.

He stood in the marked square and turned with the rifle. At six paces, the muzzle struck where a wall would be. At seven, he could bring it around without catching. He tried it twice, then once with the ash cloak over his shoulders. Cloth dragged at his elbow, but the barrel cleared.

Seven by five stayed.

Hunger could take the extra days from him. So could rain. So could a stranger passing through the lower gap. Jake dug the heel of his boot into the corner line until the mark held dark in the soil.

This was the cost he could see.

The rest would come when it chose.

He set his axe against a spruce and began clearing the cabin patch by hand. Leaves went first. Then rotten sticks. Then stones that would take a man's ankle in the dark. He used the toe of his boot to scrape a line for the sill logs. The soil was thin and full of roots. Good. Roots held banks. Bad. Roots fought every stake.

He took the knife and cut roots no thicker than his finger. Larger ones he left until the axe could be spared. A small oak root crossed one corner of the line. He dug around it with a flat stone and sawed at it until sweat ran down his neck despite the cool air. When it gave, he pulled it free and held it in both hands.

It came out black with earth, twisted hard from years of holding to stone.

He threw it aside.

The spring run came next. It crossed too near the cabin patch, spreading over moss before finding its lower channel. In rain it would widen. In winter it would freeze. In thaw it would rot anything set low near it. Jake cut brush from the run and pulled stones into a narrow side wall, not fine work, not lasting work, but enough to guide water away from where a hearth might stand.

He worked until his shirt stuck to his back.

At midday he ate dry meal mixed with spring water in his cup and a strip of smoked meat bought dear at the last post. He sat on a stone with the rifle across his knees and kept his face turned toward the river mouth while he chewed. Twice he stopped with food in his mouth and listened. Once a jay screamed high up the gorge. Once loose gravel rolled far below the cliff. Both sounds settled back into water and wind.

No horse.

No boot.

No axe but his.

He finished the meat and put the cup away.

Then he went looking for the cache.

A man with one blanket and little meal still needed a place to hide what he had. More than one hungry hand had reached for Jake's bowl in the years behind him. More than one smiling trader had counted wrong while Jake stood close enough to count each coin back. A cabin could burn. A pack could be stolen. A man sleeping could wake with nothing but his shirt.

He found the cache hollow under a boulder set into the back wall near the spring. The stone leaned out just enough to leave dry earth beneath it. Ferns grew around the lip. A fallen spruce limb crossed the front. From ten steps away it was nothing but old rock and brush.

Jake cleared it with his hands, took out three stones, and lined the bottom with bark stripped from a deadfall. He set his lead pouch there, then took it out again.

Too soon.

If he forgot the place or a storm flooded it, he would curse himself. He set only a twist of dry tinder inside and covered it. Then he stood back, walked away, and came at it from another angle. The hollow vanished. He found it again by the forked birch above it and the two stones under the moss.

Good.

He tested it with water from the spring.

One cup poured above the boulder ran left and vanished into fern. Another poured higher found a crack and dripped into the hollow. Jake pulled the bark out, packed the crack with clay and moss, then set a slanted chip of stone over the seam. He poured again. This time the water ran away from the hollow.

Tinder could live there.

Lead might.

Meal would not, not until he trusted it through a storm.

He tested it with weight too.

Three stones went in first, each wrapped in bark as if they were lead. He covered the hollow and walked the length of the shelf. From the cabin line he could not see the boulder mouth. From the spring he could see fern and old limb, nothing else. From the river gap the whole back wall blurred into shade.

He came back and took the stones out.

No animal smell had gathered there. No ants. No damp under the bark except where his own hand had carried water. The hollow would hold tinder, maybe lead, maybe a knife wrapped in hide. Not food. Food called too many noses. A hungry bear could turn a good hiding place into broken stone and torn earth before sunrise.

Jake set one sliver of charcoal under the bark and covered it again.

If he forgot where he had hidden his own fire, he deserved the cold.

Next he chose the meat pole place. Not near the cabin. Not near the spring. Not where a bear could reach from a slope or a man could see from the river mouth. Two spruce stood close together above the flat, with a third leaning behind them. He cut a sapling, trimmed it, and set it between the trees at shoulder height. The pole sagged. He took it down, cut it shorter, and set it again lower, with forked sticks under both ends. It would hold strips of meat for a day or two once he had meat to hang.

He had none now.

That came later.

He did not like later.

Later was what men said when their bellies still held food. Later was roof work before first frost, meat before snow, rails before a horse learned to wander, moss before the wall wind found every crack. Later could become never with one bad fever or one axe bite in a thigh.

He cut a second pole and set it higher, not for meat, for hides if he took any. Then he pulled both down and moved them three paces uphill because the lower place let scent drift toward the spring. A man could foul his own water by being lazy with blood.

The better place made the work harder.

He took it.

He laid snares in three places before the afternoon light began to thin.

The first went near the turkey scratching, a loop low and small with brush guiding the feet. The second went along a rabbit run under laurel. The third went wrong. He set it too near open ground, saw the mistake from five paces away, and pulled it apart. Better no snare than a bad snare that taught an animal to avoid the place.

Thorn would have struck his wrist for leaving it.

Jake reset it under a root tangle where the run pinched narrow.

He checked each one twice, not because he trusted himself less, but because a man alone had no second pair of eyes. A missed knot cost food. A loose stake cost food. A broken guide stick cost food. In winter, food cost blood.

The gorge made him pay attention to small things.

The river mouth lay quiet. The spring ran clean. The back pass held the same light. A squirrel came down a spruce trunk, saw him, and shot back up with its tail jerking. Jake watched where it had come from. Hollow limb. Possible dry tinder. Possible squirrel later, if he wasted no shot and if hunger made the shot worth powder.

He went back to the cabin patch and faced the trees.

Spruce stood thick on the upper side, but not every tree would serve. Some twisted too much. Some leaned toward stone. Some were too large for one man with one axe and no team. Some were too small and would make walls that leaked wind. He walked among them, touching bark, looking up, judging fall, drag, knot, rot, and distance.

The first log had to teach the rest.

It also had to prove he had not chosen a grave.

If one man could not cut and move the timber, the gorge was only a pretty trap. If the cabin shelf sat too far from usable trees, winter would catch him with half walls. If the first tree fell wrong and broke his leg, no Caleb would hear, no Anna would trade medicine, no Thorn would press resin to the wound. The place would keep his bones as quietly as it kept spring water.

Jake stood among the spruce until that truth settled.

Then he chose the tree anyway.

He chose a spruce no wider than his thigh, straight enough for a sill, and close enough that he could drag it downhill with rope and stubbornness. It leaned away from the spring. Good. It would fall between two stones if the cut was true. Bad if it twisted. Bad if the top caught in another crown. Bad if he misjudged and shattered the end against rock.

He cleared around the base.

The axe had come from the trading post with a handle too smooth from another man's hand. Jake had wrapped rawhide near the head. He checked the wrap now, thumbed the wedge, and set his feet. The first stroke bit shallow. Bark jumped. The sound rang in the gorge and came back thin from the far wall.

He stopped.

The sound mattered.

An axe carried farther than a footstep. Farther than a man wanted. He listened until the river took the ring and broke it apart. No answering shout came. No horse snorted. No crow lifted from the upper trees.

He struck again, lower, cutting the notch clean.

Chips fell pale on moss. Sap wet the axe edge. He worked slow at first, then found the pace. Not fast. Fast wasted breath and made poor cuts. He had learned that splitting wood for the village, then again under Thorn, then again beside Caleb when winter timber froze hard enough to make an axe glance. The gorge did not care about hurry. The spruce did not care about need.

It gave only to good blows.

Jake cut the face notch toward the fall line, then moved to the back. The axe lifted and fell. Lifted and fell. Sweat ran into his beard. His palms burned. Once the axe glanced and tore bark instead of wood. He stopped, breathed through his nose, and reset his feet. No one was there to see the mistake. That did not make it smaller.

He cut until the tree began to speak through its own grain.

A faint crack ran above him.

He stepped back.

The spruce held.

He took two more blows on the back cut. The crack deepened. Needles trembled high in the crown though no wind moved below. Jake caught up the rope and pulled from the side, not under the fall. The trunk groaned once, leaned, and then went.

It fell clean between the stones.

The top brushed a smaller spruce and shook loose a shower of needles. The butt kicked once and settled. The sound rolled down into the river gorge and died under the water's noise.

Jake stood with both hands on the axe handle.

No man shouted him back to a hide rack. No gray-haired woman pointed him toward a creek bucket. No warrior laughed over ash on his shirt. No trader weighed his worth in pelts. No McCrea mouth spoke over a counter as if the land already belonged to paper and money.

The log lay where he had meant it to lie.

That was not freedom whole and finished. A gorge could starve a man. A winter could break him. Men from the outside could still find the river mouth if smoke, tracks, or bad luck led them there. The first log did not make walls. It did not make a roof. It did not make safety.

It made a beginning a man could put his hands on.

Jake limbed the spruce before dark. He worked from the butt upward, keeping the trunk between his legs and the blade. Small limbs snapped under the axe. Larger ones needed two cuts. He trimmed them close so the log would drag clean. The branches went into a pile for later firewood and brush cover. Nothing wasted. Nothing left to show more than it had to show.

When the light fell low, he tied the rope around the butt and pulled.

The log moved one handspan.

He reset the rope, leaned his shoulder into it, and pulled again.

Another handspan.

By dusk he had dragged it halfway to the cabin line. His back ached. His hands cramped around the rope. Bark had scraped one shin raw. He left the log there because night work made broken ankles and bad cuts, and he had no woman, boy, or old man to call for help if he bled.

He set the axe under the overhang by the spring. He checked the snares once and found nothing. He drank from the spring and made a small cold camp under the back wall, no fire, no smoke. Meal and water filled his cup. He ate with the rifle across his knees while the gorge darkened from gray to black.

The river kept talking below.

Jake sat until the last light left the upper stone teeth.

He could sleep here.

He could wake here.

He could build here, if his hands held, if the game stayed, if winter did not come too early, if no man followed the river bend and saw what he had started.

The first night tested the choice.

Cold slid down the back wall after dark. Something moved near the spring and stopped when Jake lifted the rifle. Later a fox barked from the lower rocks. Wind crossed the upper spruce and never reached him full, which proved the shelf better than it had looked by day. Near midnight he rose, walked to the river mouth, and stood in shadow until his eyes found the pale stones below. A man coming there would have to show himself.

Good.

Not safe.

Good enough to work for.

He set the cup aside and lay down with the ash cloak over him.

Before sleep took him, he looked toward the pale length of spruce on the cabin line.

The first log was down.
