# Chapter 6: Warrior Training

## Scene 1

By twenty, Jake could run the first ridge before sunrise and still have breath left for Thorn Blackroot's knife work.

Six years had put length on him and taken softness away. His shoulders had narrowed into hard lines instead of farm bulk. His legs knew slopes better than flat ground. His hair hung long and dark, tied back with a strip of hide when he ran. The village still saw the pale captive in him, but fewer men laughed when he passed.

Thorn never laughed.

The old man had not taught him like a father. He had used him like a green tool and filed him down by labor. If Jake ran loud, Thorn made him run again. If Jake breathed too hard, Thorn sent him up the steeper side. If Jake carried anger into knife work, Thorn put him on his back and left him there until the lesson reached bone.

At fifteen, Jake had wanted to prove he could survive.

At twenty, he had learned proving wasted strength.

Survival had become smaller than that. One foot placed right. One breath held back. One loose stone avoided. One fire coal kept alive. One blade kept close to the body. A man stayed alive by such things, not by speeches or fury.

The old man woke him before gray light with one tap of a stick against the shelter post. No greeting. No explanation. Jake came out with bow, knife, and tomahawk because coming out empty meant walking back and losing the first climb.

The ridge trail began behind the village and rose through laurel, stone, and oak roots. Thorn ran without hurry. Jake had learned that the old man's pace was worse than speed. That pace did not break. No shortening came when the climb steepened. The trail climbed and kept climbing until a careless man spent himself fighting the mountain instead of moving with it.

Jake had fought it the first year.

The mountain had won.

Now he shortened his stride where the trail steepened and opened it where stone gave way to pine needles. He breathed through his nose until he could not, then through his mouth without letting it rasp. A loose stone rolled under his foot. He caught himself against a trunk and kept running.

At the top, Thorn pointed back down.

Jake turned and ran again.

The second climb took the strength from his calves. The third put iron in his breath. Thorn did not look back to see if Jake followed. The old man's feet found stone as if the ridge had grown for him alone. Jake slipped once near the laurel bend and struck one knee hard enough to split skin. Thorn kept running.

Jake rose and followed.

At the top the fourth time, Thorn stood beside a pine and listened to Jake breathe.

"Too loud."

Jake bent with both hands on his thighs.

The stick touched his shoulder.

"Again."

The fifth climb made his sight pulse at the edges.

Thorn did not let him sit. He took Jake by the back of the neck and turned his face toward the trail they had just run. Broken fern. A heel scrape where Jake had slipped. One stone kicked loose and lying pale side up. Thorn pointed to each mark as if reading a tally.

"Enemy reads too."

Jake swallowed against the burn in his throat.

Downhill should have been easier. It was not. Tired legs wanted to fall ahead of the body. Loose leaves hid holes between roots. The same trail that had stolen breath climbing tried to take knees descending. Thorn made him come down slow enough to leave no new sign, then climb again fast enough to make the lungs work.

By sunrise, Jake had learned the ridge twice: once by pain, once by the marks pain left behind.

Thorn pointed him away from the sitting log. He carried bark squares from a lean-to and tied them to trunks at knee height, chest height, and high in the brush. Jake strung the bow with raw fingers. He loosed and crossed the leaves to fetch the shaft. A twig broke under his heel. Thorn took the arrow and sent him back to the mark.

The bow punished hurry differently than the ridge did. A fast foot could be corrected with pain. A fast hand wasted arrows. Thorn set one bark square half hidden behind brush so Jake had to shoot through a gap no wider than his palm. The first arrow clipped a twig and spun away. The second struck dirt. The third hit the bark but tore too high.

Thorn took the fourth arrow before Jake set it.

"Why?"

Jake looked at the brush, then at the mark, then at the leaves moving behind it.

"Wind."

Thorn gave the arrow back.

Jake waited until the leaves stilled.

The shaft struck near center.

Thorn moved the bark square farther away.

By the end of that morning, Jake's fingers bled where the string had worn them.

Thorn did not bind them. He handed Jake a strip of soft hide and made him wrap the fingers himself. Too thick, and he could not feel the string. Too thin, and blood made the grip slick. Jake wrapped, drew, failed, unwrapped, wrapped again. The work was small and maddening, which meant Thorn liked it.

At last the string came back clean and left the arrow straight.

Thorn set the bark square behind brush again.

After the bow came the knife.

Close work. Low work. No wide motion. Thorn struck Jake's wrist every time the blade drifted too far from his body. The first week Jake's forearm was black with bruises. The second week the bruises moved higher because he corrected one mistake and found another.

The tomahawk came last.

It was not thrown for show. Thorn made him cut saplings, hook branches, strike low, strike close, recover fast. The hand had to know the weight before the throw mattered. Jake missed more than he hit at first. The tomahawk bounced from a stump and nearly took his shin. Thorn looked at the mark in the dirt, then at Jake.

"Dead man throws twice."

Jake picked up the tomahawk and started again.

He learned the tomahawk first as a tool because Thorn would not let him make it only a weapon. Cut a sapling clean. Trim the limbs without burying the blade too deep. Hook a branch and pull it down. Split kindling with one stroke. Shave a stake. Cut cord. Only after that came the strike.

When Jake threw from too far, Thorn made him walk to the stump and stand where the blade had bounced.

"There."

Jake looked at the mark in the dirt.

"That is where your leg was."

Then Thorn made him throw from close until the handle no longer twisted in his palm.

## Scene 2

Failure became part of the work.

Jake missed bark disks swinging from rawhide. Wet leaves took his feet out from under him. Hard throws buried the blade where he could not pull it free quickly. A bow drawn too soon left his arm shaking. One rushed knife turn caught Thorn's stick across his ribs.

Each mistake left a mark.

Thorn named almost none of them.

A tap at the elbow meant lower. A cut of the hand meant silence. Two fingers pointed at Jake's feet meant he had left tracks too wide, too deep, too easy to read. A grunt meant the work might hold. Silence meant try again.

One autumn morning, Thorn took him to a creek bend where fog lay low over stone. Three bark rounds stood across the water. Jake had to cross, shoot, retrieve, return, and leave the bank looking as if no man had crossed there.

The first crossing left mud on a rock.

The second broke a fern.

The third put an arrow in the wrong mark.

The fourth nearly cost him the bow.

He stepped where water had slicked a flat stone and both feet went out. The bow hand struck first. He saved the wood from the creek by driving his elbow into stone. Pain ran from elbow to shoulder. Thorn watched from the bank with no move to help.

Jake stood with water in one moccasin and mud across his sleeve.

Thorn pointed to the far bank.

The drill was not finished because a man had fallen. Men fell. Men who stopped there died there.

On the fourth, Jake stood still before moving. The water talked over stone. Wind came down the creek and touched the left side of his face. He stepped where moss covered rock, not where bare stone would show mud. Across the creek, he knelt behind a sycamore, drew, and loosed only when the fog thinned.

The arrow struck bark where Thorn had marked it.

Jake crossed back by a different line, brushing his last print with a pine bough.

Thorn walked the bank.

One grunt.

That was enough.

The knife breakthrough came in winter when cold made fingers slow. Thorn struck at him with a peeled sapling, not hard enough to break bone but hard enough to teach. Jake stopped trying to meet the stick. He stepped inside it, shoulder close to Thorn's chest, knife hand low, free hand checking the old man's wrist.

The stick missed his back.

Thorn stopped.

"Again."

Again meant ten more times.

Then twenty.

Cold stiffened Jake's fingers until the knife handle felt too small. Thorn struck his knuckles when the grip opened. He struck his ribs when the elbow lifted. He struck the back of Jake's head once when Jake looked at the knife instead of Thorn's shoulder.

"Shoulder tells."

Jake watched the shoulder.

The stick came lower.

He took the blow on his forearm and went inside it, not clean, but close enough that Thorn had to step back.

The old man did not grunt that time.

He struck again.

The tomahawk came after. Not a clean victory. No sudden mastery. Just one day when the blade turned the way Jake meant, struck handle high, came free, and returned to his grip without wasted motion.

Then again.

Then under rain.

Then with breath short after a ridge run.

Skill was not a thing given. It was a thing worn into the body until the body reached for it without asking.

That winter and the spring after it, the drills changed shape. Thorn set Jake to climb a ridge, loose one arrow with breath still running, drop the bow, take the knife, turn to meet the stick, then recover the tomahawk from a stump and cut a hanging cord before it stopped swinging. The first week was ugly. Jake dropped the knife twice. The cord swung past untouched. His arrow struck low because his lungs shook the bow.

Thorn made every failure leave work behind it.

A dropped knife meant Jake carried water with that hand closed around a stone for the rest of the morning. A missed cord meant he cut and hung three more cords before eating. A footstep loud enough to turn Thorn's head meant he went back through the same leaves until the path held no fresh scrape. The punishment was not anger. It was repair.

Jake began to see the shape of it.

If a bow hand shook after running, more running did not fix it by itself. He had to learn where to put breath while the string came back. If a knife fell from numb fingers, rage at the fingers did nothing. He had to warm them under his arm, flex them against the handle, and keep the grip low. If the tomahawk struck wrong when he was tired, pride would not turn the blade. Feet, distance, and handle would.

Thorn built the drills so each weakness exposed the next.

Run. Breathe. Shoot. Drop. Step in. Cut. Hook. Move. Vanish.

At first Jake tried to master each piece and carry it to the next.

By spring he stopped carrying them.

He let one become the next.

By late spring, the pieces began to join.

He could run, stop, and make his breath lie. He could step from stone to moss without looking down. He could feel when the tomahawk handle sat wrong before the throw left his hand. He could take one blow without chasing the pain. None of it made him unbeatable. It made him harder to kill quickly.

Thorn proved that whenever Jake began to trust a skill too much.

One morning the old man set no bark rounds and gave no warning. He sent Jake up the ridge with only the bow. Halfway through the climb, a stone clattered behind him. Jake turned toward the sound. Thorn came from the other side and struck the bow from his hand with the peeled sapling.

"Sound lies."

Jake picked up the bow.

The next time, he did not turn his head. He dropped low, looked through the brush ahead, and saw Thorn's foot before the stick came. The blow still struck his shoulder, but not clean.

"Better."

Better meant another climb.

Another drill came at dusk. Thorn made Jake sit beside the creek with his eyes closed and name what passed. Water over stone. Wind in beech leaves. Dog far below. A jay. A boy laughing near the village. Then a pause where no sound belonged.

Jake opened his eyes.

Thorn stood behind him with the stick raised.

That blow missed.

## Scene 3

The cloak began as hides nobody had wanted whole.

Jake chose pieces from the work area and cut around scars, thin spots, and old tears. He stitched them by firelight with sinew, keeping the seams flat so brush would not catch. It was not village finery and not settler clothing. It was cover.

He had watched deer vanish in gray timber, turkeys flatten into leaves, and old hunters step from tree shadow only when they wanted to be seen. His pale shirt betrayed him in winter woods. His skin betrayed him in moonlight. Even brown hide could shine wrong when bark was wet and the ground was ash-gray with old fire.

The cloak had to break his shape.

Not decorate it.

Thorn watched the first night and gave no word.

The second night he tossed Jake a strip of softer buckskin for the shoulder line.

Jake used it.

The ash came from old coals and burned bark. Jake ground it fine between two flat stones, mixed it with grease, then rubbed it into the buckskin until the tan hide dulled toward gray. Smoke darkened the edges. Pine soot took where the grease held. He worked the color in by hand until his fingers turned black.

The first mixture streaked too much. Rain would wash it. He scraped it off with a dull knife and started again with less grease and more soot. The second made the hide too stiff. It cracked when folded. He warmed it near coals, worked it between both hands, and rubbed deer fat into the seams until they bent without shine.

He stitched small breaks along the lower edge so the cloak would move in pieces. A straight edge caught the eye. A broken edge looked more like brush. He darkened the shoulder line where moonlight would find it first and left the back mottled, gray, brown, and smoke-black.

The inside mattered too.

Rough seams rubbed skin raw on a long crawl. Loose stitching caught twigs. A fold too thick held water and slapped against his leg when he ran. Jake turned the cloak inside out and worked each seam with his thumb. Where sinew rose too high, he flattened it with the dull back of the knife. Where hide pulled tight across the shoulder, he cut a small crescent and patched it flatter.

Thorn watched him ruin one neat line and make the cloak uglier.

Ugly worked better.

Jake darkened the inside edges with soot where wind might lift them. He rubbed dry dirt into the lower hem so fresh ash would not puff when he dropped to the ground. Then he shook the whole cloak hard beside the fire. Gray dust rose. Too much. He beat it against a log until only a little came free.

A cloak that hid a man until he moved and then marked him with ash was no cloak at all.

A boy passing the fire saw the cloak and laughed the old name.

"Whispering Ash."

Jake looked at the ash on his hands, then at the cloak.

This time the name did not land the same way.

Jake took the cloak to the timber edge before dawn. Without it, his pale shirt caught light between trunks. With it over his shoulders, gray broke his shape against bark, stone, ash, and winter grass. He crouched beside a fallen log and pulled one edge across his arm. A jay flicked from a branch above him, but the dogs below the village did not bark.

Thorn stood ten paces away.

"Move."

Jake moved.

"Too much."

Jake stopped.

"Again."

By noon, Jake had crossed the same patch of timber twenty times. The cloak snagged twice, dragged once, and trapped heat when he ran. He trimmed the lower edge. Small slits went where the hide needed to fold. More ash dulled the bright seam near his shoulder.

The third day he tested it in rain.

Rain darkened the ash and made one panel shine. Jake cut that panel out and replaced it with rougher hide from a smoke rack. The fourth day he crawled through dead grass and found burrs clinging to the lower seam. He picked them free, then rubbed the seam with ash and dry dirt until it held less. The fifth day Thorn made him cross an open patch between two pines while three boys watched from the creek bank.

One boy saw him too soon.

Jake started over.

The next crossing took longer. He moved only when wind moved the grass. He stopped when the boys looked. He kept his pale hands inside the cloak until he reached stone shadow.

No boy called out.

Thorn changed the test again.

He set Jake at the timber edge while village smoke lay low in the morning. A strip of meat hung from a pole near the dog yard. Not much meat. Enough to draw dogs, boys, and flies. Thorn pointed to it, then to Jake, then to the brush behind the pole.

"Go. Touch. Come back."

Jake looked at the dogs.

Two were sleeping. One watched the meat with its nose lifted. A boy sat near the ash pit carving a stick with a dull knife. A woman moved in and out of a shelter carrying bowls.

The test was not theft. The meat would stay where it hung. The work was passage.

Jake went on his belly for the first ten paces, then crouched where the brush rose. The cloak dragged once. He stopped and freed it one finger at a time. The watching dog lifted its head. Jake went still under the gray hide. The dog stared, sneezed, and laid its head down again.

He reached the pole and touched it with two fingers.

The boy looked up.

Jake froze on the far side of the pole with the cloak pulled close and one hand against the dirt. The boy's eyes went past him to the dog yard, then back to his carving. Jake eased away through the brush and did not breathe full until he reached the timber.

Thorn stood there with both hands folded over his stick.

"Again. Faster."

The second time, the dog barked.

The third time, the woman saw movement and threw a clod into the brush.

The fourth time, Jake reached the pole, touched it, came back, and left only one bent grass stem behind him.

Thorn found it anyway.

He bent the grass upright with two fingers and looked at Jake.

That was the lesson. A man might fool dogs, boys, and tired women. The ground still kept count.

The next test came after dark.

Thorn set no meat pole and gave no path. He took Jake to the edge of the timber where moonlight broke across dead grass and stone, then pointed to a stump on the far side of a clearing. Jake had crossed that ground by day. By moon, every pale stem looked like a bone and every shadow looked deep enough to hide in until he reached it and found it thin.

He went too fast the first time.

A dog barked before he made half the crossing.

Thorn struck the ground with his stick, and Jake returned.

The second time, he used the shadows and forgot the wind. The cloak lifted at one corner, showing his leg. Thorn's stick tapped once against stone. Back again.

The third time, he waited for cloud.

Cloud covered the moon only for a breath, but a breath was enough for five steps if the feet already knew where to go. Jake moved before the dark came, crossed while it held, and stopped before light returned. He reached the stump in three cloud breaks and came back in two.

Thorn stood close enough to touch him before Jake saw him.

"Do not trust dark."

Jake held still under the cloak.

"Use it. Do not trust it."

By evening, Thorn walked past him at six paces and did not turn his head until Jake shifted one foot.

That correction pleased Jake more than praise would have.

That night by the fire, Jake worked ash deeper into the cloth, not for the name, not for their laughter, but because gray vanished better than tan under winter trees.

The name Whispering Ash could be a wound.

It could also be cover.

## Scene 4

The first sign of a real test came with weapons gathered before dawn.

Men stood near the council fire with bows, knives, tomahawks, and trade muskets. Jake did not touch the guns. No one offered one. He saw powder horns pass hand to hand and looked away before any man could mistake his attention for claim.

This was not another drill.

The women had cooked before daylight and wrapped food in bark. Men checked bowstrings without speaking. A young hunter painted a line across his cheek, wiped it away, and painted it again. The dogs were tied back. Boys stood at the edge of the clearing and tried to look as if they were not afraid of the silence.

Jake knew the village silence before hunts.

This one had a different weight.

No one joked over poor knots.

No one argued over who carried meal.

One man checked the same knife three times and never seemed pleased with the edge. Another tied a strip of cloth around his wrist where old scars crossed. A woman handed him a bark packet and held his hand one breath longer than the giving required. He pulled away gently, not because he wanted to, but because the gathered men were watching one another for weakness.

Jake watched too.

The weapons told him enough. Too many blades for deer. Too many powder horns for turkey. Men chose darker clothing and left bright beadwork under shelter. One older warrior rubbed soot across the brass on a trade musket until it no longer caught dawn light.

Whatever waited beyond the ridge would look back.

Thorn handed him a bow.

Then the tomahawk.

Then the ash cloak.

The old man's scarred face held no softness. "Trail blood is not stump work."

Jake tied the cloak across his shoulders. The ash smell rose in the cold.

"I know."

Thorn's eyes narrowed.

"No. You will."

Before they joined the gathered men, Thorn made him run one last short drill behind the shelters.

Not for skill.

For proof that skill stayed when eyes watched.

Jake crossed from one woodpile to another under the ash cloak, took the bow from where Thorn had set it, drew on a bark chip no larger than a hand, then dropped the bow and took up the tomahawk without looking at the handle. The first throw struck low. Thorn made no sound. Jake fetched it, came back, and did it again.

The second throw cut the bark chip from the post.

"Do not chase kill," Thorn told him.

Jake looked at the cut chip on the ground.

"Do work. Leave."

That was the last lesson before real blood.

Across the clearing, men checked cords and blades. The village had gone quiet in the way it did before hunters left, only harder. No deer hunt waited beyond the ridge. The looks were different. The weapons were different. Even the dogs stayed back from the gathering men.

Jake set the bowstring with his thumb and looked toward the dark line of trees.

The body that had carried water and scraped hides was gone. The boy who had swung a farm axe was still somewhere under the scars, but the hands were different now. Bow. Knife. Tomahawk. Ash cloak. Ridge lungs. Silent feet.

None of it had been given kindly.

All of it had stayed.

When Thorn stepped toward the trail, Jake followed.
