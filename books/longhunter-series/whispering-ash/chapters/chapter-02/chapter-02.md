# Chapter 2: The Long March

## Scene 1

Smoke stayed in Jake's shirt long after the farm dropped out of sight.

It clung to the linen across his shoulders and mixed with sweat, blood, and crushed leaves. Each breath brought a taste of ash. Each step pulled the rawhide tighter around his wrists. His hands had swollen through the night, and by morning his fingers were thick and stiff, bent a little from the binding behind his back.

The men did not stop at sunrise.

The line had walked under trees until the last red of the burning cabin disappeared behind ridges. Men had crossed dark hollows where Jake could not see the feet ahead of him, only hear them. Twice he had fallen. Twice a hand or rawhide loop had hauled him up before he could get his knees under him. At dawn, when gray light came through the laurel, the party was still climbing.

The Appalachian ridges rose one behind another, dark with oak, pine, and hemlock. Mist lay low in the cuts. Wet leaves slapped Jake's shins. Roots crossed the trail like black snakes. Loose stones rolled under his boots and sent pain up his legs.

He had eaten nothing since the morning before the raid.

A strip of dried meat passed from one warrior to another ahead of him. Jake watched it move hand to hand. Nobody offered him any. One man drank from a skin, wiped his mouth with the back of his hand, and tied the skin shut. Jake's tongue pressed against the roof of his mouth, too thick and dry for his teeth.

He kept walking.

The scarred warrior stayed behind him. Jake knew him by the drag in one step and the creak of a leather strap. When Jake slowed, the man did not speak. A shove between the shoulder blades was enough. If Jake stumbled twice in the same stretch, the man struck the back of his leg with a switch cut from green wood.

The first blow made Jake bite his tongue.

The second left a hot line behind his knee.

He did not look back.

Ahead, the trail dipped into a creek bed no wider than a wagon tongue. Water ran over flat stone, clear and cold. The men crossed without stopping, placing their feet on rocks that barely broke the surface. Jake tried to follow the same stones. His balance failed on the third step. With his hands tied, he could not steady himself. One boot slid into the water.

Cold ran up his leg.

The rawhide snapped tight. The scarred warrior jerked him forward. Jake's boot struck the next rock and held. Water splashed his trousers, then the party climbed the far bank.

At the top, a warrior turned and watched Jake's wet boot drag through leaves. He made a short sound. Another answered. Jake could not tell if it was mockery or warning.

He marked the sound anyway.

The word came again at the next crossing. It came with a point of the hand toward stone. The men used it before stepping over slick rock or through mud. Jake held the sound in his mouth without speaking it.

Stone.

Maybe not. Maybe something close to it. Maybe only a command to mind footing. He did not know. He only knew it came before rock and water, and that knowing one breath earlier where the foot should go kept a man from falling.

The march wore him down in small pieces. A briar tore his sleeve and opened a red line along his arm. A low branch struck his cheek. Hunger made his knees loose on steep ground. When the trail rose, he leaned into it with the same stubborn drive he had used behind a plow, but the field had never hit back. The mountains did. Stone rolled. Mud grabbed at his wet boot. Laurel bent and whipped him after the man ahead passed through.

Once, they halted under a chestnut while two warriors vanished ahead. The rest waited in silence. Jake stood with his head down and watched ants travel across a root at his feet. The ants moved in a line, each carrying white scraps from a torn nest. One slipped. Another crossed over it and kept going.

A hand struck Jake's shoulder.

The line moved again.

By noon the trail climbed into laurel so thick it scraped both shoulders. The men passed through gaps Jake would not have seen from ten paces away. One bent a branch aside and let it fall back in Jake's face. Leaves struck his split lip. He tasted blood again.

The weaker captive fell there.

Jake had known there were other captives by their sounds before he had seen them clearly. A cough ahead. A dragging foot. A woman crying once in the dark and then cut silent by a threat. By daylight he saw two figures bound in the line beyond the pack horses. One was a grown man with gray in his beard. The other was smaller, maybe a boy, maybe a slight man, with a torn sleeve dark from shoulder to elbow.

The smaller one went down in the laurel gap.

No cry came from him. His knees folded and his shoulder struck a root. The line stopped badly. Men shifted. One warrior cursed in Cherokee and struck him across the back with the butt of a gun.

The captive tried to rise. His tied hands slipped in leaves. Blood ran from under his torn sleeve and dropped on a fern.

Jake stood four paces behind him.

The guard lifted the gun butt again.

The smaller captive saw it and tried to crawl rather than stand. That made the men angrier. A crawling captive slowed a line more than a dead one, and Jake could see that judgment pass from face to face. One warrior looked down the trail as if measuring where a body could be dragged out of sight. Another reached for a knife, not yet drawing it, only touching the handle.

The smaller captive had one chance left.

Feet under him.

Now.

If he stayed down, the line would leave him one of two ways.

Jake saw both in the men's hands. The knife handle under a thumb. The gun butt lifting. The quick glance toward the laurel where a body could be rolled out of sight and covered by leaves before the next ridge. No speech told him. The trail told him. Hunger told him. Men in haste did not carry weakness far unless weakness could walk.

The scarred warrior shoved Jake forward to keep the line moving. Jake took two steps and stopped beside the fallen captive. The man's face was gray under dirt. His eyes rolled toward Jake, then away.

Another blow lifted.

Jake moved before it fell.

He dropped to one knee beside the captive, though the rawhide behind his back pulled hard at his shoulders. With tied hands he could do little. He set one shoulder under the man's arm and pushed upward with his legs. The captive sagged against him. Jake braced both boots in the leaves and drove himself upright.

The warrior with the gun stared.

Jake could not hold the man long with his wrists bound. He hooked the captive's torn sleeve with his fingers and pulled the cloth tight above the bleeding place, twisting it around until it bit into the arm. It was a poor binding, clumsy behind his back, but it slowed the red run.

Farm cuts had taught him that much. Ax nicks. Briar tears. A scythe slice across his brother's thumb. Press the cloth tight. Lift the limb if you could. Keep the blood in the man.

The scarred warrior struck Jake across the shoulder.

Jake bent under it but did not let the captive fall.

The smaller man found his feet. He leaned against Jake for three steps, then four. The line began moving again. Jake walked with him, shoulder to shoulder, until the trail widened enough for the man to stagger on his own.

The man's breath scraped in and out, too fast. Every third step his wounded arm swung loose and struck his side. Jake shifted closer until his own bound hands brushed the torn cloth. He could feel warmth through the sleeve, wrong warmth, wet warmth. The binding he had made would not last if the man fell again.

A root lifted across the trail.

Jake stepped over it first, then leaned back into the captive just enough to slow him. The smaller man dragged one foot over instead of catching it. Behind them, the scarred warrior made a sound low in his throat. Not approval. Not patience. Only a warning that the next delay would be paid for.

Jake took more of the man's weight.

His own ribs burned with it. The rawhide cut deeper as his shoulders pulled backward. A stumble now would bring them both down, and the line would not waste time sorting one captive from another. Jake kept his feet under him because the smaller man could not.

At the next creek, the scarred warrior caught Jake by the bindings and pulled him aside.

Jake waited for the club.

Instead, the man cut one notch in the rawhide between Jake's wrists. Not enough to free him. Not enough to ease the march. The binding still held his hands close behind his back, but blood moved into his fingers again in hot pins and needles.

The warrior shoved a wooden cup toward his mouth.

Jake drank three swallows before the cup was taken away.

No word of kindness came with it. No change came in the man's face. The scarred warrior only pointed up the trail.

Jake walked.

The smaller captive glanced at him once before the line bent around a wall of rock. Jake did not answer the look. He had no answer to give.

## Scene 2

That night they camped in a narrow place between two ridges where the wind moved over the top but not through the bottom.

The captors chose the ground without talk Jake could follow. They did not build a large fire. Three small fires burned apart from one another, each tucked behind stone or fallen timber. Smoke lay low before it found the dark. Men cut green boughs and laid them against the wind side. One checked the trail they had come by, walking backward over his own prints with a pine branch in his hand.

Jake watched from the base of a hemlock.

His wrists were tied around the trunk behind him. The bark pressed between his shoulder blades. His legs were free but useless for anything except folding under him. The smaller captive sat two trees away, chin on his chest. The older bearded man lay on his side and shook under a blanket too thin for the cold.

No one spoke English at first.

The captors ate before the captives. They warmed strips of meat on green sticks and passed a cake of parched meal. Jake smelled grease, smoke, and damp wool. His stomach cramped so hard he had to bend forward against the rope.

A warrior noticed and laughed.

Another made a motion with two fingers, like a mouth opening and closing. Several men answered. Jake caught one repeated word. It came with looks toward the captives. It came again when a man pointed to the trail west. Jake stored it beside the creek word from morning.

One warrior told a story by the fire.

Jake knew it was a story by the shape of it, not the sense. The man's voice rose and dropped. His hands showed a hill, then a crawling thing, then a rifle. Men around the fire gave short laughs. One slapped his knee. Another spat into the flames. When the speaker pointed at Jake, the firelight caught the red-brown stain on Jake's shirt where the dead warrior's blood had dried.

The laughter thinned.

The scarred warrior looked at Jake across the fire. He lifted a finger and drew it across the air in front of his own throat. No smile came with the motion.

Jake held his eyes on the man's moccasins.

The threat needed no translation.

Later, a scrap of meat struck the dirt near Jake's boot. It was small, no longer than two fingers, blackened at one edge. He looked at it. No one moved to stop him. With his hands bound around the tree, he could not pick it up.

He worked one boot forward and pinned the meat under the sole. Then he bent, slow, until his mouth reached his knee. It took three tries to catch the strip with his teeth. Dirt gritted between the meat and his tongue. He chewed anyway.

The smaller captive watched from the next tree. His lips were cracked. Jake shifted the remaining half of the strip with his boot, dragging it through leaves until it lay nearer the other man's knee.

The movement was small.

The scarred warrior saw it.

The scarred man looked ready to take the meat away or strike them both. Instead, he reached down, picked up the strip, tore it in two, and tossed one piece at each captive's feet.

Then he turned back to the fire.

That was the mercy. A cut in the rawhide. Three swallows of water. Half a burned scrap of meat from the dirt. Nothing more.

Jake ate what he could reach.

Cold came down after full dark. It settled in his wet boot first, then climbed his leg. His shoulders burned from the way his arms were pinned behind the tree. His wrists had gone numb again. Each time he shifted, the bark scraped his back.

The captors slept in turns. Two men held the trail mouth. One stayed near the horses. One stood behind the captives. Later, a hand touched a shoulder, a man rose, and another folded down near the fire.

A dream came in pieces. Corn leaves. His mother at the creek. His father walking up the ridge. The dead warrior's eyes beside the woodpile.

A boot struck Jake's foot before dawn.

He woke with his mouth open and no cry in it.

The men were already breaking camp.

One kicked dirt over the coals. Another scattered the ash with a stick. A third lifted the branch screen and dragged it aside so the leaves fell back in no proper shape. The camp disappeared faster than Jake would have believed. Fire became black dirt. Hides rolled tight under leather straps. Footprints vanished under brushed leaves.

The trail took them west again before the sky was fully gray.

## Scene 3

On the second day, Jake began to watch with purpose.

The watching did not ease hunger. His belly had become a hard empty place that pulled at him every mile. It did not ease pain. His wrists were raw, his forearm swollen, and his wet boot had rubbed one heel open. It only gave him something to hold besides hurt.

The men did not travel like settlers.

The men did not keep to the widest trace. They moved along it only when stone or water broke sign. Their route cut across slopes where leaves lay thick. Moccasins found roots, fallen logs, and flat rock. At creek crossings, one man went first and waited on the far side, eyes on the banks. Another stayed behind until all had crossed.

Jake counted them when he could.

He counted by feet. He counted by weapons. Long gun. Club. Knife. Hatchet. Bow. Pack strap. A limping step. Scarred face. Torch man. Young man with red paint flaked dry along his jaw. The count changed when trees blocked his view, but he kept building it again.

At midday they stopped near a spring that came out under a stone shelf. The water ran cold into a hollow of leaves. The men drank in order. Horses first. Warriors next. Captives last.

Jake watched who took charge without needing to ask. The scarred warrior stood back from the spring and let others drink, but men looked to him before moving on. He was not the loudest. He wasted the fewest gestures. When he pointed, men obeyed.

The smaller captive swayed at the spring.

Jake shifted beside him and set his shoulder where the man could lean without making a show of it. The man's weight came onto him, light and fever-hot. Jake kept his eyes on the water. The scarred warrior watched but did not strike.

The fever had worsened since morning.

The man's mouth hung open. His skin had gone dry where rain and sweat should have shone. When the water came to him, his hands shook so hard the cup knocked against his teeth. A warrior snatched it away after one swallow and turned to the horses.

Jake could not give him more water.

He could give him the stone under his foot, the shoulder under his arm, the pace of one man who still had strength enough to spend. It was not mercy. Mercy belonged to men who had choices. This was labor against a knife not yet drawn.

Jake lowered his mouth to the spring when his turn came. He drank until a hand caught his hair and pulled him back.

Too much water too fast could sicken a man. His mother had told him that after summer field work. Jake swallowed what he had and let the rest run down his chin.

The torn sleeve on the smaller captive had loosened. Blood had dried black around the cloth. Flies found it when the day warmed. Jake used two fingers behind his bound back to twist the cloth tighter as they walked. The man gave one short hiss through his teeth.

"Walk." Jake kept the word low.

It was the first word he had spoken since the farm.

The other captive nodded once.

By afternoon the trail climbed over bare stone. Wind moved there, clean and cold, cutting the smoke smell at last from Jake's shirt. From the height he saw ridge after ridge running blue and dark into the west. No road. No field. No chimney smoke from settler cabins. Only timber, folds of land, and distant hawks riding the air.

The party halted at the high place.

One warrior crouched and moved three stones from one side of a game trail to the other. Another broke a twig and left it hanging by bark. A third scraped mud from his moccasin on a root, then covered the mark with leaves.

The trail carried its own marks.

A shove moved Jake on.

After that, each turn had marks to study. Bent grass near a turn. A cut on bark. A stone placed where water had not put it. Sometimes the party turned where no mark showed. Sometimes a mark meant nothing at all.

The same sounds came back.

The creek sound came before water. Another came before halt. A harsher one came before a blow or a raised hand. The captors used a short sound for Jake, not his name, while pointing at his pale face, tied hands, and blood-stained shirt.

Each sound fastened itself to work. One before packs were lifted. One before a man checked the back trail. One before the captives were shoved aside while horses picked through a narrow place. They were not speech yet. They were warnings. A man could live by warnings.

The smaller captive failed twice that afternoon. The first time, Jake caught him by turning his bound wrists outward and letting the man's shoulder strike his ribs instead of the ground. The second time, the scarred warrior caught the captive first and shoved him upright with no care for the wounded arm. Jake saw the man's knees bending again and stepped close enough to brace him with one hip.

No thanks came.

None was needed.

The man had begun to burn with fever.

Jake could feel it through torn cloth when their shoulders touched. Heat where the day was cold. Breath too fast. Feet landing late. A farm boy knew a hurt animal by gait before it fell, and men were not so different when pain had eaten through pride.

At the next halt, Jake used the side of his boot to push wet leaves away from a flat stone near the captive's foot. The smaller man sank onto it instead of the mud. A little thing. Enough to keep cold from taking more strength before the line moved.

The scarred warrior saw.

He did not stop it.

That was the limit of what the day allowed.

No rest longer than a breath. No cup left within reach. No loose binding. No hand freed to press cloth to the wound. Jake learned the size of help under captivity, and the size was mean. A shoulder. A step. A stone kept dry. Half a strip of burned meat dragged through dirt. Enough to keep a man walking. Not enough to save him from tomorrow.

The smaller captive lifted his face once, eyes unfixed, and seemed to look past Jake rather than at him.

Jake looked away first.

The line moved before either of them could gather more strength.

The trail bent through rhododendron where old leaves lay thick and black. A deer trail crossed it, then vanished under stone. One warrior touched two fingers to the broken edge of a fern and pointed uphill. Another nodded. Jake looked at the fern after they passed. The stem was pinched, not snapped clean. Something had gone through there before them. Maybe a man. Maybe deer. He had no way to know yet.

He kept the fern in mind with the stones, the twig, the creek word, and the order of the guards.

Evening came with colder air and a smell of woodsmoke that did not belong to their small hidden fires.

The scarred warrior lifted one hand. The party slowed.

Dogs barked somewhere ahead.

Jake raised his head.

Through the trees, beyond a screen of hemlock and rock, he saw smoke lifting from many fires. Not one cabin. Not one farm. A hollow opened between ridges, and in that hollow stood dark shapes of lodges and bark shelters. Figures moved between them. More dogs barked. A child's voice carried, then vanished under the sound of men calling from the trail.

The smaller captive sagged beside him.

Jake took the man's weight for three more steps.

The scarred warrior looked back at them, then toward the hollow. His hand closed around the rawhide at Jake's wrists and pulled him forward.

Jake walked into the last shade before the village.

He kept his eyes open.
