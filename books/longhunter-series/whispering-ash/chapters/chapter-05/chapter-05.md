# Chapter 5: The Hunt

## Scene 1

Two winters and two summers changed Jake without asking leave.

His trousers rose above his ankles and had to be cut and pieced with hide. Loads of wood widened his shoulders. Hunger, cold, smoke, and work took the last roundness from his face. The rawhide scars at his wrists faded to pale cords, but they never vanished.

His hands changed most.

The farm calluses he had carried from Virginia split, peeled, and came back harder in different places. Hide scraping thickened the base of his fingers. Bow cord cut a line near the first joint. Bark work blackened the cracks. Cold opened the old rawhide scars until they stung in winter, and smoke made every healing cut itch. He learned which pain warned and which pain only spoke.

His stride changed too. At first the mountain trails had made him lift his feet too high and spend strength like a fool. By the third year he stepped lower, let the slope take some of the work, and used roots as stairs without looking at them. A load that once bent him now rode across his back while he watched the trees ahead.

The village still called him Whispering Ash.

The name came less often with laughter now. It came as a summons to work. It came as warning. Children used it because they had never known another name for him. Jake did not answer quickly to it at first. After a while, not answering brought a blow or extra labor.

He remained apart.

Thorn Blackroot did not change that. The old man taught him knots, bark, coals, snares, dry wood, snow sign, and the difference between noise and sound. He taught by doing, by correcting, by taking a task from Jake's hands and making him do it again. Praise was a grunt if the work held. Failure was a stick tapped against the mistake.

Jake learned to take both the same way.

Years passed in tasks rather than marks on a wall.

Spring meant mud pulling at his feet on the creek path and sap rising under bark. Summer meant flies at the hide racks and meat turning bad if smoke failed. Autumn meant carrying nuts, cutting poles, checking snares, and watching men return with deer. Winter meant cold work done with hands that did not want to close.

Through all of it Thorn made him look before touching.

Look at the wood before cutting. Look at the sky before drying meat. Look at the leaves before crossing. Look at a man's feet before judging his hand. Jake had once hated the slowness. Later he found speed inside it.

In the second spring, Thorn took him beyond the creek and made him walk on wet leaves without turning them over. Jake failed before sunrise. Thorn pointed to each dark scrape where his boot had slid. The next day, Jake failed less. By the third week, he could cross a short stretch of leaf mold and leave sign only a better tracker would bother to read.

In summer, Thorn made him watch deer from downwind.

They did not hunt then. They watched. A doe would lift her head before Jake heard what she heard. Her ears turned first. Then her nose. Then the foot lifted. Thorn would tap Jake's arm and point not at the deer, but at the wind, at a jay, at a twig bent under a hoof, at chewed leaves where the edges were still wet.

"See before look," Thorn told him once.

Jake kept his eyes on the wet leaves after that.

Watching became labor of its own.

Thorn made him sit so long his legs slept under him. If Jake shifted, the old man's fingers closed on his shoulder and held him still. They watched deer feed, leave, circle back, and stop where wind changed. They watched a fox cross a trail and avoid the place where men had walked two days before. They watched turkeys scratch under beech leaves and go silent before any man heard the hawk.

Jake began to see how much of the woods moved before the animal itself appeared.

Bent grass.

One leaf turned wet side up.

A jay scolding where no jay had been.

Those signs fed a hunter before meat did.

By the third autumn, Jake could carry two deer hides from the scrape rack without stumbling. The lower ridge no longer stole all his breath before the wood stacking was done. Wood split thin under his knife for Thorn's coal work, and rain no longer killed every fire he tended. Water hauling still came, but not every day. Hide scraping still came, but sometimes another captive took the worst rack while Jake mended snares or carried smoked meat to a storage pit.

That change was small.

Small changes kept men alive.

The first time Thorn motioned him toward a hunting party, Jake looked for another task behind him.

There was none.

Four men stood near the village edge with bows, knives, and rolled hide cords. Thorn carried his own bow, dark with use, and a short knife at his belt. No one handed Jake a weapon at first. One hunter looked at Jake's empty hands and spoke the ash name with a flat mouth. Another made a small sound through his nose.

Thorn gave Jake a coil of cord and a skinning knife with a worn handle.

Not a bow.

Not yet.

Jake took both.

The placement told him the truth. He was not there to make the killing shot. He was there to carry, watch, fetch, and fail where all could see it. If the hunt went wrong, blame would find the pale captive before it found men born to the village.

Thorn did not soften that with a look.

He checked Jake's moccasins, tugged once at the tied cord around Jake's pack, and turned toward the ridge. That was permission enough.

The party left before the sun rose clear above the ridge.

Before they reached the first rise, Thorn stopped and turned Jake once by the shoulder.

Jake had tied the cord high across his chest to keep it from snagging. Thorn pulled it lower, set the coil against Jake's side, and tapped the place twice. Lower meant quieter. Lower meant faster to free. Lower meant less chance of a broken branch catching him by the ribs while other men watched.

Jake fixed the knot himself.

One hunter saw the correction and looked away as if Jake had already proved the morning's joke.

The path beyond the village was not a path to anyone who had not walked it hungry. It crossed rock, ducked under laurel, and climbed where old leaves hid stone. Frost made the roots sweat underfoot. Jake kept behind Thorn and let the old man's ankles teach pace. Long steps on flat ground. Short on slope. Weight on the outside of the foot where leaves were thin. No hand on a sapling unless the sapling had already bent under another hand.

The youngest hunter behind him breathed through his nose, impatient.

Jake did not hurry for him.

## Scene 2

Frost silvered the grass in the low places.

The hunters moved without road or open trail. Thorn walked second. Jake was placed near the rear, ahead of the youngest hunter, where every mistake could be seen and no mistake could spoil the front sign. That was plain enough.

Jake kept his eyes on the ground and his ears open.

The ridge held deer sign from the night before. A narrow track in soft mud by a seep. Droppings dark and damp under mountain laurel. Bark rubbed on a sapling no thicker than Jake's wrist. Thorn touched the rub, then held two fingers under Jake's nose.

The smell was rank and bitter.

Buck.

No word was needed.

The lead hunter let Jake see the sign but not touch it.

That was another judgment. A careless hand could break the edge of a track, smear mud, or leave its own mark over what mattered. Jake kept both hands at his sides and looked. The hoof had pressed deeper on one side. The buck had turned uphill after drinking. A smaller track crossed over it later, likely doe, lighter and newer where the mud still shone.

Thorn watched Jake's eyes move.

Only after Jake looked toward the uphill brush did Thorn step aside.

They moved into the wind. The hunters did not speak. A hand lifted. A finger angled. A chin pointed toward broken browse. Jake followed each sign and tried to see the next one before Thorn pointed.

Once he stepped on a dry twig.

The crack seemed loud enough to split the ridge.

Every man stopped.

Jake froze with his foot still raised. Thorn turned only his head. His eyes went from Jake's boot to the twig, then to a patch of moss on the next stone. Jake set his foot where the old man's eyes had pointed.

The line moved again.

No blow came.

That made the correction worse.

After that Jake counted each step before giving it weight. Leaf. Moss. Stone. Root. Not bare mud unless there was no choice. Not hollow bark. Not fern stems that would spring back and show direction. The youngest hunter behind him breathed once through his nose when Jake slowed, but the man did not push past.

The ridge narrowed near a windfall. Thorn went under it. The lead hunter went over. Jake chose under and nearly caught the cord on a broken limb. He stopped, eased back, lifted the cord free, and went on. The delay cost him three paces. It saved him a torn pack and a loud branch.

The youngest hunter saw that.

No sound came from him after.

Past the windfall, Thorn let Jake come even with him for the length of a hollow.

The old man pointed to the ground with two fingers. Not one track. Three. Deer had crossed, but a turkey had crossed after them, scratching through the hoof print and pulling leaves back over the edge. Farther on, rainwater had run through the same mark and left a tiny ridge of grit on the lower side. The buck was not as close as Jake wanted it to be.

Jake pointed upslope, then shook his head.

Thorn's face did not change.

He pointed lower, toward a narrow shelf where the wind moved cold along Jake's cheek.

Jake followed the air with his nose. Acorn mast. Wet bark. Deer musk, faint and old. The lower shelf would carry scent away from the feeding ground instead of into it. Jake nodded once.

Thorn moved him back into line.

Permission ended as quickly as it had come, but the youngest hunter had seen it. So had the lead hunter. Jake felt the weight of those looks more than the cord against his side.

Near midday, the wind shifted.

The lead hunter stopped where laurel gave way to open hardwood. He crouched. Thorn crouched. Jake lowered himself behind a fallen log and looked where the others looked.

At first he saw only gray trunks and brown leaves.

Then one branch moved against the others.

Not branch. Antler.

A deer stood beyond the open ground, head down, working at acorns under the leaves. Another shape moved behind it, smaller, hidden by brush. Jake counted breath by breath. The buck lifted its head. Its ears turned downhill.

One of the hunters began to shift for a shot.

The buck's head rose higher.

Jake saw the trouble before the hunter loosed. A jay had gone silent above them, and the wind touched the back of Jake's neck instead of his cheek. The deer would catch them in another breath.

Jake reached out and touched Thorn's sleeve.

Thorn's eyes cut to him.

Jake pointed low, not at the deer, but at a narrow dip running left through the leaves. The dip held fern and deadfall, enough cover for one man to move around and turn the deer back if the wind held long enough. He could help bring down the large game only by making it run where the hunters already had their bows.

Thorn looked once.

Then he pointed at Jake.

Jake went.

Jake kept low through the fern, cord looped across one shoulder, knife tight at his belt. Leaves brushed his face. Twice he stopped while the buck fed and raised its head. Twice he moved when the head dropped. No killing shot waited in his hand. His part was to locate the game, trust the wind, and make the deer choose the wrong escape.

The dip was narrower than it had looked from the log. Briars caught his sleeve. Wet fern brushed cold across his wrists. A rotten limb lay under leaves and would have cracked if he had stepped where his eyes first chose. He put his hand down, found the limb, and moved around it on his knees.

The buck fed beyond the brush, unaware or only half aware.

Jake could see only pieces of it. Antler. Ear. Brown shoulder through beech stems. The smaller deer moved farther back. If he rushed, both would run the wrong way. If he waited too long, the wind would betray the whole party.

He took one breath through his mouth and let it out slow.

A squirrel barked overhead.

The buck stepped back.

Jake lifted a stone and tossed it into brush beyond the deer.

The sound broke left.

The buck bounded right, straight across the opening.

The first arrow struck behind the shoulder. The second hit lower. The deer crashed through saplings, ran twenty paces, and went down in a thrash of leaves and legs.

The woods held still afterward.

Jake stayed crouched until Thorn whistled once, low and birdlike.

Only then did he stand.

The youngest hunter reached the deer first and looked back at Jake with a face that had not yet decided what to show. The lead hunter put one hand on the buck's neck, then looked toward the dip where Jake had moved. Thorn said nothing. Silence from Thorn could cut either way.

Jake kept his hands low and waited for work.

## Scene 3

The deer was larger than Jake had guessed from the brush.

It lay against a fallen chestnut with steam lifting from the wound. The hunters worked fast. One held the legs. Another cut the throat clean. Thorn watched Jake more than the deer.

The knife with the worn handle came into Jake's hand.

Jake had dressed deer before the raid, but never under these eyes and never with this much riding on each cut. His father had taught him to open the belly without piercing the gut. Thorn had taught him to look where the hide pulled, where the muscle lay, where warmth left the body fastest.

Jake set the blade under the skin and drew it up slow.

No gut stink came.

One hunter grunted.

Jake kept working.

Blood warmed his fingers. The hide pulled tough around the legs. He cut joints by feel and did not saw bone when a twist would open it. Thorn corrected one grip with a tap of two fingers. Jake changed it. No word passed.

The belly opened clean. Steam lifted into the cold air. Jake turned his face enough to keep from breathing too much of it, but he did not pull away. The lead hunter watched the blade travel. One wrong cut would spoil meat and make the hunt remember him for that instead of the turn.

Jake worked slower than pride wanted.

Slow kept the knife out of gut.

Slow saved the hide.

The liver came free dark and slick in the lead hunter's hand. He checked it, grunted, and set it aside on clean leaves. The heart followed. Thorn made Jake hold it long enough to feel the heat leaving it.

This was not killing from far off.

This was what food cost after the arrow struck.

Jake cleaned the cavity with leaves and snow scraped from a shaded patch under stone. His fingers numbed fast, then burned when blood warmed them again. A mistake here would travel home in the taste of spoiled meat and in every glance laid on him by the fires. He kept the blade low and his breathing slower than his pulse wanted.

When Thorn pointed to the back legs, Jake found the joint with his thumb before cutting. He had missed that place on smaller game before and hacked like a boy at a stick. Not here. Not under these men. The blade slid where bone gave way to socket, and the leg came loose with a turn.

The youngest hunter shifted his feet.

No laugh came.

When the cavity was opened, another hunter took over for the faster work. Jake did not resist the hand that moved him aside. He held the legs when told, cut sinew when pointed, and wiped the blade on leaves between cuts.

They quartered the deer and wrapped meat in hide.

The heaviest haunch was given to Jake.

Jake bent under it without complaint. The meat rode across his shoulders, warm at first, then cooling as they climbed. Blood soaked the back of his shirt. The load was awkward, and more than once he had to stop the swing of it with both hands. The youngest hunter watched for him to falter.

Jake did not give him that.

The return took longer than the hunt. Weight changed every slope. Wet leaves slid underfoot. Once Jake's knee struck stone, and the meat nearly pulled him sideways. He set one hand against a tree, found balance, and went on.

The haunch shifted with every downhill step. Blood ran under his collar and cooled there. Flies found him though the air was cold. The youngest hunter walked behind at first, waiting. After the second ridge, he moved ahead and took a smaller bundle from Thorn without comment.

Jake's breath turned rough near the last climb.

He shortened his stride the way Thorn had taught him and let the load settle. The meat was proof only if he carried it all the way. Dropping it would make another story, and the village had enough stories at his expense.

At the village edge, dogs came first again.

This time they barked at meat, not fear.

Children ran behind them. Women came from the shelters. Men looked up from repair work and firewood. The hunters crossed the clearing with their loads. Jake came last with the haunch across his shoulders and blood down his back.

The gray-haired woman from the work ground looked at him, then at the meat.

She held out both hands.

Jake lowered the haunch into them.

For a moment, no one used the ash name.

The lead hunter spoke to Thorn and drew the deer's turn in the dirt with one finger. He jabbed that same finger toward Jake, then toward the ridge.

Thorn listened.

The old man looked at Jake.

"Good enough."

No hand clapped Jake's shoulder. Thorn's scarred face did not change. Jake only stood there with blood on his cuffs and deer hair stuck to his sleeves while the hunters divided the load.

The lead hunter cut a small strip from near the shoulder and tossed it toward Jake's feet.

Not the best piece.

Not scraps either.

Jake picked it up, washed it once in a water bowl, and set it on a flat stone near the coals. A boy watched the meat as if expecting Jake to be told to give it back. No one spoke. When it browned, Jake ate it in three bites, hot enough to burn his tongue.

It tasted of smoke, blood, and a place he had not had before.

The next morning proved how small that place was.

He still woke before dawn. He still took orders from the gray-haired woman. He still ate after others had taken better portions. But the first task was not water. It was checking the snares near the lower brush and bringing back any that needed mending. The difference was only a few hundred steps and one less climb from the creek.

Jake took it as carefully as he had taken the meat.

The snare line showed more about his new standing than any speech. One boy followed him partway, then stopped when an older woman called him back. A young hunter passed on the trail with a bow and did not bump Jake's shoulder. At the first snare, the loop had been sprung and twisted. Jake reset it, brushing leaves over the stake. At the second, a rabbit had been taken in the night and already removed by someone else. No one accused him of it. That too was new.

When he returned, the gray-haired woman handed him broken loops and cord.

Work.

Better work.

Not freedom.

The broken loops came from men who would not have handed him cord two summers earlier.

One was stained with rabbit blood and twisted by teeth. Another had snapped at the knot where the fiber had been rolled too loose. A third still held a tuft of gray fur. Jake laid them across his knees and sorted them by failure. Bad twist. Bad stake. Bad loop. Good loop, poor placement.

The gray-haired woman watched his hands.

He retied the first one and pulled until the cord bit into his palm. It held. The second he stripped apart and rolled again against his thigh. The third he set aside because the fiber had gone too dry and would break again in frost. When the woman saw him reject it, her mouth tightened. Jake held up the cracked strand and bent it until it snapped.

She took the broken piece, looked at it, and dropped it into the fire.

Then she pushed two more loops toward him.

That was not kindness.

It was trust of a narrow kind, measured in cord and meat, ready to vanish if his hands failed.

By noon the hunters were cutting meat for smoke. Jake was sent to carry strips to the rack. He laid them where smoke would cross but flame would not touch, the way Thorn had shown in winter. One strip hung too low. A woman moved it higher and gave him a look, not a blow. Jake fixed the next one before she reached it.

Across the work ground, Thorn watched and said nothing.

That night Jake was not sent to the hide rack.

Jake waited for the order, scraper already in hand, but the gray-haired woman pointed instead toward a stack of snares needing repair. The work was still work. His fingers still cramped over cord and bent saplings. But it was not the lowest work.

A boy came close and looked at the blood dried down Jake's shirt.

"Whispering Ash," the boy muttered.

Jake tied a snare knot and pulled it tight.

Across the fire, Thorn watched the knot hold.

The next morning, no bucket was shoved into Jake's hands before dawn. Another captive took the creek path. Jake was given cord, a dull knife, and broken snare loops.

Less like a slave.

Not free. Not equal. Not one of them.

Useful.

For now, useful was meat in the pot and no scraper in his hand.

Useful had its own chain.

If he mended snares well, more snares came. If he carried smoked meat without dropping it, more meat loads found him. If a hunter needed cord, he pointed at Jake. If a boy broke a loop, the gray-haired woman sent it to Jake's hands. Better work was still work, and it filled the day as surely as water hauling had.

But useful work taught more.

A snare showed where rabbits ran. A meat rack showed how smoke moved in changing wind. A hunter's broken cord showed how a careless knot failed. Jake took each thing because each thing might keep him alive longer than pride.

Jake bent over the snare work and listened to the hunters near the village edge. Creek. Ridge. Deer. Halt. Their hands pointed where their mouths went, and the evening wind slid down from the ridge where the buck had fed.

Jake would remember the turn of that buck.

He would remember the silence before it ran.
