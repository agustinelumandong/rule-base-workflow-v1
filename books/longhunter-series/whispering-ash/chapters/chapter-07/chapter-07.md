# Chapter 7: Blood on the Trail

## Scene 1

The militia camp showed through timber as points of fire and dull iron.

Jake lay on his belly under laurel with the ash cloak pulled over his shoulders. Smoke from the camp moved low among the trees. Men in rough coats sat near wagons and stacked rifles. Horses shifted on picket lines. A kettle hung over one fire. Tin cups flashed when men lifted them.

No one in the camp looked hungry.

That was the first thing Jake marked.

Men who ate well moved differently from men who scraped bowls. They sat wider. They laughed longer. One had a slab of meat on a stick and grease shining in his beard. Another kicked at a dog nosing near a pack and cursed without lowering his cup. Blankets lay rolled under a wagon, dry under canvas. Powder horns hung from pegs driven into a tree. A stack of muskets leaned close enough to the fire that the metal caught red light.

The camp had guards, but comfort had softened them.

One sentry stood where the wagon track narrowed. He had a musket in both hands and his back to the darkest laurel because he was watching the road. Another moved near the horses, stopping too often to warm his fingers over a pipe coal. A third sat on a stump with his hat low, awake only because the night had not yet beaten him.

Hostile camp. Armed men. Poor watch.

That combination made the warriors around Jake breathe slower, not faster.

Jake had never raided with them before.

Hunts had rhythm. Trail, sign, wind, shot, blood, load. This was different. Men watched men. Guns answered arrows. Firelight made lies out of distance. A deer never raised a pistol from under a blanket. A horse never shouted for help in English.

Thorn had placed Jake where he could see the camp but not command any part of the strike. That suited the village's trust in him. Enough skill to use. Not enough standing to steer. Jake accepted the place because refusal would be foolish and because the work ahead would not care what he wanted.

Thorn crouched ten paces to Jake's left. Other warriors held the dark in a half ring around the clearing. Bows were strung. Knives sat loose in sheaths. Tomahawk handles angled across knees. A few trade muskets rested against shoulders, but none was passed to Jake.

His hands held bow and knife.

That was enough.

The signal came as a night bird call from the far side of the camp.

Arrows went first.

One sentry dropped without a cry. Another fired blind into the trees, the musket flash white in the dark. The shot slammed through leaves above Jake's head. Horses jerked against their ropes. Men shouted. A kettle tipped and hissed in the fire.

The half-sleeping camp became all elbows and gun barrels.

One militia man tripped over a blanket roll and came up with a pistol. Another swung a musket so wide the barrel struck his own wagon. A horse broke a picket line and dragged rope through the fire, scattering coals. Men in the dark answered with arrows and short cries. Trade muskets fired from the tree line, then vanished behind smoke while men reloaded by touch.

Jake stayed low until the first rush passed.

Thorn had given him that place because Jake had no firearm to answer across open ground. Bow first. Knife after. Tomahawk only if the line broke close enough. Jake's part was not glory. It was work done in the gap between shots.

Jake moved when Thorn moved.

They crossed the first open ground low and fast. Jake loosed once at a man lifting a rifle near the wagon wheel. The arrow struck the man's arm and spun him sideways before he fired. Jake did not watch him fall. Thorn had already gone left, and the fight had closed.

Smoke thickened under the trees.

A militia man came out of it with a short sword in one hand and a pistol in the other. His coat hung open. His mouth was wide. Jake saw teeth, beard, and the black hole of the pistol muzzle.

The pistol fired past Jake's ear.

Heat slapped his cheek.

Jake drove in before the man could cock it again. The knife went low because Thorn had struck his wrist a hundred times for reaching high. The man folded over the blade and grabbed Jake's shoulder. They hit the ground together.

Mud filled Jake's mouth. The man's weight drove air from his chest. Jake could smell wool, powder, sour breath, and blood. The man's hand clawed at Jake's face. Jake pulled the knife free and struck again, close under the ribs.

The hand stopped clawing.

Jake did not pull away at once.

The man had his coat twisted in one fist. Dead fingers held well. Jake had to pry them loose one by one. His own breath came hard through dirt and smoke. The pistol lay near his knee, cock half drawn, useless now. The short sword had fallen under the dead man's hip. Jake did not take either weapon. Taking what he did not know how to use would slow him.

The knife in his hand was enough.

Too much.

For one breath, the camp vanished.

Jake saw a burning porch rail. He saw the red-clay warrior by the woodpile. He saw his mother's bonnet through creek brush, pale and gone under smoke.

Then a horse screamed and the world came back.

Jake pushed the dead weight off him and rolled to one knee. The knife was slick. His hand did not fit right around the handle. A warrior ran past him toward the wagons. A musket fired from near the fire and struck bark from a tree beside Thorn.

Jake rose.

The fight ended in pieces. One man ran and was brought down beyond the wagons. Another threw his rifle aside and tried to crawl under a cart. Someone shouted in English for mercy. Jake did not know if mercy came. He saw only boots, smoke, and hands taking weapons from the ground.

Near the horses, a Cherokee warrior bent over his own arm, blood running between his fingers. Another man dragged him back by the belt. A militia man under the cart kicked at anyone who came near until a tomahawk handle struck his wrist and the rifle beside him was pulled away. The dog that had nosed at the pack ran circles under the wagons, whining, then bolted into brush.

Jake stood with his knife down by his leg.

No one looked at him long. Each man had his own blood, goods, or dead to count. That helped. It also left him alone with the body at his feet.

The man he had killed lay on one side, coat open, one hand still bent like it held Jake's cloth. The face had gone slack. In firelight it looked older than it had in the rush. Jake looked away from the face and saw the pistol half buried in mud. A good weapon. A useful one for a man trained to it. Jake did not reach for it.

The man's pouch had spilled open beside his hip. Lead balls rolled in the mud. A strip of tobacco lay dark with water. A comb with two broken teeth rested near Jake's knee, carved from bone, small enough for a boy's hand.

Jake stared at the comb longer than he stared at the pistol.

His own house had held little things like that. A horn cup with a cracked rim. A blue thread caught on a chair peg. A button his mother kept in a cup because it might serve later. Small things outlived people when fire moved fast.

A warrior bent past him and swept the lead into his palm. He took the tobacco too. The comb stayed where it was.

Jake stepped over it.

Behind the wagon, one of the captured horses had tangled itself in a broken rope. Its eyes rolled white. A Cherokee man tried to quiet it with both hands raised, moving slow. The horse struck once with a forehoof and cracked the wagon board. Men cursed and pulled away. Jake saw the rope running under a wheel, looped once around the animal's fetlock.

He moved before anyone told him to.

The horse smelled of sweat, smoke, and singed hair. Jake kept low near its shoulder, where the hoof could not reach him clean. He cut the rope at the wheel, then caught the loose end and pulled it free by inches. The horse jerked hard enough to burn the rope across his palm. Jake held until the loop fell.

The animal backed, trembling.

Thorn saw him with the rope in his bleeding hand.

Jake dropped the cut length by the wagon and went back to the dead man because that was where he had been placed. His palm stung. Blood from the rope mark mixed with older blood on his fingers. He rubbed the hand once against his leg and stopped when the smell rose again.

Thorn crossed behind the wagons and stopped near the body.

He looked at the knife in Jake's hand, then at the dead man, then at the camp beyond them. No comfort. No rebuke. He pointed to a spilled powder horn near a wagon wheel.

Work.

Jake picked it up.

The horn was cracked at the mouth. Powder leaked in a thin black trail. Jake plugged it with his thumb and carried it to the pile where salvage was being sorted. His hand left a smear on the horn that was not powder.

When silence finally came, it was not clean.

Men groaned. Horses stamped. Fire popped in spilled grease. The dead lay where they had fallen, settler and Cherokee both, though Jake did not count them.

Thorn looked across the clearing.

Jake wiped his knife once on wet grass.

The blood did not come off.

## Scene 2

They returned before dawn with powder, lead, blankets, dried meal, and two captured horses.

Men spoke low on the trail. Some carried packs from the camp. One wore a militia belt over his own. Another had a tin cup tied to his bundle. The spoils knocked softly as they walked, metal against horn, horn against wood.

Jake carried a sack of meal for three miles before another man took it from him.

The sack smelled of flour and smoke. Blood from his sleeve dried against the rough cloth. He shifted it from one shoulder to the other when the strap dug too deep. No one asked if he could carry it. No one offered until the hill beyond the creek, when the warrior behind him took the load without looking at his face and handed him a powder horn instead.

The horn was lighter.

It felt worse.

Every few steps the image of the militia man's hand came back, fingers locked in Jake's coat. Jake flexed his own hand until the knuckles cracked. The knife rode at his belt, wiped once but not clean. He could smell it when the wind shifted.

The trail home crossed a shallow ford. Men stepped through without slowing. Jake stopped long enough to dip his hand, then the knife, then his sleeve. Cold water darkened the cloth and ran red for two breaths. The warrior behind him bumped his shoulder with the powder horn and pointed uphill.

Jake went on.

Water had not taken the smell.

At the village edge, dogs barked and children came running. Women took the packs apart near the fires. Powder was set aside. Lead went to the men who used muskets. Blankets were counted. Meat and meal were divided under watchful eyes.

Jake stood with the knife in his hand.

It was already clean enough.

He cleaned it again.

First with wet grass. Then with ash. Then with a scrap of hide until the edge shone dull in the morning light. A dark line stayed where blade met handle. Jake worked at that line with a sliver of wood.

Thorn passed behind him.

Jake kept his eyes on the knife.

The old man's shadow crossed his hands and moved on.

The shadow stopped once at Jake's shoulder.

Jake heard Thorn breathe. That was all. No question. No command. No judgment spoken where others might hear it. The old man could have taken the knife and cleaned the stubborn line himself. He could have told Jake to stop. He did neither.

That made Jake work harder at the blade.

Someone pushed a blanket toward Jake's feet, a narrow strip cut from the militia camp. He did not pick it up right away. A boy took one corner and tugged. Jake let him have it. The boy ran with the cloth trailing behind him.

Jake kept cleaning the knife.

By noon, the village had turned the raid into use. Meal went into pots. Powder horns were hung under shelter. A musket with a cracked stock was stripped for parts. Men talked near the fire and marked the fight with hands in the dirt. One man set a stick for a wagon tongue, staggered back over it, and dropped flat.

Laughter came.

Jake walked to the creek.

Behind him, the captured goods became ordinary faster than blood dried.

The cracked musket gave up its lock, screws, and barrel bands. The tin cup went to a boy who beat it against a stump until someone took it from him. A blanket with a hole through it was cut into strips. Powder was measured and hidden under bark. The camp had been men a few hours before. Now it was parts.

The cut rope from the horse lay near the sorting place. Someone had tossed it aside because the burn mark and blood made it poor for tying packs. Jake picked it up when no one used it. The fibers were stiff with dirt. He coiled it once, felt the roughness, then uncoiled it again.

Rope made a snare if it was split. Rope made a belt if a knot held. Rope could bind a bundle to a shoulder or lower a body down a stone bank. A man with rope had one more answer than a man without it.

He cut away the bloodiest span and dropped it in the fire.

The rest he hung near the smoke poles as if it belonged there. Later, when women moved hides and men argued over lead, he took two thumb-length strands and twisted them into the ash cloak's inner fold. Not enough for anyone to miss. Enough to make his hidden coil stronger.

His burned palm left a smear on the cloth.

He turned the smear inward.

Jake kept seeing the hand on his coat.

He washed the knife again in cold water. Red loosened from under the handle and curled away downstream in a thin thread. He watched it go until the current broke it apart on stone.

At the far bank, a patrol path cut between two pines.

Jake looked at it longer than he needed.

The path curved west, then bent north toward the ridge trail. One guard took it at dusk most nights. Another took the lower creek line after moonrise. Jake had seen both, but had not counted the time between them.

That evening, he counted.

The first guard came while the sky still held brown light. He walked with a spear over one shoulder and stopped at the same creek bend to spit. He looked downstream, never upstream. His left moccasin made a soft slap where the sole had loosened.

The second came after the cooking fires had settled low. He carried a bow and paused behind the hide racks to speak with a woman before taking the lower path. That pause mattered. It opened ten breaths between the racks and the creek bend.

The third did not come by the creek. He crossed behind the woodpile and went toward the ridge trail, slow, older, listening more than looking.

Jake sat with the knife across his knees and counted each man's steps by the beat of water over stone.

He counted where sound covered sound.

Creek over stone hid three steps near the bend. Dogs barking at scraps hid the crossing behind the hide rack. Women pounding corn hid a man moving by the woodpile until the pounding stopped. The village had always been a cage. Now he began to see where the bars did not meet cleanly.

## Scene 3

The first flint went into the cloak hem two nights later.

Jake had taken it from a broken fire kit, no larger than a thumbnail, dark and good at the edge. He did not put it in his pouch. Pouches could be taken. The cloak was watched less because it had become part of him, gray and smoked and useful in timber.

He opened a seam near the lower edge and slid the flint inside, then stitched it shut with sinew.

The second store was dried meat.

Not much. A strip from his ration, then half a strip later, then a narrow piece cut from a smoked haunch when he was sent to carry it. Each piece was wrapped in bark and tucked into the cloak lining where ash and grease hid the smell. He did not take enough to be noticed.

Enough to live one more day if he ran.

The thorn needle came from Thorn's old repair bundle, though Jake did not take it while Thorn watched. It lay with broken awls, sinew scraps, and bone points near the weapon rack after a lesson in binding handles. Jake waited until rain drove most of the village under shelter. He carried wet wood past the rack, stumbled as if the mud had taken him, and came up with the needle pressed against his palm.

He expected Thorn's voice.

None came.

That night the needle went into the cloak hem with a coil of sinew no longer than his hand. Needle meant torn moccasins could be closed. Sinew meant a snare, a repair, a binding, a chance. Small things made the difference between running one night and living long enough for a second.

He tested each hidden piece by touch in the dark.

The flint sat near the left hem, two finger-widths from a patched tear. Meat lay flat along the back edge where the cloak folded naturally. The needle rode higher, wrapped so it would not prick through and betray itself. Sinew coiled near the seam under his right hand. He could find each with his eyes shut.

He practiced that too.

At night, under the shelter edge, he would wake, touch flint, meat, needle, sinew, and then lie still until his heart slowed. If he had to search for them while men shouted, they were already lost.

At night, he watched patrols.

One man walked the creek path at dusk and spat into the water at the same bend. Another crossed behind the hide racks when the fires burned low. Thorn sometimes sat awake longer than all of them, back to a tree, eyes half closed and not sleeping.

Jake did not watch Thorn too openly.

He watched shadows instead.

Thorn's shadow stayed long after others had gone to bed. It sat against the tree and did not slump. When the old man rose, he did it without bracing a hand on the ground. Jake learned that hour too. Not for running past Thorn. For knowing when not to run.

The creek guard changed on cold nights. The man with the loose moccasin took shorter rounds when frost came. The older man behind the woodpile took longer rounds when the moon was bright. Rain made everyone hurry except Thorn.

Weather mattered as much as men.

The knife stayed clean now.

Too clean.

He worked the edge after meals, after training, after any talk of the raid. When men spoke of the camp, Jake checked the blade. When someone laughed over the captured cup, Jake rubbed ash from the handle. When Thorn looked toward him, Jake found some nick in the steel that needed attention.

The ash cloak grew a little heavier.

Flint. Meat. A thorn needle. A coil of sinew. Nothing large. Nothing that would show if a hand gripped the cloth. Each small thing had a place.

One evening Thorn stood beside him while Jake stitched the cloak hem.

"Torn?"

Jake pulled the stitch tight. "Brush caught it."

Thorn looked at the seam.

No word came.

The old man walked away.

Jake waited until the shadows joined under the trees before tying the last knot.

The village fires burned behind him. The creek path darkened ahead. A guard passed between the pines, paused to listen, then moved on.

Jake counted his steps without walking them.

From shelter to woodpile. Woodpile to creek bend. Creek bend to stone shelf. Stone shelf to laurel. Laurel to dark timber.

He counted what he could not carry too.

No kettle. No blanket unless it could be stolen late. No bow if he had to run from a gathering. No rifle; none was his, and a stolen one would raise every hand before he reached the creek. Knife. Tomahawk if it stayed at his belt. Cloak. Flint. Meat. Needle. Sinew. Maybe one strip of bark for dry tinder.

That was not enough.

It would have to be.

The next day he tested a piece of the route while carrying wood.

He took the long way from the timberline, passing the woodpile, then the creek bend, then the stone shelf, with a bundle across his shoulder so anyone watching saw only work. At the creek bend he let one stick fall. When he stooped to lift it, he looked under the alder roots. Dry space there. Room for one wrapped strip of meat if he ever needed a store outside the cloak.

At the stone shelf he shifted the load and set one foot on the rock that would take him toward laurel. Moss made it slick. Bad in rain. He marked a better step two handspans higher, where a root crossed stone and gave a toe hold.

He tested the sound next.

On the return, he let the wood bundle ride loose enough for one stick to knock another. The clack covered his first step off the main track. Creek water covered the second. A jay scolded from the laurel and covered the third. Then the woods went quiet, and Jake froze with one foot still lifted.

Quiet could catch a man faster than a shout.

He set the foot down only when a woman struck corn in a wooden bowl near the shelters. Thump. Step. Thump. Step. He counted six covered steps before the pounding stopped. Six steps would put him behind the stone shelf. Six would not reach the laurel.

Not enough.

He needed rain, dogs barking, men talking, or a night when wind dragged branches together.

That evening he made himself useful near every noise.

When boys chased each other around the hide frames, he carried bark past the lower path and counted how far their feet covered his. When dogs fought over a bone, he crossed behind the woodpile and listened for his own moccasin on leaves. When a woman shook a hide and the hide snapped like a sail, he moved three steps toward the creek and back.

Each trial gave him less than he wanted.

The creek bend hid sound but exposed shape. The woodpile hid shape but left dry leaves underfoot. The stone shelf gave a clean path into brush but shone pale under moon. Laurel swallowed a body if a man reached it, but reaching it took open ground.

Jake stored no map on bark.

He put it in feet, breath, and hand.

Shelter to woodpile: twelve steps if he carried a load, ten if he ran. Woodpile to creek bend: eighteen by the low path, twenty-three by the rack shadows. Creek bend to stone shelf: eleven if the water was low, fourteen if he had to keep his feet dry. Stone shelf to laurel: seven open steps with no cover.

Seven steps could kill him.

He practiced standing still before those seven steps, because moving too soon would kill him faster.

No one called out.

That did not mean no one saw.

Thorn sat near the hide racks with a broken bow across his knees. His hands worked the wood. His eyes did not seem to move. Jake carried the wood past him and stacked it by the shelter. When he turned back, Thorn was still working the bow.

The old man had seen enough.

He said nothing.

He did not run.

Not yet.

But the cloak held flint and meat, and the trail had begun to mark itself in his feet.
