# Chapter 1: The Raid

## Scene 1

The morning began at dawn with hoe work and plain light.

Jake Moses stood in the corn rows with the sun on the back of his neck and the taste of dust in his mouth. The field ran down a mild slope from the oak timber to the creek bottom, each row crooked where roots and stones had turned the plow. Corn leaves brushed his trousers. The green was knee high in places, lower in others, and weeds had come up fast after the last rain.

He set the hoe blade close to a stalk and chopped low. Dry dirt broke loose in little clods. The iron cut through wild mustard and pigweed, then struck a buried stone with a hard ring that traveled up the handle into his wrists.

Jake paused only long enough to shake the sting from his hands.

At fourteen, he was still narrow through the chest, though farm labor had made his arms tough and corded. His shirt clung to his back. His homespun trousers were held by leather straps, one mended where a button had torn loose the previous fall. He had tied the strap with the same square farm knot his father used on feed sacks and harness repairs. The knot had held through winter woodcutting, spring plowing, and now the first hard weed chopping of summer.

He tugged it once with two fingers, saw that it held, and bent again to the row.

His mother worked three rows over with her bonnet pulled low and her sleeves rolled to the elbows. She moved slower than Jake, but she wasted no motion. Her hands found the weeds and twisted them out roots and all. She dropped them in a pile at her feet and stepped on them before moving on.

Down near the rail fence, his sisters carried water from the creek in wooden buckets. They were small enough that the buckets banged their knees if they hurried. One of them spilled half a bucket into the dust, then laughed and tried to cover the loss with her skirt.

Jake looked up.

His mother did not. "Let them be."

"That was near half."

"Then they will walk twice."

The girls heard none of it. They were whispering over something found at the creek bank, a feather or a stone or a dead beetle. Their skirts were wet at the hems. One had a smear of mud along her cheek.

The sight sat easy in Jake for a moment. Corn, creek, cabin smoke, his mother bent to the row, his sisters making poor work look like play. That was the morning. That was the world as it had been when he woke before sunup to split kindling and carry mash to the hog pen.

His father was gone with Jake's older brothers, hunting up beyond the ridge. They had left before dawn with the long rifle, two powder horns, and a sack of cold corn bread. Jake had watched them cross the pasture in gray light, three dark figures with the rifle between them and the dogs nosing ahead. He had wanted to go. His father had given him the hoe handle instead.

"Corn feeds more days than deer," his father had told him.

Jake had taken the handle without complaint.

That was how most mornings went when his father kept him home. Jake worked where he was told. He carried water, cut weeds, fed hogs, split kindling, and hauled rails from one poor corner of the farm to another. None of it made a tale worth telling. It was work done because the sun had come up and the ground would not tend itself.

Still, there were easy pieces in it.

His youngest sister had a habit of singing nonsense while she carried water, never loud enough for his mother to scold her, never quiet enough for Jake not to hear. The other one always tried to balance a bucket on one hip like a grown woman and always failed before the fence. His mother kept a scrap of rag tied at her waist for wiping sweat from her eyes. When the rag grew too dirty, she rinsed it in the water bucket and tied it back on without stopping.

Jake knew those small things the way he knew the pull of the hoe and the shape of stones under a field. They belonged to the farm as much as the rails and the cabin door.

Now he drove the blade into the row and worked until sweat ran from his hairline. Dust stuck to his throat. The sun climbed. The cabin stood at the upper edge of the clearing, its log walls pale where new chinking had dried between them. A small thread of breakfast smoke had risen from the stone chimney earlier, thin and blue. Behind the cabin sat the barn, the woodpile, the chopping block, and the well sweep.

Everything had its place.

The farm was not large. It was cut out of forest by ax and fire, a ragged square of worked ground held against the trees by rails, labor, and stubbornness. Beyond the field, western Virginia rose in timbered ridges and shadowed hollows. The woods were close enough to hear. Squirrels barked there in the mornings. Crows fought in the dead pine above the spring path. Jays shrieked when the hogs broke into leaf mold.

As the dawn light climbed over the ridge, the crows went quiet.

Jake straightened with the hoe in his hands. He listened.

The field had sounds of its own. Corn leaves rasped against his trousers. A bucket handle squeaked by the fence. His mother pulled weeds with dry snaps. Somewhere under the leaves, a cricket ticked and stopped.

The woods held still.

Jake looked toward the oak line above the cabin. The trees made a dark wall, thick with laurel and green shadow. No bird crossed the opening. No squirrel ran along the fence rail. Even the hogs in the pen had stopped rooting.

His mother rose from the row. One hand pressed against the small of her back. Her face had gone hard under the bonnet brim.

"Jake."

He turned toward her.

She was not looking at him. She was looking beyond the cabin, toward the ridge where his father and brothers had gone at dawn.

A cry broke from the timber.

It was not a bird and not a man calling across a field. It came thin and high, then split into other cries, closer than the ridge and moving fast. The sound lifted the hair along Jake's arms.

His sisters stopped by the fence. One bucket tipped from a hand and spilled into the dust.

Another cry came from behind the cabin. Then a horse screamed.

His mother crossed two rows in a rush, skirts dragging through corn leaves.

"To the creek brush," she ordered.

The girls stood frozen.

"Now."

Jake gripped the hoe handle until the wood bit his palms. Smoke rose over the cabin roof, not the thin blue thread of breakfast fire, but a dark roll that opened and spread above the trees.

For one breath, nobody moved.

Then the morning broke apart.

## Scene 2

Jake dropped the hoe and ran.

Corn leaves whipped his face. Dry stalks snapped under his boots. The rows fought him, each ridge of turned earth catching at his feet. Behind him his mother drove the girls toward the creek, one hand on each small shoulder, pushing them low toward the alder and willow brush along the bank.

Jake did not follow.

He ran uphill toward the cabin.

The first shot cracked from the clearing. It struck the air flat and hard. Smoke rolled past the well sweep. A second shot followed, then a shout in a tongue Jake did not know. The farm horse screamed again from behind the barn.

Jake came out of the corn at the lower edge of the yard and dropped beside a stump. His breath tore at his throat. Sweat ran into his eyes. He blinked it away and looked.

The cabin door stood open.

Smoke poured out black and thick. Flame licked through the cedar shingles near the chimney, bright in the sun. The porch rail had caught. Fire crawled along it in quick yellow tongues, eating dry wood that Jake and his brothers had split and pegged last fall.

The barn was not burning yet, but a warrior stood near its corner with a pine knot torch. Pitch bubbled orange at the end. His hair was bound back. Soot marked his face in dark streaks. He thrust the torch under the straw edge of the roof and held it there until the straw took.

Another warrior dragged blankets from the cabin and flung them into the yard. An iron kettle rolled after them, thudding once in the dirt. A third man stood near the tethering rail with a long gun in his hand, watching the field and creek.

Jake saw no father. No brothers. No long rifle above the hearth, unless it was hidden in the smoke.

His mother and sisters had reached the creek brush. He could see movement there, skirts and pale hands under alder leaves. His mother turned once toward the yard.

A warrior near the fence saw the motion. He lifted his arm and shouted.

Jake moved before the shout ended.

He sprang from behind the stump and ran along the edge of the yard, low and fast, keeping the smoke between himself and the man with the long gun. Heat struck his face. Sparks spun across the dirt. The cabin popped and cracked as flame found the dry chinking.

The woodpile stood by the chopping block, twenty paces from the barn. A felling axe was sunk deep in a round of pine. Jake's father had left it there the night before after splitting kindling. The ash handle leaned toward the yard.

Jake reached it and pulled.

The axe did not come free.

He set one boot against the pine round and heaved again. The iron head tore loose with a wet wooden squeal. The handle came back hard against his ribs. He staggered, caught himself, and turned with the axe held crosswise before him.

The warrior from the fence was already coming.

He was not old. He moved quick, bare arms shining with sweat and soot, a short tomahawk in his right hand. Red clay marked his face from cheekbone to jaw. His mouth opened in a cry that was all teeth and breath.

Jake backed into the woodpile.

The tomahawk cut down.

He jerked aside. The iron blade struck a split log and threw chips against his neck. The warrior wrenched it free and came in close, too close for the long axe. Jake shoved the axe handle into the man's chest. The man grunted and drove forward, forcing Jake's back against stacked wood.

The smell of him was sweat, smoke, and deer hide.

Jake's hands slipped on the axe handle. He clamped tighter. The warrior grabbed for the shaft. Their feet tore through chips and bark. Jake saw the tomahawk lift again, saw the black edge flash in smoke.

He ducked and drove his shoulder into the man's middle.

Both of them lurched. The axe head struck the ground. Jake nearly lost it. The warrior struck him across the side of the head with an open hand. White light burst behind Jake's eyes. He tasted blood where his teeth cut his lip.

The tomahawk rose again.

Jake swung from low and close.

There was no clean stroke, no practiced mark. He hauled the felling axe with both hands and let the weight carry it. The iron bit into the warrior below the ribs. The man's cry stopped as if cut. His body folded against the axe head.

Jake held the handle.

For a moment he could not pull it free.

The man's hands clutched at the iron. His eyes were open. His breath came in a wet rattle.

Jake planted one boot in the dirt, pulled hard, and tore the blade loose.

The warrior dropped beside the woodpile.

Jake stood over him with the axe hanging from both hands. Blood ran down the blade and fell in dark spots on the dust. The barn roof caught behind him. Fire ran along the straw and climbed to the ridgepole.

Someone screamed from the creek.

Jake turned.

The scream had come from one of his sisters, high and thin through the brush. His mother was half hidden under alder limbs, one arm raised before the girls. A warrior pushed through the creek grass toward them.

Jake tried to run that way.

Two men struck him from behind.

The first blow hit his shoulder and drove him to one knee. The axe dragged through the dirt. Jake twisted and swung blind. The blade struck nothing. A hickory club smashed across his forearm. His hand opened. The axe fell.

He reached for it.

A knee drove into his back. His face hit the dirt. Grit filled his mouth. Someone seized his hair and pulled his head back until his throat stretched. A knife blade pressed cold under his jaw.

Jake went still.

The cabin roared. Heat rolled across the yard. Smoke covered the sun and made the clearing dim.

The warrior above him spoke in English, each word rough and hard.

"Still."

Jake breathed through his nose. Dirt stuck to his lips. His arm throbbed from wrist to elbow. Blood from his split lip dripped onto the ground beneath his face.

His mother shouted once from the creek.

Then the yard filled with too many sounds to separate. Fire. Hooves. Men calling. The barn roof falling in. One of the girls crying. A dog yelping and then cut short.

Jake pressed his fingers into the dirt.

Jake's mouth stayed shut around dirt and blood.

Only the ground under his hands, warm with sun and shaken by feet.

A boot rolled him onto his side. Smoke dragged across the yard in a low sheet, thick enough to sting his eyes and hide the creek brush in pieces. Through it he saw movement near the alders. His mother had one arm around a girl and the other out before her, palm open, as if a hand could stop a blade or a gun. The second girl was lower, half on her knees in the mud.

Jake tried to pull his legs under him.

The knife touched his throat again.

He stopped.

The warrior above him barked to the others. Smoke and fire cut the Cherokee sounds into hard pieces. Another man answered from near the barn. A third laughed once, short and mean, while Jake lay in the dirt.

He fixed his eyes on the creek brush until smoke covered it.

## Scene 3

They bound him beside the burning woodpile.

One warrior knelt on his legs while another dragged his arms behind his back. A strip of rawhide went around his wrists. It was green and damp, and when the man pulled it tight the hide cut into Jake's skin like wire. The knot was not a farm knot. It had no mercy in it. It took every bit of slack from his hands and left his fingers swelling before he was even pulled upright.

Jake bit down and kept silent.

The man with the scarred face took the felling axe from the dirt. He looked at the dead warrior by the woodpile, then at Jake. His eyes were flat, but not empty. He spoke to another man in Cherokee. Jake caught none of it. The scarred man wiped the axe blade on grass and threw it into the flames at the cabin door.

The tool struck the threshold and vanished in smoke.

Jake stared at the place where it had gone.

That axe had split rails, stove wood, kindling, and fence posts. It had hung in his father's hand through winter mornings and spring rains. Now its handle burned with the porch boards.

The scarred man caught Jake's stare and stepped close. He lifted Jake's bound hands by the rawhide until pain climbed both arms. Then he pointed toward the dead warrior and slapped two fingers against Jake's bloody shirt. The others went quiet.

The boy had killed one of theirs.

That fact would march with him.

A shove between his shoulders sent him stumbling toward the trail.

He looked once toward the creek.

Brush shook under the alder limbs. Smoke drifted low along the bank. He saw his mother's bonnet, pale through leaves, then a dark shape moved between them. One of his sisters cried again, farther off or muffled by a hand. Jake tried to turn his head for a clearer look.

The rawhide jerked his wrists. A warrior caught the bindings and pulled hard enough to bend Jake backward.

"Walk."

Jake walked.

They crossed the yard past the cabin. The logs had begun to blacken. White chunks of clay fell from between them. The roof sagged inward, then gave a deep crack. A storm of sparks lifted. Heat slapped Jake's face and dried the blood on his mouth.

Near the door lay the kettle that had rolled from the cabin. The handle glowed dull red. A blanket smoldered beside it. A pewter cup, flattened by a boot or hoof, shone bright among ashes.

At the barn, the horse lay tangled in its reins, kicking once against the dirt. Smoke poured through the slats. The smell of burned hair and straw made Jake's throat close. He swallowed against it and kept walking.

No father came from the ridge.

No brothers came through the trees.

The men gathered fast. Some carried goods from the cabin. Some had horses. Others moved on foot with rifles, clubs, knives, and hatchets. The dead warrior by the woodpile was left where he had fallen. One man took his tomahawk. Another covered his face with a scrap of hide.

Jake watched without turning his head too far.

The scarred man came close and looked into Jake's face. He touched the blood at Jake's mouth with two fingers, then pointed toward the dead warrior and snapped a few Cherokee sounds. The others turned on Jake then, colder than they had turned on the kettle or blankets.

Jake lowered his eyes to the ground.

That did not make him meek. It let him see feet, weapons, pace, and direction. His father had taught him that much while mending fence near bad-tempered cattle. Watch the feet first. Shoulders lie. Hands lie. Feet tell where a man means to go.

The warriors' feet turned toward the timber trail above the cabin.

They were taking him west.

The first part of the climb was steep. Roots crossed the path like old rope. Jake's boots slipped on loose pine needles. With his hands tied behind him he could not catch himself when he stumbled. Twice he went down on one knee. Twice the rawhide snapped tight and hauled him forward before he could rise cleanly.

He did not cry out.

That was all he had left to spend. No gun. No axe. No open hand. No father on the ridge and no brothers breaking through the brush. His silence was not courage with a name on it. It was a thing clenched between his teeth, as plain as blood and dirt.

Behind him, the farm burned louder as the wind found it. Flames moved from cabin to shed, from shed to fence rails, from rails into the dry grass along the yard edge. Smoke blew uphill and wrapped the men in its bitter dark. Jake's eyes watered, but he kept them open.

At the bend where the trail entered the timber, he turned for his last look.

The clearing lay below, broken by smoke. The cabin was a red hole behind black logs. The barn roof had fallen. The cornfield shone green and untouched beyond the yard, foolishly bright under the noon sun. Near the creek, alder brush moved once, then vanished behind smoke.

Jake could not tell who still breathed.

He fixed the sight in himself without naming it.

A hand struck the back of his head and turned him forward.

The trail narrowed under oak and pine. Heat gave way to shade. The air smelled of sap, old leaves, and smoke carried on their clothing. A crow called from a dead branch above the path. Far behind, something inside the cabin collapsed with a low boom.

Jake kept his feet moving.

Rawhide cut deeper as his wrists swelled. Blood from the dead warrior had dried in a dark smear across Jake's shirt. His left forearm ached where the club had struck him. Each breath pulled smoke into his chest and pushed it out hot.

The men set a hard pace once the farm fell from sight. They did not run, but they did not walk like men with a road under them either. They took game paths, dry washes, deer tracks, and bare stone where prints would not hold. Jake learned the shape of their march because he had nothing else to study. A man ahead stepped over a root. Jake stepped over it. A man ahead ducked under laurel. Jake bent his head and let the leaves drag across his hair.

When he faltered, the rawhide corrected him.

When the trail split, they chose the higher line.

West, then higher, then west again.

The warriors did not speak much once they reached the timber. A man ahead carried the long gun from the cabin yard. Another had the iron kettle tied to a pack. The scarred one walked behind Jake, close enough that Jake could hear the scrape of his moccasins over dry leaves.

The old life fell away step by step.

Corn rows. Creek buckets. His mother's hands in the weeds. His sisters laughing over a spilled pail. His father and brothers gone up the ridge with the rifle before dawn.

Jake did not look back again.

The trail rose toward the mountains, and the smoke of home followed until the trees swallowed it.
