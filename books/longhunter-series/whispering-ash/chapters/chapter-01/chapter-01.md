# Chapter 1: The Raid

## Scene 1

The morning sun sat warm on the back of Jake's neck. A small circle of dampness soaked the gray linen of his shirt where his suspenders crossed. He held the hickory handle of the hoe with both hands, his thumbs pressed along the rough grain of the wood. The iron blade cut into the dry Virginia dirt, slicing the roots of the wild mustard weeds that grew between the rows.

Dust rose around his leather boots. The soil was dry and sandy, turning up in thin clods that crumbled under the heat. Jake took two steps forward, his boots sinking into the loose earth. He leaned his weight into the next strike, clearing the space between two young corn stalks. The green leaves brushed against his shins.

At fourteen, his shoulders were still narrow. The daily labor of the farm had pulled his muscles tight, but he lacked the broad bulk of his father. He wore his homespun trousers high, held by home-tanned leather straps. A small farm knot secured the left strap where the horn button had split the previous autumn. He adjusted the knot with a quick pull of his fingers.

His mother worked three rows over, her wool apron tucked into her waistband. She bent low, pulling weeds by hand from the base of the stalks. Her blue bonnet blocked the sun, the fabric faded to a pale gray along the seams. Further down the slope, his two younger sisters carried wooden buckets of water from the creek, their long skirts dragged through the wild grass.

Jake stopped. He wiped his forehead with his sleeve, leaving a streak of gray dirt across his skin. The air was still. No wind stirred the green leaves of the corn.

Above the field, the woods rose up the ridge in a dark wall of oak and pine. The crows had been calling from the high branches since dawn. Now, they were quiet. The woods were silent. Jake looked toward the ridge, his fingers tightening on the smooth hickory wood.

His mother stood up, her hand pressing against the small of her back. She looked toward the creek where the girls had stopped. A blue jay flitted through the low birch trees, its feathers bright against the dark bark. It did not make a sound.

"The wind has died," Jake said. He stuck the hoe into the dirt.

His mother did not look up from the row. "Work the ground while the sun is high."

Jake gripped the handle again. He swung the blade into the dry soil. A beetle scurried from beneath a clod, disappearing into the shade of a corn leaf.

The girls set the buckets down by the fence line. Their laughter carried across the rows, thin and clear in the warm air. Jake watched them brush the flies from their faces.

A shadow crossed the field. It was not a cloud. A hawk circled high above the ridge, its wings spread wide against the blue sky. It did not cry out.

Jake wiped his palms on his trousers. The leather was rough and stiff from old sweat. He took another step, his eyes fixed on the line of forest along the ridge.

The pines seemed darker under the midday light. A strange smell came on the air, faint and sweet. It was not the smell of pine needles or wet creek moss.

His mother stopped weeding. She turned her head toward the cabin, her bonnet tilting back. Her shoulders went stiff under her linen dress.

"Jake," she said.

He stepped out of the row. He followed her gaze toward the trees.

## Scene 2

A gray smudge rose above the oak trees on the northern slope. It was not the thin steam of the cooking fire. The column grew thick and dark, spreading wide across the blue sky.

Jake dropped the hoe. The hickory handle hit the loose soil with a soft thud. He looked at his mother. Her face was pale under the brim of her bonnet.

"Run to the brush," she said. Her voice was low and flat.

She did not wait. She scrambled down the slope toward the creek, her boots kicking up dust. She grabbed the two girls by their arms, pulling them toward the thick alder bushes by the water.

Jake turned toward the cabin. He ran through the rows of corn, the green leaves slapping against his face and chest. The soil was deep, catching at his boots. His breath came short and hot in his throat.

He cleared the edge of the cornfield. The dirt road to the cabin was packed hard and dry. Smoke poured from the open windows of the small log structure, rising from the dry cedar shingles of the roof. Red flames licked the pine logs of the chimney wall.

Two horses stood in the clearing, their reins trailing in the weeds. A warrior held the bridle of the larger bay horse, his face marked with black soot. He carried a long rifle in his left hand.

Jake stopped at the edge of the woods. His father's hunting rifle was inside the cabin, hanging above the stone fireplace. The door stood wide, smoke rolling out in thick clouds.

Another warrior emerged from the cabin door. He carried a bundle of blankets and a iron kettle. He threw them onto the dirt path.

A third warrior walked from behind the barn. He held a burning pine branch, the pitch bubbling orange in the heat. He pressed the torch against the dry straw of the roof.

The thatch caught quickly. A sheet of yellow flame swept up the side of the barn, the smoke rising thick and white. The horses reared, their hooves striking the dry ground.

Jake pressed his back against a pine trunk. His heart hammered against his ribs. He looked at the axe by the woodpile.

The woodpile sat twenty paces away, near the splitting stump. A broad felling axe was embedded in the pine wood. The handle stood straight and pale against the dark logs.

Jake took a deep breath. He leaped from the shadow of the trees.

## Scene 3

He reached the woodpile in three strides. His fingers wrapped around the ash handle of the axe, pulling it free from the wood. The iron head was cold and solid in his grip.

A warrior turned at the sound. He carried a short iron tomahawk in his right hand, his face painted with red clay. He ran toward Jake, his moccasins silent on the dirt.

The warrior lunged. He swung the tomahawk in a broad circle, the iron blade whistling through the smoke. Jake ducked, his boots slipping on the dry wood chips. The tomahawk chopped into the pine logs behind him, throwing splinters.

Jake swung the felling axe. He put his shoulders into the arc, the iron head cutting through the air. The blade struck the warrior's side, biting deep through the deer hide shirt.

The warrior gasped. He fell back against the woodpile, his hands clawing at the iron head. Jake pulled the handle back, freeing the blade with a dull tear.

A second warrior ran from the smoke of the cabin. He was older, his face scarred and his jaw set. He carried a heavy hickory war club.

Jake raised the axe again, but his foot caught on a split log. He went down hard on his side, the dry wood chips scraping his palms. The axe flew from his hands, landing in the weeds.

He tried to scramble toward it. His fingers brushed the rough wood of the handle, but a heavy blow struck his shoulder, pinning him to the dirt.

The world went dark for a second. The smell of burning pine and sweat filled his nose.

"Do not move," a voice said. The words were English, but the accent was thick and strange.

Jake did not speak. He lay flat in the dirt, the heat of the fire burning his face.

## Scene 4

Rough hands pulled his arms behind him. A strip of green deerskin wrapped around his wrists, biting into the flesh as it was pulled tight. The knot was double-turned, leaving no slack.

The warriors hauled him to his feet. One of them pointed toward the trail that led up the ridge. The smoke from the cabin was thick now, turning the sun to a dim orange ball.

Jake stood in the clearing. He did not call out. He looked at the burning logs of the porch, the timbers shifting and throwing a shower of yellow sparks.

The group moved into the timber. The trail was narrow and steep, rising quickly through the pines. Jake walked with his chin down, his boots tripping on the roots that crossed the path.

The fire grew smaller behind them. A single crow called from the high branches of a dead pine, its voice loud in the quiet woods. Jake did not look back again.

He marched in silence. The leather bindings cut into his wrists, but he did not make a sound. His eyes were fixed on the moccasins of the warrior ahead of him.

The forest grew dense. The trail rose higher, leaving the valley behind. Jake kept his feet moving, his face set against the rising heat of the afternoon.
