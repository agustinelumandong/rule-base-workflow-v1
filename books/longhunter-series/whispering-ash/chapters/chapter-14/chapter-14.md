# Chapter 14: Cabin in the Wild

## Scene 1

Jake built by the spring because water was life and because the stone shoulders guarded his back.

The first log from the spruce did not reach the cabin line before dark on the day he cut it. At dawn he put the rope around it again and dragged until the bark tore in long strips. The log moved badly over stone. It caught on roots. It slid sideways when the ground tilted. Twice Jake had to wedge a branch under it, lift with his shoulder, and roll the butt free.

By noon he had it on the line scraped into the dirt.

One log did not make a cabin.

It made the size of the work plain.

Jake stood over it with sweat drying cold on his back. The spring ran behind him. The river sounded below the cliff. The gorge held quiet except for water, wind in spruce tops, and the small knocks of his own breathing. No man gave orders. No one waited to take the finished thing. No village woman pointed to more work. No trader counted wrong. No Winston McCrea laughed from a doorway.

That left all failure to Jake.

If the cabin leaked, he would sleep wet. If the roof sagged, it would sag over him. If the sill rotted because he chose poor ground, winter would come through the floor and teach him. No hand would correct the work unless his own did.

He set the first log on stones, not dirt.

Farm fence posts had taught him rot. Thorn Blackroot had taught him bark, wind, and ground. Caleb Thorne had pointed out west-mouth glare and spring stone. Anna Ray's awl needle sat in his pouch, small and useful, bought fair under hard eyes. Even War Chief Red Hollow, far behind him in blood and smoke, had taught the cost of a weak place. Every lesson that had not killed him stood around the cabin line.

Jake used them.

The second log came easier only because he cut it closer. It was crooked near the top, so he took the better half and left the rest for firewood. He trimmed limbs close with the axe and shaved knots with the knife. Sap stuck to his fingers. Bark dust clung to sweat. When the log split at one end, he cut past the split and lost length rather than build with a fault he would curse in January.

The first corner went badly.

He cut the notch too shallow. The top log rocked when he leaned on it. He took it off, deepened the cut, and set it again. It still rocked. He cursed once, low, then carried the log aside and studied the notch. Farm rails could be ugly and still hold cattle. Cabin corners needed a better bargain. Wood had to rest on wood, with weight carried where the grain would bear it.

He tried again.

This time the log settled.

By evening he had two courses laid on three sides and one side no better than a hope. He slept under the rock overhang, not inside the beginning of the cabin, because a man did not sleep under work until the work deserved trust.

The next days made a narrow world.

Cut. Trim. Drag. Notch. Lift. Set. Check. Take down. Cut again.

His hands opened at the old scars. The rope burned a stripe across one shoulder. A falling limb struck his forearm and raised a knot under the skin. Rain came one afternoon and showed him which corner would drink water first. He tore that corner apart before dark, though it cost him a full day. Better to lose daylight in autumn than heat in winter.

The cabin rose waist high, then chest high.

He left the door narrow and set it away from the river mouth. Any man coming from the gap would have to cross open ground and turn before entering. The window was no true window, only a firing slit under the roof line, covered by a shutter pegged from inside. It would give little light. Light could be bought with an open door. Warmth could not.

The sill logs taught him where the cabin wanted to fail.

One corner settled lower after the first hard rain, not much, only the width of a finger. Enough. Jake saw the door line change before the bar was even made. He unloaded that corner, stone by stone, levered the sill up with a pole, and packed a flatter base beneath it. The work cost half a day and one torn palm.

He did not leave it for later.

Later had killed too many plans before winter ever touched them.

When the corner settled true, he marked the stones with charcoal so he would know if they shifted again. A cabin was not built once. It had to be watched into staying.

Moss filled the cracks first. Mud came after. He mixed clay with grass, pressed it deep, and smoothed it with a flat stick. Where wind still touched his cheek from inside, he marked the seam with charcoal and packed it again. Smoke from a small test fire told him more. It curled toward one wall and leaked at a corner. He shut the door, watched the smoke crawl, then opened the wall seam and packed it tighter.

The hearth took three days.

Stone had to be chosen, hauled, fitted, and cursed. River stones cracked under heat, so he took from the slope, knocking each one with the axe poll and listening for the dead sound of a flaw. He set a low back into the earth wall and made the floor of flat pieces bedded in clay. The chimney was not pretty. It leaned a little. It drew well enough after he narrowed the throat and raised the back.

The first time smoke climbed right, Jake stood inside the half-roofed cabin and watched it leave through the stone throat.

No fire in his boyhood home had ever meant so much.

Then the roof humbled him.

Walls could be forced up by muscle. A roof needed order. Poles first, peeled and laid close. Bark sheets over them with each seam turned from weather. More poles to pin the bark. Stones along the edges. Spruce boughs under the eaves to slow driven rain. He knew the parts from Thorn's winter lessons and from every poor shelter that had failed him, but a cabin roof was larger than memory.

The first rain found three leaks.

One dripped near the hearth. One ran down the back wall. One fell directly where his bedding would go.

Jake set a cup under the bedding leak and listened to water strike tin through the night.

At dawn he climbed the roof and fixed it.

The second rain found one leak.

The third found none.

Only then did he move his blanket inside.

The door came from split planks he made with wedges and more patience than skill. It hung on leather hinges and closed against a bar set into two wood rests. The first bar was too thin. He split it across his knee and made another. The latch was nothing a determined man could not break. A determined man would make noise breaking it. Noise was worth something.

By the time the cabin could hold fire, Jake had grown leaner.

His food ran low.

That pressure sat with him each night. A cabin without meat was only a dry place to starve. The gorge had squirrels, rabbits, turkey scratching, deer trails, and fish under the river stones, but winter would close its hand on all of them. Food had to be cut, smoked, wrapped, hidden, and kept from fox, bear, rot, and men.

He built the barn next because tools needed cover and because someday a mule or horse might mean the difference between staying and losing everything.

It was no grand barn. Four posts, a sloped roof, one side closed against the worst wind, and a dry rack under the back. He set it lower than the cabin and away from the spring. If it burned, the cabin might stand. If a thief found it first, the cabin might stay hidden another minute. He hung the axe, spare rope, bark rolls, and a broken kettle under its roof. A small pen came after, made of saplings and rails cut short enough for one man to drag.

He did not own stock.

But a man building only for what he owned today stayed poor tomorrow.

The barn floor mattered more than the walls.

Mud ruined tools by slow theft. Damp took handles, rusted iron, and softened rope. Jake cut poles and laid them crosswise above the ground, then packed brush beneath them so air could move. The first rack sagged when he stepped on it. He pulled it apart and set the poles closer. The second held his weight and the broken kettle both.

He made pegs from hardwood and drove them into the back posts at different heights. Axe high. Rope higher, away from mice. Bark rolls under the eave. Wedges near the door. Nothing on the ground unless it could rot there without grief.

The pen took longer than he expected because rails needed more than stacking. A frightened animal would push through a lazy pen. A thief would smile at one. Jake notched the posts, set rails into them, and wedged the corners with split stone. Empty rails looked foolish without an animal inside.

He built them anyway.

The caches came after the barn.

One under the boulder by the spring. One higher under a split root. One across the creek in a hollow marked by two stones and a bent sapling. Each cache had bark lining, a stone lip, and a cover meant to look like weather had put it there. He tested each with water. The first one leaked. He pulled it apart and lined it again. The second held but smelled too much of fresh bark, so he packed it with dry leaves for two days before using it. The third sat too close to a fox run, and he moved it uphill.

Food could not all sleep in one place.

Neither could powder.

He made a dry horn rest inside the cabin and a second wrapped bundle in the boulder cache. Lead went separate. Salt went separate. If one place failed, he would not lose all.

Then he made himself fail each plan on purpose.

He walked from the cabin to the spring cache in dark and found the route too open. A man at the river mouth could see him cross the pale stone if moonlight lay right. He changed the path, moving behind two spruce and stepping over a root tangle that would catch anyone hurrying. He went to the root cache with one hand over his eyes until touch found the bent sapling. Too easy. He moved the marker to a stone his boot could feel instead.

He carried one wrapped food bundle to the wrong place and left it there until morning.

By dawn, mice had found it.

That bundle went to the fire. The cache was moved. A mistake made before hunger was a lesson. A mistake made after snow could be a grave.

He tested the caches against himself next.

At dusk he left the cabin with no light and tried to reach each hidden store as if wounded, one hand pressed against his side, the other holding the rifle. The spring cache could be reached that way. The root cache could not. A low limb caught the rifle barrel twice, and the second time the sound carried farther than he liked.

The limb came down at dawn.

The creek cache had another fault. To reach it fast, he had to cross a strip of soft earth that kept prints. Jake laid flat stones under the leaves, one at a time, and tested them with wet boots. Two rocked. One clicked against another. He bedded them deeper until his weight crossed without sound.

No hidden place stayed hidden if panic made a road to it.

The first long hunt began under a gray morning with frost on the grass.

Jake left before daylight with the rifle, knife, rope, salt, and one strip of smoked bear meat from the last of Caleb's shared kill. He barred the cabin door from outside with a hidden peg, then brushed his tracks from the spring run and climbed the back pass. At the stone teeth, he stopped and looked down.

The cabin barely showed.

Smoke would show more.

He let the fire die before leaving.

For five days he moved beyond the gorge. He followed deer sign through beech flats and laurel cuts. He crossed ridges where wind came cold enough to numb his ears. He slept under rock twice and under a fallen hemlock once. The first day gave him tracks too old. The second gave rain that wiped out sign and soaked his blanket. The third gave a doe with a fawn still running near her, and he let them pass because meat bought with poor judgment cost later.

On the fourth morning, he found buck sign near a salt lick.

The track was deep on one side where the animal favored a leg. Browse showed high on striped maple. Bark rubbed from a sapling held hair near the splinter. Jake took the downwind side and moved slower than hunger wanted.

By midday he saw the buck.

It stood beyond a screen of young beech, head lowered, one antler broken near the tip. Jake knelt behind a log and set the rifle across it. Wind touched the back of his neck, steady enough. The buck lifted its head. Jake waited. A poor shot would send it into miles of timber and leave him with nothing but regret and less powder.

The buck stepped once.

Jake fired.

The shot struck clean behind the shoulder. The buck ran hard for twenty paces, stumbled, and fell among fern gone brown with frost.

Jake reloaded before standing.

Then he waited.

No answering shot. No shout. No horse. Only the last kick of the deer and the wind moving through dry beech leaves.

The waiting had a reason.

A shot could call more than echo. Men could hear it. Wolves could hear the panic after it. A wounded deer could rise if a man rushed too soon and carry half a winter's meat into country too rough to follow. Jake counted one hundred breaths, then one hundred more, rifle ready, eyes moving from the deer to the trees and back again.

Only when the buck lay still through wind, leaf fall, and a crow's first call did he stand.

He dressed the buck where it fell. The work took time and care. Meat cooled fast in the frost. Blood steamed on leaves. He hung quarters from a low limb while he cleaned the knife and packed what he could carry. The first load was too much. He took it ten steps, stopped, and cut it down. Pride did not carry meat home. Legs did.

Two trips would be needed.

Maybe three.

That was the cost of hunting alone.

He cached part of the meat in a tree fork wrapped in hide and brush, marked the place by a stone split like a jaw, and carried the first load toward the gorge. He reached the cabin after dark with shoulders burning and knees stiff. No fire waited. No voice. No lamp. The cabin stood black against the stone.

He touched the door before opening it.

Still there.

The next day he returned for the rest and found fox sign below the cache but no loss. He carried until his back cramped. He rested only where stone hid his trail. Snow grains ticked against dead leaves before he reached the gorge with the last load.

Then preservation ruled everything.

He cut meat thin and hung it over smoke. He rendered fat in the broken kettle. He salted strips Anna Ray's fair trade had made possible and wrapped them in bark. He buried one cache, hung another, and kept the best cuts near the hearth where he could watch them. Smoke stung his eyes for three days. His clothes took the smell. His hair took it. The cabin took it and became less like cut wood and more like a place that had begun to live.

For two days the cabin was meat, smoke, and salt.

Jake slept in short turns, waking to feed the fire, shift a strip, or drive away sparks before they touched the rack. Too much flame would ruin what hunger had bought. Too little smoke would leave rot under the surface. He cut the thick pieces thinner. He saved bones for broth. He poured rendered fat into a scraped bark cup and set it where cold would harden it.

The first batch taught him pride had no place near a smoke rack.

Three strips near the edge dried too fast outside and stayed soft within. Jake found the fault by pressing one between finger and thumb. It gave too much. He cut it open, saw the damp center, and put the pieces into the kettle for immediate eating. They would not keep. Better to lose three strips now than find rot in a cache when snow lay too deep for hunting.

After that he sorted by thickness every time.

Thin strips high. Thick strips lower until heat began, then moved. Fat pieces watched closest. Lean pieces turned less. He marked one pole for meat that could travel and another for meat that had to stay near the hearth. Salt went only where it paid. Anna Ray's fair measure looked small when spread against winter.

He used none of it carelessly.

Once, near midnight, he woke to silence and knew the fire had sunk too low.

He was on his feet before his eyes were fully open.

The coals still lived. He fed them and watched smoke rise again around the meat. That was how winter would be: sleep broken by need, warmth paid for in advance, every mistake waiting until he was tired enough to make it.

Every cache was checked twice.

Fox sign near the spring earned a snare. A bear track above the gorge made him move one hanging bundle higher and rub the pole with ash. Mice found a meal sack, so he hung the sack from a roof pole with a tin scrap below the cord. The first night they tried it, he heard their feet on the scrap and smiled in the dark without making a sound.

The work did not end.

It changed shape.

There was firewood to cut before snow covered deadfall. There were roof stones to set again after wind shifted one. There was mud daubing to patch where frost cracked it. The barn needed another brace. The spring run needed clearing of leaves. The back pass needed a deadfall moved so Jake could leave fast if men came from below.

Men might come.

Winston McCrea's mouth had not vanished because Jake walked west. Red Hollow remained behind him in a past written with blood. Caleb had gone north, Anna stayed at her counter, Thorn Blackroot stayed in memory and scar. None of them would stand between Jake and the cabin door if trouble came through the gorge mouth.

That knowledge kept him from softening.

The firewood pile became a wall of its own.

Jake stacked it under the barn side in three ranks: small split wood for quick flame, wrist-thick pieces for cooking, larger cuts for night. He turned bark side up where rain might blow in. He left a narrow gap behind the stack so damp would not rot it against the wall. A poor stack fell in weather. A fallen stack meant wet wood. Wet wood meant smoke, cold, and wasted strength.

He knocked the pile with his shoulder.

It held.

On the last clear day before winter, he walked the perimeter with the rifle and checked every approach. River mouth. Lower stone slope. Back pass. Laurel screen. Cache hollow. Meat pole. Barn. Cabin. Spring.

Each place had a use.

Each use had a cost if neglected.

The back pass had changed with leaf fall.

Summer cover had thinned. What had hidden the cabin shelf in green now showed pieces of roof through bare limbs. Jake climbed above it and looked down until his eyes found every pale cut he had made. Too many. Fresh stumps showed where spruce had stood. Drag marks had faded, but not vanished. The cabin itself hid well enough from below; from above it needed help.

He cut brush from farther off and set it against the most open sight lines, not in a wall, but in broken pieces like storm fall. He darkened two pale stumps with mud and ash. He scattered old leaves across the worst drag path and left the rest alone. Too much hiding showed a man's hand.

When he climbed back to the pass near sundown, the roof had broken into shadow again.

Good enough.

Not safe.

Nothing was.

He checked himself the same way.

His hands had split at three knuckles. He sealed them with fat and ash because blood on tools made handles slick. His right shoulder pulled from dragging logs. He changed the way he lifted firewood. One boot sole had begun to peel. Anna Ray's awl needle closed it with sinew before the gap could widen. A man alone had to mend his own body as if it were another shelter.

He set one rule for winter before the first snow came.

No chore left because he was tired if cold could make it worse by morning.

That rule added hours to every day.

He found one problem at the river mouth. A man could come along the lower rocks at dusk and stay under the lip longer than Jake liked. He cut brush from a higher patch and wove it into a dead screen, not enough to stop a man, enough to make him choose a louder way. Above it, Jake loosened three stones and set them where a pull of rope would drop them onto the lower path. Crude work. Useful work.

That night the cold changed.

It came through the gorge without rain ahead of it. The spring ran clear but slower at the edges. The mud near the barn hardened. Jake woke before dawn because the cabin had gone too quiet. No drip from the eaves. No mouse on tin. No wind in loose bark.

He opened the door.

Snow fell in small white grains.

The gorge had changed while he slept. Spruce limbs held pale lines. The cabin roof wore a thin skin of white. The barn leaned under it but held. Smoke from the chimney rose straight for a few feet before the cold broke it apart. The spring cut a dark thread through the whitened ground.

Jake stepped outside with the ash cloak around his shoulders.

The door he had built closed behind him.

It held.

He stood there with bare hands despite the cold and looked at the work. Cabin. Barn. Caches. Meat smoke staining the roof poles. Firewood stacked under cover. Rifle dry inside the door. Knife at his belt. Powder divided. Salt wrapped. Food hidden where fox, bear, and men would have to work to find it.

The burned cabin in Virginia came back to him in smoke and snow.

He did not turn away from it.

That cabin had been his father's, his mother's, his sisters', his brothers'. This one was not a replacement. Dead places did not trade with living ones. This one stood because Jake had lived long enough to cut logs, set stone, learn from enemies, take help without kneeling to it, leave settlement without begging from it, and put his hands to the wild until the wild gave him a narrow place to stand.

Snow gathered on his sleeves.

No village owned him.

No town held him.

No family roof waited behind him.

The gorge did not love him. It would freeze him if he grew foolish, starve him if he grew lazy, and hide enemies if he stopped watching. That made it honest.

Jake Moses stood before the cabin alone.

Not a boy at a farm row.

Not a captive under ash laughter.

Not a trader's joke.

A Long Hunter, made by work, loss, weather, and blood, with winter beginning at his door.

He went inside before the fire burned low.
