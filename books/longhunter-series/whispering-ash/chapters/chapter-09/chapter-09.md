# Chapter 9: Chaos and Flight

## Scene 1

The raid broke before it became a fight.

Men had gone out in a long line before dawn, more than Jake had seen gathered for any hunt or raid before. Bows, knives, tomahawks, trade muskets, powder horns, spare cords, packs, dried food, paint, and anger. The war party moved hard through broken country toward the river road, and Jake moved with them under the ash cloak.

The hidden stores lay sewn into the hem where his fingers could find them without looking.

Flint.

Dried meat.

Thorn needle.

Sinew.

Small things. Poor things. Enough to matter if a man reached the timber alone.

Red Hollow led from near the front, not always seen but always known. His voice carried through brush when men spread too far. His hand lifted and the line bent. His hand dropped and the line stopped. Jake had watched him since the village, watched the way younger men leaned toward his commands before they even came. Red Hollow did not need to shout often. That made the shouting worse when it came.

By noon, smoke rose ahead where none should have been.

It did not rise from one cooking fire or one cabin chimney. It rolled low beyond the ridge in several dark columns, torn by wind. Men in front slowed. A runner came back along the line, bent low, speaking fast. Another man swore and checked the priming in his musket. The horses, kept below on a rough trail with packs and spare goods, began to fret.

Jake smelled burned powder before he heard the first shot.

The shot came from the wrong side of the ridge.

Men dropped into cover. A second shot cracked below. A third answered from higher ground. Leaves snapped above Jake's head, cut by ball or splinter. Horses screamed. Someone shouted that the trail was blocked. Another voice called from behind them, saying men had come around the lower side.

The line folded wrong.

A war party that had moved like one body split into pieces that could not see each other. Men ran toward smoke and away from it. Two tried to pull a packhorse uphill and lost it when the animal reared against the rope. A man with a red-painted cheek fired too soon into brush and cursed while trying to reload with shaking hands. Another fell hard near a stone and crawled behind it with one leg dragging.

Jake went down behind a fallen log.

He did not yet know who had sprung the trap. Settlers, militia, rival men, scouts from some fort. It did not matter. The road ahead had been ready. The lower trail had guns. Smoke covered the ridge. Orders came from three places at once, and every order fought another.

The plan had torn open.

No one could see the whole ground.

Jake could see enough.

A pack had split near the horses below. Corn cakes and powder paper lay in mud. One musket had been dropped and trampled. A wounded man called for help, then stopped calling when a ball struck the log above him. Smoke rolled through laurel, thick enough to hide a crawling man if the man did not hurry.

Another horse tore free and ran uphill with a broken pack frame banging against its side. Men cursed and ducked away from it. A powder horn rolled under a root. Someone shouted for the horses. Someone else shouted to leave them. The lower trail fired again, and the argument vanished under lead.

Disaster made its own cover.

That was the opening.

Jake did not run at first.

A running man drew eyes.

He crawled backward under laurel, one elbow at a time. A thorn tore his sleeve. He let it tear rather than jerk free. He kept his cheek near the ground and moved only when shots covered the sound. A warrior leaped over the log where Jake had been, fired downhill, and never looked under the brush behind him.

The first ten paces were the hardest.

They were still part of the fight. Men knew his place there. Thorn might look for him there. Red Hollow might count him there. If a hand reached down and found the ash cloak under laurel, the escape would end before it had a name. Jake kept his fingers out of sight and pulled the cloak close so the gray broke against dead leaves rather than sliding over them in one long shape.

Another wounded man rolled near the log and saw the brush move.

Jake stopped with one hand buried in mud.

The man blinked blood from his lashes, looked toward the firing below, and dragged himself the other way. Maybe he had not seen. Maybe he had seen and chose his own pain first. Jake did not wait to know.

Jake reached a dry wash.

It ran slantwise through leaves and stone, half hidden under deadfall. Water had cut it deep in spring and left it dry by summer. He slid into it on his belly and let loose dirt cover the ash cloak. Above him men shouted. Below him a horse screamed again and crashed through brush with packs swinging under its belly.

He followed the wash down, not far, only until smoke hid him from the nearest fighting.

Then he stopped.

The easiest line was not the safest line.

The wash would lead to water. Men would look there. Men always looked for water when a trail vanished. Thorn had taught him that by making him follow his own poor tracks after rain. Red Hollow would know it too.

Jake climbed out where stone showed through leaves. He stepped on rock, then root, then the side of a fallen trunk. He crossed the trunk and dropped into fern on the far side, landing with knees bent and one hand down. No straight line. Straight lines were for men who wanted to be found.

Behind him, Red Hollow's voice rose over the broken fighting.

The sound cut through gun smoke and horse noise.

Not panic.

Command.

Men answered him. The scattered shouts began to gather around that voice. A runner went past Jake's old position toward the lower trail. Two men above the wash turned back uphill. Red Hollow was pulling the torn pieces together before the trap could eat them all.

Jake moved faster.

Not running. Moving.

He took three steps under fern, then stopped behind a tree and looked. He crossed a patch of moss on the balls of his feet. He ducked under laurel and used one hand to lift branches so they did not whip back. The ash cloak dragged over rock. He caught the hem before it snagged and tucked it higher under his belt, fingers touching the hidden seam.

Still there.

Another shot cracked behind him.

Then he heard his name.

Not Jake.

The call was Whispering Ash.

It came from behind, angry and certain.

The name passed from one voice to another. A man near the wash repeated it. Another lower down answered. Red Hollow's voice followed, shorter, harder. Jake could not understand every sound, but he understood enough. The war chief had seen the gap. He knew one man was gone.

The escape had begun.

So had the hunt.

Jake angled uphill instead of down. Down meant water, and water meant searchers. Up meant stone, laurel, and slower legs. He took a deer path for six paces, then left it before it became too easy. A musket ball struck timber somewhere behind him. Bark jumped from a trunk. He did not know if the shot had been meant for him or for the broken fight. He did not turn to learn.

At a shelf of rock, he stopped long enough to take one corn cake from a torn pack he had snatched in the smoke.

No.

Too plain.

The cake was militia meal, or trade meal, or stolen from the raid stores. If he dropped crumbs, a man could read it. He wrapped it in leaves and put it into the ash cloak. Dried meat from his hidden hem would serve better until dark.

He also took a strip of torn cord from the same pack.

The cord was not much, frayed at one end and stained with meal dust, but cord could bind a broken moccasin, tie brush behind him, or hang meat away from fox. He looped it once around his wrist, then thought better of that and tucked it into the cloak. Anything on the wrist could catch. Anything that caught could stop a man at the wrong breath.

Behind him, the call of Whispering Ash came again.

Closer.

The slope steepened.

Jake climbed on hands and feet. Laurel roots held the earth where rain had cut under it. Twice he had to pull himself by roots. Once a root tore free, and he slid three feet before his boot caught stone. Loose dirt ran below him.

Too much sign.

He climbed sideways from the slide, crossed stone, and used a pine bough to brush the worst of the dirt mark. It would not fool Red Hollow long. It might slow a younger scout. Slowing one man could open a space wide enough to live in.

The noise of the collapsed raid faded behind him.

It did not vanish.

Smoke still drifted through the trees. Shots still sounded, fewer now, more spread. The war party had not been destroyed. That was bad. Men left alive could hunt. Men ashamed by surprise could hunt harder.

Jake reached the ridge near midafternoon.

From there he could see pieces of the broken ground through trees. Smoke along the road. Horses scattered. Men in dark lines moving under it. A body near a rock. Another dragged by two others. The raid had failed, but it had not ended.

Red Hollow stood on a lower spur with three men near him.

Jake saw him only for a breath through branches: broad shoulders, painted face, one arm lifted, then cutting toward the timber. Men moved at once.

Pursuit.

Red Hollow did not run after him himself.

That would have been easier.

Instead the war chief sent men in pieces. Two toward the wash. One up the ridge. Another angling wide where a runaway might circle. A fool chased the last print. Red Hollow hunted the next choice. Jake saw that in the way the men spread before they had even reached his trail.

The distance he had bought in smoke began to shrink.

Jake dropped from the skyline before any eye could catch him there.

The far side of the ridge fell steeper than the side he had climbed.

Jake went down facing the slope, using hands, heels, and roots. Twice loose shale slid below him and rattled into brush. He stopped after each slide and waited for a shout. None came, but the sound had gone somewhere. Every sound went somewhere. He moved left, away from the fall line, and found a seam of moss between rocks.

Moss held water.

Water held less sign than dirt.

He followed it until the seam ended at a dead pine, then crossed the pine trunk and dropped into laurel. The move cost time. It saved tracks. By then Red Hollow's men had reached the top behind him. A call came high and thin through the trees.

They had found the ridge.

Not him.

## Scene 2

By late afternoon, the first scouts were on him.

Jake heard them before he saw them. A twig touched and released. A stone shifted under a careful foot. A bird scolded once and stopped. Two men, maybe three, moving apart so one could catch what another missed. Red Hollow had not waited for dark.

Jake had crossed into country he did not know.

That was both knife and shield.

No village path told him where to go. No cache waited. No Thorn sat by a fire. No old hunting loop lay in memory. But Red Hollow's men did not know every stone either, not here beyond the broken ridge. Strange ground could betray all men alike if one used it with care.

A shelf of rhododendron leaned over a rock hollow ahead.

Jake slid under it and pulled the ash cloak over his head and shoulders. The gray broke his outline against stone and dead leaves. His face went into the crook of one arm. He slowed his breath until it moved only the dirt under his mouth.

The scouts came through the brush above him.

One moved too fast and snapped a twig. The other stopped. Jake saw moccasins through leaves. A hand parted brush. The scout looked into the shadow under the shelf and saw only ash-gray cloth, stone, and leaf rot.

A fly crawled across Jake's cheek.

He did not move.

The hand stayed there.

Another man spoke low from above.

The scout answered, annoyed. His foot shifted closer. Jake's knife lay under his chest, hilt against his palm. If the man reached in, Jake would have one cut. Maybe two. Then the other scout would call out, and Red Hollow would have him before dark.

The hand withdrew.

The scouts went on.

Jake waited.

He waited until his arms trembled from holding still. He waited until their steps thinned into creek noise below. He waited longer because boys eager to prove skill often doubled back. No one returned.

Then he backed out of the hollow and went uphill instead of down.

His stomach cramped. No food since before the fight. He dared one strip of dried meat from the cloak hem and chewed while walking, keeping the bark wrapper in his fist so no scrap fell. Salt touched his tongue and made thirst worse. He swallowed anyway.

Near dusk, Red Hollow's voice came again from below.

Closer than it should have been.

The scouts had found something. A bent fern. A slide mark. A broken root. Not enough to close on him yet. Enough to keep the line true.

Jake crossed a creek on stones, then stepped backward in the water for ten paces before climbing out on a root web. Cold soaked his moccasins. He used pine boughs to brush the bank where mud had taken his heel. The work was poor in fading light, but better than leaving a clean print.

He went ten paces uphill, then returned to the creek by stone and moved downstream in the water until his feet went numb.

A man following too fast might take the first exit.

A patient man would find the second.

Red Hollow was patient.

Jake needed distance before patience reached him.

Night gathered under the trees. The strange ridge beyond the creek rose black with hemlock and laurel. The ground changed from leaf mold to broken stone. That helped. Stone took less sign. Stone also twisted ankles. Jake slowed, choosing each step by touch when sight failed. Twice he stopped and listened behind him.

Once he heard nothing.

Once he heard a horn.

Low. Short.

Not a hunting call. A signal.

Men answered far apart.

They were spreading.

Jake climbed harder. His legs shook. Sweat chilled under the ash cloak. A branch caught the hidden hem and almost tore the seam. He stopped in the dark and worked it free with both hands. The flint knocked against his knuckle.

Still there.

Without that, freedom would be a cold word and nothing more.

At the top of the ridge, he found a split in the rocks just wide enough to hold his body. It opened under a leaning slab and ran back no farther than his legs. No comfort. Good. Comfort made a man careless. He crawled into it feet first, then turned with trouble, pulling the cloak around himself. The stone pressed his shoulder and hip. He could see a narrow slice of slope below.

Torches moved there after full dark.

Not many.

Enough.

Red Hollow had men working the lower ground with fire while others held the crossings. Jake watched one torch pause near the creek. Another moved higher, then dropped back. Voices rose and fell. A dog barked once far away, then was silenced.

Jake checked the hidden seam by touch.

Flint.

Meat.

Needle.

Sinew.

He had a knife, one small stolen corn cake, damp moccasins, and the ash cloak. No blanket beyond it. No sure country ahead. No friend. No roof. No permission from any living man to exist beyond this night.

He counted what each thing could buy.

Flint could buy fire if rain spared him. Meat could buy one day, two if he cut it thin and chewed slow. Needle and sinew could close moccasin, cloak, skin, or snare. The corn cake could betray him with crumbs if he ate in haste. The knife could take meat, cut cord, open bark, or make a last answer if Red Hollow's men found the split.

None of it bought safety.

For the first time since the farm, no hand held him.

No voice ordered him.

No village owned his labor.

Freedom had teeth.

Cold came into the rock split before midnight. It settled in his wet feet first, then climbed. Jake folded his knees, pulled the cloak tighter, and did not sleep. Sleep would make him shift. Shifting might scrape stone. Stone might carry sound.

The corn cake stayed wrapped under his chest.

He wanted it each time his belly cramped. He did not eat. Eating made crumbs, and crumbs drew small things. Small things drew eyes. He took one thread of dried meat instead and let it soften under his tongue until salt reached him slowly.

At some hour past midnight, rain began again. Not hard. Enough to bead on the stone lip and slide down the back of his neck. It helped him and punished him together. Rain softened sign. Rain stole heat. Rain made torches hiss below and turned every grip on rock uncertain.

Near dawn, one scout came close enough for Jake to see the man's outline against gray sky.

The scout moved along the ridge above the split, looking down into hollows. He paused where Jake had climbed, then crouched. Jake's fingers closed around the knife. The scout touched a stone, lifted it, smelled his fingers, and looked toward the lower creek.

Then Red Hollow called from below.

The scout stood and went toward the voice.

Jake stayed in the rock until morning light touched the opposite slope.

The wait was harder than the climb.

Cold had settled into his wet moccasins and made his feet wooden. His left calf cramped twice. He did not straighten it. Stone pressed into his hip. Ants moved somewhere near his sleeve, slow from cold but living. One crawled across his wrist. He let it pass.

Below, Red Hollow spoke with the scout. The distance and stone broke the sounds, but the shape of command carried. One man was sent downstream. Another crossed higher. A third stayed near the creek. They were not done. They had only widened the net.

Jake looked at the pale sky through the slit above him and counted breaths until the voices thinned.

Only then did he move.

He climbed out stiff and cold, with stone dust on his face and the ash cloak damp at the edges. Below, the torches were gone. That did not mean the men were gone. It meant daylight had given them eyes.

Jake moved west because the ridge forced him west.

He left no prayer in the rocks, no boast, no mark for Red Hollow to find. He kept to stone when he could, moss when he could not, water when he had to. By noon he had crossed into timber that did not match the village hunting grounds. The trees grew differently. The streams ran a direction he had not learned. The ridges folded wrong.

That wrongness slowed him more than the cold.

In known country, the body chose before the eye had finished. Here every hollow had to be read. A laurel tunnel that looked useful ended in a rock fall. A creek that should have bent north cut west between stone walls. One ridge promised cover and gave open oak leaves where a man could be seen from below. Jake lost time correcting for ground he had never walked.

Red Hollow's men would lose time too.

That was the hope.

Hope was not enough to stand on.

Unfamiliar country.

Useful country.

Dangerous country.

He found no friendly sign in it.

No old cut marks from Thorn. No bent sapling from a known trail. No hidden cache under stone. No shelter whose bark he had set himself. The ground offered only what it offered all men: water if he found it, cover if he earned it, death if he stepped wrong.

His feet hurt from wet moccasins. His legs had begun to shake on climbs. Twice he stopped to listen and heard nothing but his own breath. That was not comfort. Silence could mean distance. It could also mean men moving well.

He chose a line toward lower water because thirst had become louder than caution.

At the first seep, he drank with one hand on the knife and his eyes on the brush. Then he muddied the place with a branch, not enough to hide use from a master tracker, enough to blur the shape of his knees.

He did not drink his fill.

A full belly of cold water could slow a man and turn his gut against him on a climb. He took enough to wet his mouth, enough to swallow one thread of dried meat, and no more. The seep smelled of iron and leaf rot. Good enough. He filled no vessel because he had none worth showing and because a dripping trail could speak as plainly as a footprint.

Before leaving, he put one broken twig back where his knee had moved it.

The twig did not fit right.

He turned it twice, then left it. Too much mending became another kind of sign.

Behind him, once, faint and far, the ash name rose again.

Whispering Ash.

Jake did not answer.

He went on with one hand near the hidden flint and the other brushing leaves back into place behind him.

He was out.

He was hunted.

And the country ahead owed him nothing.
