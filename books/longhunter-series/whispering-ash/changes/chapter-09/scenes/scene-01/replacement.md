The fog off the river had not yet cleared when the first shot broke the line.

It was a single, flat crack that sounded small against the rush of the water, but the lead horse in the column went down at once, its front legs buckling into the muddy bank. The rider, a young scout from the lower village, was thrown forward into the grey willow brush before he could shout.

Then the ridge above them came alive with fire.

It was not a clean fight. The war party had been moving in a narrow file, three dozen men strung out between the steep clay bluff and the river, their horses tired from the night march. In the first three minutes, the formation ceased to exist. Smoke from black powder drifted low under the trees, thick and smelling of rotten eggs, blinding the men as they tried to turn their mounts.

Jake dropped from his horse before the animal could panic. He kept his head low, one hand on the rifle's lock to keep the priming dry in the damp air.

Around him, the raid collapsed.

A horse screamed, a high, thin sound that ended in a wet rattle. Men were shouting names in the brush—cries of warning that turned into groans when the arrows found them. Jake saw two warriors from the hawk clan try to climb the clay bank, their fingers digging into the wet roots, but a volley of buckshot from the ridge swept them clean, sending them rolling back into the mud at his feet.

"Hold the line!" Red Hollow's voice rose above the noise, hard and heavy as iron. "To the timber! Back to the timber!"

The War Chief was on his feet, his shirt torn at the shoulder, swinging a long-barreled trade gun toward the smoke on the ridge. His face was smeared with black soot, his eyes wide and bright with the fury of a man who had never planned for retreat. But the warriors were already scattering. The retreat was not a maneuver; it was a rout.

Jake did not look for a target. This was the moment he had waited for through the long weeks of silent preparation, the one clean crack in the stone.

He backed into the willows, his shoulders low, using the white smoke of the enemy's rifles as a curtain.

The brush was thick here, catching at his hunting shirt, but he worked through it with the short, sideways steps Thorn had drilled into him. To run made noise, shook the branches, and left deep heels in the mud. He stepped only where the willow roots offered solid wood, or where the gravel of the riverbank remained clear of grass.

Behind him, the shouting grew more distant, though the firing continued in irregular pops.

He reached the base of the bluff where the clay gave way to sandstone. A small, dry creek bed cut through the stone—a seam of shadow that led upward into the heavy timber of the ridge. The entrance was choked with dead briars and hemlock boughs.

Jake stopped. He listened.

The wind was coming from the river, carrying the smell of gunsmoke and wet mud. Beneath it, he heard the heavy, rhythmic crashing of horses through the undergrowth—men fleeing wild, leaving trails a child could follow. Red Hollow would be trying to gather them, but the scouts on the ridge would already be moving down to scalp the dead and hunt the stragglers.

Jake reached into the hollow oak at the creek's mouth. His fingers found the oiled deer-hide packet he had hidden there three nights before.

He pulled it free. Inside was his horn, his extra lead, two dry shirts, and the ash cloak.

The wool of the cloak was heavy, dyed the grey-brown of winter oak bark. He threw it over his shoulders and drew the hood low. The color matched the sandstone seam perfectly. He stepped into the dry bed, his moccasins silent on the gravel, and began to climb.

He did not look back.

The timber on top of the ridge was old growth, hemlock and chestnut, where the ground was covered in a thick carpet of needles that swallowed all sound. Here, the air was cold and still. The battle below had subsided into sporadic shots, the hunters now searching the flats.

Jake moved through the trees, a silent shadow.

He kept to the low ground between the ridges, avoiding the skyline where a man’s shape would stand out against the grey clouds. He used the trunks of the great chestnuts for cover, never stepping into the open without checking the branches above and the ground ahead.

Thorn had always said: *The forest does not watch you. The forest watches itself. If you move, move with the wind through the leaves.*

He heard the first signal three miles north of the river.

It was the cry of a scrub jay, sharp and repeated twice. But the pitch was too high, the rhythm too steady. A scout from the war party was signaling to another. They were searching the timber, looking for the men who had broken away from the main group.

Jake froze against the trunk of a fallen hemlock. He pulled the ash cloak tight, letting the grey wool blend into the mossy bark of the log.

A minute later, a scout passed within twenty yards.

The man was moving fast, his eyes scanning the ground for tracks, a light trade gun held across his chest. He was one of Red Hollow’s men, a tracker from the upper town who knew how to read bent grass and broken twigs. He tipped his head, listening.

Jake held his breath, his hand steady on the stock of his rifle. He did not move a muscle.

The scout stood still for what seemed a full hour, then turned toward the jay’s call and disappeared into the laurel.

Jake waited until the birds began to chatter naturally again before he stood. The pursuit was organized, and Red Hollow would not stop until he had accounted for every man who had marched with him. Jake was no longer part of the war party; he was a deserter in a territory that belonged to neither side.

He turned north, away from the settlements, toward the high, broken ridges where the map ended.

The sun went down behind a screen of grey pine, leaving the forest cold and dark. He had no fire, no shelter, and three days of parched corn in his pouch. The country was strange to him, all steep rock and dry runs, but he kept moving.

He was alone now, and the hunt had begun.
